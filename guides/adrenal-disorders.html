Adrenal Gland Physiology

The adrenal glands are small, triangular endocrine glands sitting on top of each kidney. Each gland has two distinct regions that function as separate endocrine organs:

Adrenal Cortex (Outer Layer)

Produces three classes of steroid hormones — remember "GFR" or "Salt, Sugar, Sex":

Zone Hormone Mnemonic Key Functions
Zona Glomerulosa Mineralocorticoids (Aldosterone) "Salt" Retains sodium & water, excretes potassium. Regulated by RAAS. Controls blood pressure and fluid volume.
Zona Fasciculata Glucocorticoids (Cortisol) "Sugar" Raises blood glucose, anti-inflammatory, immunosuppressive, stress response, protein/fat metabolism.
Zona Reticularis Androgens (DHEA) "Sex" Precursors to sex hormones. Minor role in adults but significant in congenital adrenal hyperplasia.

Adrenal Medulla (Inner Layer)

Produces catecholamines: epinephrine (80%) and norepinephrine (20%). Part of the sympathetic nervous system ("fight or flight"). Relevant in pheochromocytoma.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Cortisol Does Everything — Know It, Know Both Diseases
If you understand what cortisol does, you can predict every symptom of both Cushing's and Addison's. Cortisol: (1) raises blood glucose, (2) retains sodium and water, (3) suppresses immune system, (4) breaks down protein and redistributes fat, (5) increases gastric acid. Too MUCH cortisol (Cushing's) = hyperglycemia, hypertension, immunosuppression, muscle wasting, central obesity. Too LITTLE cortisol (Addison's) = hypoglycemia, hypotension, prone to infection, muscle weakness, weight loss. Opposites in every way.

The HPA Axis

Cortisol is regulated by the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, a negative feedback loop similar to the thyroid:

  • Hypothalamus releases CRH (corticotropin-releasing hormone)
  • Anterior pituitary releases ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone)
  • Adrenal cortex produces cortisol
  • High cortisol inhibits CRH and ACTH (negative feedback)
Key Concept: ACTH Levels Tell You WHERE the Problem Is

High ACTH + High cortisol = pituitary tumor making too much ACTH (Cushing's disease). Low ACTH + High cortisol = adrenal tumor making cortisol independently (pituitary is suppressed). High ACTH + Low cortisol = primary adrenal failure (Addison's — pituitary is yelling at failing adrenals). Low ACTH + Low cortisol = pituitary failure or steroid suppression (secondary adrenal insufficiency).

Bottom Line: Adrenal cortex makes cortisol (sugar), aldosterone (salt), and androgens (sex). Adrenal medulla makes catecholamines. Know cortisol's functions and you can predict both Cushing's and Addison's. ACTH levels differentiate the source of the problem.

Cushing's Syndrome

Cushing's syndrome results from prolonged exposure to excessive cortisol from any source. It is a clinical syndrome, not a single disease — understanding the cause determines the treatment.

Causes (Most Common to Least)

  1. Iatrogenic (exogenous steroids) — #1 cause overall. Long-term prednisone, dexamethasone, hydrocortisone for conditions like asthma, RA, lupus, transplant
  2. Pituitary adenoma (Cushing's disease) — #1 endogenous cause. ACTH-producing pituitary tumor (70% of endogenous cases)
  3. Adrenal tumor — Adrenal adenoma or carcinoma producing cortisol independently
  4. Ectopic ACTH production — Non-pituitary tumor (small cell lung cancer) producing ACTH
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Iatrogenic Cushing's: Always Ask About Steroids
The #1 cause of Cushing's is NOT a tumor — it's prescription steroids. Before working up a patient for endogenous Cushing's, always ask: "Are you taking any steroid medications?" This includes oral prednisone, inhaled steroids at high doses, topical steroids over large areas, and steroid injections. Many patients don't think to mention these. Iatrogenic Cushing's is treated by gradually tapering the offending medication, not surgery.

Signs and Symptoms

System Manifestation Why (Cortisol Effect)
Body Habitus Moon face, buffalo hump, truncal obesity with thin extremities Fat redistribution to face/trunk; protein catabolism in limbs
Skin Purple/dark striae, thin fragile skin, easy bruising, poor wound healing, hirsutism, acne Protein breakdown weakens connective tissue; androgen excess
Cardiovascular Hypertension, edema Sodium/water retention from cortisol's mineralocorticoid effect
Metabolic Hyperglycemia (steroid-induced diabetes), hyperlipidemia Cortisol increases gluconeogenesis and insulin resistance
Musculoskeletal Muscle wasting, weakness, osteoporosis, pathologic fractures Protein catabolism; calcium loss from bones
Immune Frequent infections, delayed wound healing Cortisol suppresses WBC function and inflammatory response
Psychiatric Mood swings, depression, insomnia, psychosis CNS effects of excess cortisol
Electrolytes Hypokalemia, hypernatremia Cortisol retains sodium, wastes potassium
Memory Aid: Cushing's Features

Think "Cushing's = Cortisol CUSHION" — the body accumulates fat like a cushion in the face (moon face), upper back (buffalo hump), and trunk. But the limbs are thin from muscle wasting. The skin is so fragile it tears and bruises easily, leaving purple stretch marks (striae).

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Cushing's Patients May Not Show Infection Signs
This is a critical safety concept. Excess cortisol suppresses the immune system so effectively that Cushing's patients may NOT develop fever, elevated WBC, or typical inflammatory signs even with a serious infection. A "normal" temperature or WBC in a Cushing's patient should raise suspicion, not reassurance. Monitor for subtle signs: new confusion, increased fatigue, tachycardia, or poor wound healing. Infection is a leading cause of mortality in Cushing's syndrome.

Diagnostic Tests

  • 24-hour urine free cortisol: Elevated (> 3x normal confirms Cushing's)
  • Late-night salivary cortisol: Should be low at night; elevated in Cushing's (loss of diurnal rhythm)
  • Low-dose dexamethasone suppression test: Give dexamethasone (synthetic steroid) at night → measure AM cortisol. Normal = cortisol suppressed. Cushing's = cortisol NOT suppressed
  • ACTH level: Differentiates the cause (high ACTH = pituitary/ectopic; low ACTH = adrenal tumor)
  • AM cortisol: Cortisol is highest in the morning (6-8 AM) due to diurnal rhythm. Draw blood before 9 AM for accurate baseline.
Bottom Line: Cushing's = too much cortisol. #1 cause is iatrogenic steroids. Classic features: moon face, buffalo hump, purple striae, hypertension, hyperglycemia, easy bruising, immunosuppression. Infection may be masked. Diagnose with 24-hr urine cortisol and dexamethasone suppression test.

Cushing's Treatment

Treatment targets the underlying cause of excess cortisol production.

Treatment by Cause

1
Iatrogenic (Exogenous Steroid Use) — Most Common

Gradually taper the corticosteroid over weeks to months. NEVER stop abruptly — the HPA axis is suppressed and needs time to recover. Reduce to the lowest effective dose or switch to alternate-day dosing. Consider steroid-sparing agents for the underlying condition.

2
Pituitary Adenoma (Cushing's Disease)

Transsphenoidal surgery (through the nose/sphenoid sinus to remove the pituitary tumor) is first-line. Success rate 65-90%. Post-op: monitor for diabetes insipidus (excessive urination, thirst), cortisol deficiency, and other pituitary hormone deficits. Radiation therapy if surgery fails.

3
Adrenal Tumor

Adrenalectomy (surgical removal of affected adrenal gland). Unilateral for adenoma, bilateral may be needed for bilateral disease. After bilateral adrenalectomy, patient needs lifelong cortisol AND aldosterone replacement.

4
Medical Management (Adjunct or Inoperable)

Ketoconazole, metyrapone, or mitotane — inhibit cortisol synthesis. Used when surgery is not possible or as a bridge to surgery. Monitor liver function (ketoconazole), adrenal function, and electrolytes.

Nursing Management for Cushing's

Priority Nursing Interventions
  • Infection prevention: Meticulous hand hygiene, avoid crowds, monitor for subtle infection signs (patients may not spike a fever)
  • Fall prevention: Muscle wasting + osteoporosis = high fracture risk. Assist with ambulation, remove hazards
  • Skin care: Handle gently (fragile skin bruises and tears easily). Use paper tape, avoid adhesive removal trauma
  • Blood glucose monitoring: Steroid-induced hyperglycemia is common; may need insulin coverage
  • Blood pressure monitoring: Hypertension from sodium/water retention. Low-sodium diet
  • Emotional support: Body image changes (moon face, weight gain, hirsutism) cause significant distress
  • Diet: High protein (to counteract muscle wasting), high calcium/vitamin D (to protect bones), low sodium, low calorie
Bottom Line: Cushing's treatment = remove the cause. Iatrogenic: taper steroids slowly. Pituitary: transsphenoidal surgery. Adrenal: adrenalectomy. Nursing priorities: infection prevention, fall prevention, skin care, glucose monitoring, and emotional support for body image changes.

Addison's Disease (Primary Adrenal Insufficiency)

Addison's disease results from destruction of the adrenal cortex, causing deficiency of ALL three adrenal cortex hormones: cortisol, aldosterone, and androgens. It is the opposite of Cushing's in every clinical manifestation.

Causes

  • Autoimmune adrenalitis — #1 cause in developed countries (80% of cases). Immune system destroys adrenal cortex
  • Tuberculosis — #1 cause worldwide
  • Bilateral adrenalectomy
  • Adrenal hemorrhage (Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome in meningococcal sepsis)
  • Metastatic cancer to adrenals
  • Infections (HIV, fungal)

Signs and Symptoms

System Manifestation Why (Hormone Deficiency)
Skin Bronze hyperpigmentation (especially skin folds, knuckles, scars, buccal mucosa) Elevated ACTH stimulates melanocytes (ACTH rises because cortisol is low)
Cardiovascular Hypotension, orthostatic hypotension, weak pulse Aldosterone deficiency → sodium/water loss
Metabolic Hypoglycemia (especially during fasting or stress) Cortisol deficiency → impaired gluconeogenesis
Electrolytes Hyponatremia, hyperkalemia Aldosterone deficiency → cannot retain sodium or excrete potassium
GI Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, salt craving Sodium loss drives salt craving; cortisol deficiency causes GI symptoms
General Fatigue, weakness, weight loss, anorexia Loss of cortisol's metabolic and energy-sustaining effects
Musculoskeletal Muscle weakness, joint/back pain Cortisol and androgen deficiency
Memory Aid: Addison's Key Features

"Addison's = ADD what's missing" — The body needs to ADD back cortisol (hydrocortisone) and aldosterone (fludrocortisone). The patient is losing sodium (hyponatremia), losing blood pressure (hypotension), losing glucose (hypoglycemia), and gaining potassium (hyperkalemia). Bronze skin is the hallmark visual finding.

Diagnostic Tests

  • AM cortisol level: Low (< 3 mcg/dL is highly suggestive)
  • ACTH stimulation test (cosyntropin test): Gold standard. Give synthetic ACTH → measure cortisol response. In Addison's, cortisol does NOT rise (adrenals cannot respond). Normal response rules out primary adrenal insufficiency.
  • ACTH level: Elevated in primary Addison's (pituitary trying to stimulate failing adrenals)
  • Electrolytes: Hyponatremia, hyperkalemia
  • Adrenal antibodies: Present in autoimmune Addison's
Bottom Line: Addison's = too little cortisol + aldosterone. Bronze skin, hypotension, hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, hypoglycemia, salt craving. #1 cause: autoimmune. Diagnose with ACTH stimulation test (cortisol won't rise). Electrolyte triad: low Naâº, high Kâº, low glucose.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Addison's Patient Teaching: The Survival Essentials
Addison's patients depend on their medication for survival — missing doses can be fatal. Teach: (1) NEVER skip or stop medication — there is no cortisol reserve. (2) Wear a medical alert bracelet at all times. (3) Carry an emergency injection kit (IM hydrocortisone 100 mg) and teach family how to use it. (4) Stress dosing: double the hydrocortisone dose for minor illness (cold, flu), triple for severe illness, and get IV hydrocortisone for surgery or major trauma. (5) High-sodium diet (to compensate for aldosterone deficiency). (6) Report any vomiting (can't absorb oral meds → need injectable form).
Nursing Alert: Steroid Tapering

Never stop corticosteroids abruptly. Long-term exogenous steroid use (even 2+ weeks at high doses) suppresses the HPA axis. The adrenal glands atrophy and cannot produce cortisol on their own. Abrupt withdrawal can trigger a life-threatening adrenal crisis. Always taper gradually under provider guidance. This applies to ALL patients on chronic steroids, not just Addison's patients.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Never Stop Steroids Abruptly
This is one of the most tested steroid concepts. When a patient takes exogenous corticosteroids for more than ~2 weeks, the HPA axis is suppressed — the pituitary stops making ACTH and the adrenal glands atrophy. If steroids are stopped suddenly, the adrenals cannot produce cortisol on demand. The patient develops acute adrenal insufficiency (adrenal crisis): hypotension, shock, and potentially death. Always taper slowly and teach patients to never run out of their medication.
Bottom Line: Addison's = lifelong replacement: hydrocortisone (â…” AM, â…“ PM) + fludrocortisone. NEVER stop steroids abruptly. Stress dose for illness (double/triple). Medical alert bracelet + IM hydrocortisone kit. High-sodium diet.

Adrenal Crisis (Addisonian Crisis)

Adrenal crisis is a life-threatening emergency caused by acute cortisol deficiency. It can occur in known Addison's patients or in patients on chronic steroids who experience abrupt withdrawal or physiologic stress without adequate steroid coverage. Mortality is high without rapid treatment.

Triggers

  • Abrupt withdrawal of corticosteroids — most common preventable cause
  • Infection, illness, or physiologic stress in a patient with adrenal insufficiency
  • Failure to increase steroid dose during stress (surgery, trauma, illness)
  • Bilateral adrenal hemorrhage (meningococcal sepsis — Waterhouse-Friderichsen)
  • Pituitary apoplexy (sudden pituitary infarction/hemorrhage)
  • Vomiting/diarrhea preventing oral steroid absorption
Nursing Alert: Recognizing Adrenal Crisis
  • Profound hypotension / circulatory shock (refractory to fluids and vasopressors)
  • Severe hyponatremia
  • Hyperkalemia (risk of cardiac arrhythmias)
  • Hypoglycemia
  • Altered mental status → confusion → loss of consciousness
  • Severe abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting (can mimic acute abdomen)
  • High fever (if triggered by infection)
  • Severe dehydration

Emergency Treatment

Adrenal Crisis Management Protocol

1
IV Hydrocortisone 100 mg STAT

Do NOT wait for lab confirmation. If adrenal crisis is suspected, treat immediately. Hydrocortisone at stress doses also has mineralocorticoid activity, so it addresses both cortisol and aldosterone deficiency. Continue 50-100 mg IV every 6-8 hours, then taper as patient stabilizes.

2
Aggressive IV Normal Saline (0.9% NS)

Large-volume NS bolus to correct hypotension, dehydration, and hyponatremia. May need 1-3 liters in first few hours. Add D5NS if hypoglycemic. Monitor for fluid overload in elderly.

3
Correct Hypoglycemia

IV dextrose (D50W) if blood glucose is critically low. Monitor glucose frequently. Cortisol replacement will help normalize glucose over time.

4
Monitor and Correct Electrolytes

Monitor potassium closely (hyperkalemia risk for cardiac arrhythmias). Cardiac monitoring. Sodium will improve with NS and hydrocortisone. Do NOT give potassium-containing IV fluids (patient is already hyperkalemic).

5
Identify and Treat the Trigger

Was it steroid non-compliance? Infection? Surgery? Address the precipitating cause while stabilizing the patient.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Adrenal Crisis: Don't Wait for Labs
Adrenal crisis is a clinical diagnosis treated emergently. If you suspect it (hypotension + known adrenal insufficiency or steroid use + any trigger), give IV hydrocortisone 100 mg immediately. Waiting for cortisol levels to confirm the diagnosis before treatment can be fatal. You can always draw a cortisol level BEFORE giving the hydrocortisone (it won't delay treatment), but don't wait for results to act. The treatment (hydrocortisone + IV NS) is safe and can be life-saving.
Bottom Line: Adrenal crisis = hypotension + hyponatremia + hyperkalemia + altered LOC. Treat immediately: IV hydrocortisone 100 mg STAT + IV NS bolus. Don't wait for labs. No potassium-containing fluids. Most common preventable cause: abrupt steroid withdrawal.

Pheochromocytoma

Pheochromocytoma ("pheo") is a rare catecholamine-producing tumor of the adrenal medulla. It releases massive amounts of epinephrine and norepinephrine, causing severe, paroxysmal (episodic) hypertension and sympathetic nervous system activation. 90% are benign; 10% are malignant.

Memory Aid: The 5 H's of Pheochromocytoma
  • Hypertension (severe, paroxysmal or sustained — can exceed 250/150 mmHg)
  • Headache (severe, pounding — from acute hypertension)
  • Hyperhidrosis (profuse sweating)
  • Hypermetabolism (tachycardia, palpitations, tremors, anxiety, weight loss)
  • Hyperglycemia (catecholamines stimulate glycogenolysis)

Diagnosis

  • 24-hour urine for metanephrines and catecholamines: Gold standard. Elevated levels confirm diagnosis.
  • Plasma free metanephrines: Highly sensitive screening test
  • CT/MRI of adrenals: Localize the tumor after biochemical confirmation

Treatment

Critical: Alpha-Blocker BEFORE Beta-Blocker

Pre-operative preparation (10-14 days before surgery):

  • Step 1: Alpha-adrenergic blocker FIRST — Phenoxybenzamine (Dibenzyline) to control hypertension by blocking alpha receptors
  • Step 2: Beta-blocker AFTER alpha blockade is established — Propranolol to control tachycardia
  • NEVER give a beta-blocker ALONE without prior alpha blockade. Beta-blockers block vasodilation (beta-2 effect) while leaving alpha vasoconstriction unopposed → severe hypertensive crisis and potential stroke
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Alpha Before Beta — Always
This is one of the most tested pharmacology concepts for pheochromocytoma. Think of it like controlling a fire: alpha-blockers open the escape route (vasodilation), then beta-blockers slow the fire (heart rate). If you use beta-blockers alone, you slam shut the escape route (beta-2 vasodilation blocked) while the fire (alpha vasoconstriction) rages unopposed — blood pressure can skyrocket to stroke-level. Remember: "A before B" = Alpha before Beta.

Surgical Treatment

Adrenalectomy (surgical removal of the tumor) is the definitive treatment. Key surgical considerations:

  • Must have adequate alpha blockade for 10-14 days pre-op
  • High-sodium diet and liberal fluids pre-op (to expand volume after chronic vasoconstriction)
  • Avoid activities that increase intra-abdominal pressure (can cause catecholamine release)
  • Intraoperative: severe BP fluctuations expected during tumor manipulation
  • Post-op: risk of severe hypotension (catecholamine source removed) and hypoglycemia (rebound from hyperglycemia)
Bottom Line: Pheochromocytoma = catecholamine tumor causing the 5 H's. Diagnose with 24-hr urine metanephrines. Pre-op: alpha-blocker FIRST (phenoxybenzamine), THEN beta-blocker. NEVER beta-block alone. Definitive treatment: adrenalectomy. Post-op: watch for hypotension and hypoglycemia.

Adrenalectomy Nursing Care

Pre-Operative

  • Correct electrolyte imbalances and optimize fluid status
  • For pheochromocytoma: ensure adequate alpha-blockade for 10-14 days
  • For Cushing's: optimize glucose control, address infection risk
  • Pre-operative stress-dose steroids may be ordered

Post-Operative Priorities

Post-Adrenalectomy Nursing Care

1
Hemodynamic Monitoring

Vital signs every 15-30 minutes initially. Watch for hypotension (especially after pheo removal — catecholamine source is gone) and hypertension rebound (if cortisol-producing tumor removed). Vasopressors may be needed.

2
Steroid Replacement

After bilateral adrenalectomy: patient needs lifelong cortisol AND aldosterone replacement. After unilateral adrenalectomy: the remaining adrenal may be suppressed and may need temporary steroid support until HPA axis recovers (weeks to months).

3
Blood Glucose Monitoring

After Cushing's surgery: glucose may drop as cortisol levels fall (no longer driving hyperglycemia). After pheo surgery: rebound hypoglycemia (no longer catecholamine-driven). Monitor closely.

4
Electrolyte Monitoring

Sodium and potassium levels may shift rapidly. Watch for signs of adrenal insufficiency: hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, hypotension.

5
Wound Care & Infection Prevention

Cushing's patients have impaired wound healing and high infection risk due to prior cortisol excess. Monitor incision closely.

Bottom Line: Post-adrenalectomy: monitor BP closely (hypotension after pheo, shifts after cortisol tumor). Bilateral = lifelong steroid replacement. Monitor glucose (rebound changes). Watch electrolytes. Cushing's patients have poor wound healing.

Master Comparison: Cushing's vs Addison's

Cushing's Syndrome (Excess Cortisol)
  • Moon face, buffalo hump, truncal obesity
  • Hypertension
  • Hyperglycemia
  • Hypernatremia
  • Hypokalemia
  • Weight gain (central obesity)
  • Purple striae, easy bruising
  • Immunosuppression
  • Osteoporosis
  • Mood swings, depression
  • #1 cause: exogenous steroids
  • Tx: remove cause (taper/surgery)
Addison's Disease (Cortisol Deficiency)
  • Bronze hyperpigmentation
  • Hypotension
  • Hypoglycemia
  • Hyponatremia
  • Hyperkalemia
  • Weight loss, anorexia
  • Salt craving
  • Fatigue, weakness
  • GI symptoms (N/V/D)
  • Depression, irritability
  • #1 cause: autoimmune
  • Tx: lifelong replacement
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Ultimate Shortcut: Everything Is Opposite
On the exam, if you know Cushing's, you automatically know Addison's — just flip everything. Cushing's has HIGH blood pressure, HIGH glucose, HIGH sodium. Addison's has LOW blood pressure, LOW glucose, LOW sodium. Cushing's retains sodium and wastes potassium. Addison's wastes sodium and retains potassium. Cushing's causes weight gain. Addison's causes weight loss. The only thing they share is that both can cause mood changes and both are endocrine emergencies if severe.

Blood Pressure ↑ Hypertension ↓ Hypotension
Blood Glucose ↑ Hyperglycemia ↓ Hypoglycemia
Sodium ↑ Hypernatremia ↓ Hyponatremia
Potassium ↓ Hypokalemia ↑ Hyperkalemia

Bottom Line: Cushing's and Addison's are mirror images. Everything in Cushing's is HIGH (BP, glucose, sodium) while everything in Addison's is LOW (except potassium). Know one, flip everything, and you know the other.

Pediatric Considerations

Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (CAH)

CAH is the most common adrenal disorder in children — an autosomal recessive enzyme deficiency (most commonly 21-hydroxylase) that impairs cortisol and aldosterone synthesis.

  • Newborn screening is mandatory in all 50 states (17-hydroxyprogesterone level)
  • Without cortisol feedback, ACTH rises → adrenal hyperplasia → excess androgen production
  • Salt-wasting form: Life-threatening in first weeks of life (adrenal crisis with hyponatremia, hyperkalemia, dehydration)
  • Virilizing form: Ambiguous genitalia in females at birth; early puberty signs in males
  • Treatment: lifelong glucocorticoid replacement (hydrocortisone) + mineralocorticoid (fludrocortisone) for salt-wasting form
  • Stress dosing is critical during illness, injury, or surgery
Steroid Use in Children
  • Children on chronic steroids (asthma, nephrotic syndrome, IBD) are at risk for growth suppression — monitor growth charts closely
  • Even inhaled corticosteroids at high doses can suppress the HPA axis in children
  • Cushing's features may develop: weight gain, moon face, behavioral changes
  • Stress dosing required during intercurrent illness for children on chronic steroids
  • Adrenal crisis in children often presents with hypoglycemia more prominently than in adults (smaller glycogen stores)
Bottom Line: CAH is the most common pediatric adrenal disorder — newborn screening is mandatory. Salt-wasting CAH is life-threatening in neonates. Children on chronic steroids: monitor growth, never stop abruptly, stress dose for illness. Pediatric adrenal crisis often presents with prominent hypoglycemia.
