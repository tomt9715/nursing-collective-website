Adrenal Disorders

Two diseases that are the same disease inverted, one emergency you treat before the labs come back, and the drug order that kills people when you get it the wrong way round.

By the end, without notes

  • Predict every sign of both diseases from what cortisol does
  • Read an ACTH and a cortisol together and say where the problem sits

01

What actually gets you

This topic looks like two long symptom lists to memorise. It is not. It is one hormone, and two directions.

Learn what cortisol does and you can derive both diseases. Points get lost here in three predictable ways.

You memorised two lists instead of one hormone

Cortisol raises glucose. It retains sodium and water. It suppresses immunity. It breaks down protein and moves fat centrally.

Too much of that is Cushing's. Too little is Addison's. Every sign in both diseases falls straight out of those four actions.

You waited for the cortisol level

Adrenal crisis is a clinical diagnosis. Hypotension in a patient with adrenal insufficiency, or on chronic steroids, is enough.

You can draw the cortisol first, because that takes seconds. You cannot wait for the result. Patients die during that wait.

You forgot that steroids are the commonest cause

The most common cause of Cushing's is not a tumour. It is prednisone.

And the most common preventable cause of adrenal crisis is stopping those same steroids too fast. One drug class sits at both ends of this guide.

Bottom Line: Learn cortisol's four actions, then run them up and down. Treat crisis on suspicion. And always ask what steroids the patient is on.
02

The four things

For every condition below, you should be able to say four things with the book closed: the one-sentence definition, the clue you would actually see in a patient, the priority nursing action, and the first-line treatment with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Condition Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line treatment
Cushing's syndrome Too much cortisol, from any source. Moon face, buffalo hump, thin limbs, purple striae, high glucose. Prevent infection and prevent falls. Remove the cause. Taper the steroid, or operate. Infection may be silent.
Addison's disease The adrenal cortex is destroyed, so cortisol and aldosterone are both lost. Bronze skin, hypotension, low sodium, high potassium, salt craving. Fluid status and blood pressure. Hydrocortisone plus fludrocortisone, for life. Never skip a dose.

Write these four rows on one index card, by hand. Too much cortisol, too little cortisol, none at all, and the tumour that is not about cortisol. That card is most of the exam.

03

One hormone, four actions

The adrenal glands sit on top of the kidneys. Each one is really two organs stacked together.

The cortex, on the outside

Three layers, three hormone classes. Salt, sugar, sex, from outside in.

LayerHormoneNicknameWhat it does
Zona glomerulosa Mineralocorticoids, meaning aldosterone Salt Holds on to sodium and water, gets rid of potassium. Controlled by RAAS. Sets blood pressure and volume.
Zona fasciculata Glucocorticoids, meaning cortisol Sugar Raises glucose, suppresses inflammation and immunity, drives the stress response, breaks down protein and fat.
Zona reticularis Androgens, meaning DHEA Sex Precursors to sex hormones. Minor in adults, major in congenital adrenal hyperplasia.

The medulla, on the inside

Makes catecholamines: epinephrine at about 80 percent and norepinephrine at about 20. It is functionally part of the sympathetic nervous system, which is why a medulla tumour looks like permanent fight or flight.

The four actions that generate both diseases

Cortisol raises blood glucose. It retains sodium and water. It suppresses the immune system. It breaks down protein and redistributes fat toward the trunk.

Run those up: hyperglycemia, hypertension, infections you cannot see, wasted limbs with a full face. That is Cushing's.

Run those down: hypoglycemia, hypotension, weakness, weight loss. That is Addison's.

You do not need two symptom lists. You need one hormone and a direction.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Derive It, Do Not Memorise It
If you can state cortisol's four actions, you can produce every sign of both diseases on demand. That is faster than memorising twenty findings, and it survives a question you have not seen before.

The HPA axis

Cortisol is controlled by a negative feedback loop, the same shape as the thyroid axis.

The hypothalamus releases CRH. The anterior pituitary releases ACTH. The adrenal cortex makes cortisol. High cortisol then shuts down CRH and ACTH.

ACTH plus cortisol tells you where the fault is
  • High ACTH, high cortisol. A pituitary tumour making too much ACTH. That is Cushing's disease.
  • Low ACTH, high cortisol. An adrenal tumour making cortisol on its own, with the pituitary correctly suppressed.
  • High ACTH, low cortisol. Primary adrenal failure, which is Addison's. The pituitary is shouting at adrenals that cannot answer.
  • Low ACTH, low cortisol. Pituitary failure, or suppression from exogenous steroids. That is secondary adrenal insufficiency.

Read the two numbers together and the location falls out. Read either alone and it does not.

Bottom Line: Cortex makes salt, sugar and sex. Medulla makes catecholamines. Cortisol's four actions generate both diseases, and the ACTH level tells you which organ is at fault.
04

Too much cortisol

Cushing's syndrome is prolonged exposure to excess cortisol, from any source. It is a syndrome rather than one disease, and the source decides the treatment.

Where it comes from

  1. 1
    Iatrogenic, meaning prescribed steroids. The commonest cause overall. Long-term prednisone, dexamethasone or hydrocortisone for asthma, rheumatoid arthritis, lupus or transplant.
  2. 2
    Pituitary adenoma. The commonest cause the body produces itself, about 70 percent of endogenous cases. An ACTH-secreting tumour. This one has its own name: Cushing's disease.
  3. 3
    Adrenal tumour. An adenoma or carcinoma making cortisol independently.
  4. 4
    Ectopic ACTH. A tumour somewhere else making ACTH, classically small cell lung cancer.
Ask about steroids before anything else

Before working anyone up for endogenous Cushing's, ask what steroids they take.

That includes oral prednisone, high-dose inhalers, topical steroids over large areas, and joint injections. Patients rarely volunteer any of these, because they do not think of a cream or an inhaler as a drug.

Iatrogenic Cushing's is treated by tapering, not by surgery. Getting the cause wrong means getting the whole plan wrong.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Commonest Cause Is a Prescription
Not a tumour. Prednisone. Ask about creams, inhalers and injections too, because patients do not count those as medication. The treatment is a taper, not an operating theatre.

What you see, and why

SystemWhat you findWhich cortisol action did it
Shape Moon face, buffalo hump, truncal obesity with thin limbs Fat moves centrally while protein breaks down in the limbs
Skin Purple striae, thin fragile skin, easy bruising, poor healing, hirsutism, acne Protein breakdown weakens connective tissue; androgen excess does the rest
Cardiovascular Hypertension and edema Cortisol's mineralocorticoid effect holds sodium and water
Metabolic Hyperglycemia, sometimes steroid-induced diabetes, and hyperlipidemia Gluconeogenesis rises and insulin resistance follows
Musculoskeletal Muscle wasting, weakness, osteoporosis, fractures Protein catabolism and calcium loss from bone
Immune Frequent infections, wounds that will not close Cortisol suppresses white cell function and inflammation
Psychiatric Mood swings, depression, insomnia, occasionally psychosis Direct CNS effect of excess cortisol
Electrolytes Hypokalemia and hypernatremia Sodium retained, potassium wasted
A normal temperature is not reassuring here

Excess cortisol suppresses the immune response so effectively that a Cushing's patient with a serious infection may have no fever and a normal white count.

So the usual signs are missing exactly when you most need them. Watch instead for new confusion, rising fatigue, tachycardia, or a wound that has stopped healing.

Infection is a leading cause of death in Cushing's syndrome, and this is why.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
No Fever Does Not Mean No Infection
In a Cushing's patient the absence of fever and a normal white count should raise your suspicion rather than settle it. Look for confusion, new tachycardia, and wounds that stop progressing.

How it is confirmed

  • 24-hour urine free cortisol. More than three times normal confirms it.
  • Late-night salivary cortisol. Should be low at night. In Cushing's the diurnal rhythm is lost, so it is not.
  • Low-dose dexamethasone suppression test. Give a synthetic steroid at night, measure cortisol in the morning. Normally it suppresses. In Cushing's it does not.
  • ACTH level. This is what separates the causes from each other.
  • Morning cortisol. Cortisol peaks between 6 and 8 in the morning, so draw before 9 for a usable baseline.
Bottom Line: Too much cortisol, usually from a prescription. Central fat and thin limbs, purple striae, high pressure and high glucose. Infection may be completely silent.
05

Treating too much

Treatment follows the cause. Find the source of the cortisol and remove it.

  1. 1
    Iatrogenic. Taper the steroid gradually over weeks to months. Never stop it abruptly; the HPA axis is suppressed and needs time to wake up. Reduce to the lowest effective dose, consider alternate-day dosing, and look for a steroid-sparing agent for the underlying condition.
  2. 2
    Pituitary adenoma. Transsphenoidal surgery, through the nose and sphenoid sinus, is first-line. Success runs 65 to 90 percent. Afterwards watch for diabetes insipidus, cortisol deficiency and other pituitary hormone losses. Radiation if surgery fails.
  3. 3
    Adrenal tumour. Adrenalectomy. Unilateral for an adenoma. After a bilateral adrenalectomy the patient needs cortisol and aldosterone replacement for life.
  4. 4
    Medical management. Ketoconazole, metyrapone or mitotane inhibit cortisol synthesis. Used when surgery is not possible, or as a bridge to it. Monitor liver function, adrenal function and electrolytes.
What the nursing actually is
  • Infection prevention. Hand hygiene, avoid crowds, and remember the usual signs may not appear.
  • Fall prevention. Wasted muscle plus osteoporosis is a fracture waiting to happen.
  • Skin. Handle gently. Paper tape. Adhesive removal tears this skin.
  • Glucose. Steroid-induced hyperglycemia is common and may need insulin cover.
  • Blood pressure. Sodium and water retention drives it up. Low sodium diet.
  • Body image. Moon face, weight gain and hirsutism cause real distress. Say so rather than working around it.
  • Diet. High protein against the wasting, high calcium and vitamin D for the bones, low sodium, low calorie.
Bottom Line: Remove the cause. Taper if it is a prescription, operate if it is a tumour. Nursing is infection, falls, skin, glucose and the way the patient feels about their face.
06

Too little cortisol

Discrimination axis: same hormone, opposite direction, and aldosterone goes with it.

Addison's disease is destruction of the adrenal cortex. All three cortex hormones are lost: cortisol, aldosterone and the androgens.

That second one matters. Cushing's is a cortisol problem. Addison's is a cortisol and aldosterone problem, and aldosterone is why the sodium and potassium go the way they do.

What destroys it

  • Autoimmune adrenalitis. The commonest cause in developed countries, about 80 percent.
  • Tuberculosis. The commonest cause worldwide.
  • Bilateral adrenalectomy.
  • Adrenal hemorrhage, including Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome in meningococcal sepsis.
  • Metastatic cancer.
  • Infection, including HIV and fungal disease.
SystemWhat you findWhich hormone is missing
Skin Bronze hyperpigmentation, worst in skin folds, knuckles, scars and inside the mouth Cortisol is low, so ACTH rises, and ACTH stimulates melanocytes
Cardiovascular Hypotension, orthostatic drops, a weak pulse Aldosterone deficiency loses sodium and water
Metabolic Hypoglycemia, especially fasting or under stress Cortisol deficiency impairs gluconeogenesis
Electrolytes Hyponatremia and hyperkalemia Without aldosterone, sodium is not held and potassium is not excreted
Gastrointestinal Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, and salt craving Sodium loss drives the craving; cortisol deficiency does the rest
General Fatigue, weakness, weight loss, anorexia Cortisol's metabolic and energy effects are gone
Musculoskeletal Muscle weakness, joint and back pain Cortisol and androgen deficiency
Why the skin goes bronze

This one confuses people, because it looks like a pigment problem in a hormone disease.

Low cortisol removes the brake on ACTH, so ACTH climbs. ACTH is structurally similar to melanocyte-stimulating hormone, so it drives pigment production as a side effect.

That is also why hyperpigmentation appears in primary Addison's and not in secondary adrenal insufficiency. In the secondary form ACTH is low, so there is nothing to stimulate the melanocytes.

How it is confirmed

  • Morning cortisol. Low. Under 3 mcg/dL is strongly suggestive.
  • ACTH stimulation test, the cosyntropin test. The gold standard. Give synthetic ACTH and see whether cortisol rises. In Addison's it cannot, because the adrenals are destroyed.
  • ACTH level. High in primary Addison's, because the pituitary is trying.
  • Electrolytes. Low sodium, high potassium.
  • Adrenal antibodies. Present in the autoimmune form.
Bottom Line: Cortisol and aldosterone both gone. Bronze skin, low pressure, low sodium, high potassium, low glucose, salt craving. Confirmed when ACTH cannot raise the cortisol.
07

Replacing what is gone

Treatment is lifelong hormone replacement. There is no reserve and no day off.

WhatDrugHow it is given
Cortisol replacementHydrocortisone, orallyTwo thirds in the morning, one third in the afternoon, to copy the natural rhythm
Aldosterone replacementFludrocortisone, orallyUsually once daily
DietHigh sodium, because aldosterone is not there to hold it
Stress dosingSame drug, more of itDouble for minor illness, triple for severe, intravenous for surgery or major trauma
The teaching that keeps them alive
  • Never skip a dose. There is no reserve to fall back on.
  • Wear a medical alert bracelet, always. Someone else may have to work this out.
  • Carry an emergency injection. 100 mg of intramuscular hydrocortisone, and teach the family to give it.
  • Stress dose. Double for a cold or flu, triple for serious illness, intravenous for surgery.
  • High sodium diet.
  • Report vomiting. A patient who cannot keep tablets down cannot absorb their cortisol, and needs the injectable form.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Vomiting Is an Emergency Here
For most patients, vomiting means a missed dose. For an Addison's patient it means no cortisol at all, during an illness that has already raised the requirement. That is the injectable kit's whole purpose.
Never stop steroids abruptly, in anyone

Two weeks or more of high-dose steroids suppresses the HPA axis. The pituitary stops making ACTH and the adrenals atrophy from disuse.

Stop the drug suddenly and there is nothing left to make cortisol on demand. The patient goes into acute adrenal insufficiency: hypotension, shock, and possibly death.

This applies to every patient on chronic steroids, not only to people with Addison's. Always taper, and teach patients never to run out.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Adrenals Atrophy From Disuse
Chronic steroids do the adrenals' job for them, and the gland shrinks. Take the drug away suddenly and there is no organ left to step in. That is the whole reason for the taper.
Bottom Line: Hydrocortisone in two doses plus fludrocortisone, for life. Never stop abruptly. Double or triple for illness. Bracelet, injection kit, high sodium diet.
08

Adrenal crisis

Acute cortisol deficiency causing shock. It happens in known Addison's patients, and in anyone on chronic steroids who stops them or meets a stress they are not covered for.

Mortality is high if treatment waits.

What sets it off

  • Stopping corticosteroids abruptly. The commonest preventable cause.
  • Infection, illness or physiological stress in someone with adrenal insufficiency.
  • Failure to increase the dose for surgery, trauma or illness.
  • Bilateral adrenal hemorrhage, as in meningococcal sepsis.
  • Pituitary apoplexy, meaning sudden pituitary infarction or hemorrhage.
  • Vomiting or diarrhea stopping oral steroid absorption.
What it looks like
  • Profound hypotension that does not respond to fluids or vasopressors
  • Severe hyponatremia
  • Hyperkalemia, with the arrhythmia risk that carries
  • Hypoglycemia
  • Altered mental status, from confusion to unconsciousness
  • Severe abdominal pain, nausea and vomiting, which can look like an acute abdomen
  • High fever if infection triggered it
  • Severe dehydration

Shock that will not respond to pressors is the finding that should make you think of this.

What you do

  1. 1
    IV hydrocortisone 100 mg now. Do not wait for lab confirmation. At stress doses hydrocortisone has mineralocorticoid activity too, so it covers both missing hormones. Continue 50 to 100 mg every 6 to 8 hours, then taper as the patient stabilises.
  2. 2
    Normal saline, and a lot of it. Large volume bolus for the hypotension, dehydration and low sodium. One to three litres in the first few hours is usual. Use D5NS if the glucose is low. Watch for overload in older patients.
  3. 3
    Correct the glucose. IV dextrose if it is critically low. Recheck often. The hydrocortisone will normalise it over time.
  4. 4
    Watch the potassium. Cardiac monitoring, because hyperkalemia causes arrhythmias. Sodium improves with the saline and the hydrocortisone. Give nothing containing potassium; the patient already has too much.
  5. 5
    Find the trigger. Missed doses, an infection, recent surgery. Treat the cause while you stabilise the patient.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Draw It, Then Give It
You can draw a cortisol level before giving the hydrocortisone; that costs seconds and preserves the diagnosis. What you cannot do is wait for the result. Hydrocortisone and saline are safe, and the delay is not.
Bottom Line: Hypotension, low sodium, high potassium, altered consciousness. IV hydrocortisone 100 mg and a saline bolus, before the labs. Nothing with potassium in it.
09

The tumour that is not about cortisol

Discrimination axis: medulla, not cortex, so catecholamines instead of steroids.

Pheochromocytoma is a catecholamine-producing tumour of the adrenal medulla. It releases epinephrine and norepinephrine in surges, so the hypertension comes in attacks. About 90 percent are benign.

The five H's
  • Hypertension. Severe, often paroxysmal, and it can exceed 250 over 150.
  • Headache. Severe and pounding, from the pressure itself.
  • Hyperhidrosis. Drenching sweats.
  • Hypermetabolism. Tachycardia, palpitations, tremor, anxiety, weight loss.
  • Hyperglycemia. Catecholamines break down glycogen.

All five are what adrenaline does. That is the only thing to remember here.

Finding it

  • 24-hour urine for metanephrines and catecholamines. The gold standard.
  • Plasma free metanephrines. A highly sensitive screen.
  • CT or MRI of the adrenals. To locate the tumour, after the biochemistry has confirmed it.

The drug order that matters

Alpha first, beta second, for 10 to 14 days

Step one: an alpha-blocker. Phenoxybenzamine, to bring the blood pressure down by blocking alpha receptors.

Step two: a beta-blocker, only once alpha blockade is established. Propranolol, for the tachycardia.

Never a beta-blocker alone. Beta-2 receptors mediate vasodilation. Block them while alpha-driven vasoconstriction is running unopposed and the blood pressure climbs to stroke levels.

The order is the answer. Alpha before beta.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Alpha Before Beta
Alpha-blockers open the escape route. Beta-blockers slow the heart. Give the beta-blocker first and you shut the escape route while the pressure is still rising. A alphabetically before B, and clinically before B too.

Surgery

Adrenalectomy is the definitive treatment. The preparation is what gets tested.

  • Alpha blockade for 10 to 14 days before the operation, without exception.
  • High sodium diet and liberal fluids beforehand, to refill a circulation that has been chronically vasoconstricted.
  • Avoid anything that raises intra-abdominal pressure, because squeezing the tumour releases catecholamines.
  • Expect violent blood pressure swings during the operation as the surgeon handles the tumour.
  • Afterwards, watch for severe hypotension, since the catecholamine source has gone, and hypoglycemia as the hyperglycemia rebounds.
Bottom Line: Five H's, all of them adrenaline. Urine metanephrines to diagnose. Alpha-blocker first and never beta alone. After surgery expect the pressure and the glucose to fall.
10

After the gland comes out

Before

  • Correct the electrolytes and optimise fluid status.
  • For pheochromocytoma, confirm 10 to 14 days of alpha blockade.
  • For Cushing's, get the glucose under control and deal with the infection risk.
  • Expect an order for stress-dose steroids.

After

  1. 1
    Blood pressure, closely. Vital signs every 15 to 30 minutes at first. Expect hypotension after a pheo, because the catecholamine source is gone. Expect shifts after a cortisol-producing tumour. Vasopressors may be needed.
  2. 2
    Steroid replacement. After a bilateral adrenalectomy the patient needs cortisol and aldosterone for life. After a unilateral one the remaining gland is often suppressed and needs temporary support until the axis recovers, which takes weeks to months.
  3. 3
    Glucose. After Cushing's surgery it falls, because nothing is driving it up. After pheo surgery it rebounds low. Monitor either way.
  4. 4
    Electrolytes. Sodium and potassium can move fast. Low sodium, high potassium and hypotension together mean adrenal insufficiency.
  5. 5
    The wound. Cushing's patients heal badly and get infected easily, and that does not resolve the moment the tumour is out.
Bottom Line: Watch the pressure, replace the steroids, follow the glucose down, and expect a wound that heals slowly.

The one row that does not invert

Potassium. Everything else in Cushing's is high and everything else in Addison's is low, so students flip potassium the same way and get it wrong.

Aldosterone excretes potassium. Cushing's has cortisol acting like aldosterone, so potassium goes down. Addison's has no aldosterone at all, so potassium climbs.

Say it as one line: sodium and potassium always move in opposite directions here.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Know One, Invert It
Cushing's is high pressure, high glucose, high sodium, low potassium, weight gain. Addison's is every one of those reversed. Learn one column properly and the other costs you nothing.
Write this down

Blank paper. Two columns. Write only the Cushing's column from memory, then fill the Addison's column by inverting each row. Then check. If any row did not invert, that row is the one to study.

Bottom Line: They are mirror images. Everything high in Cushing's is low in Addison's, and potassium is the row that runs the other way.
12

Children

Congenital adrenal hyperplasia

The commonest adrenal disorder in children. An autosomal recessive enzyme deficiency, usually 21-hydroxylase, that blocks cortisol and aldosterone synthesis.

  • Newborn screening is mandatory in all 50 states, using a 17-hydroxyprogesterone level.
  • Without cortisol feedback, ACTH climbs. The adrenals enlarge and pour out androgens instead.
  • The salt-wasting form is life-threatening in the first weeks of life, presenting as adrenal crisis with low sodium, high potassium and dehydration.
  • The virilising form shows as ambiguous genitalia in newborn girls, or early puberty in boys.
  • Treatment is lifelong hydrocortisone, plus fludrocortisone for the salt-wasting form.
  • Stress dosing is critical during illness, injury and surgery.
Children on long-term steroids
  • Chronic steroids for asthma, nephrotic syndrome or inflammatory bowel disease suppress growth. Plot the growth chart at every visit.
  • Even inhaled steroids at high dose can suppress the HPA axis in a child.
  • Cushing's features appear the same way: weight gain, moon face, behaviour change.
  • Stress dosing is required during any intercurrent illness.
  • Adrenal crisis in a child shows hypoglycemia more prominently than in an adult, because glycogen stores are smaller.
Bottom Line: CAH is the commonest paediatric adrenal disorder and newborn screening is mandatory. Salt-wasting CAH kills neonates. In children, crisis shows up as hypoglycemia first.
