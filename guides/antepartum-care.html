High-Yield Summary — Review This in 60 Seconds

The must-know facts from this guide, distilled for quick review.

  • Prenatal visit schedule: Every 4 weeks (weeks 4-28), every 2 weeks (weeks 28-36), then weekly (weeks 36-40). First visit is the most comprehensive.
  • Only positive signs confirm pregnancy: Fetal heartbeat on Doppler/ultrasound, fetal movement felt by examiner, and visualization of fetus on ultrasound. Everything else is presumptive or probable.
  • Naegele's Rule: First day of LMP - 3 months + 7 days + 1 year = Estimated Due Date. Assumes a 28-day cycle.
  • Key cardiovascular change: Blood volume increases 40-50% by 32 weeks. Physiologic anemia of pregnancy occurs because plasma increases more than RBCs (hemodilution).
  • Never position a pregnant patient supine after 20 weeks — the gravid uterus compresses the vena cava, causing supine hypotensive syndrome. Left lateral position is preferred.
  • Fundal height in cm approximates gestational age in weeks between 16-36 weeks (McDonald's rule). Discrepancies of more than 2 cm warrant further evaluation.
  • Reactive NST = reassuring: 2 or more accelerations of 15 bpm lasting 15 seconds within a 20-minute window. For preterm fetuses less than 32 weeks: 10 bpm x 10 seconds.
  • Folic acid (400-800 mcg daily) prevents neural tube defects and should be started ideally before conception. Women with history of NTD need 4 mg daily.
  • Danger signs requiring immediate evaluation: Vaginal bleeding, severe headache with visual changes, epigastric pain, sudden facial/hand edema, decreased fetal movement, gush of fluid from vagina.
  • RhoGAM timing: Give at 28 weeks gestation AND within 72 hours after delivery (if newborn is Rh-positive). Also give after any sensitizing event (amniocentesis, miscarriage, abdominal trauma).

Overview of Antepartum Care

Antepartum care (also called prenatal care) refers to the health care provided to a pregnant woman and her developing fetus from conception through the onset of labor. The goal is to promote the health of both the mother and the fetus, identify risk factors early, prevent complications, and prepare the family for birth and parenthood.

Goals of Prenatal Care

  • Monitor maternal and fetal well-being through regular assessments and screening tests
  • Identify and manage risk factors such as pre-existing medical conditions, genetic risks, and psychosocial concerns
  • Prevent complications through early detection of conditions like preeclampsia, gestational diabetes, and preterm labor
  • Educate and support the pregnant patient about nutrition, activity, warning signs, and preparation for labor, delivery, and newborn care
  • Provide emotional support and address anxiety, depression, and relationship changes during pregnancy

Schedule of Prenatal Visits

Gestational Age Visit Frequency Key Activities
4-28 weeks Every 4 weeks Routine assessments, lab work, first-trimester screening, anatomy scan (18-22 wk)
28-36 weeks Every 2 weeks Glucose screening (24-28 wk), RhoGAM if indicated (28 wk), GBS screening (36-37 wk)
36-40 weeks Every week Cervical checks, fetal position assessment, labor signs education, NST if indicated

First Prenatal Visit vs. Return Visits

First Prenatal Visit

Most Comprehensive

  • Complete health history (medical, surgical, OB, family, psychosocial)
  • Full physical exam including pelvic exam
  • Baseline vital signs and weight
  • Confirm pregnancy and establish EDD
  • Comprehensive lab panel (CBC, blood type, Rh, antibody screen, rubella, hepatitis B, HIV, STI screening, UA, Pap)
  • Risk assessment and care plan development
  • Initiate prenatal vitamins with folic acid
Return Visits

Focused Assessments

  • Weight, blood pressure, urine dipstick (protein, glucose)
  • Fundal height measurement
  • Fetal heart rate auscultation
  • Fetal movement assessment (after quickening)
  • Review of current symptoms and concerns
  • Edema assessment
  • Age-appropriate screening tests as scheduled
Clinical Pearl: Gravida & Para

Gravida = total number of pregnancies (including current). Para = number of deliveries reaching viability (20 weeks or more). Use GTPAL for a detailed OB history:

  • G = Gravida (total pregnancies)
  • T = Term births (37+ weeks)
  • P = Preterm births (20-36 weeks)
  • A = Abortions/miscarriages (before 20 weeks)
  • L = Living children
Bottom Line: Prenatal visits follow a predictable schedule: monthly through 28 weeks, biweekly through 36 weeks, then weekly until delivery. The first visit is the most comprehensive and sets the baseline for the entire pregnancy.

Confirmation of Pregnancy

Signs of pregnancy are categorized into three groups based on their reliability. Only positive signs definitively confirm pregnancy. Understanding this classification is frequently tested on the NCLEX.

Presumptive Signs

Subjective (woman reports)

  • Amenorrhea (missed period)
  • Nausea/vomiting (morning sickness)
  • Breast tenderness and enlargement
  • Urinary frequency
  • Fatigue
  • Quickening (maternal perception of fetal movement, 16-20 wk)
  • Skin changes (linea nigra, chloasma)

Could be caused by other conditions

Probable Signs

Objective (examiner detects)

  • Positive pregnancy test (serum/urine hCG)
  • Hegar sign (softening of lower uterus)
  • Goodell sign (softening of cervix)
  • Chadwick sign (bluish vaginal mucosa)
  • Ballottement (rebound of fetus when tapped)
  • Braxton Hicks contractions
  • Abdominal enlargement

Strongly suggest but do not confirm pregnancy

Positive Signs

Diagnostic (only pregnancy)

  • Fetal heartbeat detected by Doppler (10-12 wk) or fetoscope (18-20 wk)
  • Fetal movement felt by examiner (not the mother)
  • Visualization of fetus on ultrasound

These CANNOT be attributed to any other condition

NCLEX Tip: Positive Pregnancy Test

A positive pregnancy test (hCG) is only a probable sign, NOT a positive sign! hCG can be elevated in conditions like hydatidiform mole (molar pregnancy) or certain cancers. Only the three positive signs listed above definitively confirm pregnancy.

Human Chorionic Gonadotropin (hCG)

  • Produced by: Trophoblast cells after implantation
  • Detected in urine: As early as 7-10 days after conception (home pregnancy tests)
  • Detected in serum: As early as 6-8 days after conception (more sensitive)
  • Peaks: Around 8-11 weeks gestation, then gradually declines
  • Doubles every 48-72 hours in early normal pregnancy (used to assess viability)
  • Abnormal patterns: Slow rise may indicate ectopic pregnancy; very high levels may suggest molar pregnancy or multiple gestation

Establishing the Estimated Due Date (EDD)

Memory Aid: Naegele's Rule

First day of Last Menstrual Period (LMP) - 3 months + 7 days + 1 year = EDD

Example: LMP = March 15, 2026

  • March - 3 months = December
  • 15 + 7 days = 22
  • 2026 + 1 year = 2026 (same year because we went back to December)
  • EDD = December 22, 2026

Assumes a regular 28-day menstrual cycle. Ultrasound in the first trimester is the most accurate dating method.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Naegele's Rule Made Easy
Struggling with Naegele's Rule on exams? Think of it as "go back 3, go forward 7." Take the first day of the last menstrual period, subtract 3 months (go back 3), then add 7 days (go forward 7). This assumes a 28-day cycle. If the cycle is longer or shorter than 28 days, adjust accordingly. For NCLEX purposes, first-trimester ultrasound is the most accurate method for determining gestational age if there is a discrepancy.
Bottom Line: Only three signs positively confirm pregnancy: fetal heartbeat on Doppler/ultrasound, fetal movement felt by the examiner, and visualization of the fetus on ultrasound. A positive hCG is only a probable sign. Use Naegele's Rule (LMP - 3 months + 7 days) to calculate the EDD.

Maternal Physiological Adaptations

Pregnancy triggers profound physiological changes in virtually every organ system to support the growing fetus. Understanding these adaptations is essential for distinguishing normal pregnancy findings from pathological conditions.

Body System Key Changes Clinical Significance
Cardiovascular Blood volume increases 40-50%; cardiac output increases 30-50%; heart rate increases 10-15 bpm; BP decreases in 2nd trimester then normalizes Physiologic anemia (hemodilution), systolic murmur common, supine hypotensive syndrome after 20 weeks
Respiratory Tidal volume increases 30-40%; oxygen consumption increases 15-20%; diaphragm elevates 4 cm; respiratory rate unchanged Dyspnea of pregnancy is common (especially in 3rd trimester); mild respiratory alkalosis is normal
Gastrointestinal Decreased GI motility (progesterone effect); relaxed cardiac sphincter; delayed gastric emptying; displaced appendix Nausea/vomiting (1st trimester), heartburn/GERD, constipation, increased risk of aspiration, atypical appendicitis presentation
Renal/Urinary GFR increases 40-50%; renal plasma flow increases; ureters dilate (progesterone); bladder capacity decreases Urinary frequency, physiologic glycosuria possible, increased UTI risk, decreased creatinine/BUN is normal
Musculoskeletal Progressive lordosis; relaxin softens ligaments and joints; diastasis recti possible; center of gravity shifts Low back pain, waddling gait, increased fall risk, pelvic girdle pain, carpal tunnel syndrome
Hematologic Plasma volume increases more than RBC mass; WBC increases (up to 15,000); hypercoagulable state (increased clotting factors) Physiologic anemia of pregnancy (Hgb may drop to 10.5-11 g/dL), elevated WBC is normal, increased DVT/PE risk
Endocrine hCG peaks at 8-11 wk; progesterone maintains uterine lining and relaxes smooth muscle; estrogen stimulates uterine growth; hPL promotes fetal growth and has anti-insulin effect hCG causes morning sickness; progesterone causes constipation/heartburn; hPL causes insulin resistance (diabetogenic effect, peaks in 3rd trimester)
Integumentary Hyperpigmentation (linea nigra, chloasma/melasma, darkened areolae); striae gravidarum (stretch marks); spider angiomas; palmar erythema Skin changes are estrogen-related, usually fade postpartum; chloasma worsened by sun exposure
Critical Alert: Supine Hypotensive Syndrome

Never position a pregnant patient flat on her back after 20 weeks gestation!

The weight of the gravid uterus compresses the inferior vena cava and aorta when the patient is supine, reducing venous return and cardiac output. Symptoms include dizziness, lightheadedness, pallor, tachycardia, and hypotension.

  • Prevention: Left lateral position (displaces uterus off the vena cava)
  • If supine is necessary (e.g., for a procedure): Place a wedge or rolled towel under the right hip to tilt the uterus to the left
  • Treatment: Immediately turn the patient to her left side
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Supine Hypotensive Syndrome
After 20 weeks, never lay a pregnant patient flat on her back! The heavy uterus compresses the inferior vena cava, reducing blood return to the heart. This causes dizziness, hypotension, and can reduce uteroplacental blood flow to the fetus. Always position pregnant patients in the left lateral position or with a wedge under the right hip. If a patient suddenly becomes dizzy or pale while supine, turn her immediately to her left side. This is a heavily tested concept on NCLEX.

Understanding Physiologic Anemia of Pregnancy

During pregnancy, plasma volume increases by about 40-50%, but red blood cell mass only increases by about 20-30%. This disproportionate increase leads to hemodilution, causing hemoglobin and hematocrit to decrease. This is called physiologic anemia of pregnancy and is a normal finding.

Normal Lab Values in Pregnancy

Hemoglobin
10.5-14 g/dL (lower normal)
Hematocrit
32-42% (lower normal)
WBC Count
5,000-15,000 (elevated normal)
Fasting Glucose
< 92 mg/dL
Bottom Line: Pregnancy affects every body system. Key adaptations include a 40-50% increase in blood volume (causing physiologic anemia), increased cardiac output, decreased GI motility, and a hypercoagulable state. Never lay a patient supine after 20 weeks — left lateral position is preferred.

Prenatal Assessment & Screening

Prenatal screening involves a combination of laboratory tests, physical assessments, and imaging studies performed at specific intervals to monitor maternal and fetal health and detect potential complications early.

Routine Prenatal Laboratory Tests

Test Timing Purpose
CBC with differential First visit, 28 weeks Screen for anemia, infection, thrombocytopenia
Blood type, Rh, antibody screen First visit Identify Rh-negative mothers; screen for irregular antibodies
Rubella titer First visit Determine immunity (if non-immune, vaccinate postpartum — live vaccine, contraindicated in pregnancy)
Hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg) First visit Screen for active hepatitis B (newborn needs HBIG + vaccine at birth if positive)
HIV screening First visit (repeat in 3rd trimester if high risk) Early detection allows antiretroviral therapy to reduce vertical transmission
STI screening (syphilis, gonorrhea, chlamydia) First visit Treat infections to prevent perinatal transmission and complications
Urinalysis and urine culture First visit; dipstick each visit Screen for UTI, bacteriuria, proteinuria (preeclampsia), glycosuria
Pap smear First visit (if due) Cervical cancer screening per guidelines
1-hour glucose challenge test (GCT) 24-28 weeks Screen for gestational diabetes (abnormal if ≥ 140 mg/dL; some use 130 mg/dL)
3-hour glucose tolerance test (GTT) If GCT abnormal Diagnostic for GDM (2+ abnormal values: fasting ≥ 95, 1hr ≥ 180, 2hr ≥ 155, 3hr ≥ 140)
Group B Streptococcus (GBS) culture 36-37 weeks Vaginal/rectal swab; positive patients receive IV antibiotics during labor (penicillin G)
Indirect Coombs test First visit, 28 weeks (if Rh-negative) Detect maternal antibodies against fetal RBCs; negative result required before giving RhoGAM

Physical Assessment Techniques

Fundal Height Assessment (McDonald's Rule)

Measure from the symphysis pubis to the top of the uterine fundus with a tape measure. Between 16 and 36 weeks, the fundal height in centimeters should approximately equal the gestational age in weeks (within 2 cm).

Clinical Pearl: Fundal Height Discrepancies

A discrepancy of more than 2 cm from the expected measurement warrants further investigation:

  • Larger than expected: Multiple gestation, polyhydramnios, macrosomia, inaccurate dates, fibroids
  • Smaller than expected: Intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR), oligohydramnios, inaccurate dates, transverse lie
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Fundal Height = Gestational Age
Between 16 and 36 weeks, fundal height in cm approximately equals gestational age in weeks. For example, at 24 weeks the fundal height should measure about 24 cm. A discrepancy greater than 2 cm in either direction requires further evaluation with ultrasound. After 36 weeks this measurement becomes less reliable because of fetal descent into the pelvis (lightening). Also remember key landmarks: at 12 weeks the fundus is at the symphysis pubis, at 20 weeks it is at the umbilicus, and at 36 weeks it is at the xiphoid process.

Leopold Maneuvers

Leopold maneuvers are a systematic method of abdominal palpation performed in the third trimester to determine fetal lie, presentation, position, and engagement. There are four maneuvers:

Four Leopold Maneuvers

1
First Maneuver (Fundal Grip)

Palpate the fundus to determine which fetal part occupies the upper uterus. The head feels round, hard, and ballottable. The breech (buttocks) feels irregular, soft, and less mobile.

2
Second Maneuver (Umbilical Grip)

Palpate the sides of the abdomen to locate the fetal back and extremities. The back feels smooth, firm, and continuous. The extremity side feels irregular with small knobs (knees, elbows).

3
Third Maneuver (Pawlik Grip)

Grasp the lower uterine segment just above the symphysis pubis to determine the presenting part. Confirms whether the presenting part is the head or breech and whether it is engaged.

4
Fourth Maneuver (Pelvic Grip)

Face the patient's feet and palpate downward toward the pelvis with fingertips. Determines the degree of flexion and descent of the presenting part into the pelvis.

Bottom Line: Prenatal labs follow a specific schedule — blood type/Rh at the first visit, glucose screening at 24-28 weeks, GBS at 36-37 weeks. Fundal height in cm equals gestational age in weeks (16-36 weeks). Leopold maneuvers identify fetal position in the third trimester.

Fetal Assessment & Monitoring

Fetal assessment techniques are used to evaluate fetal well-being throughout pregnancy. The nurse must understand each method, when it is indicated, how to interpret results, and what follow-up is needed for abnormal findings.

Fetal Heart Tones

  • Doppler ultrasound: Detects FHR as early as 10-12 weeks gestation
  • Fetoscope (Pinard): Detects FHR at 18-20 weeks gestation
  • Normal FHR range: 110-160 bpm (baseline)
  • Fetal bradycardia: Baseline FHR < 110 bpm
  • Fetal tachycardia: Baseline FHR > 160 bpm

Fetal Movement Counting (Kick Counts)

Fetal movement counting is a simple, non-invasive method of fetal surveillance that the mother can perform at home beginning at 28 weeks. The most common method is the Cardiff Count-to-Ten method.

Cardiff Count-to-Ten Method

Starting at 28 weeks: Have the patient count fetal movements at approximately the same time each day (ideally after a meal when the fetus is active).

  • The fetus should make at least 10 movements within 2 hours
  • If fewer than 10 movements in 2 hours, the patient should contact her provider immediately
  • Decreased fetal movement may indicate fetal distress, hypoxia, or placental insufficiency

Non-Stress Test (NST)

The NST evaluates fetal well-being by assessing the fetal heart rate response to fetal movement. It is the most commonly used antepartum surveillance test and is non-invasive.

Reactive NST (Reassuring)

Normal Finding

  • 2+ accelerations of fetal heart rate
  • Each acceleration rises 15 bpm above baseline
  • Each acceleration lasts at least 15 seconds
  • Occurs within a 20-minute window
  • Indicates intact autonomic nervous system
  • No further immediate testing needed

For preterm < 32 weeks: 10 bpm x 10 seconds

Non-Reactive NST (Non-Reassuring)

Requires Follow-Up

  • Fewer than 2 accelerations in 20 minutes
  • Accelerations do not meet criteria
  • Extended to 40 minutes before calling non-reactive
  • May indicate fetal sleep cycle (try vibroacoustic stimulation)
  • Follow up with BPP, CST, or extended monitoring
  • Does NOT always mean fetal distress

Consider maternal medications (sedatives, beta-blockers) as cause

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Reactive NST = 15 x 15 x 2 in 20
A reactive NST requires 2 or more accelerations of 15 bpm above baseline, each lasting at least 15 seconds, within a 20-minute window. Remember: 15-15-2-20. Reactive = reassuring = good. Non-reactive = may need further testing (BPP or CST). The fetus may simply be sleeping, so try vibroacoustic stimulation (VAS) or extend the monitoring period to 40 minutes before concluding non-reactive. For preterm fetuses less than 32 weeks, the criteria are lower: 10 bpm x 10 seconds.

Biophysical Profile (BPP)

The BPP combines the NST with four ultrasound assessments to provide a comprehensive evaluation of fetal well-being. Each component scores 0 (abnormal) or 2 (normal), for a maximum score of 10.

Component Normal (Score = 2) Abnormal (Score = 0)
Non-Stress Test (NST) Reactive (2+ accelerations meeting criteria) Non-reactive
Fetal Breathing Movements 1+ episodes of rhythmic breathing lasting 30+ seconds in 30 minutes Absent or < 30 seconds
Fetal Movement 3+ discrete body or limb movements in 30 minutes Fewer than 3 movements
Fetal Tone 1+ episodes of active extension and flexion of limbs or trunk; opening/closing of hand Slow extension/flexion or absent movement
Amniotic Fluid Volume (AFI) At least 1 pocket measuring 2+ cm in two perpendicular planes (or AFI ≥ 5 cm) Largest pocket < 2 cm (or AFI < 5 cm)
NCLEX Tip: BPP Scoring
  • 8-10: Normal — reassuring (repeat testing as indicated)
  • 6: Equivocal — repeat within 24 hours or consider delivery if near term
  • 4 or below: Abnormal — strongly consider delivery; indicates possible fetal compromise
  • Amniotic fluid is the most important single component — it reflects long-term fetal status

Contraction Stress Test (CST)

The CST evaluates fetal response to uterine contractions (simulating the stress of labor). Contractions are induced with nipple stimulation or IV oxytocin (Pitocin) until 3 contractions occur in a 10-minute window.

Result Definition Interpretation
Negative CST No late decelerations with any contraction Reassuring — fetus tolerates contractions well; good uteroplacental function
Positive CST Late decelerations with 50% or more of contractions Non-reassuring — suggests uteroplacental insufficiency; fetus may not tolerate labor
Equivocal-Suspicious Intermittent late decelerations or significant variable decelerations Requires repeat testing or further evaluation
Equivocal-Hyperstimulatory Decelerations occurring with contractions more frequent than every 2 minutes or lasting > 90 seconds Inadequate test — hyperstimulation, not fetal intolerance
CST Contraindications

The CST is contraindicated in patients with:

  • Preterm labor or preterm premature rupture of membranes
  • Placenta previa
  • Previous classical (vertical) cesarean scar
  • Multiple gestation
  • Cervical incompetence/cerclage

Comparison of Fetal Surveillance Tests

Feature NST BPP CST
Method External FHR monitoring NST + ultrasound (5 components) FHR monitoring during contractions
Invasiveness Non-invasive Non-invasive Semi-invasive (may require IV oxytocin)
Duration 20-40 minutes 30-60 minutes 1-2 hours
Normal Result Reactive Score 8-10 Negative (no late decels)
Abnormal Result Non-reactive Score ≤ 4 Positive (late decels)
Best For Initial screening, routine surveillance Comprehensive assessment after non-reactive NST Evaluating fetal tolerance of labor stress
Bottom Line: NST is the most common antepartum test — reactive (2 accels of 15 bpm x 15 sec in 20 min) is reassuring. BPP scores 10 possible points; amniotic fluid is the most important component. CST: negative = good (no late decels), positive = concerning. Remember the confusing naming: a "negative" CST and a "reactive" NST are both normal/good.

Nutrition & Lifestyle in Pregnancy

Proper nutrition during pregnancy is critical for maternal health and optimal fetal development. The nurse plays a key role in educating pregnant patients about dietary needs, supplementation, and lifestyle modifications.

Weight Gain Recommendations

Pre-Pregnancy BMI Category Recommended Total Weight Gain Rate (2nd/3rd Trimester)
< 18.5 Underweight 28-40 lbs (12.5-18 kg) ~1 lb/week
18.5-24.9 Normal weight 25-35 lbs (11.5-16 kg) ~1 lb/week
25-29.9 Overweight 15-25 lbs (7-11.5 kg) ~0.6 lb/week
≥ 30 Obese 11-20 lbs (5-9 kg) ~0.5 lb/week

First trimester: approximately 1-4 lbs total weight gain. Most weight gain occurs in the second and third trimesters. For twin pregnancies, add approximately 10-15 lbs to the recommended range.

Key Nutrient Requirements

Nutrient Daily Requirement Purpose Food Sources
Folic Acid 400-800 mcg (4 mg if history of NTD) Prevents neural tube defects (spina bifida, anencephaly) Leafy greens, fortified cereals, legumes, oranges
Iron 27 mg Supports expanded blood volume and prevents anemia Red meat, spinach, beans, fortified cereals (take with vitamin C for absorption)
Calcium 1,000 mg (1,300 mg if < 18 years old) Fetal bone and teeth development; prevents maternal bone loss Dairy products, fortified juice, broccoli, almonds
DHA (Omega-3) 200-300 mg Fetal brain and eye development Fatty fish (salmon, sardines), DHA supplements, walnuts
Protein 71 g/day (additional 25 g over non-pregnant) Fetal tissue growth and maternal tissue repair Meat, poultry, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes, nuts
Vitamin D 600 IU Calcium absorption and fetal bone mineralization Fortified milk, fatty fish, sunlight exposure
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Folic Acid Prevents Neural Tube Defects
Folic acid supplementation should ideally start BEFORE conception! The neural tube closes by day 28 of embryonic development — often before a woman even knows she is pregnant. That is why the recommendation is for all women of childbearing age to take 400-800 mcg of folic acid daily. Women who have had a previous pregnancy affected by a neural tube defect need the higher dose of 4 mg (4,000 mcg) daily, starting at least 1 month before conception. This is one of the most commonly tested nutrition topics on the NCLEX.

Foods to Avoid During Pregnancy

  • Raw or undercooked meat, eggs, and seafood — risk of Salmonella, E. coli, Toxoplasma
  • High-mercury fish (shark, swordfish, king mackerel, tilefish) — neurotoxic to the developing fetus
  • Deli meats and hot dogs (unless heated steaming hot) — risk of Listeria monocytogenes
  • Unpasteurized milk, cheese, and juices — risk of Listeria and other bacteria
  • Raw sprouts — risk of Salmonella and E. coli
  • Excess caffeine — limit to 200 mg/day (about one 12-oz cup of coffee); excessive intake linked to miscarriage
  • Alcohol — no safe amount during pregnancy; causes fetal alcohol spectrum disorders (FASD)

Exercise Guidelines

ACOG Exercise Recommendations
  • 30 minutes of moderate exercise most days of the week (150 minutes/week total)
  • Safe activities: walking, swimming, stationary cycling, prenatal yoga, low-impact aerobics
  • Avoid: contact sports, activities with fall risk (skiing, horseback riding), scuba diving, hot yoga/hot tubs
  • Stop exercise and notify provider if: vaginal bleeding, fluid leaking, dizziness, chest pain, calf pain/swelling, contractions, decreased fetal movement
  • Avoid exercising in the supine position after the first trimester

Substance Avoidance

  • Tobacco/Nicotine: Associated with preterm birth, low birth weight, placental abruption, and SIDS. Offer smoking cessation counseling at every visit.
  • Alcohol: No safe amount. Causes fetal alcohol spectrum disorders (FASD), the leading preventable cause of intellectual disability.
  • Marijuana: Associated with low birth weight and neurodevelopmental effects. Not considered safe during pregnancy.
  • Illicit drugs: Cocaine causes placental abruption and vasoconstriction. Opioids cause neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS). Refer for substance abuse treatment.
Bottom Line: Folic acid (400-800 mcg daily) before conception prevents neural tube defects. Iron (27 mg daily) supports expanded blood volume. Avoid raw/undercooked foods, high-mercury fish, alcohol, and tobacco. Weight gain recommendations are based on pre-pregnancy BMI.

Common Discomforts of Pregnancy

Most pregnant women experience discomforts throughout their pregnancy that are considered normal but can significantly affect quality of life. Nurses play a vital role in educating patients about self-care measures to manage these discomforts and recognizing when a "normal" discomfort may indicate a serious complication.

Discomfort Trimester Cause Nursing Interventions
Nausea/Vomiting (morning sickness) 1st (peaks 8-12 wk) Rising hCG and estrogen levels Eat small, frequent meals; avoid empty stomach; eat dry crackers before rising; ginger tea; vitamin B6 (10-25 mg TID); avoid fatty/spicy foods
Fatigue 1st and 3rd Hormonal changes, increased metabolic demands Rest when able; prioritize sleep; moderate exercise; assess for anemia if severe
Urinary frequency 1st and 3rd Uterine pressure on bladder; increased GFR Maintain fluid intake (do not restrict); limit fluids before bedtime; Kegel exercises; report dysuria (may indicate UTI)
Breast tenderness 1st, continues Estrogen and progesterone stimulation Supportive bra; avoid underwire; wear bra at night if needed
Heartburn (GERD) 2nd and 3rd Progesterone relaxes cardiac sphincter; uterine pressure on stomach Small frequent meals; avoid lying down after eating; elevate HOB; avoid spicy/fatty foods; antacids (calcium-based) as needed; avoid sodium bicarbonate
Constipation 2nd and 3rd Progesterone slows GI motility; iron supplements; pressure on intestines Increase fiber and fluids; moderate exercise; stool softeners (docusate); avoid laxatives/enemas
Hemorrhoids 2nd and 3rd Increased venous pressure; constipation/straining Avoid straining; sitz baths; witch hazel pads; increase fiber; topical anesthetics; side-lying position
Varicose veins 2nd and 3rd Increased venous pressure; progesterone relaxation of vein walls Elevate legs; avoid prolonged standing/sitting; support stockings; avoid crossing legs; exercise regularly
Low back pain 2nd and 3rd Progressive lordosis; relaxin loosening ligaments; center of gravity shift Pelvic tilt exercises; supportive shoes (low heel); body mechanics education; pregnancy support belt; sleep with pillow between knees
Round ligament pain 2nd Stretching of the round ligament as uterus grows Change positions slowly; flex knees to abdomen; warm compresses; rest; reassure this is normal (sharp, brief pain in lower abdomen/groin)
Leg cramps 2nd and 3rd Altered calcium/phosphorus ratio; pressure on nerves; fatigue Dorsiflex the foot (do NOT point toes); stretch calf muscles; adequate calcium/magnesium intake; avoid standing for long periods
Braxton Hicks contractions 3rd (can start 2nd) Irregular uterine contractions (practice contractions) Change position; rest; hydrate; differentiate from true labor (irregular, do not increase in frequency/intensity, relieved by rest/position change)
Dyspnea 3rd Elevated diaphragm; increased oxygen demand Sleep propped up; good posture; reassure this is normal; report if accompanied by chest pain or severe SOB
Ankle edema 3rd Increased venous pressure; sodium and water retention Elevate feet; avoid prolonged standing; left lateral position; increase water intake; avoid tight clothing; report sudden facial/hand edema (preeclampsia sign)
When Normal Becomes Dangerous

Nausea/vomiting that leads to weight loss > 5%, dehydration, or ketonuria may indicate hyperemesis gravidarum — requires IV fluids, electrolyte replacement, and antiemetics.

Ankle edema that is bilateral and dependent is normal; sudden swelling of the face and hands is a warning sign of preeclampsia and requires immediate evaluation.

Headache that is persistent and severe, especially with visual changes, may indicate preeclampsia — not just a normal pregnancy discomfort.

Bottom Line: Most pregnancy discomforts are managed with non-pharmacologic measures. Key nursing interventions: dry crackers and ginger for nausea, fiber and fluids for constipation, pelvic tilts for back pain, and dorsiflexion (not pointing toes) for leg cramps. Always distinguish normal discomforts from danger signs.

Danger Signs in Pregnancy

Recognizing danger signs during pregnancy is one of the most critical competencies for nurses. These signs may indicate life-threatening conditions for the mother and fetus. This section is HIGH-YIELD for NCLEX and clinical practice.

Danger Signs Requiring IMMEDIATE Medical Evaluation
  • Vaginal bleeding — any amount, any trimester (placenta previa, abruption, miscarriage)
  • Severe, persistent headache — unrelieved by rest or acetaminophen (preeclampsia)
  • Visual disturbances — blurred vision, scotomata (spots), diplopia (preeclampsia, eclampsia)
  • Epigastric or right upper quadrant pain — liver capsule distension (HELLP syndrome, severe preeclampsia)
  • Sudden edema of face and hands — especially rapid onset (preeclampsia)
  • Decreased or absent fetal movement — fewer than 10 movements in 2 hours (fetal compromise)
  • Gush or continuous leaking of fluid from vagina — premature rupture of membranes (PROM)
  • Regular contractions before 37 weeks — preterm labor
  • Fever > 100.4F (38C) — infection (chorioamnionitis, UTI, pyelonephritis)
  • Dysuria with fever/chills — pyelonephritis risk (can trigger preterm labor)
  • Persistent vomiting beyond first trimester — hyperemesis gravidarum or other pathology

Preeclampsia Warning Signs

Preeclampsia is one of the most dangerous pregnancy complications, affecting 2-8% of all pregnancies. It can progress rapidly to eclampsia (seizures) or HELLP syndrome.

NCLEX Tip: Preeclampsia Triad

The classic triad of preeclampsia (diagnosed after 20 weeks gestation):

  • Hypertension: Systolic BP ≥ 140 mmHg or Diastolic BP ≥ 90 mmHg (on two occasions at least 4 hours apart)
  • Proteinuria: ≥ 300 mg in 24-hour urine collection or protein/creatinine ratio ≥ 0.3
  • End-organ damage: May include elevated liver enzymes, thrombocytopenia, renal insufficiency, cerebral or visual symptoms

Severe features: SBP ≥ 160 or DBP ≥ 110, platelet count < 100,000, liver transaminases 2x normal, persistent RUQ pain, pulmonary edema, new-onset cerebral or visual disturbances, creatinine > 1.1 mg/dL

Memory Aid: Danger Signs — "ABCDEFGH"
  • A — Abdominal pain (epigastric/RUQ)
  • B — Bleeding (vaginal, any amount)
  • C — Contractions (regular, before 37 weeks)
  • D — Decreased fetal movement
  • E — Edema (sudden, face/hands)
  • F — Fever (> 100.4F)
  • G — Gush of fluid from vagina
  • H — Headache (severe) and visual changes

When to Call the Provider vs. Go to the ER

Call the Provider
  • Mild headache that resolves with rest
  • Mild ankle edema at end of day
  • Persistent nausea/vomiting not responding to home remedies
  • Dysuria without fever
  • Unusual vaginal discharge (color, odor change)
  • Braxton Hicks that increase in frequency
  • Mild abdominal cramping without bleeding
Go to the ER Immediately
  • Any vaginal bleeding (especially bright red)
  • Severe headache with visual changes
  • Sudden severe abdominal pain
  • Gush of fluid from vagina
  • No fetal movement for 2+ hours
  • Signs of severe preeclampsia
  • Regular contractions before 37 weeks
Bottom Line: Danger signs are HIGH-YIELD for NCLEX. Use the mnemonic ABCDEFGH: Abdominal pain, Bleeding, Contractions (preterm), Decreased fetal movement, Edema (face/hands), Fever, Gush of fluid, Headache with visual changes. Any of these require immediate evaluation. Preeclampsia develops after 20 weeks and is characterized by hypertension, proteinuria, and end-organ damage.

Prenatal Education & Nursing Interventions

Patient education is one of the most important nursing interventions during the antepartum period. Teaching should be developmentally appropriate, culturally sensitive, and timed to the stage of pregnancy so information is relevant and actionable.

Teaching by Trimester

First Trimester Education (Weeks 1-13)

1
Nutrition and Supplementation

Begin prenatal vitamins with folic acid. Discuss balanced diet, foods to avoid, caffeine limitations. Address nausea management strategies.

2
Danger Signs and When to Call

Teach danger signs (vaginal bleeding, severe cramping). Provide provider contact information. Discuss ectopic pregnancy warning signs.

3
Lifestyle Modifications

Substance avoidance (alcohol, tobacco, drugs). Environmental hazards (cat litter/toxoplasmosis, hot tubs, certain chemicals). Exercise guidelines. Emotional changes and support resources.

4
Screening and Testing

Explain upcoming lab work and screening tests. Discuss genetic screening options (NIPT, first-trimester screening). Provide information for informed decision-making.

Second Trimester Education (Weeks 14-27)

1
Fetal Development and Bonding

Discuss fetal growth milestones. Encourage awareness of quickening (first fetal movement felt, usually 16-20 weeks). Begin discussing birth preferences.

2
Common Discomforts Management

Address heartburn, constipation, round ligament pain, back pain. Teach safe self-care measures. Discuss when discomforts become concerning.

3
Dental Care and Safety

Dental care is safe and recommended during pregnancy. Good oral health reduces preterm birth risk. Seat belts should be worn with lap belt below the belly and shoulder belt between the breasts.

Third Trimester Education (Weeks 28-40)

1
Fetal Movement Counting (Kick Counts)

Teach Cardiff method beginning at 28 weeks. Emphasize importance of reporting decreased movement immediately. Have the patient count at the same time daily after a meal.

2
Signs of Labor

Differentiate true labor from false labor (Braxton Hicks). Discuss lightening, bloody show, rupture of membranes. When to go to the hospital (5-1-1 rule: contractions every 5 minutes, lasting 1 minute, for 1 hour).

3
Birth Plan and Preparation

Discuss pain management options (natural, epidural, systemic). Tour birthing facility. Review postpartum expectations. Pack hospital bag checklist.

4
Breastfeeding Education

Discuss benefits of breastfeeding (immunity, bonding, uterine involution). Colostrum production begins in late pregnancy. Provide resources (lactation consultant referral). Discuss breast pump options if returning to work.

5
Newborn Care Preparation

Car seat safety (rear-facing until age 2). Safe sleep (ABCs: Alone, Back, Crib with firm mattress). Cord care, circumcision decisions, pediatrician selection. Infant CPR class recommended.

Clinical Pearl: Tdap Vaccination

The Tdap vaccine should be given during each pregnancy between 27 and 36 weeks gestation, regardless of previous vaccination history. This allows maternal antibodies to transfer to the fetus, providing passive immunity against pertussis (whooping cough) in the first months of life before the infant can be vaccinated.

Bottom Line: Prenatal education should be timed to each trimester's needs: nutrition and danger signs early, discomfort management in mid-pregnancy, and labor preparation, kick counts, and breastfeeding education in the third trimester. Tdap vaccination is given between 27-36 weeks in every pregnancy.

Rh Incompatibility & RhoGAM

Rh incompatibility occurs when an Rh-negative mother is carrying an Rh-positive fetus. If fetal Rh-positive blood cells enter the maternal circulation, the mother's immune system can produce anti-Rh antibodies (isoimmunization/sensitization) that attack the fetal red blood cells in subsequent pregnancies, causing hemolytic disease of the newborn (erythroblastosis fetalis).

How Sensitization Occurs

  • During delivery (most common time of exposure)
  • After miscarriage, abortion, or ectopic pregnancy
  • After amniocentesis, chorionic villus sampling, or cordocentesis
  • After abdominal trauma during pregnancy
  • Placental abruption
Clinical Pearl: First Pregnancy Is Usually Safe

Isoimmunization typically does NOT affect the first pregnancy. It takes time for the mother to produce enough antibodies after the first exposure. The risk increases with each subsequent Rh-positive pregnancy if the mother was not given RhoGAM. The maternal antibodies (IgG) cross the placenta and attack fetal RBCs, causing anemia, jaundice, hydrops fetalis, and potentially fetal death.

RhoGAM (Rho(D) Immune Globulin)

RhoGAM prevents the Rh-negative mother from producing anti-Rh antibodies by destroying any fetal Rh-positive cells in the maternal circulation before her immune system can respond.

Timing Dose Indication
28 weeks gestation 300 mcg IM Routine prophylaxis for all Rh-negative unsensitized mothers
Within 72 hours postpartum 300 mcg IM If newborn is confirmed Rh-positive (cord blood test)
After miscarriage/abortion (< 13 weeks) 50 mcg (MICRhoGAM) or 300 mcg Prevent sensitization from fetal-maternal hemorrhage
After amniocentesis, CVS, or abdominal trauma 300 mcg IM Any time fetal-maternal blood mixing may occur
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
RhoGAM Timing Is Critical
RhoGAM must be given at 28 weeks AND within 72 hours postpartum if the newborn is Rh-positive. The key points to remember: (1) Must be given to unsensitized Rh-negative mothers only — check the indirect Coombs test first (must be negative). (2) The 72-hour postpartum window is critical — if missed, the mother may become sensitized. (3) RhoGAM is also given after any sensitizing event (miscarriage, ectopic pregnancy, amniocentesis, abdominal trauma). (4) If the newborn is Rh-negative, postpartum RhoGAM is NOT needed. (5) If the mother is already sensitized (positive indirect Coombs), RhoGAM is ineffective and will not be given.

Key Lab Tests

Rh Testing Quick Reference

Indirect Coombs (Mother)
Detects maternal antibodies against Rh+ cells. Negative = give RhoGAM
Direct Coombs (Newborn)
Detects antibodies attached to newborn's RBCs. Positive = hemolysis occurring
Kleihauer-Betke Test
Quantifies fetal-maternal hemorrhage volume. Used to determine if additional RhoGAM doses are needed
Cord Blood Type
Determines newborn's blood type and Rh status at delivery. Guides postpartum RhoGAM decision
NCLEX Tip: Coombs Test Direction

Remember: Indirect = In mom (screens maternal blood for antibodies). Direct = on Da baby (tests newborn's RBCs for attached antibodies). Indirect Coombs must be negative before giving RhoGAM.

Bottom Line: RhoGAM is given at 28 weeks and within 72 hours postpartum to Rh-negative, unsensitized mothers carrying Rh-positive babies. Check the indirect Coombs test first (must be negative). Also give RhoGAM after any sensitizing event. Indirect Coombs = In mom, Direct Coombs = on Da baby.
