01

What actually gets you

Prenatal care is not a hard topic. It is a large one. The points are lost in a few predictable places, not spread evenly.

Read these three before you read anything else.

You learned pregnancy as a happy timeline

Then a stem gives vaginal bleeding. Or a headache with visual changes. Or an epigastric pain. You treat it as a discomfort and reassure her.

The danger signs are a separate list. They mean call now. Learn that list cold and a whole block of questions falls out.

You skipped the Rh management

An Rh-negative mother carries an Rh-positive fetus. She needs RhoGAM at 28 weeks. She needs it again within 72 hours postpartum. She needs it after any bleeding event.

Miss the timing and the question is gone. This is memorised, not reasoned.

You fumbled the dates or the labs

Naegele's rule. GTPAL. A hemoglobin of 11 that is normal in pregnancy because of hemodilution. These look small.

They are quick points, and they are quick to lose. Calculate them until they are automatic.

Bottom Line: Study prenatal care as four jobs, not one long chapter. Confirm the pregnancy. Date it. Screen it on schedule. Catch the danger signs.
02

The four things

Prenatal care is four jobs. Each one has a goal, a cue you act on, a priority action, and a teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

The job Goal in one line The cue you act on Priority action Teaching point
Confirm Only positive signs confirm pregnancy. Heartbeat, examiner feels movement, or ultrasound. Do not call it confirmed on a positive hCG alone. hCG is only probable; it rises in molar pregnancy too.
Date Fix the estimated due date. First day of the LMP. Apply Naegele's rule; use first-trimester ultrasound if dates are unsure. Fundal height in cm equals weeks from 16 to 36.
Screen Right test at the right visit. Gestational age drives the schedule. Blood type and Rh first visit, glucose at 24-28 weeks, GBS at 36-37 weeks. A normal pregnancy hemoglobin runs low from hemodilution.
Watch Separate a discomfort from an emergency. Bleeding, severe headache with vision changes, decreased movement, fluid gush. Send for immediate evaluation; do not reassure. Preeclampsia can look like a normal discomfort at first.
Copy this down

Write the four jobs on one index card. Confirm. Date. Screen. Watch. Everything below hangs off those four words.

03

Confirm and date the pregnancy

Discrimination axis: does this sign confirm, or only suggest.

Signs of pregnancy split into three tiers by how reliable they are. Only positive signs confirm it. This tier is tested often, so keep the lists apart.

Presumptive: she reports it
  • Amenorrhea, a missed period
  • Nausea and vomiting, morning sickness
  • Breast tenderness and enlargement
  • Urinary frequency and fatigue
  • Quickening, the mother feeling movement at 16-20 weeks
  • Skin changes: linea nigra, chloasma

Subjective. Another condition could cause any of these.

Probable: the examiner detects it
  • Positive pregnancy test, serum or urine hCG
  • Hegar sign, softening of the lower uterus
  • Goodell sign, softening of the cervix
  • Chadwick sign, bluish vaginal mucosa
  • Ballottement, rebound of the fetus when tapped
  • Braxton Hicks contractions, abdominal enlargement

Objective, but still not proof.

Positive: only pregnancy explains it
  • Fetal heartbeat by Doppler at 10-12 weeks, fetoscope at 18-20 weeks
  • Fetal movement felt by the examiner, not the mother
  • Visualization of the fetus on ultrasound

These three cannot be attributed to anything else.

The trap: a positive test is only probable

A positive hCG feels like confirmation. It is not. hCG rises in a hydatidiform mole and in certain cancers.

Only the three positive signs confirm pregnancy. When a stem hands you a positive test and nothing more, the answer is probable.

hCG, the hormone behind the test

  • Made by trophoblast cells after implantation
  • Urine detection as early as 7-10 days after conception
  • Serum detection as early as 6-8 days after conception, more sensitive
  • Peaks around 8-11 weeks, then gradually declines
  • Doubles every 48-72 hours in early normal pregnancy
  • Abnormal patterns: a slow rise may mean ectopic; very high levels may mean molar or multiple gestation

Dating with Naegele's rule

Naegele's rule fixes the estimated due date from the LMP. Use the first day of the last period.

The formula

First day of LMP, minus 3 months, plus 7 days, plus 1 year. That is the EDD.

Example: an LMP of March 15, 2026. Back 3 months is December. Add 7 days is the 22nd. The due date is December 22, 2026.

It assumes a regular 28-day cycle. A first-trimester ultrasound dates most accurately when the cycle is irregular.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Go back 3, go forward 7
Take the first day of the last period. Go back 3 months, then forward 7 days. That is Naegele's rule, and it assumes a 28-day cycle. If the cycle is longer or shorter, adjust. When dates and exam do not agree, a first-trimester ultrasound wins.
Bottom Line: Three signs confirm pregnancy: heartbeat, examiner-felt movement, ultrasound. A positive hCG is only probable. Date with Naegele's rule off the first day of the LMP.
04

Maternal adaptations

Pregnancy changes nearly every system. Your job is to tell a normal change from a warning sign.

System Key changes What it means at the bedside
Cardiovascular Blood volume up 40-50%; cardiac output up 30-50%; heart rate up 10-15 bpm; BP dips in the 2nd trimester then normalizes Physiologic anemia from hemodilution, a soft systolic murmur is common, supine hypotensive syndrome after 20 weeks
Respiratory Tidal volume up 30-40%; oxygen use up 15-20%; diaphragm rises 4 cm; rate unchanged Dyspnea of pregnancy is common, worse in the 3rd trimester; a mild respiratory alkalosis is normal
Gastrointestinal Slowed motility from progesterone; relaxed cardiac sphincter; delayed emptying; displaced appendix Nausea early, heartburn and constipation later, higher aspiration risk, atypical appendicitis
Renal GFR up 40-50%; renal plasma flow up; ureters dilate; bladder capacity drops Urinary frequency, possible physiologic glycosuria, higher UTI risk, a lower creatinine and BUN are normal
Musculoskeletal Progressive lordosis; relaxin softens joints; possible diastasis recti; shifted center of gravity Low back pain, waddling gait, higher fall risk, pelvic girdle pain, carpal tunnel
Hematologic Plasma rises more than RBC mass; WBC up to 15,000; a hypercoagulable state Physiologic anemia (Hgb may fall to 10.5-11), an elevated WBC is normal, higher DVT and PE risk
Endocrine hCG peaks at 8-11 weeks; progesterone relaxes smooth muscle; estrogen grows the uterus; hPL has an anti-insulin effect hCG drives morning sickness; progesterone drives constipation; hPL drives insulin resistance, peaking in the 3rd trimester
Integumentary Linea nigra, chloasma, darkened areolae; striae gravidarum; spider angiomas; palmar erythema Estrogen-related, usually fade postpartum; chloasma worsens with sun
Supine hypotensive syndrome

After 20 weeks, never lay her flat on her back. The heavy uterus compresses the vena cava and aorta. Venous return drops and so does cardiac output.

She goes dizzy, pale, and hypotensive. Turn her to the left side. If supine is unavoidable for a procedure, wedge a rolled towel under the right hip.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Left lateral after 20 weeks
After 20 weeks, supine compresses the vena cava and cuts blood flow to the placenta. If a patient goes suddenly dizzy or pale while flat, turn her to the left side first. Left lateral, or a wedge under the right hip, is the standard position for a pregnant patient.

Physiologic anemia of pregnancy

Plasma volume rises about 40-50%. Red cell mass rises only about 20-30%. The blood is diluted, so hemoglobin and hematocrit fall.

This is called physiologic anemia of pregnancy. It is a normal finding, not a deficiency.

LabNormal in pregnancy
Hemoglobin10.5-14 g/dL, lower end is normal
Hematocrit32-42%, lower end is normal
WBC count5,000-15,000, an elevated count is normal
Fasting glucoseUnder 92 mg/dL

GTPAL, the obstetric shorthand

Gravida is the total number of pregnancies, including the current one. Para is deliveries reaching viability at 20 weeks or more. GTPAL spells out the detail.

Reading GTPAL
  • G = Gravida, total pregnancies
  • T = Term births at 37 weeks or more
  • P = Preterm births, 20 to 36 weeks
  • A = Abortions and miscarriages before 20 weeks
  • L = Living children
Bottom Line: Every system adapts. Blood volume climbs 40-50%, which dilutes the hemoglobin. Never lay her supine after 20 weeks. GTPAL turns a history into five numbers.

Prenatal assessment and screening

Screening is a schedule. The right test belongs to the right visit. Learn the visit that owns each lab.

Visit schedule

Gestational ageFrequencyWhat happens
4-28 weeksEvery 4 weeksRoutine checks, labs, first-trimester screening, anatomy scan at 18-22 weeks
28-36 weeksEvery 2 weeksGlucose screen at 24-28 weeks, RhoGAM at 28 weeks if indicated, GBS at 36-37 weeks
36-40 weeksEvery weekCervical checks, fetal position, labor-sign teaching, NST if indicated

The first visit is the most comprehensive. It sets the baseline for the whole pregnancy. Return visits are focused: weight, blood pressure, urine dipstick, fundal height, and fetal heart rate.

Fundal height

Measure from the symphysis pubis to the top of the fundus. Between 16 and 36 weeks, the height in centimeters roughly equals the gestational age in weeks.

A gap larger than 2 cm needs a closer look. Larger than expected suggests multiples, polyhydramnios, or macrosomia. Smaller suggests growth restriction or low fluid.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
cm equals weeks, from 16 to 36
At 24 weeks the fundal height should read about 24 cm. A gap over 2 cm in either direction earns an ultrasound. After 36 weeks the number drifts as the fetus descends. Landmarks help too: symphysis at 12 weeks, umbilicus at 20 weeks, xiphoid at 36 weeks.

Leopold maneuvers

Leopold maneuvers palpate the abdomen in the third trimester. They find fetal lie, presentation, position, and engagement. There are four.

  1. 1
    Fundal grip. Palpate the top of the uterus. The head feels round and hard. The breech feels soft and irregular.
  2. 2
    Umbilical grip. Palpate the sides. The back feels smooth and firm. The limb side feels knobby.
  3. 3
    Pawlik grip. Grasp just above the symphysis. This finds the presenting part and whether it is engaged.
  4. 4
    Pelvic grip. Face her feet and palpate toward the pelvis. This gauges flexion and descent of the presenting part.
Bottom Line: Blood type and Rh at the first visit, glucose at 24-28 weeks, GBS at 36-37 weeks. Fundal height in cm equals weeks from 16 to 36. Leopold maneuvers place the fetus late in pregnancy.
06

Fetal assessment

Fetal tests answer one question: is this fetus doing well right now. Know each test, its normal result, and the follow-up for an abnormal one.

Heart tones and movement

  • Doppler detects the heart rate at 10-12 weeks
  • Fetoscope detects it at 18-20 weeks
  • Normal FHR baseline is 110-160 bpm
  • Bradycardia is a baseline under 110 bpm; tachycardia is over 160 bpm
  • Quickening is the mother first feeling movement, usually 16-20 weeks

Kick counts

Kick counts are home surveillance the mother does from 28 weeks. The common method is Cardiff count-to-ten.

She counts at the same time daily, ideally after a meal. She should feel at least 10 movements within 2 hours. Fewer than 10 in 2 hours means call the provider.

Non-stress test

The NST watches how the heart rate responds to movement. It is the most common antepartum test, and it is non-invasive.

Reactive: reassuring
  • Two or more accelerations of the heart rate
  • Each rises at least 15 bpm above baseline
  • Each lasts at least 15 seconds
  • Within a 20-minute window
  • No further immediate testing

Preterm under 32 weeks uses 10 bpm for 10 seconds.

Non-reactive: needs follow-up
  • Fewer than two qualifying accelerations in 20 minutes
  • Often a sleeping fetus, not distress
  • Try vibroacoustic stimulation
  • Extend to 40 minutes before calling it
  • Follow with a BPP or CST

Maternal sedatives or beta-blockers can flatten it.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
15 by 15, twice in 20
A reactive NST is two accelerations of at least 15 bpm, each lasting at least 15 seconds, within 20 minutes. Reactive means reassuring. Non-reactive may just be a sleeping fetus, so stimulate or extend to 40 minutes before you worry. Preterm under 32 weeks drops the bar to 10 bpm for 10 seconds.

Biophysical profile

The BPP adds four ultrasound measures to the NST. Each of the five scores 0 or 2, for a maximum of 10.

ComponentNormal, score 2Abnormal, score 0
Non-stress testReactiveNon-reactive
Fetal breathingOne or more spells of rhythmic breathing for 30 seconds in 30 minutesAbsent or under 30 seconds
Fetal movementThree or more body or limb movements in 30 minutesFewer than three
Fetal toneOne or more spells of extension and flexionSlow or absent movement
Amniotic fluidA pocket of 2 cm or more, or an index of 5 cm or moreLargest pocket under 2 cm
Reading the BPP score
  • 8-10: reassuring, repeat as indicated
  • 6: equivocal, repeat within 24 hours or deliver if near term
  • 4 or below: abnormal, strongly consider delivery

Amniotic fluid is the single most telling component; it reflects long-term status.

Contraction stress test

The CST watches the heart rate during contractions, mimicking the stress of labor. Contractions come from nipple stimulation or IV oxytocin, until three occur in 10 minutes.

ResultDefinitionMeaning
NegativeNo late decelerationsReassuring, good uteroplacental function
PositiveLate decelerations with half or more of contractionsNon-reassuring, suggests uteroplacental insufficiency
Equivocal-suspiciousIntermittent late or significant variable decelerationsRepeat or evaluate further
Equivocal-hyperstimulatoryDecelerations with contractions closer than every 2 minutes or over 90 secondsInadequate test, not fetal intolerance
When the CST is off limits

The CST is contraindicated in several settings. Contractions are the whole point, so anything that makes contractions dangerous rules it out.

  • Preterm labor or preterm rupture of membranes
  • Placenta previa
  • A previous classical, vertical cesarean scar
  • Multiple gestation
  • Cervical incompetence or cerclage

Maternal serum screening

Maternal serum tests screen the fetus for defects. AFP and the quad screen are drawn in the second trimester, around 15-20 weeks.

A high AFP points toward a neural tube defect. A low AFP points toward a chromosomal problem. These screen; they do not diagnose.

Bottom Line: The NST is the workhorse; reactive is reassuring. The BPP scores out of 10, and fluid matters most. Watch the naming: a negative CST and a reactive NST are both good.

Nutrition and lifestyle

Most of this section is teaching. You give the dose, the target, and the short list of things to avoid.

Weight gain by pre-pregnancy BMI

Pre-pregnancy BMICategoryTotal weight gainRate, 2nd and 3rd trimester
Under 18.5Underweight28-40 lbAbout 1 lb per week
18.5-24.9Normal25-35 lbAbout 1 lb per week

The first trimester adds only about 1-4 lb. Most gain comes later. For twins, add about 10-15 lb to the range.

Key nutrients

NutrientDaily amountWhy
Folic acid400-800 mcg, or 4 mg with prior NTDPrevents neural tube defects
Iron27 mgSupports the expanded blood volume; take with vitamin C
Calcium1,000 mg, or 1,300 mg under age 18Fetal bone and teeth; protects maternal bone

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Folic acid starts before conception
The neural tube closes by day 28, often before she knows she is pregnant. That is why every woman who could conceive takes 400-800 mcg of folic acid daily. A prior pregnancy with a neural tube defect raises the dose to 4 mg, started at least a month before conception.

Foods and substances to avoid

  • Raw or undercooked meat, eggs, and seafood: Salmonella, E. coli, Toxoplasma
  • High-mercury fish such as shark, swordfish, king mackerel, tilefish: neurotoxic to the fetus
  • Deli meats and hot dogs unless heated steaming hot: Listeria
  • Unpasteurized milk, cheese, and juice: Listeria and other bacteria
  • Raw sprouts: Salmonella and E. coli
  • Caffeine: limit to 200 mg per day, about one 12-oz coffee
  • Alcohol: no safe amount; it causes fetal alcohol spectrum disorders
  • Tobacco: preterm birth, low birth weight, abruption; offer cessation at every visit

Exercise

Moving safely
  • About 30 minutes of moderate exercise most days, roughly 150 minutes a week
  • Safe: walking, swimming, stationary cycling, prenatal yoga
  • Avoid: contact sports, fall-risk activities, scuba diving, hot tubs
  • Avoid exercising supine after the first trimester
  • Stop and call for bleeding, leaking fluid, dizziness, chest pain, calf pain, contractions, or reduced movement

Immunizations

  • Tdap: every pregnancy at 27-36 weeks, so antibodies reach the fetus before pertussis can
  • Inactivated influenza: safe in any trimester
  • MMR and varicella: live vaccines, held until postpartum
Bottom Line: Folic acid 400-800 mcg before conception prevents neural tube defects. Iron is 27 mg. Weight-gain targets follow pre-pregnancy BMI. Avoid raw foods, high-mercury fish, alcohol, and tobacco.

Danger signs

This is the list that separates a discomfort from an emergency. Each sign points at a specific threat. Know the reason and the sign sticks.

Danger signWhy it matters
Vaginal bleeding, any amount, any trimesterPlacenta previa, abruption, or miscarriage

Visual changes: blurring, spots, double visionPreeclampsia or eclampsia
Epigastric or right upper quadrant painLiver capsule stretch of HELLP or severe preeclampsia

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Three that scream preeclampsia
Severe headache, visual changes, and sudden face or hand edema, together in the third trimester, mean preeclampsia until proven otherwise. That combination sends her to labor and delivery, not to a clinic slot. Do not treat it as a normal headache.

Preeclampsia in one look

Preeclampsia is diagnosed after 20 weeks. It can move fast toward seizures or HELLP syndrome.

The diagnostic picture
  • Hypertension: systolic 140 or higher, or diastolic 90 or higher, on two readings 4 hours apart
  • Proteinuria: 300 mg or more in 24 hours, or a protein-to-creatinine ratio of 0.3 or higher
  • End-organ signs: high liver enzymes, low platelets, rising creatinine, or cerebral and visual symptoms

Severe features include a systolic of 160 or a diastolic of 110, platelets under 100,000, and persistent right upper quadrant pain.

Call the provider or go to the hospital

Call the provider
  • Mild headache that resolves with rest
  • Mild ankle edema at the end of the day
  • Nausea not settling with home measures
  • Dysuria without fever
  • Braxton Hicks that increase in frequency
  • Mild cramping without bleeding
Go to the hospital now
  • Any vaginal bleeding, especially bright red
  • Severe headache with visual changes
  • Sudden severe abdominal pain
  • Gush of fluid from the vagina
  • No fetal movement for 2 hours
  • Regular contractions before 37 weeks
Bottom Line: The call-now list: bleeding, severe headache, visual changes, epigastric pain, sudden face or hand edema, decreased movement, fluid gush, preterm contractions, fever. Preeclampsia starts after 20 weeks.

Rh incompatibility and RhoGAM

The mechanism is one line. An Rh-negative mother carrying an Rh-positive fetus can make anti-Rh antibodies that attack fetal red cells in a later pregnancy.

The first pregnancy is usually safe. It takes time to build antibodies after the first exposure. Each later Rh-positive pregnancy carries more risk if RhoGAM was skipped.

How sensitization happens

  • During delivery, the most common exposure
  • After miscarriage, abortion, or ectopic pregnancy
  • After amniocentesis, chorionic villus sampling, or cordocentesis
  • After abdominal trauma or placental abruption

RhoGAM timing

RhoGAM destroys any fetal Rh-positive cells in the maternal blood before her immune system reacts. Timing is the whole game.

WhenDoseWhy
28 weeks300 mcg IMRoutine prophylaxis for every Rh-negative, unsensitized mother
Within 72 hours postpartum300 mcg IMOnly if the newborn is confirmed Rh-positive
After early miscarriage or abortion50 mcg or 300 mcgPrevent sensitization from fetal-maternal bleeding
After amniocentesis, CVS, or trauma300 mcg IMAny time fetal and maternal blood may mix
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
28 weeks, then within 72 hours
Give RhoGAM at 28 weeks and within 72 hours postpartum if the newborn is Rh-positive. Check the indirect Coombs first; it must be negative. Give it again after any sensitizing event. If the newborn is Rh-negative, skip the postpartum dose. If the mother is already sensitized, RhoGAM will not help.

The Coombs tests

TestWhat it shows
Indirect Coombs, motherMaternal antibodies against Rh-positive cells; negative means give RhoGAM
Direct Coombs, newbornAntibodies stuck to the newborn's cells; positive means hemolysis is happening
Kleihauer-BetkeVolume of fetal-maternal bleed; decides if extra RhoGAM is needed
Cord blood typeNewborn blood type and Rh; guides the postpartum dose
Which Coombs is which

Indirect is in mom; it screens her blood for antibodies. Direct is on the baby; it tests the newborn's cells for attached antibodies. Indirect must be negative before RhoGAM.

Bottom Line: RhoGAM goes to Rh-negative, unsensitized mothers at 28 weeks and within 72 hours postpartum, plus after any sensitizing event. Check the indirect Coombs first. Indirect is in mom, direct is on the baby.
