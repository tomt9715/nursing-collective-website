Cardiac Arrhythmias

Every rhythm question is really three questions. Where did the beat start, is the patient holding up, and does this one get a shock.

01

What actually gets you

Rhythms feel impossible because they are taught as pictures to memorise. They are not pictures. Each one is a short list of answers to the same five questions, and once you ask the questions in the same order every time, the strip tells you what it is.

Points get lost here in four predictable ways.

You memorised the strips instead of the questions

So a rhythm you have seen in a textbook looks unfamiliar on an exam, because the artist drew it differently.

Rate, rhythm, P waves, PR interval, QRS width. Five questions, always in that order. The name falls out at the end.

You treated the monitor instead of the patient

The same rhythm gets a different answer depending on how the patient looks. Stable SVT gets vagal maneuvers. Unstable SVT gets electricity.

If a question gives you a blood pressure or a mental status, it is telling you which branch to take. Do not skip past it to the rhythm name.

You shocked something that does not get shocked

Asystole and PEA are not shockable. They look like emergencies, and the instinct is to reach for the defibrillator.

Only two rhythms get defibrillated: V-fib and pulseless V-tach. Everything else in arrest gets CPR and epinephrine.

You learned the blocks as four names

First degree, Type I, Type II, third degree. Four names is four things to confuse.

There is one axis: what the PR interval does before a beat gets dropped. Lengthening, constant, or unrelated. That single question sorts all four.

Bottom Line: Stop naming rhythms from memory. Ask five questions in a fixed order, then check the pulse, then decide.
02

The four things

These are the six rhythms that carry most of the exam. For each you should be able to say four things with the book closed: the one-sentence definition, the clue you would see on the strip, the priority action, and the first-line drug with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Rhythm Definition in one sentence Clue on the strip Priority action First-line drug
Atrial fibrillation The atria quiver instead of contracting, so the ventricles fire unpredictably. Irregularly irregular, no P waves anywhere. Rate control, then anticoagulation. The clot is the real threat. Beta-blocker or diltiazem for rate. Anticoagulant to prevent stroke.
SVT A reentry circuit above the ventricles drives a fast, regular rhythm. Regular, 150 to 250, narrow QRS, P waves hidden. Vagal maneuvers first if the patient is stable. Adenosine 6 mg rapid IV push, then 12 mg. Half-life under 10 seconds.
V-tach with a pulse The ventricles are driving a fast rhythm on their own. Wide QRS over 0.12 seconds, regular, 100 to 250. Check stability. Unstable means synchronized cardioversion. Amiodarone if stable. Electricity if not.
V-fib Chaotic ventricular activity with no organised contraction and no output. No QRS at all, only wavy chaos. Patient is pulseless. Defibrillate immediately, then high-quality CPR. Epinephrine 1 mg IV every 3 to 5 minutes. Amiodarone 300 mg IV.
Mobitz Type II Conduction fails below the AV node without warning, so beats drop suddenly. PR is constant, then a QRS is simply missing. Prepare for pacing. It can progress to complete block. Atropine does not work here. Pacing is the treatment.
Third degree block Atria and ventricles beat independently; nothing crosses the AV node. P waves regular, QRS regular, no relationship between them. Pacemaker. Temporary first, then permanent. No drug fixes this. Atropine is a holding measure at best.
Copy this down

Six rows on one index card, by hand. Irregularly irregular with no P waves is A-fib. Fast, regular and narrow is SVT. Fast and wide is ventricular. Constant PR with a missing beat is Type II. That card is most of the exam.

03

Where the beat comes from

The heart has a chain of command. Each level can start a beat, and each lower level is slower than the one above it.

That one sentence explains most of what you have to memorise here. A slow rhythm usually means a lower pacemaker took over.

Pacemaker siteIntrinsic rateRhythm it producesWhat you see
SA node, in the right atrium60 to 100 bpmNormal sinus rhythmUpright P wave before every QRS
AV junction40 to 60 bpmJunctional rhythmP waves absent or inverted, QRS still narrow
Purkinje fibres and ventricles20 to 40 bpmIdioventricular rhythmWide QRS over 0.12 s, no P waves, life-threatening
The rule that saves you memorising three lists

The lower the pacemaker, the slower the rate and the wider the QRS.

A wide QRS always means the impulse started below the AV node. That is not a fact to memorise separately; it follows from the anatomy. Below the AV node the impulse crawls through muscle instead of running down the conduction system, so the complex takes longer to write.

The path a beat takes

StepStructureWhat happens there
1SA nodeFires the impulse. This is the P wave
2Internodal pathwaysCarry it across the atria, which contract
3AV nodeHolds the impulse briefly so the atria can empty. This delay is the PR interval
4Bundle of HisCarries it into the septum, then splits

Step 3 is the one worth understanding. The AV node deliberately slows the signal down. Without that pause the ventricles would contract before the atria had finished filling them, and every heart block you learn is a problem with that pause.

Bottom Line: SA node 60 to 100, AV junction 40 to 60, ventricles 20 to 40. Lower means slower and wider.
04

Reading a strip in five steps

Do this in the same order every time. The order matters because each answer narrows what the rhythm can be.

  1. 1
    Rate. Count the QRS complexes in a 6 second strip and multiply by 10. Under 60 is bradycardia. Over 100 is tachycardia.
  2. 2
    Rhythm. Measure R to R. Even spacing is regular. No pattern at all is irregularly irregular, and that phrase means A-fib until proven otherwise.
  3. 3
    P waves. Present? Upright? One before each QRS? Absent P waves point to A-fib or a junctional rhythm.
  4. 4
    PR interval. Start of P to start of QRS. Normal is 0.12 to 0.20 seconds. Longer means block. Shorter means an accessory pathway.
  5. 5
    QRS width. Normal is under 0.12 seconds. Wide means the beat started below the AV node, or a bundle branch is blocked.

Steps 2 and 5 do most of the work. Regular or irregular, narrow or wide; those two answers put you in the right quarter of the map before you have named anything.

05

Sinus rhythms, and why they are easy

Sinus rhythms start in the SA node. Everything about the shape is normal: upright P waves, one before each QRS, normal PR, narrow QRS. Only the rate or the regularity changes.

That is why they are the easy group. If the shape is normal and only the speed is off, the question is not about the rhythm. It is about the cause.

RhythmRate and regularityCommon causesWhat you do
Normal sinus 60 to 100, regular Normal Nothing
Sinus bradycardia Under 60, regular Athletic conditioning, sleep, beta-blockers, digoxin, inferior MI, hypothyroidism, raised intracranial pressure Nothing if the patient is well. Atropine 0.5 mg IV and pacing if symptomatic
Sinus tachycardia Over 100, usually 100 to 160, regular Exercise, fever, pain, anxiety, hypovolemia, anemia, hyperthyroidism, caffeine, medications Treat the cause. Fluids, antipyretics, pain control. The rate is a symptom
Sinus arrhythmia 60 to 100, irregular with breathing Normal in children and young adults Nothing. Rate rises on inspiration and falls on expiration
Sinus tachycardia is a sign, not a diagnosis

A rate of 130 in a sinus rhythm means the body wants more output. Fever, pain, blood loss and fear all do that.

So the wrong answer is almost always a drug to slow it down. The right answer is to find what the heart is compensating for. Slow the rate without fixing the cause and you take away the compensation.

When bradycardia becomes an emergency

A slow rate matters only when it is not perfusing. Look for these:

  • Systolic blood pressure under 90 mmHg
  • Altered mental status or confusion
  • Chest pain, or other signs of ischemia
  • Cool, clammy, diaphoretic skin
  • Acute heart failure

Then treat: atropine 0.5 to 1 mg IV, to a maximum of 3 mg, and consider transcutaneous pacing.

Bottom Line: Sinus rhythms have a normal shape and an abnormal speed. Treat the cause, and treat the patient only when the rate stops perfusing.
06

Above the ventricle

Discrimination axis: regular or irregularly irregular.

Everything in this section starts above the ventricles, so the QRS stays narrow. The impulse still runs down the normal conduction system; it just did not start where it should have.

Narrow QRS plus fast is your signal that you are in this section, not the next one.

Atrial fibrillation
  • Rate variable, 60 to over 180
  • Rhythm irregularly irregular, with no pattern at all
  • P waves absent, replaced by chaotic fibrillatory activity
  • PR interval cannot be measured
  • QRS usually narrow

The real danger: blood pools in atria that are not contracting, and clots form. A clot that leaves goes to the brain.

Treatment: rate control with a beta-blocker, a calcium channel blocker or digoxin. Rhythm control with amiodarone or cardioversion. Anticoagulation almost always.

Atrial flutter
  • Atrial rate 250 to 350
  • Ventricular rate depends on the block; 2:1 gives about 150
  • Rhythm usually regular
  • Sawtooth flutter waves, best seen in II, III and aVF

Common ratios: 2:1 gives about 150. 3:1 gives about 100. 4:1 gives about 75.

Treatment: like A-fib. Rate control and anticoagulation. It often converts at low energy and responds well to ablation.

SVT
  • Rate 150 to 250
  • Rhythm regular
  • P waves buried in the preceding T wave, or not visible
  • QRS narrow, under 0.12 seconds

The giveaway: it starts and stops abruptly. A reentry circuit either runs or it does not.

Treatment: vagal maneuvers first if stable. Then adenosine 6 mg rapid IV push, then 12 mg. Synchronized cardioversion if the patient is unstable.

Premature atrial contractions
  • An early beat that interrupts the underlying rhythm
  • P wave present but shaped differently
  • QRS narrow and normal
  • Usually followed by a compensatory pause

Causes: caffeine, stress, alcohol, tobacco, electrolyte imbalance.

Treatment: usually none. Cut the stimulants. Investigate only if they are frequent.

The axis that separates them

Regularity, then P waves. A-fib is irregularly irregular with no P waves. Flutter is usually regular with sawtooth waves. SVT is regular, fast, and the P waves have hidden inside the T waves.

If a strip is fast, narrow and irregular with nothing recognisable in front of the QRS, you are done. That is A-fib.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
A-Fib Means Anticoagulation
Atria that quiver do not empty, so blood sits still and clots. The clot travels to the brain, which is why stroke prevention outranks rate control in the long run. The CHA2DS2-VASc score is how that risk gets quantified.
Adenosine, and why technique is the whole drug

Adenosine has a half-life under 10 seconds. Give it slowly and it is gone before it reaches the heart.

  • Use the IV site closest to the heart. Antecubital is the usual choice
  • Have a 20 mL saline flush ready in the same moment, not in the next one
  • Push the drug fast, then push the flush immediately behind it
  • Elevate the arm as you give it
  • Have the defibrillator at hand; the rhythm may pause before it resets

Warn the patient first. Brief chest discomfort, flushing and a sense of doom are expected, and they pass in seconds.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Adenosine Technique
Half-life under 10 seconds, so the push and the 20 mL flush have to be one motion. Closest IV site to the heart, arm elevated. Warn the patient about the few seconds of chest discomfort before you give it, not after.
Bottom Line: Narrow QRS means it started above the ventricles. Irregularly irregular with no P waves is A-fib, and A-fib means anticoagulate.
07

Below the AV node

Discrimination axis: is there a pulse, and is the rhythm shockable.

Ventricular rhythms have a wide QRS, over 0.12 seconds, because the impulse spreads through muscle instead of the conduction system. Muscle is slow, so the complex is wide.

Wide and fast is the group that kills people. Learn these four cold.

Shockable or not, in four rhythms
  • Ventricular fibrillation. Chaotic activity, no output. Shockable. Defibrillate now.
  • Pulseless ventricular tachycardia. Organised, wide, fast, and no pulse. Shockable. Defibrillate now.
  • Asystole. No electrical activity at all. Not shockable. CPR and epinephrine.
  • Pulseless electrical activity. An organised rhythm with no pulse. Not shockable. CPR and find the cause.

Two shockable, two not. If you remember nothing else from this section, remember which two.

Ventricular tachycardia
  • Rate 100 to 250
  • Rhythm usually regular
  • P waves absent, or marching independently underneath
  • QRS wide, over 0.12 seconds, and oddly shaped

Monomorphic: every QRS the same shape, usually a reentry circuit.

Polymorphic: the shape changes, usually ischemia or a long QT.

Treatment: with a pulse, amiodarone, or synchronized cardioversion if unstable. Pulseless, defibrillate and start CPR.

Ventricular fibrillation
  • Rate cannot be counted
  • Rhythm chaotic and disorganised
  • No P waves and no QRS complexes, only fibrillatory waves

The patient: pulseless, unresponsive, not breathing. This is cardiac arrest.

Treatment: defibrillate immediately, then high-quality CPR. Epinephrine 1 mg IV every 3 to 5 minutes. Amiodarone 300 mg IV.

Torsades de pointes
  • Rate 150 to 300
  • Wide QRS that twists around the baseline
  • Sits on top of a prolonged QT interval

Causes: long QT syndrome, low potassium, low magnesium, and drugs that stretch the QT including antiarrhythmics, antibiotics and antipsychotics.

Treatment: magnesium sulfate 1 to 2 g IV. Correct the electrolytes. Defibrillate if pulseless. Stop the offending drug.

Premature ventricular contractions
  • An early beat with a wide, odd QRS
  • No P wave in front of it
  • A full compensatory pause behind it

Patterns worth naming: bigeminy is every other beat, trigeminy is every third, a couplet is two together, and three or more in a row is a run of V-tach.

Treatment: occasional PVCs are usually benign. Treat the cause. Frequent or symptomatic ones get beta-blockers or antiarrhythmics.

R on T

The T wave is a vulnerable window. Ventricular muscle is partly repolarised there, and a stimulus arriving at that moment can throw the heart into fibrillation.

So a PVC that lands on a T wave is not just an extra beat. It is how a stable patient becomes a coding one, and it is why frequent PVCs get watched closely.

The H's and T's

PEA and asystole have no shock to give, so the treatment is finding the reversible cause.

  • H: hypovolemia, hypoxia, hydrogen ion (acidosis), hypokalemia or hyperkalemia, hypothermia
  • T: tension pneumothorax, tamponade, toxins, thrombosis in the lung or the coronary arteries
Write this down

Two columns: shockable and not shockable. Put the four arrest rhythms in the right column from memory. Then write the first drug for each column.

Bottom Line: Wide QRS means ventricular. V-fib and pulseless V-tach get shocked. Asystole and PEA get CPR and a search for the cause.
08

Where the signal stops

Discrimination axis: what the PR interval does before a beat gets dropped.

Four blocks, one question. Look at the PR intervals leading up to the missing QRS and ask what they were doing.

Constant and normal, nothing is dropped: first degree. Getting longer: Type I. Staying the same: Type II. Completely unrelated to the QRS: third degree. That is the whole section.

BlockPR intervalP to QRSWhat it isWhat you do
First degree Over 0.20 s, prolonged but constant 1:1 Every P conducts, just late. Usually benign Usually nothing. Monitor and review the medications
Second degree Type I, Wenckebach Lengthens progressively Variable, some P waves blocked PR stretches until a beat drops, then the cycle restarts. Sits at the AV node. Usually benign Monitor. Atropine if symptomatic. Pacing rarely needed
Second degree Type II, Mobitz II Constant, then a QRS is missing Variable, some P waves blocked No warning before the drop. Sits below the AV node. Can become complete block Often needs a pacemaker. Atropine does not help
Third degree, complete Variable and meaningless; P waves and QRS are independent No relationship at all Complete AV dissociation. Atria and ventricles run separate rhythms Pacemaker. Temporary first, then permanent

Type I or Type II

Type I, Wenckebach: the better one
  • PR lengthens progressively
  • Beats come in repeating groups
  • The block is at the AV node itself
  • Often reversible
  • Responds to atropine

The sound of it: longer, longer, longer, drop. Then it starts again.

Type II, Mobitz II: the bad one
  • PR is fixed
  • Beats drop without warning
  • The block is below the AV node, in the bundles
  • More likely to progress
  • Does not respond to atropine

The sound of it: same, same, same, drop. No pattern to warn you.

Why Type II is worse

Type I is a tired AV node. It fatigues, drops a beat, recovers, and starts again. Atropine speeds the AV node, so atropine helps.

Type II is damaged tissue below the node. There is nothing for atropine to speed up, and the failure is all-or-nothing. That is why it progresses to complete block without warning, and why the answer is a pacemaker.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Type I vs Type II Block
Type I is longer, longer, longer, drop. Type II is same, same, same, drop. Type I is a tired AV node and atropine speeds it up. Type II is damaged tissue below the node, so atropine does nothing and the answer is pacing.
Reading complete heart block

The atria and the ventricles have stopped talking. Each keeps its own time.

  • P waves march out regularly at their own rate
  • QRS complexes march out regularly at a different, slower rate
  • The two never line up in any consistent way
  • The ventricular rate depends on which escape pacemaker took over: 40 to 60 junctional, 20 to 40 ventricular

Measure the P to P and the R to R separately. Both regular, neither related, is the finding.

Bottom Line: Ask what the PR did before the drop. Lengthening is Type I and benign. Constant is Type II and needs pacing. Unrelated is complete block.
09

Shock or sync

Discrimination axis: does the patient have a pulse.

Both deliver electricity. The difference is timing, and the timing depends on one finding at the bedside.

The decision, in three lines
  • No pulse. This is arrest. Defibrillate, unsynchronized, for V-fib or pulseless V-tach.
  • Pulse, but unstable. Synchronized cardioversion, and sedate first if there is time.
  • Pulse, and stable. Drugs first. Adenosine, amiodarone, beta-blockers.
Defibrillation

What it is: an unsynchronized shock that stops all electrical activity at once, so the heart's own pacemaker can restart.

Used for: ventricular fibrillation, and pulseless ventricular tachycardia. Those two only.

  • No pulse means defibrillate
  • Unsynchronized, delivered immediately
  • Higher energy, 120 to 200 J biphasic
  • In a witnessed arrest, the shock comes first
Synchronized cardioversion

What it is: a shock timed to land on the R wave, so it avoids the T wave and does not trigger V-fib.

Used for: unstable A-fib with rapid ventricular response, unstable atrial flutter, unstable SVT after adenosine has failed, and V-tach with a pulse.

  • A pulse means cardiovert
  • Synchronized to the R wave
  • Lower energy, 50 to 200 J
  • The patient is awake, so they need sedation
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Defib vs Cardioversion
Check the pulse and the decision is made. No pulse is defibrillation. A pulse with instability is synchronized cardioversion, and that patient is awake, so sedate them. The sync mode resets after every shock, so re-engage it each time.
The sync button

Synchronized cardioversion needs the sync button pressed. It tells the machine to fire on the R wave and skip the vulnerable T wave.

Sync mode usually resets itself after each shock. If the first attempt fails, press it again before the second. Defibrillation never uses sync at all.

A fast rhythm becomes an emergency when it stops perfusing. Four findings decide it.

The exception worth understanding

Polymorphic V-tach breaks the pulse rule. The patient may have a pulse, and you still defibrillate.

The reason is mechanical, not clinical. The QRS shape keeps changing, so the machine cannot reliably find an R wave to synchronize to. No stable R wave means no sync. Give magnesium sulfate 1 to 2 g IV alongside it.

Bottom Line: Pulse check decides it. No pulse is defibrillation. Pulse with instability is synchronized cardioversion, and that patient needs sedation.
10

What you do first

Priority questions here give you a rhythm and four defensible actions. The rhythm is rarely the answer on its own; the patient is.

The ordering rules
  • Assess the patient before the monitor. A frightening strip on a comfortable patient is often artifact or a loose lead.
  • Pulse before rhythm name. It decides shock or no shock faster than identification does.
  • Stability before treatment. Stable buys you time for drugs; unstable does not.
  • Least invasive first, unless there is no pulse. Vagal before adenosine, adenosine before electricity.
  • Fix the cause when the rhythm is a symptom. Sinus tachycardia from hypovolemia needs fluid, not a beta-blocker.

Standing nursing actions

  1. 1
    Assess the patient, not just the monitor. Level of consciousness, vital signs, symptoms. A rhythm looks different when you know how the person holding it looks.
  2. 2
    Keep the monitoring good. Correct lead placement, clean electrode contact, sensible alarm limits. Document strips per policy and report changes as they happen.
  3. 3
    Have IV access. Every antiarrhythmic needs a line, and adenosine needs a good one close to the heart. Know where the crash cart is before you need it.
  4. 4
    Watch the electrolytes. Potassium and magnesium cause and worsen arrhythmias. Check them, report them, replace them as ordered.
  5. 5
    Give antiarrhythmics carefully. Most have narrow therapeutic windows, and many can cause the arrhythmia they are meant to treat. Know what each one does before you push it.
  6. 6
    Be ready. Know where the defibrillator is and how it works. Keep your ACLS current. Document every intervention and what the patient did after it.

Vagal maneuvers, before the drug

Stable SVT gets a vagal attempt first. All of these slow conduction through the AV node.

  • Valsalva: bear down as though having a bowel movement. The most effective of the group
  • Carotid massage: only if there is no bruit, and not in older adults, because of stroke risk
  • Cold water to the face: triggers the diving reflex
  • Coughing: sometimes enough on its own

What the patient needs to hear

  • Symptoms that matter: palpitations, dizziness, shortness of breath, chest discomfort.
  • Medications: take them as prescribed, and never stop an antiarrhythmic abruptly.
  • Triggers: caffeine, alcohol and tobacco can set arrhythmias off.
  • When to get help now: fainting, severe chest pain, trouble breathing, or new neurological symptoms.
  • Home monitoring: some patients benefit from checking their own pulse or using a home ECG device.
  • Anticoagulation: for A-fib, teach bleeding precautions, monitoring, and the dietary consistency warfarin needs.
Bottom Line: Assess the patient, not the monitor. Check the pulse, then stability, then the rhythm name.

No pulse = defibrillate. Pulse but unstable = synchronized cardioversion.

Asystole and PEA are not shockable. CPR, epinephrine, find the cause.

Longer, longer, drop = Type I. Same, same, drop = Type II.

Assess the patient, not the monitor. Stability decides the treatment.
