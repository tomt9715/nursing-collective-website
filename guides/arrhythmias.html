Cardiac Conduction System

The heart has its own electrical system that controls heart rate and rhythm. Understanding the conduction pathway is essential for interpreting ECG rhythms and recognizing where abnormalities originate.

Anatomy of the Conduction System

The cardiac conduction system consists of specialized cells that generate and conduct electrical impulses through the heart in an organized sequence:

Electrical Conduction Pathway

1
SA Node (Sinoatrial Node)

The "pacemaker" of the heart. Located in the right atrium. Fires at 60-100 bpm. Generates the P wave on ECG.

2
Internodal Pathways

Conduct impulse through the atria, causing atrial contraction (P wave). Three pathways: anterior, middle (Wenckebach), posterior (Thorel).

3
AV Node (Atrioventricular Node)

Located at the floor of the right atrium. Creates a delay (PR interval) to allow atria to empty before ventricles contract. Backup pacemaker at 40-60 bpm.

4
Bundle of His

Conducts impulse from AV node into the interventricular septum. Divides into left and right bundle branches.

5
Bundle Branches (Left & Right)

Carry impulse down the interventricular septum. Left bundle has anterior and posterior fascicles.

6
Purkinje Fibers

Terminal network spreading throughout ventricular myocardium. Causes rapid, coordinated ventricular contraction (QRS complex). Backup pacemaker at 20-40 bpm.

Clinical Pearl: Pacemaker Hierarchy

The heart has a "backup" system. If the SA node fails, the AV node can take over (40-60 bpm). If both fail, the ventricles can generate their own rhythm through Purkinje fibers (20-40 bpm). This "escape" mechanism prevents cardiac arrest but produces abnormal rhythms.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Pacemaker Hierarchy
Think of the heart's electrical system like a chain of command! The SA node is the "general" (60-100 bpm), the AV node is the "captain" (40-60 bpm), and the ventricles are the "soldiers" (20-40 bpm). If the boss is out, the next in line takes over - but they're slower at making decisions. That's why a junctional or ventricular escape rhythm is slower than normal sinus rhythm. The lower the pacemaker, the slower the rate and the wider the QRS.

Intrinsic Rates

Pacemaker Site Intrinsic Rate Resulting Rhythm Clinical Significance
SA Node 60-100 bpm Normal Sinus Rhythm Normal pacemaker, produces upright P waves
AV Junction 40-60 bpm Junctional Rhythm Absent or inverted P waves, narrow QRS
Purkinje/Ventricles 20-40 bpm Idioventricular Rhythm Wide QRS (>0.12s), no P waves, life-threatening
NCLEX Tip: The Lower the Pacemaker, the Slower the Rate

Remember: As you go lower in the conduction system, the intrinsic rate gets slower and the QRS gets wider. SA node = 60-100, AV junction = 40-60, Ventricles = 20-40. A wide QRS always means the impulse originated below the AV node.

ECG Basics & Interpretation

The electrocardiogram (ECG/EKG) is a graphic representation of the electrical activity of the heart. Each waveform corresponds to a specific electrical event.

ECG Waveforms and What They Represent

Normal ECG Complex

PR Interval = Start of P to start of QRS
PR Segment = End of P to start of QRS
QRS Complex = Ventricular depolarization
ST Segment = End of QRS to start of T
QT Interval = Start of QRS to end of T
Component Represents Normal Value Abnormal Findings
P Wave Atrial depolarization <0.12 sec, <2.5mm tall Absent (A-fib), peaked (RAE), notched (LAE)
PR Interval Time from atrial to ventricular depolarization 0.12-0.20 sec (3-5 small boxes) Short (<0.12) = WPW; Long (>0.20) = heart block
QRS Complex Ventricular depolarization 0.06-0.12 sec (<3 small boxes) Wide (>0.12) = bundle branch block or V-origin
ST Segment Time between depolarization and repolarization Isoelectric (flat at baseline) Elevation = MI; Depression = ischemia
T Wave Ventricular repolarization Upright (same direction as QRS) Inverted = ischemia; Peaked = hyperkalemia
QT Interval Total ventricular electrical activity <0.44 sec (varies with HR) Prolonged = risk of Torsades de Pointes

Systematic ECG Interpretation

Use this step-by-step approach to systematically analyze every rhythm strip:

5-Step ECG Analysis

1
Rate

Count the number of QRS complexes in a 6-second strip and multiply by 10. Normal = 60-100 bpm. Bradycardia <60, Tachycardia >100.

2
Rhythm

Is the R-R interval regular or irregular? Measure the distance between consecutive R waves. Regular = consistent spacing. Irregularly irregular = atrial fibrillation.

3
P Waves

Are P waves present? Are they upright and uniform? Is there one P wave before each QRS? Absent P waves suggest atrial fibrillation or junctional rhythm.

4
PR Interval

Measure from the beginning of P wave to beginning of QRS. Normal = 0.12-0.20 sec. Prolonged PR = AV block. Short PR = WPW syndrome.

5
QRS Width

Measure the QRS complex duration. Normal = <0.12 sec (3 small boxes). Wide QRS indicates ventricular origin or bundle branch block.

Memory Aid: ECG Interpretation

5 R's of ECG Interpretation:

  • Rate - How fast?
  • Rhythm - Regular or irregular?
  • Round P waves - Present and normal?
  • Relationship - PR interval normal? P before each QRS?
  • RRS width - Narrow or wide QRS?

Sinus Rhythms

Sinus rhythms originate from the SA node. They are characterized by upright P waves in lead II (with one P wave before each QRS), a normal PR interval, and a narrow QRS complex.

Normal Sinus Rhythm (NSR)

Characteristics:

  • Rate: 60-100 bpm
  • Rhythm: Regular
  • P waves: Upright, uniform, one before each QRS
  • PR interval: 0.12-0.20 sec
  • QRS: <0.12 sec (narrow)

Clinical Significance:

Normal rhythm. No intervention needed unless symptomatic.

Sinus Bradycardia

Characteristics:

  • Rate: <60 bpm
  • Rhythm: Regular
  • P waves: Normal
  • PR/QRS: Normal

Causes:

Athletes, sleep, beta-blockers, digoxin, inferior MI, hypothyroidism, increased ICP

Treatment:

If symptomatic: Atropine 0.5mg IV, transcutaneous pacing, treat underlying cause

Sinus Tachycardia

Characteristics:

  • Rate: >100 bpm (usually 100-160)
  • Rhythm: Regular
  • P waves: Normal (may be buried in preceding T wave at high rates)
  • PR/QRS: Normal

Causes:

Exercise, fever, pain, anxiety, hypovolemia, anemia, hyperthyroidism, caffeine, medications

Treatment:

Treat underlying cause (fluids, antipyretics, pain control). Usually not treated directly.

Sinus Arrhythmia

Characteristics:

  • Rate: 60-100 bpm (variable)
  • Rhythm: Irregular (varies with respiration)
  • P waves: Normal
  • PR/QRS: Normal

Clinical Significance:

Common in children and young adults. Rate increases with inspiration, decreases with expiration. Benign - no treatment needed.

Critical: Symptomatic Bradycardia

Bradycardia becomes an emergency when the patient is symptomatic:

  • Hypotension (SBP <90 mmHg)
  • Altered mental status/confusion
  • Chest pain/ischemia
  • Signs of shock (cool, clammy, diaphoretic)
  • Acute heart failure

Treatment: Atropine 0.5-1mg IV (max 3mg), then consider transcutaneous pacing

Atrial Arrhythmias

Atrial arrhythmias originate above the ventricles (in the atria or AV junction). They typically have narrow QRS complexes because ventricular conduction follows normal pathways.

Atrial Fibrillation (A-Fib)

Characteristics:

  • Rate: Variable (60-180+ bpm)
  • Rhythm: Irregularly irregular
  • P waves: Absent - chaotic fibrillatory waves
  • PR: Not measurable
  • QRS: Usually narrow

Risks:

Blood pools in atria → clot formation → stroke. Anticoagulation critical!

Treatment:

Rate control (beta-blockers, CCBs, digoxin), rhythm control (amiodarone, cardioversion), anticoagulation

Atrial Flutter

Characteristics:

  • Atrial rate: 250-350 bpm
  • Ventricular rate: Varies with block (typically 150 with 2:1 block)
  • Rhythm: Usually regular
  • P waves: "Sawtooth" flutter waves (best seen in leads II, III, aVF)

Common Ratios:

2:1 (ventricular rate ~150), 3:1 (~100), 4:1 (~75)

Treatment:

Similar to A-fib: rate control, anticoagulation. Often responds well to cardioversion or ablation.

SVT (Supraventricular Tachycardia)

Characteristics:

  • Rate: 150-250 bpm
  • Rhythm: Regular
  • P waves: Often hidden in preceding T wave or absent
  • QRS: Narrow (<0.12 sec)

Sudden Onset/Offset:

Starts and stops abruptly. Often caused by reentry circuit (AVNRT, AVRT).

Treatment:

Vagal maneuvers (Valsalva, carotid massage), Adenosine 6mg rapid IV push → 12mg if no response, cardioversion if unstable

Premature Atrial Contractions (PACs)

Characteristics:

  • Early beat that interrupts the rhythm
  • P wave: Present but different morphology
  • QRS: Narrow (normal)
  • Usually followed by compensatory pause

Causes:

Caffeine, stress, alcohol, tobacco, electrolyte imbalances

Treatment:

Usually benign - no treatment needed. Reduce stimulants. Investigate if frequent.

NCLEX Tip: A-Fib and Stroke Risk

The "irregularly irregular" rhythm of atrial fibrillation is a classic NCLEX identifier. Remember: stagnant blood in fibrillating atria forms clots. A clot can embolize to the brain causing stroke. Anticoagulation (warfarin, DOACs) is essential unless contraindicated. CHA₂DS₂-VASc score determines stroke risk.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
A-Fib & Stroke Risk
A-fib = Anticoagulation! When the atria quiver instead of contract, blood pools and can form clots. These clots love to travel to the brain and cause strokes. That's why nearly every A-fib patient needs blood thinners (unless they have a major contraindication). The CHA₂DS₂-VASc score helps us quantify stroke risk. Remember: irregularly irregular + no P waves = think A-fib, think anticoagulation, think stroke prevention!
Clinical Pearl: Adenosine Administration

Adenosine has a half-life of <10 seconds. It must be given as a rapid IV push followed immediately by a 20mL saline flush. Use the closest IV site to the heart (antecubital preferred). Warn patients they may feel brief chest discomfort, flushing, or sense of impending doom - this is normal and transient.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Adenosine Technique
Adenosine is like a reset button for the heart - but you only have 10 seconds! This drug has an incredibly short half-life, so technique is everything. Use the IV site closest to the heart (antecubital is best), give it as a rapid push, and immediately chase it with a 20mL saline bolus. Warn your patient they might feel like they're having a heart attack for a few seconds - that's normal! The brief sensation of doom passes quickly as the drug works.

Ventricular Arrhythmias

Ventricular arrhythmias originate below the AV node. They are characterized by wide QRS complexes (>0.12 seconds) because the impulse spreads through ventricular muscle rather than the normal conduction system. Many are life-threatening.

Life-Threatening Rhythms - Immediate Action Required

Ventricular Fibrillation (V-Fib)

Chaotic, disorganized electrical activity. No cardiac output. SHOCK immediately (defibrillation).

Pulseless Ventricular Tachycardia

Organized wide-complex tachycardia without pulse. SHOCK immediately (defibrillation).

Asystole

Flatline - no electrical activity. Non-shockable. CPR + epinephrine.

Pulseless Electrical Activity (PEA)

Organized rhythm without pulse. Non-shockable. CPR + treat reversible causes (H's and T's).

Ventricular Tachycardia (V-Tach)

Characteristics:

  • Rate: 100-250 bpm
  • Rhythm: Usually regular
  • P waves: Usually absent or dissociated
  • QRS: Wide >0.12 sec, bizarre morphology

Types:

  • Monomorphic: Same QRS shape - often reentry circuit
  • Polymorphic: Varying QRS shapes - often ischemia or long QT

Treatment:

  • With pulse: Amiodarone, synchronized cardioversion if unstable
  • Pulseless: Defibrillation + CPR
Ventricular Fibrillation (V-Fib)

Characteristics:

  • Rate: Cannot determine (chaotic)
  • Rhythm: Chaotic, disorganized
  • P waves: None
  • QRS: None - only fibrillatory waves

Clinical Presentation:

Pulseless, unresponsive, no breathing. This is cardiac arrest!

Treatment:

IMMEDIATE DEFIBRILLATION + high-quality CPR. Epinephrine 1mg IV q3-5min. Amiodarone 300mg IV.

Torsades de Pointes

Characteristics:

  • Rate: 150-300 bpm
  • QRS: Wide, "twisting" around the baseline
  • Associated with prolonged QT interval

Causes:

Long QT syndrome, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, certain drugs (antiarrhythmics, antibiotics, antipsychotics)

Treatment:

Magnesium sulfate 1-2g IV. Correct electrolytes. Defibrillate if pulseless. Avoid drugs that prolong QT.

Premature Ventricular Contractions (PVCs)

Characteristics:

  • Early beat with wide, bizarre QRS
  • No P wave precedes the beat
  • Full compensatory pause follows

Patterns to Know:

  • Bigeminy: Every other beat is a PVC
  • Trigeminy: Every third beat is a PVC
  • Couplet: Two PVCs in a row
  • Triplet/Run: Three+ PVCs in a row (= V-tach if sustained)

Treatment:

Occasional PVCs often benign. Treat underlying cause. Frequent/symptomatic: beta-blockers, antiarrhythmics.

Warning: "R on T" Phenomenon

If a PVC occurs during the T wave (the relative refractory period), it can trigger ventricular fibrillation. The T wave represents a vulnerable period in the cardiac cycle. Monitor patients with frequent PVCs closely for "R on T" pattern.

Memory Aid: H's and T's of PEA/Asystole

Reversible causes to check during cardiac arrest:

  • Hypovolemia, Hypoxia, Hydrogen ion (acidosis), Hypo/Hyperkalemia, Hypothermia
  • Tension pneumothorax, Tamponade (cardiac), Toxins, Thrombosis (pulmonary/coronary)

Heart Blocks

Heart blocks occur when conduction through the AV node is delayed or blocked. They are classified by degree of block and have different clinical implications.

Type PR Interval P:QRS Ratio Characteristics Treatment
1st Degree >0.20 sec (prolonged but constant) 1:1 Every P wave conducts, just delayed. Usually benign. Usually none. Monitor. Review medications.
2nd Degree Type I (Wenckebach/Mobitz I) Progressively lengthens Variable (some P's blocked) PR lengthens until a beat is dropped. Pattern repeats. Usually benign. Monitor. Atropine if symptomatic. Usually no pacing needed.
2nd Degree Type II (Mobitz II) Constant (then dropped QRS) Variable (some P's blocked) Fixed PR, then suddenly dropped beats. More serious - can progress to 3rd degree. Often requires pacemaker. May progress to complete heart block.
3rd Degree (Complete) Variable (P's and QRS independent) No relationship Complete AV dissociation. Atria and ventricles beat independently. Pacemaker required. Temporary pacing, then permanent.
Type I (Wenckebach) - "Better Block"

Key Features:

  • Progressive PR lengthening
  • Grouped beating pattern
  • Occurs at AV node level
  • Often reversible
  • Responds to atropine

Memory Trick:

"Longer, longer, longer, DROP" - then the cycle repeats

Type II (Mobitz II) - "Bad Block"

Key Features:

  • Fixed PR interval
  • Sudden dropped beats
  • Occurs below AV node (bundle branches)
  • More likely to progress
  • Does NOT respond to atropine

Memory Trick:

"Same, same, same, DROP" - unpredictable, dangerous

NCLEX Tip: Distinguishing Type I vs Type II

For second-degree blocks, focus on the PR interval before the dropped beat:

  • Type I: PR interval gets progressively longer before the drop ("Wenck-BAD" = worsening PR)
  • Type II: PR interval stays the same before the drop (like a "sudden surprise")

Type II is more serious because it often progresses to complete heart block suddenly.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Type I vs Type II Block
Here's my favorite way to remember the difference: Type I (Wenckebach) = "Longer, longer, longer, DROP!" The PR interval progressively lengthens until it finally drops a beat, then the cycle repeats. Type II (Mobitz II) = "Same, same, same, DROP!" The PR intervals are all the same length, then suddenly and unpredictably a beat is dropped. Type II is the bad one because it can suddenly progress to complete heart block without warning - these patients often need a pacemaker!
Critical: Third-Degree (Complete) Heart Block

In complete heart block, the atria and ventricles beat independently of each other. Look for:

  • Regular P waves at their own rate
  • Regular QRS complexes at a different (slower) rate
  • No consistent relationship between P waves and QRS
  • Ventricular rate depends on escape pacemaker (40-60 junctional, 20-40 ventricular)

This is a life-threatening emergency requiring pacemaker insertion!

Defibrillation vs Cardioversion

Both defibrillation and cardioversion deliver electrical shocks to the heart, but they serve different purposes and are used in different situations. Understanding when to use each is critical for NCLEX and clinical practice.

Defibrillation

Purpose:

Unsynchronized shock to terminate chaotic rhythms and allow the heart's natural pacemaker to reset

When to Use:

  • Ventricular Fibrillation (V-Fib)
  • Pulseless Ventricular Tachycardia

Key Points:

  • NO pulse = Defibrillation
  • Unsynchronized (immediate shock)
  • Higher energy: 120-200J biphasic
  • Time-critical: shock first in witnessed arrest

Rhythm Recognition:

These are shockable rhythms - disorganized electrical activity causing no cardiac output

Synchronized Cardioversion

Purpose:

Synchronized shock timed to R wave to avoid the vulnerable T wave period and prevent triggering V-fib

When to Use:

  • Unstable A-Fib with RVR
  • Unstable Atrial Flutter
  • Unstable SVT (after adenosine fails)
  • V-Tach WITH pulse (unstable)

Key Points:

  • HAS pulse = Cardioversion
  • Synchronized to R wave
  • Lower energy: 50-200J
  • Patient is awake - sedation needed!

Rhythm Recognition:

These are unstable tachyarrhythmias - organized rhythms causing hemodynamic instability

Critical Decision Point

Ask yourself: Does the patient have a pulse?

  • NO pulse: This is cardiac arrest. DEFIBRILLATE immediately for V-Fib or pulseless V-Tach
  • HAS pulse but unstable: This is a tachyarrhythmia causing symptoms. Consider SYNCHRONIZED CARDIOVERSION
  • HAS pulse and stable: Use medications first (adenosine, amiodarone, beta-blockers)
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Defib vs Cardioversion
Pulse check is EVERYTHING! Here's the simple rule: No pulse = Defibrillate (unsynchronized shock for V-Fib or pulseless V-Tach). Has pulse but unstable = Synchronized Cardioversion. The sync button tells the defibrillator to time the shock with the R wave, avoiding the vulnerable T wave period. Remember: cardioversion patients are awake and need sedation! Defibrillation patients are in cardiac arrest - just shock and start CPR.

Signs of Hemodynamic Instability

Use synchronized cardioversion when the tachyarrhythmia is causing any of these symptoms:

Unstable Signs (Need Immediate Cardioversion)

Blood Pressure
Hypotension (SBP <90)
Mental Status
Altered, confused
Chest Pain
Ischemic symptoms
Heart Failure
Acute pulmonary edema
NCLEX Tip: The "Sync" Button

For synchronized cardioversion, you must press the SYNC button on the defibrillator. This tells the machine to deliver the shock during the R wave, avoiding the vulnerable T wave period. Important: After each shock, the sync mode often resets - you must re-engage it before each subsequent cardioversion. In contrast, defibrillation does NOT use sync mode.

Energy Levels

Rhythm Intervention Energy (Biphasic) Notes
V-Fib / Pulseless V-Tach Defibrillation 120-200J (max output) Unsynchronized, immediate
Unstable A-Fib Synchronized Cardioversion 120-200J May need higher energy due to irregular rhythm
Atrial Flutter Synchronized Cardioversion 50-100J Usually converts at lower energy
SVT Synchronized Cardioversion 50-100J Try adenosine first if stable
Monomorphic V-Tach with pulse Synchronized Cardioversion 100J Synchronized because patient has pulse
Polymorphic V-Tach (Torsades) Defibrillation 120-200J Treat as V-Fib (sync may not work)
Special Case: Polymorphic V-Tach (Torsades de Pointes)

Although the patient may have a pulse, treat polymorphic V-Tach as defibrillation, not synchronized cardioversion. Why? The machine may have difficulty finding a consistent R wave to synchronize with due to the varying QRS morphology. Also give Magnesium sulfate 1-2g IV.

Nursing Interventions

Nursing care for patients with arrhythmias focuses on continuous monitoring, early recognition of deterioration, safe medication administration, and emergency response readiness.

General Nursing Interventions for Arrhythmias

Priority Nursing Actions

1
Assess the Patient, Not Just the Monitor

Always check the patient's clinical status. A rhythm may look dangerous on the monitor but be artifact. Conversely, a "normal-looking" rhythm can mask clinical deterioration. Assess level of consciousness, vital signs, and symptoms.

2
Continuous Cardiac Monitoring

Ensure proper lead placement and electrode contact. Set appropriate alarm parameters. Document rhythm strips per facility policy. Report any significant changes immediately.

3
Establish/Maintain IV Access

Antiarrhythmic medications require IV access. For emergency situations, ensure at least one large-bore IV. Know where emergency equipment is located.

4
Monitor Electrolytes

Electrolyte imbalances (especially potassium and magnesium) can cause or worsen arrhythmias. Monitor lab values and report abnormalities. Replace as ordered.

5
Medication Administration Safety

Know the indications, contraindications, and side effects of antiarrhythmic drugs. Monitor for proarrhythmic effects (drugs that can worsen or cause new arrhythmias). Many antiarrhythmics have narrow therapeutic windows.

6
Emergency Preparedness

Know location and function of defibrillator/crash cart. Maintain current ACLS certification. Be prepared to initiate CPR if needed. Document all interventions and patient responses.

Common Antiarrhythmic Medications

Medication Class Used For Nursing Considerations
Adenosine (Adenocard) Endogenous nucleoside SVT (first-line) Rapid IV push + flush. Half-life <10 sec. Warn patient of brief chest discomfort.
Amiodarone (Cordarone) Class III V-Tach, V-Fib, A-Fib Causes hypotension, bradycardia. Monitor thyroid, liver, lungs. Long half-life (weeks).
Atropine Anticholinergic Symptomatic bradycardia 0.5-1mg IV. May cause tachycardia, dry mouth, urinary retention. Not effective for Mobitz II.
Diltiazem (Cardizem) CCB A-Fib rate control, SVT Causes hypotension, bradycardia. Avoid in heart failure with reduced EF.
Metoprolol (Lopressor) Beta-blocker Rate control (A-Fib, SVT) Monitor HR, BP. Avoid in asthma, decompensated HF, severe bradycardia.
Magnesium Sulfate Electrolyte Torsades de Pointes 1-2g IV over 5-20 min. Monitor for hypotension. Check reflexes if on continuous infusion.
Epinephrine Catecholamine Cardiac arrest (all rhythms) 1mg IV q3-5 min during arrest. Causes vasoconstriction, increases BP.
Clinical Pearl: Adenosine Technique

Adenosine has a half-life of less than 10 seconds, so technique matters:

  • Use the IV site closest to the heart (antecubital preferred)
  • Prepare a 20mL normal saline flush in the same syringe or immediately available
  • Inject adenosine as a rapid push followed immediately by the flush
  • Elevate the arm during administration
  • Have the defibrillator ready - rhythm may temporarily pause or deteriorate
NCLEX Tip: Vagal Maneuvers for SVT

Before administering adenosine for stable SVT, attempt vagal maneuvers:

  • Valsalva maneuver: Bear down as if having a bowel movement (most effective)
  • Carotid massage: Only if no bruit, not in elderly (risk of stroke)
  • Cold water to face: Triggers diving reflex
  • Coughing: May terminate the rhythm

These stimulate the vagus nerve, slowing AV nodal conduction.

Patient Education

  • Symptom recognition: Teach patients to recognize palpitations, dizziness, shortness of breath, and chest discomfort
  • Medication compliance: Emphasize importance of taking antiarrhythmics as prescribed; never stop abruptly
  • Trigger avoidance: Reduce caffeine, alcohol, and tobacco which can precipitate arrhythmias
  • When to seek help: Syncope, severe chest pain, difficulty breathing, or new neurological symptoms require immediate evaluation
  • Home monitoring: Some patients may benefit from pulse checks or home ECG devices
  • Anticoagulation teaching: For patients on blood thinners (A-Fib), teach about bleeding precautions, dietary considerations (warfarin), and monitoring

Key Points Summary

Conduction System: SA node (60-100) → AV node (40-60) → Bundle/Purkinje (20-40). Lower site = slower rate, wider QRS.

ECG Basics: P wave = atrial depolarization. QRS = ventricular depolarization. T wave = ventricular repolarization.

A-Fib Recognition: Irregularly irregular rhythm, absent P waves, narrow QRS. Major stroke risk - anticoagulation essential.

Lethal Rhythms: V-Fib and pulseless V-Tach require immediate defibrillation. Asystole and PEA are non-shockable - give CPR + epinephrine.

Defib vs Cardio: No pulse = defibrillate. Has pulse but unstable = synchronized cardioversion. Has pulse and stable = medications first.

Heart Blocks: Type I (Wenckebach) = progressive PR lengthening. Type II = fixed PR then dropped beat (worse prognosis). Complete = AV dissociation.

Electrolytes Matter: Hypokalemia and hypomagnesemia predispose to arrhythmias. Hyperkalemia causes peaked T waves and can lead to asystole.

Assess the Patient: Always correlate rhythm with clinical presentation. A "dangerous" rhythm in an asymptomatic patient needs different management than in an unstable patient.
