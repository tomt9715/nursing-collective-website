01

What actually gets you

Assessment feels like the easy topic. It is a list of normal values and a few mnemonics, and it looks like something you already know.

Then the exam asks which finding you act on, and in what order. Points get lost in four predictable ways.

You read a number instead of a trend

A blood pressure of 100/70 is normal for one patient and a crisis for another who lives at 160/90.

A patient who has gone 140/80, then 120/70, then 100/60 across four hours is deteriorating in front of you. Every one of those readings is inside the normal range.

You assumed higher always means worse

Most scales work that way. Braden does not. A Braden of 9 is a patient in serious danger; a Braden of 19 is nearly fine.

Braden measures the patient's ability to protect themselves. Less ability, lower score, higher risk.

You were reassured by a child's blood pressure

Children hold their blood pressure normal until they cannot, and then they crash.

Tachycardia is the early sign. Hypotension is the late one, and by the time it appears the child is already in decompensated shock.

You decided the patient looked fine

A patient who says their pain is 8 out of 10 while chatting comfortably still has pain of 8 out of 10. Self-report is the measurement, not a claim to be verified.

Behavioural tools exist for patients who cannot report. They do not exist to overrule patients who can.

Bottom Line: Study this as trends against baselines, scales with thresholds, and a short list of findings that mean call now.
02

The four things

This guide has no diseases and no first-line drugs, so the four things shift slightly. For every tool below, say what it measures, when you reach for it, the number that changes your behaviour, and what you do at that number.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Tool What it measures in one sentence When you use it The number that acts What you do at that number
Vital signs The basic physiology, measured objectively. Every patient, every shift, and any time something changes. Any value outside the range, or any trend moving one way. Compare to their baseline, then look for the cause.
Glasgow Coma Scale Level of consciousness, as eye, verbal and motor response. Any neurological injury or altered consciousness. 8 or below. They cannot protect the airway. Anticipate intubation.
Braden Scale How likely this patient is to develop a pressure injury. On admission, every shift, and after any status change. 12 or below. High risk. Specialty surface, frequent turns, dietary review.
Pain scale How much pain the patient has, by report or by behaviour. Every assessment, and after every intervention. Whatever they tell you it is. Treat, then reassess. 30 minutes oral, 15 minutes IV.
Orientation and AVPU Whether the patient knows who and where they are, and what rouses them. Every assessment, and urgently if anything has changed. Any change from their own baseline. New confusion is an emergency. Look for a cause.
Copy this down

Five rows on one index card, by hand. GCS 8 and you protect the airway. Braden 12 and you protect the skin. Trends beat single values. New confusion is an emergency. That card is most of the exam.

03

The order that never changes

Assessment is the first step of the nursing process. Everything after it is built on what you found. A diagnosis from an incomplete assessment is a guess with a label.

Four kinds of assessment

TypeWhen you do itHow much you cover
ComprehensiveAdmission, or an annual physicalFull head to toe, with a complete history
FocusedA specific complaint or problemTargeted at the chief complaint
Ongoing, or shiftEvery shift, as routine monitoringSystem-specific, hunting for change from baseline
EmergencySomething is immediately life-threateningAirway, breathing, circulation. A rapid primary survey

The four techniques, in order

IPPA
  • Inspection. Look. Always first
  • Palpation. Touch and feel
  • Percussion. Tap
  • Auscultation. Listen

Least invasive to most invasive, which is why looking comes before touching.

The one exception, and why it exists

For the abdomen the order becomes inspect, auscultate, percuss, palpate.

The reason is mechanical. Pressing on the abdomen stimulates or suppresses bowel sounds. Palpating first changes the thing you were about to measure. Listen while the gut is undisturbed.

That is the whole exception. Every other region keeps IPPA.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
IPPA, Except the Abdomen
Inspect, palpate, percuss, auscultate. For the abdomen it is inspect, auscultate, percuss, palpate, because touching the belly changes the bowel sounds you were about to listen for. One exception, one reason, and it gets asked constantly.
TechniqueWhat it isWhat it tells you
InspectionLooking, and using smellColour, size, shape, symmetry, movement, lesions, drainage
PalpationUsing the handsTemperature, moisture, texture, tenderness, masses, pulses
PercussionTapping to produce soundDensity underneath: resonance, dullness, tympany
AuscultationListening with a stethoscopeHeart, lung and bowel sounds, and bruits
Bottom Line: Inspect, palpate, percuss, auscultate. The abdomen is the exception, and the reason is that touching it changes the bowel sounds.
04

Subjective or objective

Discrimination axis: did the patient tell you, or did you measure it.

The two get documented differently. Mixing them is how an assumption ends up in a chart as a fact.

Subjective: what they tell you
  • Symptoms: pain, nausea, dizziness
  • Feelings and perceptions
  • The chief complaint
  • Health history
  • Allergies
  • Medications
  • Family history
  • Psychosocial information

How you write it: in quotation marks. Patient states, then their words.

Objective: what you measure
  • Vital signs
  • Physical examination findings
  • Lab values
  • Diagnostic results
  • Observable behaviour
  • Wound appearance
  • Intake and output
  • Weight and height

How you write it: in measurable terms. BP 140/90, not blood pressure elevated.

SAMPLE, for a history in a hurry
  • Signs and symptoms. What is wrong
  • Allergies. Drugs, food, environment
  • Medications. Prescribed, over the counter, supplements
  • Past medical history. Surgery, chronic conditions
  • Last oral intake. When they last ate or drank
  • Events leading up. What happened before this started
PQRST, for pain
  • Provokes and palliates. What makes it worse, what makes it better
  • Quality. What does it feel like. Sharp, dull, burning, crushing
  • Radiates. Does it go anywhere. Chest to left arm is the classic
  • Severity. Rate it, on the right scale for this patient
  • Timing. When did it start. Constant or coming and going
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
PQRST
Provokes, quality, radiates, severity, timing. The R is the one people skip, and it is often the most diagnostic; chest pain radiating to the left arm or jaw is a different conversation from chest pain that stays put.
Bottom Line: Subjective is what they say, in quotes. Objective is what you measure, in numbers. Keep them separate on the page.

Vital signs, and why trends beat values

Vital signNormal adult rangeWhat to remember

Pulse60 to 100 bpmRate, rhythm and quality, not just the number

One reading tells you almost nothing

A normal range is a population statement. Your patient is one person with their own baseline.

So the useful question is never whether this number is normal. It is whether it is normal for them, and which way it is moving. Chart the values so the pattern shows. A trend you can see is a deterioration you can catch.

Cushing's triad

Three findings together mean rising intracranial pressure:

  • A widening pulse pressure, as the systolic climbs and the diastolic falls
  • Bradycardia
  • Irregular respirations

Note what is odd about this. A rising pressure with a falling pulse is the opposite of shock. It is also a late sign. Notify immediately.

Bottom Line: Know the ranges, but read the trend against their baseline. Cuff too small reads high. Rising pressure with falling pulse is Cushing's triad.

Glasgow Coma Scale

Three components, scored separately and added: eye opening, verbal response, motor response. The total runs 3 to 15.

Notice that the minimum is 3, not 0. Every component scores at least 1, even in a patient with no response at all.

Eight or below, intubate

At a GCS of 8 the gag and cough reflexes are unreliable. The patient cannot keep their own airway clear, and aspiration risk is very high.

This is not a rule about the brain. It is a rule about the airway. That is why it is a nursing priority.

Why the total alone is not enough

Two patients can both score 8 and be in completely different trouble.

One is E2 V2 M4: eyes to pain, moaning, withdrawing. The other is E1 V1 M6: no eyes, no voice, but following commands. Same total. Different patients. Different next steps.

So document the components. E3 V4 M5 tells the next nurse which function changed. A total of 12 tells them nothing.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Document the Components
Never chart just GCS 12. Chart E3 V4 M5. Two patients with the same total can be in very different trouble, and only the breakdown shows which function slipped. A fall of 2 points in any single component gets reported.
When the score is not reliable
  • Intubated: verbal cannot be tested. Document as E_ VT M_ and note it
  • Sedated or paralysed: the drugs mask the neurology. Record what they had and when
  • Facial or orbital swelling: eye opening cannot be tested. Say so explicitly
  • Spinal cord injury: motor response may be absent below the lesion. Assess above it
  • Pre-existing aphasia, deafness or dementia: document the baseline and compare to that
Bottom Line: Eye 4, verbal 5, motor 6, totalling 3 to 15. Eight or below and the airway is at risk. Always chart the components, never just the total.
07

Braden Scale

Discrimination axis: this is the scale where lower is worse.

Six subscales, scored and added, predicting pressure injury risk. What it really measures is how well this patient can protect their own skin.

SubscaleWhat it measuresRangeWho scores low
Sensory perceptionCan they feel and respond to pressure discomfort1 to 4Neuropathy, spinal cord injury, sedation
MoistureHow much the skin is exposed to moisture1 to 4Incontinence, sweating, wound drainage
ActivityHow much they physically move around1 to 4Bedfast scores 1, walks frequently scores 4
MobilityCan they change and control their position1 to 4Completely immobile scores 1
NutritionUsual food intake1 to 4Nothing by mouth, poor intake, inadequate protein
Friction and shearHow much the skin drags across surfaces1 to 3Patients who slide down the bed or need lifting
Lower is worse, and here is why

Braden runs backwards compared to almost every other scale. That catches people out.

Each subscale scores the patient's ability. To feel pressure, to move, to stay dry, to eat. More ability scores higher. More ability means less risk.

So a patient at 9 is in far more danger than one at 19. Read it as a capability, not a severity.

TotalRiskWhat you put in place
19 to 23No risk or mildStandard care. Skin check every shift, encourage movement
15 to 18MildTurn every 2 hours, pressure-reducing mattress, moisture management, nutrition review
13 to 14ModerateAll the above, plus heel elevation, foam wedges, an incontinence protocol
10 to 12HighAll the above, plus a specialty mattress, frequent skin checks, dietary consult for protein
9 or belowVery highMaximum intervention. Specialty bed, aggressive nutrition, wound care team
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Score Moves Within a Shift
A patient admitted walking scores Activity 4. Take them to theatre, sedate them and put them on bed rest, and Activity, Mobility and Sensory all collapse. That is an eight-point swing in a few hours. Reassess every shift and after any change: surgery, intubation, going nil by mouth, new incontinence, new sedation.
Bottom Line: Six subscales, and lower means higher risk because the score measures ability. 12 or below is high risk, 9 or below is very high. Reassess every shift.
08

Reading a wound

Wound assessment tracks change over time. So the technique has to be identical each time, or the comparison means nothing.

MEASURE
  • Measure. Length by width by depth, in centimetres, using a consistent clock-face orientation with 12 toward the head
  • Exudate. How much, what type, and whether it smells
  • Appearance. The tissue in the wound bed, and roughly what percentage of each
  • Suffering. Pain at rest and during dressing changes
  • Undermining. Tissue destroyed beneath intact edges, documented by clock position and depth
  • Re-evaluate. Wounds should show measurable progress every 2 to 4 weeks
  • Edge. Attached, rolled, undermined or macerated, plus the skin around it

What the colours mean

TissueColourWhat it isWhat you do
GranulationBeefy red, moistHealthy healing tissue with new vessels formingProtect it. Keep it moist, do not disturb it
EpithelialPink, pearlyNew skin growing in from the edgesProtect it. This is the final phase
SloughYellow or tan, stringyMoist dead tissue. It holds healing upDebridement, autolytic, enzymatic or sharp
EscharBlack or brown, hard, leatheryDry dead tissue. Nothing heals under itUsually debridement. Stable heel eschar is left intact
HypergranulationDark red, raised above the edgeToo much granulation, blocking the skin closing overSilver nitrate, a corticosteroid, or surgical correction

What the drainage means

TypeLooks likeWhat it tells you
SerousClear, watery, straw colouredNormal early on. Plasma leaking
SanguineousRed, bloodyActive bleeding. Expected just after surgery, concerning later
SerosanguineousPink, blood-tingedNormal healing. A mixture of the two

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Measure It the Same Way Every Time
Twelve o'clock is always toward the head. Length runs 12 to 6, width runs 3 to 9, depth is the deepest point on a sterile applicator. Centimetres, not inches. Inconsistent technique is the commonest reason a healing wound looks like it has stalled.
Bottom Line: Red granulation is healthy, yellow slough and black eschar are dead, purulent drainage is infection. Measure identically every time or the trend is meaningless.

Pain, and picking the tool

Discrimination axis: can this patient tell you.

That one question picks the tool. Everything else follows from it.

ToolWho it is forHow it worksScale
Numeric rating scaleVerbal adults, and children 8 and overSelf-report0 to 10, where 0 is none and 10 is the worst imaginable
Wong-Baker FACESChildren 3 to 8, and cognitively impaired adultsSelf-report0 to 10, across 6 faces. Say no hurt to hurts worst, not happy to sad
FLACCInfants and nonverbal children, 2 months to 7 yearsBehavioural observation0 to 10. Face, Legs, Activity, Cry, Consolability, each scored 0 to 2
CPOTIntubated or unconscious adults in intensive careBehavioural observation0 to 8. Facial expression, body movement, muscle tension, ventilator compliance
AbbeyPatients with advanced dementiaBehavioural observation0 to 18, across vocalisation, expression, body language and physical change
Self-report outranks your observation

If a patient says 8 out of 10 while smiling and chatting, the answer is 8 out of 10. People cope with pain differently. Some go quiet, some laugh, some cry.

The behavioural tools are for patients who cannot report. They are not there to check up on patients who can. If they can tell you, their number is the measurement.

The step that gets skipped

Assessment without reassessment is not finished. After giving analgesia:

  • 30 minutes after an oral dose
  • 15 minutes after an intravenous dose

Those intervals match when each route peaks. Document the score before, the intervention, and the score after.

And the target is usually not zero. Agree a comfort-function goal instead: the level at which they can actually do the things recovery requires, like breathing deeply and walking.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Reassess, and Chart Both Numbers
Thirty minutes after oral, fifteen after IV, because that is when each one peaks. Chart the score before and the score after; a single number with no follow-up shows an assessment that was never closed. If it is still uncontrolled, try repositioning, ice or distraction, then notify.
Bottom Line: Can they tell you decides the tool. Self-report always wins over observation. Reassess at 30 minutes oral, 15 minutes IV, and chart both scores.

Children are not small adults

Why the ranges differ

Infants run faster hearts and faster respirations. Their metabolic demand is higher, their stroke volume smaller, their lungs less compliant.

Blood pressure starts low and climbs with age. Judge a child against adult ranges and you either miss deterioration or panic about nothing.

AgeHeart rateRespiratory rateSystolic BP
Newborn, 0 to 28 days100 to 16030 to 6060 to 80
Infant, 1 to 12 months100 to 15025 to 5070 to 100
Toddler, 1 to 3 years90 to 13020 to 3080 to 110
Preschool, 3 to 5 years80 to 12020 to 2580 to 110
School age, 6 to 12 years70 to 11018 to 2285 to 120
Adolescent, 13 to 18 years60 to 10012 to 20100 to 130
Tachycardia first, hypotension last

A child compensating for shock, hypoxia or dehydration does it by raising the heart rate. That is the early sign.

They hold the pressure up by vasoconstricting hard, and they hold it a long time. So a normal pressure in a tachycardic child is not reassurance. It is compensation still working.

By the time the pressure falls, compensation has failed. That is decompensated shock. A tachycardic child is a sick child until proven otherwise.

They look well right up until they do not

Adults decline gradually. Children hold the line and then fall off it.

  • A child can be in compensated shock with an almost normal blood pressure
  • Once the pressure drops they may be minutes from arrest
  • The early signs are subtle: tachycardia, capillary refill over 2 to 3 seconds, mottled or pale skin, less urine, irritability or lethargy

Trust the trend, not one reassuring set of numbers.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Count Respirations Before You Touch Them
The order matters as much as the numbers. Watch respirations while the child is calm, because once you touch a toddler they cry and the rate is unusable. Then the apical pulse for a full 60 seconds, which is the standard under 2. Blood pressure and temperature go last, because they upset the child most. A parent holding them helps a lot.
Bottom Line: Know the ranges by age. Tachycardia is the earliest sign and hypotension is the last. Count respirations first, before you disturb them.
11

Head to toe, in order

A system stops you missing things. Head to toe, least invasive first, every single time.

Start before you touch them

  • Appearance: do they look their age, grooming, hygiene, build.
  • Behaviour: alert, cooperative, anxious, lethargic.
  • Mobility: gait, posture, any assistive devices.
  • Communication: clarity of speech, understanding, language barriers.
  1. 1
    Neurological. Level of consciousness and orientation to person, place, time and situation. Pupils equal, round, reactive to light and accommodation, normally 3 to 5 mm. Grip strength and movement in all four limbs.
  2. 2
    Head, eyes, ears, nose, throat. Symmetry and lesions. Sclera and conjunctiva. Hearing and any drainage. Nasal patency. Mucous membranes, teeth, and swallowing.
  3. 3
    Neck. Symmetry, jugular distension, tracheal position. Lymph nodes, thyroid, and carotid pulses one side at a time. Range of movement.
  4. 4
    Respiratory. Chest symmetry, effort, accessory muscle use. Auscultate all fields front and back. Chest expansion and tactile fremitus.
  5. 5
    Cardiovascular. Heart sounds at the five points: aortic, pulmonic, Erb's point, tricuspid, mitral. Peripheral pulses, capillary refill under 3 seconds, edema, skin colour and temperature.
  6. 6
    Gastrointestinal. Contour, symmetry, distension, scars. Bowel sounds in all four quadrants, normally 5 to 30 a minute. Then light palpation, then deep. Percuss for tympany against dullness. Remember the order changes here.
  7. 7
    Genitourinary. Urine output, colour, odour, continence. Any catheter and its drainage. Palpate for a distended bladder. Note when they last voided.
  8. 8
    Musculoskeletal. Symmetry, deformity, swelling. Range of movement active and passive, strength on the 0 to 5 scale, gait. Joints for warmth, crepitus and tenderness.
  9. 9
    Skin. Colour, lesions, bruising, rashes, wounds. Turgor, moisture, temperature. Document wound size, drainage and staging. Check the high-risk pressure areas.
  10. 10
    Psychosocial. Mood, affect, anxiety, coping, support at home. Eye contact, body language, how they are with family. Screen for depression and suicidal thoughts where indicated.
Bottom Line: Same order every time, head down and least invasive first. The abdomen is the only place the technique order changes.

When you do not do all of it

A focused assessment targets one system or one problem. It is what you actually do most of the time. Choosing the focus is the skill.

Altered mental statusConsciousness, pupils, glucose, vitals, medication reviewUnequal pupils, new weakness, severe hypoglycemia or hyperglycemia

Neurovascular checks

The six P's

For a limb in a cast, after orthopedic surgery, or where circulation is a concern.

  • Pain, especially on passive stretch
  • Pulses, present, quality, compared side to side
  • Pallor, the colour of the limb
  • Paresthesia, numbness or tingling
  • Paralysis, can they move it
  • Poikilothermia, temperature. A cool limb is a concern
Compartment syndrome

Pain out of proportion to the injury is the earliest sign, especially on passive stretch. It appears before anything else on the list.

Pallor, pulselessness and paralysis are late signs. Waiting for a pulse to vanish means waiting too long. This is a surgical emergency. Notify immediately.

Bottom Line: Focus on the system the complaint points at. In a limb, pain on passive stretch is the earliest compartment syndrome sign, and pulselessness is a late one.
13

Writing it down

Documentation is a legal record. It protects the patient by carrying information forward. It protects you by showing what you did and when.

If it was not documented, it was not done

That is the rule people quote. The forgotten half is that vague documentation is barely better than none.

Wound healing well is not a finding. Wound 3 cm by 2 cm, pink granulation, no drainage is a finding. The next nurse can tell whether it improved.

DoDo not
Use objective, measurable termsUse vague words like good, normal or adequate
Record what you saw, heard, smelled and feltRecord assumptions or interpretations
Quote the patient directlyParaphrase into something they did not say
Use approved abbreviations onlyInvent your own
Chart in real time, or as close as possibleLeave it all until the end of the shift
Correct an error with a single line through itScribble it out or use correction fluid
Sign and date every entryLeave entries unsigned
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Objective, Not Vague
Tolerated well, appears comfortable and voiding adequately are all opinions wearing a clinical coat. Write what you measured. Quote the patient in their own words. Six months later, in a deposition, the specific note is the one that helps you.

The formats

SBAR, for calling a provider
  • Situation. I am calling about Mr Smith in 302, who has chest pain
  • Background. He is 65, admitted with heart failure, previous MI
  • Assessment. His pressure is 90/60, heart rate 110, he is sweaty and anxious
  • Recommendation. I think he needs an ECG and a review. Can you come

The R is the part people leave off, and it is the part that gets something to happen.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
SBAR, and Say the R Out Loud
Situation, background, assessment, recommendation. New nurses often stop after the assessment and wait to be told what to do. Say what you think is needed. You are the one who has seen the patient, and a clear recommendation is what turns a phone call into an order.
FormatLettersWhere it is used
DARData, Action, ResponseFocus charting. What you found, what you did, what happened
SOAPSubjective, Objective, Assessment, PlanProgress notes, the traditional format
SBARSituation, Background, Assessment, RecommendationHandover, and calling a provider
Abbreviations that are not allowed

The Joint Commission do-not-use list, and every one of them exists because it killed someone.

  • U for units. Write units, because U reads as a zero
  • IU for international units. Write it out; it reads as IV
  • QD and QOD. Write daily, or every other day
  • A trailing zero, as in 1.0 mg. Write 1 mg, because a missed decimal point gives ten times the dose
  • A missing leading zero, as in .5 mg. Write 0.5 mg, for the same reason
  • MS, MSO4, MgSO4. Write morphine sulfate or magnesium sulfate, which are very different drugs
Bottom Line: Objective and specific, in real time, signed. SBAR for calls, and do not skip the recommendation. The do-not-use abbreviations exist because of dosing errors.

Mental status

This belongs in every assessment, not just psychiatric ones. A change here is often the first sign of a physical problem elsewhere.

AVPU

LevelWhat it looks likeWhat it means
AlertAwake, eyes open on their own, interacting normallyNormal consciousness
VerbalResponds when spoken toReduced consciousness. Assess further
PainResponds only to a painful stimulusSignificantly impaired. Get a GCS and escalate
UnresponsiveNo response to anythingEmergency. Rapid response or code team

Orientation

DomainWhat you askWhat to know
PersonWhat is your name. What is your date of birthUsually the last to go, and present even in moderate confusion
PlaceWhere are you. What kind of building is thisCommonly impaired in delirium and dementia
TimeWhat is the date. What day is it. What yearUsually the first to go, and the most sensitive early sign
SituationWhy are you in hospital. What brought you hereTests insight as well as memory
The order it goes, and comes back

Orientation is lost in a predictable sequence: time, then place, then situation, then person. It returns in reverse.

So a patient who does not know the date is early. A patient who does not know their own name is much further along. Time goes first, self goes last.

The rest of the exam

  • Mood and affect. Mood is what they say they feel. Affect is what you observe: flat, blunted, labile, appropriate. If the two do not match, that mismatch is itself a finding.
  • Speech. Rate, rhythm, volume and clarity. Pressured, slow, slurred, mumbled.
  • Thought process. Logical and goal-directed, or tangential, circumstantial or disorganised.
  • Thought content. Delusions, paranoia, obsessions, and any thoughts of harming themselves or others.
  • Perception. Hallucinations. Ask directly whether they ever see or hear things others do not.
  • Memory. Immediate, by repeating three words. Recent, by asking about breakfast or recalling those words after five minutes. Remote, by asking where they were born.
  • Judgment and insight. What would you do if you smelled smoke at home. Why do you think you are in hospital.
Ask about suicide directly

Asking does not plant the idea, and does not raise the risk. It gives permission to say something they were frightened to raise.

  • Ideation: are you having thoughts of harming yourself
  • Plan: have you thought about how you would do it
  • Means: do you have access to medication, firearms, or anything else
  • Intent: do you intend to act on these thoughts

If they say yes, do not leave them alone. Start safety precautions, remove sharps and hazards from the room, and notify immediately.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Know Their Baseline
Oriented times two may be completely normal for a patient with dementia. The same patient at oriented times zero is a crisis. Ask the family whether this is how they usually are. New confusion in someone previously alert is delirium, and delirium is a medical emergency: infection, hypoxia, drug toxicity, stroke or a metabolic crisis until proven otherwise.
Bottom Line: AVPU, then orientation across four domains. Time goes first and self goes last. New confusion against their own baseline is an emergency.

What you escalate

  • An acute change in mental status
  • New difficulty breathing
  • Heart rate under 40, or over 130
  • Systolic under 90 mmHg
  • Respiratory rate under 8, or over 28
  • Saturation under 90 percent despite oxygen
  • A staff member is worried about the patient

That last one is on the official list at most facilities. It is there because it works.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Worried Is a Valid Criterion
Worried about the patient is written into rapid response criteria on purpose. You have spent hours with them and you notice the change before the numbers do. Nobody has ever been criticised for calling a rapid response on a patient who then improved.
Bottom Line: Know the critical values cold. New neurological change, respiratory distress and signs of shock all mean call now. So does being worried.

Key Takeaways

IPPA, except the abdomen. Touching it changes the bowel sounds.

Trends beat single values. Compare to their own baseline.

GCS 8 or below means the airway is at risk. Chart the components.

Braden runs backwards. Lower score, less ability, higher risk.

Self-report wins. Behavioural tools are for patients who cannot speak.

Tachycardia first, hypotension last in a deteriorating child.

Objective and specific. Vague charting is barely better than none.

Worried about the patient is a valid rapid response trigger.
