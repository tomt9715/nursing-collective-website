Introduction to Assessment

Assessment is the first step of the nursing process and forms the foundation for all nursing care. A thorough assessment helps identify patient problems, establish baselines, and detect changes in condition.

Types of Nursing Assessment

Type When Used Scope
Comprehensive Admission, annual physical Complete head-to-toe, full history
Focused Specific complaint or problem Targeted to chief complaint
Ongoing/Shift Each shift, routine monitoring System-specific, changes from baseline
Emergency Life-threatening situations ABCs, rapid primary survey

The Four Assessment Techniques

Memory Aid: IPPA

Remember the order of assessment techniques:

  • Inspection - Look (always first!)
  • Palpation - Touch/Feel
  • Percussion - Tap
  • Auscultation - Listen

Exception: For abdominal assessment, auscultate BEFORE palpation to avoid altering bowel sounds!

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
IPPA Order
IPPA gives you the correct sequence for physical assessment! Inspect first - always look before you touch. Palpate next - use your hands to feel for abnormalities. Percuss - tap to assess density of underlying structures. Auscultate - listen with your stethoscope. But there's one important exception! For the abdominal assessment, you must auscultate BEFORE palpation. Why? Touching the abdomen can stimulate or diminish bowel sounds, giving you a false reading. So for the abdomen, the order is I-A-P-P.
Technique Description What You're Assessing
Inspection Visual examination using sight and smell Color, size, shape, symmetry, movement, lesions, drainage
Palpation Using hands to feel body parts Temperature, moisture, texture, tenderness, masses, pulses
Percussion Tapping to produce sounds Density of underlying structures (resonance, dullness, tympany)
Auscultation Listening with stethoscope Heart, lung, bowel sounds, bruits
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Assessment is the foundation of nursing care â€” without accurate assessment, every subsequent step (diagnosis, planning, interventions) is built on incomplete information. Assess first, always.

Data Collection Methods

Effective assessment requires collecting both subjective and objective data from multiple sources.

Subjective Data

What the Patient Tells You

  • Symptoms (pain, nausea, dizziness)
  • Feelings and perceptions
  • Chief complaint
  • Health history
  • Allergies
  • Medications
  • Family history
  • Psychosocial information

Documentation Note

Use quotes: "Patient states..."

Objective Data

What You Observe/Measure

  • Vital signs
  • Physical exam findings
  • Lab values
  • Diagnostic test results
  • Observable behaviors
  • Wound appearance
  • I&O measurements
  • Weight, height

Documentation Note

Use measurable terms: "BP 140/90"

Health History Components

Clinical Pearl: SAMPLE History

Use SAMPLE for quick history-taking:

  • Signs/Symptoms - What's wrong?
  • Allergies - Medications, food, environmental
  • Medications - Current prescriptions, OTC, supplements
  • Past medical history - Surgeries, chronic conditions
  • Last oral intake - When did they last eat/drink?
  • Events leading up - What happened before symptoms started?

Pain Assessment

Memory Aid: PQRST Pain Assessment
  • Provokes/Palliates - What makes it better/worse?
  • Quality - What does it feel like? (sharp, dull, burning)
  • Radiates - Does it spread anywhere?
  • Severity - Rate 0-10 scale
  • Timing - When did it start? Constant or intermittent?
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Pain Assessment: PQRST
PQRST ensures you get a complete pain history! Provokes/Palliates - "What makes it better or worse?" (movement, rest, medications). Quality - "What does it feel like?" (sharp, dull, burning, stabbing, aching). Radiates - "Does it spread anywhere?" (chest pain to left arm is classic MI). Severity - "On a scale of 0-10, how bad is it?" Use age-appropriate scales (Wong-Baker for children). Timing - "When did it start? Is it constant or does it come and go?" This thorough assessment helps identify the cause and guide treatment.
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Subjective data = patient's perspective (what they tell you). Objective data = your observations and measurements. Both are essential â€” document them separately and don't interpret subjective data as fact.

Interpreting Vital Sign Trends

Key Concept: Trends Over Single Values

A single vital sign reading provides limited information. Trends over time are far more valuable for detecting deterioration. Always compare to the patient's baseline and previous readings.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Trends Over Single Values
One vital sign reading tells you almost nothing - it's the trend that matters! A blood pressure of 100/70 might be normal for one patient but dangerously low for another who normally runs 160/90. Always compare to baseline and track trends over time. A patient whose BP has dropped from 140/80 to 120/70 to 100/60 over 4 hours is clearly deteriorating, even though 100/60 might look "acceptable" in isolation. Chart your vital signs so patterns become visible. Early detection of trends can catch problems before they become emergencies.

Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)

The Glasgow Coma Scale is the most widely used tool for assessing level of consciousness in patients with neurological injury. It evaluates three components: eye opening, verbal response, and motor response. Total scores range from 3 (deep coma) to 15 (fully alert).

GCS Scoring Breakdown

Component Response Score
Eye Opening (E) Spontaneous â€” eyes open without stimulation 4
To voice â€” eyes open when spoken to 3
To pain â€” eyes open only with painful stimulus 2
None â€” no eye opening 1
Verbal Response (V) Oriented â€” knows who, where, when, why 5
Confused â€” speaks in sentences but disoriented 4
Inappropriate words â€” random words, no conversation 3
Incomprehensible sounds â€” moaning, groaning only 2
None â€” no verbal response 1
Motor Response (M) Obeys commands â€” performs requested movements 6
Localizes pain â€” reaches toward and attempts to remove pain source 5
Withdrawal (flexion) â€” pulls away from painful stimulus 4
Abnormal flexion (decorticate) â€” arms flex inward, wrists flex 3
Extension (decerebrate) â€” arms extend, internally rotate 2
None â€” no motor response 1

Key GCS Scores to Remember

Score Classification Clinical Significance
15 Normal Fully alert and oriented
13-14 Mild injury Minor impairment; monitor closely for changes
9-12 Moderate injury Significant impairment; frequent neuro checks needed
â‰¤ 8 Severe injury Cannot protect airway â€” INTUBATION likely needed!
3 Lowest possible Deep coma or brain death; no responses to any stimuli
NCLEX Rule: GCS â‰¤ 8 = Intubate

A GCS of 8 or below means the patient cannot reliably protect their airway. The gag and cough reflexes are impaired, and aspiration risk is extremely high. This is the threshold for considering endotracheal intubation. Think: "GCS less than 8, intubate!" This is one of the most tested GCS concepts on the NCLEX.

Clinical Pearl: Factors That Interfere with GCS

Several factors can make GCS unreliable:

  • Intubated patients: Cannot assess verbal response â€” document as "E_VTM_" (T = tube) and note "verbal not testable"
  • Sedation/paralysis: Medications mask true neurological status â€” note medications and timing
  • Orbital/facial swelling: Cannot assess eye opening â€” document "eye opening not testable (swelling)"
  • Spinal cord injury: Motor response may be absent below injury level â€” assess above the level of injury
  • Pre-existing conditions: Aphasia, hearing impairment, dementia â€” document baseline and compare to it
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Always Document Component Scores
Never document just "GCS 12" â€” always break it down as E_V_M_ (e.g., E3V4M5 = 12). Why? Because two patients can both have a GCS of 8, but one might be E2V2M4 (eyes open to pain, incomprehensible sounds, withdraws from pain) while another is E1V1M6 (no eye opening, no verbal, but obeys commands). These are very different clinical pictures requiring different interventions. The component breakdown shows exactly which neurological function changed, making it easier to detect subtle deterioration. A drop of even 1-2 points in any component is clinically significant and should be reported immediately.
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: GCS assesses Eye (4) + Verbal (5) + Motor (6) = 3-15. GCS â‰¤ 8 means the patient cannot protect their airway â€” anticipate intubation. Always document component scores (E_V_M_), not just the total, and report any decline of 2+ points immediately.

Braden Scale for Pressure Injury Risk

The Braden Scale is the most widely used tool to assess a patient's risk for developing pressure injuries (formerly called pressure ulcers or decubitus ulcers). It evaluates six risk factors, with lower scores indicating higher risk.

The Six Braden Subscales

Subscale What It Measures Score Range Key Considerations
Sensory Perception Ability to feel and respond to pressure-related discomfort 1-4 Patients with neuropathy, spinal cord injury, or sedation score low
Moisture Degree to which skin is exposed to moisture 1-4 Incontinence, diaphoresis, wound drainage increase risk
Activity Degree of physical activity 1-4 Bedfast (1) vs. walks frequently (4)
Mobility Ability to change and control body position 1-4 Completely immobile (1) vs. makes frequent major position changes (4)
Nutrition Usual food intake pattern 1-4 NPO, poor oral intake, or inadequate protein = higher risk
Friction & Shear Degree of sliding and skin friction 1-3 Patients who slide down in bed or need assistance repositioning

Score Interpretation

Total Score Risk Level Nursing Interventions
19-23 No Risk / Mild Risk Standard preventive care; skin assessment every shift; encourage mobility
15-18 Mild Risk Reposition every 2 hours; pressure-reducing mattress; moisture management; nutritional assessment
13-14 Moderate Risk All above PLUS heel elevation; foam wedges; incontinence care protocol; wound care consult if needed
10-12 High Risk All above PLUS specialty mattress (low-air-loss or alternating pressure); frequent skin checks; dietary consult for protein supplementation
â‰¤ 9 Very High Risk All above PLUS maximum interventions; consider specialty bed; aggressive nutrition; wound care team involvement
Remember: Lower Score = Higher Risk

The Braden Scale is counterintuitive! Unlike most clinical scales where higher = worse, the Braden Scale uses lower scores to indicate higher risk. A patient scoring 9 is at MUCH higher risk than a patient scoring 19. Think of it this way: the score reflects the patient's ability to protect themselves â€” lower ability = higher risk.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Braden Score Changes Rapidly
A patient's Braden score can change dramatically within a single shift! A patient admitted walking independently (Activity = 4) who undergoes surgery requiring sedation and bed rest may suddenly score Activity = 1, Mobility = 1, and Sensory = 2. That's a swing of 8+ points. Reassess the Braden every shift and whenever the patient's status changes â€” new surgery, intubation, change to NPO, onset of incontinence, or initiation of sedation can all drastically increase risk. Don't rely on yesterday's score for today's care plan.
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: The Braden Scale assesses 6 risk factors for pressure injury. Lower score = higher risk (counterintuitive!). â‰¤12 = high risk, â‰¤9 = very high risk. Reassess every shift and with any status change. Prevention is always easier than treatment.

Wound Assessment

Accurate wound assessment is essential for tracking healing progress and determining appropriate interventions. Use a systematic approach to ensure complete documentation every time.

MEASURE Framework

Mnemonic: MEASURE
  • Measure â€” Length x Width x Depth in centimeters (use consistent clock-face orientation: 12 o'clock = head)
  • Exudate â€” Amount (none, scant, moderate, copious), type (serous, sanguineous, serosanguineous, purulent), odor
  • Appearance â€” Wound bed tissue type and percentage (granulation, slough, eschar, epithelial)
  • Suffering â€” Pain level at rest and during dressing changes; assess with appropriate pain tool
  • Undermining â€” Tissue destruction beneath intact wound edges; document using clock positions and depth
  • Re-evaluate â€” Monitor healing trajectory; wounds should show measurable progress every 2-4 weeks
  • Edge â€” Wound edges (attached, rolled/epibole, undermined, macerated) and periwound skin condition

Wound Bed Appearance

Tissue Type Color What It Means Action
Granulation Beefy red, moist Healthy healing tissue; new blood vessels forming Protect! Keep moist, avoid disrupting
Epithelial Pink/pearly New skin growing from wound edges inward Protect; wound is in final healing phase
Slough Yellow/tan, stringy Dead tissue (moist necrotic); delays healing Debridement needed (autolytic, enzymatic, or sharp)
Eschar Black/brown, hard, leathery Dead tissue (dry necrotic); prevents healing Usually needs debridement unless stable heel eschar (leave intact)
Hypergranulation Dark red, raised above wound edge Overgrowth of granulation tissue; prevents epithelialization May need silver nitrate, corticosteroid, or surgical correction

Drainage Types

Type Appearance Significance
Serous Clear, watery, straw-colored Normal in early wound healing; plasma leakage
Sanguineous Red, bloody Active bleeding; expected early post-op, concerning if delayed
Serosanguineous Pink, blood-tinged Normal healing; mixture of serous and blood
Purulent Thick, yellow/green, often foul-smelling Infection! Notify provider; obtain wound culture
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Consistent Wound Measurement
Always measure wounds the same way to track progress accurately! Use the clock-face method: 12 o'clock is always toward the patient's head, 6 o'clock toward the feet. Measure length (12 to 6 o'clock direction) x width (3 to 9 o'clock direction, perpendicular to length) x depth (deepest point using a sterile cotton-tipped applicator). Document in centimeters, not inches. Take a photo with a measuring ruler if your facility allows it. Inconsistent measurement technique is the #1 reason wound healing appears to stall â€” it might actually be healing, but measured differently each time.
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Use MEASURE for systematic wound assessment. Red granulation = healthy (protect it). Yellow slough and black eschar = necrotic (needs debridement). Purulent drainage = infection (culture and notify). Always measure consistently using the clock-face method in centimeters.

Pain Assessment Tools

Pain is the "5th vital sign" and must be assessed regularly using validated, age- and population-appropriate tools. The right tool depends on the patient's ability to self-report.

Choosing the Right Pain Assessment Tool

Tool Population Type Scale Key Features
NRS (Numeric Rating Scale) Verbal adults and children â‰¥8 years Self-report 0-10 Most common; quick; "Rate your pain 0-10." 0 = no pain, 10 = worst possible
Wong-Baker FACES Children ages 3-8; cognitively impaired adults Self-report 0-10 (6 faces) Patient points to face matching their pain. Avoid saying "happy/sad" â€” use "no hurt" to "hurts worst"
FLACC Infants and nonverbal children (2 mo - 7 yr) Behavioral observation 0-10 Face, Legs, Activity, Cry, Consolability â€” each scored 0-2
CPOT (Critical-Care Pain Observation) Intubated/unconscious ICU adults Behavioral observation 0-8 Facial expression, body movements, muscle tension, ventilator compliance (or vocalization)
Abbey Pain Scale Patients with advanced dementia Behavioral observation 0-18 Vocalization, facial expression, body language, behavioral change, physiological change, physical change
NCLEX Rule: Self-Report Is the Gold Standard

The patient's self-report of pain is ALWAYS the most reliable indicator. If a patient says their pain is 8/10 but they're smiling and chatting, do NOT dismiss or downgrade their pain score. People cope with pain differently â€” some laugh, some become quiet, some cry. Behavioral observation tools (FLACC, CPOT) are only used when the patient CANNOT self-report. On the NCLEX, if the patient can verbalize, their report takes priority over your observation.

Mnemonic: PQRST for Comprehensive Pain Assessment
  • Provokes/Palliates â€” What makes it worse? What makes it better?
  • Quality â€” What does it feel like? (sharp, dull, burning, aching, pressure, stabbing)
  • Radiates/Region â€” Where is it? Does it spread anywhere else?
  • Severity â€” Rate it 0-10 (or use appropriate tool)
  • Timing â€” When did it start? Constant or intermittent? Getting better or worse?
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Pain Reassessment: The Step Most Nurses Miss
Assessment without reassessment is incomplete! After administering pain medication, you MUST reassess: 30 minutes after oral medication and 15 minutes after IV medication (aligned with peak onset times). Document BOTH the initial score, the intervention, AND the reassessment score. If pain is not adequately managed, try non-pharmacological interventions (repositioning, ice, distraction, relaxation) and notify the provider if pain remains uncontrolled. The goal is not always 0/10 â€” work with the patient to establish a comfort-function goal (the pain level at which they can participate in recovery activities like deep breathing and ambulation).
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Match the pain tool to the patient: NRS for verbal adults, FACES for children 3+, FLACC for nonverbal children, CPOT for intubated patients, and Abbey for dementia patients. Always reassess after interventions (30 min PO, 15 min IV) and document both scores.

Pediatric Vital Sign Ranges

Why Pediatric Ranges Are Different

Children are not small adults. Their vital sign ranges differ significantly from adults due to developmental physiology. Infants have higher heart and respiratory rates because of higher metabolic demands, smaller cardiac stroke volumes, and less compliant lungs. Blood pressure is lower in young children and gradually increases with age. Using adult ranges to assess a child can cause you to miss critical deterioration or create unnecessary alarm.

Normal Vital Sign Ranges by Age

Age Group Heart Rate (bpm) Respiratory Rate (/min) Systolic BP (mmHg)
Newborn (0-28 days) 100-160 30-60 60-80
Infant (1-12 months) 100-150 25-50 70-100
Toddler (1-3 years) 90-130 20-30 80-110
Preschool (3-5 years) 80-120 20-25 80-110
School Age (6-12 years) 70-110 18-22 85-120
Adolescent (13-18 years) 60-100 12-20 100-130
Critical Alert: Tachycardia Is the Earliest Sign

Tachycardia is the EARLIEST and most reliable sign of decompensation in children. Children initially compensate for shock, hypoxia, and dehydration by increasing their heart rate. Blood pressure is often the LAST vital sign to drop because children maintain vascular tone through intense sympathetic response. By the time a child becomes hypotensive, they are in decompensated shock and cardiovascular collapse may be imminent.

Key rule: A tachycardic child is a sick child until proven otherwise. Do not be reassured by a "normal" blood pressure in a tachycardic child.

Clinical Pearl: Children Compensate Until They Don't

Pediatric patients can look deceptively well right before they crash. Unlike adults who show a gradual decline, children maintain their vital signs through powerful compensatory mechanisms â€” until those mechanisms are exhausted. When compensation fails, deterioration is rapid and dramatic. This means:

  • A child can be in compensated shock with near-normal blood pressure
  • By the time BP drops, they may be minutes from cardiac arrest
  • Look for subtle early signs: tachycardia, prolonged capillary refill (>2-3 sec), mottled or pale skin, decreased urine output, irritability or lethargy
  • Trust the trend, not a single reassuring set of vital signs
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Age-Appropriate Vital Sign Assessment
How you assess pediatric vital signs matters as much as the numbers! Here's the trick: count respirations FIRST, before touching the child. Once you touch or disturb an infant or toddler, they'll start crying, which makes respiratory rate impossible to assess accurately. Use this order: (1) Observe respirations while the child is calm or sleeping, (2) Auscultate heart rate for a full 60 seconds (apical pulse is the standard for children under 2), (3) Then take blood pressure and temperature last since these are the most distressing. Also, always use age-appropriate cuff sizes for blood pressure â€” too small a cuff gives falsely high readings, just like in adults. Having a parent hold the child during assessment can reduce anxiety significantly.
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Pediatric vital signs vary significantly by age â€” know the normal ranges. Tachycardia is the earliest warning sign of trouble. Children compensate well then crash fast, so never be falsely reassured by a "normal" BP in a tachycardic child. Count respirations before disturbing the child.

Systematic Head-to-Toe Assessment

A systematic approach ensures nothing is missed. Always work from head to toe and least invasive to most invasive.

General Survey

Begin with overall observations before touching the patient:

  • Appearance: Age-appropriate, grooming, hygiene, body habitus
  • Behavior: Alert, cooperative, anxious, lethargic
  • Mobility: Gait, posture, use of assistive devices
  • Communication: Speech clarity, comprehension, language barriers

Head-to-Toe Assessment Sequence

1
Neurological

LOC: Alert, oriented x4 (person, place, time, situation). PERRLA: Pupils Equal, Round, Reactive to Light and Accommodation (3-5mm normal). Motor/Sensory: Grip strength, movement of all extremities.

2
Head, Eyes, Ears, Nose, Throat (HEENT)

Head: Symmetry, lesions, tenderness. Eyes: Sclera color, conjunctiva, drainage. Ears: Hearing, drainage, tympanic membrane. Nose: Patency, discharge. Throat: Mucous membranes, dentition, swallowing.

3
Neck

Inspect: Symmetry, JVD, tracheal position. Palpate: Lymph nodes, thyroid, carotid pulses (one at a time!). ROM: Flexion, extension, rotation.

4
Respiratory

Inspect: Chest symmetry, respiratory effort, accessory muscle use. Auscultate: All lung fields anteriorly and posteriorly. Note breath sounds (clear, crackles, wheezes, diminished). Palpate: Chest expansion, tactile fremitus.

5
Cardiovascular

Auscultate: Heart sounds at 5 auscultation points (Aortic, Pulmonic, Erb's Point, Tricuspid, Mitral - "APE To Man"). Assess: Peripheral pulses, capillary refill (<3 seconds), edema, skin color/temperature.

6
Gastrointestinal

Inspect: Contour, symmetry, distension, scars. Auscultate: Bowel sounds in all 4 quadrants (5-30/min normal). Palpate: Light then deep; tenderness, masses, organomegaly. Percuss: Tympany vs. dullness.

7
Genitourinary

Assess: Urinary output, color, odor, continence. Inspect: Presence of Foley catheter, drainage. Palpate: Bladder distension (suprapubic area). Note last voiding.

8
Musculoskeletal

Inspect: Symmetry, deformities, swelling. Assess: ROM (active and passive), strength (0-5 scale), gait. Palpate: Joints for warmth, crepitus, tenderness.

9
Integumentary

Inspect: Color, lesions, bruising, rashes, wounds. Assess: Skin turgor, moisture, temperature. Document: Wound size, drainage, staging (for pressure injuries). Check high-risk areas.

10
Psychosocial

Assess: Mood, affect, anxiety level, coping mechanisms, support systems. Observe: Eye contact, body language, interaction with family. Screen: Depression, suicidal ideation if indicated.

Clinical Pearl: Abdominal Assessment Order

For the abdomen, the order changes: Inspect â†’ Auscultate â†’ Percuss â†’ Palpate

Auscultate BEFORE palpation because touching the abdomen can stimulate or alter bowel sounds, giving you inaccurate findings.

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Systematic head-to-toe assessment ensures nothing is missed. Follow the same order every time: neuro â†’ head/neck â†’ respiratory â†’ cardiac â†’ GI (auscultate before palpating!) â†’ GU â†’ musculoskeletal â†’ skin.

Focused Assessment

A focused assessment targets a specific body system or problem. It's used for ongoing monitoring, follow-up on abnormal findings, or when a patient presents with a specific complaint.

Neurovascular Assessment (6 P's)

Memory Aid: The 6 P's

For extremity assessment (casts, post-op orthopedic, vascular concerns):

  • Pain - Especially with passive stretch (compartment syndrome sign)
  • Pulses - Present, quality, compare bilaterally
  • Pallor - Color of extremity
  • Paresthesia - Numbness, tingling
  • Paralysis - Ability to move
  • Poikilothermia - Temperature (cool extremity is concerning)
Compartment Syndrome Alert

Pain out of proportion to injury, especially pain with passive stretch, is the EARLIEST sign of compartment syndrome. This is a surgical emergency - notify provider immediately!

Other signs: Paresthesia, pallor, pulselessness, paralysis (late signs)

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Focused assessments are targeted and efficient â€” assess the relevant body system based on the chief complaint. Chest pain = cardiac/respiratory. Fall = neuro + musculoskeletal + skin. Post-op = surgical site + system-specific.

Documentation Techniques

Accurate documentation is a legal requirement that protects both the patient and the nurse. It ensures continuity of care and provides evidence of nursing care provided.

Principles of Documentation

The Golden Rule of Documentation

"If it wasn't documented, it wasn't done."

Documentation should be timely, accurate, complete, and objective. Avoid vague terms and personal opinions.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Document Objectively
"If it wasn't documented, it wasn't done" - and vague documentation is almost as bad! Avoid subjective terms like "good," "normal," "appears fine," or "tolerated well." Instead, use measurable, objective terms: "Wound 3cm x 2cm, pink granulation tissue, no drainage" is far better than "wound healing well." Document exactly what you see, hear, smell, and feel. Quote patient statements directly: "Patient states 'I have sharp pain in my chest.'" Good documentation protects your patient by ensuring continuity of care, and protects YOU legally.

Documentation Formats

SBAR - Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation

SBAR Communication Format
  • Situation - "I'm calling about Mr. Smith in 302 who is having chest pain"
  • Background - "He's a 65-year-old admitted for CHF, has history of MI"
  • Assessment - "His BP is 90/60, HR 110, he's diaphoretic and anxious"
  • Recommendation - "I think he needs an EKG and evaluation. Can you come see him?"
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
SBAR Communication
SBAR keeps your provider communication clear and efficient! When calling a doctor, use this structure: Situation - Start with why you're calling ("I'm calling about Mrs. Jones who is having difficulty breathing"). Background - Give relevant history ("She's 72, admitted for pneumonia, has COPD"). Assessment - Share your findings ("Her SpO2 dropped to 88%, she's using accessory muscles, lung sounds have coarse crackles"). Recommendation - State what you think is needed ("I think she needs a chest X-ray and possibly increased oxygen or BiPAP"). This format respects everyone's time and ensures critical information isn't missed.

DAR - Data, Action, Response

Used in focus charting:

  • Data - Subjective and objective findings
  • Action - Nursing interventions performed
  • Response - Patient's response to interventions

SOAP - Subjective, Objective, Assessment, Plan

Traditional format often used in progress notes:

  • Subjective - What the patient reports
  • Objective - Observable, measurable data
  • Assessment - Your nursing analysis
  • Plan - Next steps, interventions planned

Mental Status Assessment

Mental status assessment evaluates a patient's cognitive function, emotional state, and behavioral presentation. It is a critical component of the neurological exam and should be incorporated into every nursing assessment, particularly when patients present with altered behavior, confusion, or psychiatric symptoms.

Level of Consciousness (LOC) â€” AVPU Scale

Level Description Clinical Implication
A - Alert Awake, eyes open spontaneously, interacts appropriately Normal consciousness
V - Verbal Responds to verbal stimuli; may open eyes or speak when called Decreased consciousness; further evaluation needed
P - Pain Responds only to painful stimuli (sternal rub, nail bed pressure) Significantly impaired; consider GCS and urgent evaluation
U - Unresponsive No response to any stimuli Medical emergency; activate rapid response/code team

Orientation Assessment

Orientation is assessed across four domains, documented as "oriented x4" (or x3, x2, x1 if impaired):

Domain Assessment Question Examples Notes
Person "What is your name?" "What is your date of birth?" Usually the LAST to be lost; present even in moderate confusion
Place "Where are you right now?" "What type of building is this?" Commonly impaired in delirium and dementia
Time "What is today's date?" "What day of the week is it?" "What year is it?" Usually the FIRST to be lost; most sensitive indicator of confusion
Situation "Why are you in the hospital?" "What happened to bring you here?" Tests awareness of current circumstances and insight
Memory Aid: Orientation Order of Loss

Orientation is typically lost in this order: Time â†’ Place â†’ Situation â†’ Person

And regained in the reverse order: Person â†’ Situation â†’ Place â†’ Time

Think: "Time goes first, self goes last." A patient who doesn't know their own name is significantly more impaired than one who doesn't know the date.

Components of Mental Status Exam

Mood and Affect

  • Mood: The patient's subjective emotional state ("I feel sad," "I feel anxious")
  • Affect: The observable expression of emotion (flat, blunted, labile, appropriate)
  • Congruence: Does the affect match the mood? Incongruent affect (laughing while describing sadness) is clinically significant

Speech

  • Rate: Normal, rapid (pressured), or slow
  • Rhythm: Regular, stuttering, or dysarthric
  • Volume: Normal, loud, or soft/whispered
  • Clarity: Clear, slurred, or mumbled

Thought Process and Content

  • Process: Logical and goal-directed vs. tangential, circumstantial, disorganized, or loose associations
  • Content: Presence of delusions, paranoia, obsessions, phobias, or suicidal/homicidal ideation
  • Perceptions: Hallucinations (auditory, visual, tactile) â€” ask "Do you ever see or hear things that others don't?"

Memory

  • Immediate: Repeat 3 words back to you right now (tests attention/registration)
  • Recent (Short-term): "What did you have for breakfast?" or recall 3 words after 5 minutes
  • Remote (Long-term): "Where were you born?" "Where did you go to school?"

Judgment and Insight

  • Judgment: Can the patient make appropriate decisions? ("What would you do if you smelled smoke in your house?")
  • Insight: Does the patient understand their condition? ("Why do you think you're in the hospital?")
Always Screen for Suicidal Ideation

Asking about suicidal ideation does NOT increase the risk of suicide. It opens the door for patients to share what they may be afraid to bring up on their own. When assessing mental status, incorporate screening for:

  • Suicidal ideation: "Are you having thoughts of harming yourself?" "Have you thought about ending your life?"
  • Plan: "Do you have a plan for how you would do it?"
  • Means: "Do you have access to medications, firearms, or other means?"
  • Intent: "Do you intend to act on these thoughts?"

If the patient endorses suicidal ideation, do not leave them alone. Initiate safety precautions, remove sharps and dangerous items from the room, and notify the provider immediately.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Know Your Patient's Baseline
Mental status changes are only meaningful when compared to the patient's baseline! A patient with dementia who is "oriented x2" may be at THEIR normal. But if that same patient is usually oriented x2 and is now oriented x0, that's a critical change. Always ask the family or review the chart: "Is this how they normally are?" New confusion (delirium) in someone who was previously alert is a medical emergency â€” it could indicate infection, hypoxia, medication toxicity, stroke, or metabolic crisis. Document the patient's baseline mental status on admission and compare to it at every assessment. The CAM (Confusion Assessment Method) tool can help differentiate delirium from dementia if you suspect a change.
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Mental status assessment includes LOC (AVPU), orientation x4 (person, place, time, situation), mood/affect, speech, thought process/content, memory, and judgment. Time orientation is lost first and regained last. Always screen for suicidal ideation, and know your patient's baseline to detect meaningful changes.

Rapid Response Criteria

Many facilities use Rapid Response Teams (RRT). Common triggers include:

  • Acute change in mental status
  • New onset of difficulty breathing
  • Heart rate <40 or >130
  • Systolic BP <90 mmHg
  • Respiratory rate <8 or >28
  • SpO2 <90% despite oxygen
  • Staff member is worried about the patient

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Trust Your Gut
"I'm worried about this patient" is a completely valid reason to call a Rapid Response! Experienced nurses develop clinical intuition - that uneasy feeling when something isn't right, even if you can't pinpoint exactly what. Research shows nurses often detect deterioration before vital signs become critical. If your gut says something is wrong, trust it. Don't wait for numbers to "prove" your concern. Document your observations, escalate to charge nurse or call the Rapid Response Team. You are your patient's advocate. It's always better to call and be wrong than to wait and have something bad happen.
