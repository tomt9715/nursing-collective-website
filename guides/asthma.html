Asthma

Two inhalers that look identical and do opposite jobs, and one finding that means the patient is about to stop breathing. Sort those and the topic is yours.

The Nursing Collective

By the end, without notes

  • Sort any inhaler into rescue or controller, and say what happens if they are swapped
  • Explain why a silent chest is worse than a loud wheeze
  • Grade an exacerbation from what the patient can still say in one breath
  • Say why asthma gets high-flow oxygen and COPD does not

01

What actually gets you

Asthma looks simple. Airways narrow, you give a puffer, the patient improves. The exam is not testing that. It is testing the four places where the obvious move is wrong.

You were reassured by a quiet chest

Wheezing is the sound of air squeezing past a narrowed airway. It takes moving air to make it.

So a distressed patient who has stopped wheezing has not improved. They have stopped moving air. Silent chest in a struggling patient is a pre-arrest finding.

You reached for the wrong inhaler

An inhaled steroid takes days to work. In an acute attack it does nothing useful, and the patient stays in bronchospasm while you wait.

Sort every inhaler into rescue or controller before you learn its name. That single sort answers most of the medication questions.

You carried the COPD oxygen rule across

You learned to be careful with oxygen in COPD, and then applied it here. Asthma patients are not chronic CO2 retainers, and their drive is intact.

In status asthmaticus hypoxia is the thing that kills. Give oxygen freely and target 92 percent or above.

You stepped up the drugs without watching them use the inhaler

Most patients use an inhaler wrong. Uncontrolled asthma is far more often bad technique or missed doses than an inadequate prescription.

Watch them do it. Asking whether they know how is not the same as seeing it.

Bottom Line: Study this as four discriminations: rescue or controller, severity or control, wheeze or silence, and asthma or COPD when the oxygen decision comes.
02

The four things

For every level below, you should be able to say four things with the book closed: the one-sentence definition, the clue you would see in a patient, the priority nursing action, and the first-line drug with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Level Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line drug
Intermittent Symptoms on 2 days a week or fewer, with normal lung function between. Occasional wheeze, no night waking, FEV1 over 80 percent. Teach trigger avoidance and inhaler technique. SABA as needed. No daily controller.
Mild persistent Symptoms more than twice a week but not every day. Waking 3 to 4 nights a month. FEV1 still over 80 percent. Start a daily controller and explain why it is taken when well. Low-dose inhaled corticosteroid. Rinse the mouth after.
Moderate persistent Daily symptoms, and daily rescue inhaler use. Waking more than once a week. FEV1 60 to 80 percent. Check technique and adherence before adding anything. Medium-dose ICS with a LABA. Never the LABA alone.
Severe persistent Symptoms through the day and most nights, with activity limited. Rescue inhaler several times a day. FEV1 under 60 percent. Escalate, and look for what is driving it. High-dose ICS with LABA, sometimes oral steroids or a biologic.
Status asthmaticus A severe attack that is not responding to bronchodilators. Speaking in single words, tripod, silent chest, peak flow under 40 percent. Oxygen and continuous nebuliser. Escalate now. Continuous albuterol, IV steroids, IV magnesium. Prepare for intubation.
Copy this down

Five rows on one index card, by hand. Twice a week is the line between intermittent and persistent. Persistent means a daily controller. Silent chest means now. That card is most of the exam.

03

What is actually happening

Three things narrow the airway in asthma, and they do not all respond to the same drug. That is the reason there are two kinds of inhaler.

  1. 1
    Chronic inflammation. Mast cells, eosinophils and T-lymphocytes sit in the airway wall releasing histamine, leukotrienes and prostaglandins. The wall swells and the glands pour out mucus. Steroids treat this. Bronchodilators do not.
  2. 2
    Bronchospasm. The smooth muscle wrapped around the airway contracts and the lumen narrows in seconds. This is the wheeze, the tightness and the breathlessness. Bronchodilators fix this, and fast.
  3. 3
    Hyperresponsiveness. The airway becomes twitchy and overreacts to things that would not bother anyone else. Cold air, exercise, smoke. Ongoing inflammation makes it worse, which is why untreated asthma escalates.
Asthma or COPD

One word separates them: reversible.

Asthma obstruction opens with a bronchodilator. COPD obstruction largely does not, because the damage is structural. That difference drives the treatment, the prognosis, and the oxygen decision later in this guide.

Two phases, two drugs

Early phase, within minutes

Timing: 10 to 30 minutes after exposure.

  • Mast cells degranulate
  • Histamine and leukotrienes are released
  • Immediate bronchoconstriction
  • Responds to a SABA

This is the phase the rescue inhaler was built for.

Late phase, hours later

Timing: 4 to 12 hours after exposure.

  • Eosinophils and T-cells move into the airway wall
  • Inflammation continues
  • Mucus and edema build
  • Needs corticosteroids

This is why a patient can feel better, go home, and come back worse that night.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Identify Personal Triggers
Every patient has their own set, and a diary of symptoms, exposures and peak flows is how you find them. Trigger avoidance does as much work as the medication does. Beta-blockers and NSAIDs are the two that patients never think to mention.
Why untreated asthma stops being reversible

Inflammation left running for years remodels the airway. Smooth muscle thickens, fibrosis builds under the epithelium, and mucus-producing cells multiply.

Those changes are permanent. That is the real argument for taking a controller inhaler on days you feel completely well, and it is a better answer than telling a patient the doctor said so.

Bottom Line: Inflammation, bronchospasm, hyperresponsiveness. Bronchodilators fix the second one in minutes; only steroids touch the first. Asthma is reversible, COPD is not.
04

Where it lands

Discrimination axis: severity or control.

These two words are used loosely in conversation and precisely on exams.

The axis that separates them

Severity is how bad the disease is on its own, judged before any treatment starts. It sets the step you begin at.

Control is how the patient is doing on the treatment they are taking. It decides whether you step up or down.

A patient can have severe asthma that is beautifully controlled, or mild asthma that is a mess. Read the question for which one it is asking.

Two, two, two

Well-controlled asthma looks like this:

  • Symptoms on 2 days a week or fewer
  • Night waking 2 times a month or fewer
  • Rescue inhaler on 2 days a week or fewer

Break any one of them and the asthma is not controlled. Notice that all three thresholds are the same number, which is the only reason this is worth remembering as a rule.

Step Therapy
Step up if the asthma is not controlled after 2 to 6 weeks. Step down after 3 months of good control, to find the smallest dose that holds. Before stepping up, always check technique and adherence; those explain most uncontrolled asthma.
Bottom Line: Severity sets the starting step and is judged before treatment. Control decides the next move. Twice a week is the line that turns intermittent into persistent, and persistent means a daily controller.
05

Rescue or controller

Discrimination axis: does this drug work in minutes or in weeks.

Everything in this section goes in one of two boxes. Sort it first and the details attach themselves afterwards.

Rescue: for the attack happening now
  • Onset in minutes
  • Lasts 4 to 6 hours
  • Treats bronchospasm
  • Does nothing about inflammation
  • Needing it often means the control is failing

Taken as needed, not on a schedule.

Controller: for the attack next month
  • Onset in days to weeks
  • Protection lasts as long as it is taken
  • Reduces inflammation
  • Prevents airway remodelling
  • Only works if taken consistently

Taken daily, whether or not the patient has symptoms.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Rescue vs Controller
A rescue inhaler is the fire extinguisher; a controller is the fireproofing. Extinguishers do not stop fires starting, and fireproofing will not put one out. A patient living on rescue inhalers alone is putting out fires and never fixing the wiring.

Rescue drugs

ClassExamplesOnsetWhat you watch for
SABAAlbuterol, levalbuterol5 to 15 minutesFirst line. Tachycardia and tremor. Also used before exercise
SAMAIpratropium15 to 30 minutesWeaker on its own, so it is usually paired with a SABA in an acute attack
SABA with SAMAAlbuterol with ipratropium5 to 15 minutesTwo mechanisms at once, for exacerbations
The rescue inhaler is a control monitor

How often a patient reaches for it is the most useful single number you can ask for.

  • Needing it more than 2 days a week, not counting pre-exercise use, means poor control
  • Getting through more than one canister a month is linked to severe exacerbations
  • Relying on a SABA without a controller raises mortality

If they are overusing it, check the controller adherence, the technique and the triggers before you add a drug.

Controller drugs

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
ICS Is the Most Effective Controller
Inhaled steroids work locally in the lung with little systemic effect, which is what separates them from prednisone. They take days to weeks to reach full benefit, so they are taken daily and never used to rescue. Rinse the mouth after every dose.
Never a LABA on its own in asthma

This carries a black box warning. LABA monotherapy raises the risk of asthma-related death.

A LABA is only ever given combined with an inhaled corticosteroid, and only when the asthma is not controlled on the controller alone. Learn this as a hard rule.

Using the inhaler

The metered dose inhaler, step by step
  1. Shake it for 5 to 10 seconds
  2. Breathe all the way out, away from the inhaler
  3. Seal the lips on the mouthpiece, or hold it 1 to 2 inches away
  4. Start a slow deep breath in, then press the canister
  5. Keep breathing in for 3 to 5 seconds
  6. Hold the breath for 10 seconds if they can
  7. Wait a minute before a second puff
  8. Rinse the mouth after any inhaled steroid

A spacer improves delivery and cuts how much lands in the mouth. Use one wherever possible.

Bottom Line: Albuterol rescues in minutes; inhaled steroids control over weeks. Rinse after every steroid dose, and never give a LABA alone in asthma.
06

Assessment, in order

Discrimination axis: wheezing, or no longer wheezing.

  1. 1
    Breathing. Rate, rhythm, depth, effort. Wheeze on expiration more than inspiration, a long expiratory phase, accessory muscles, nasal flaring, retractions. Then saturation and peak flow.
  2. 2
    How much they can say. Sentences, phrases, or single words. This is a bedside severity score that costs nothing and needs no equipment.
  3. 3
    How often it happens. Days a week with symptoms, nights woken, rescue inhaler use, activities given up. The Asthma Control Test puts a number on it.
  4. 4
    Triggers. Home and work environment, allergens, irritants, recent infection, exercise, weather, aspirin or beta-blockers, stress.
  5. 5
    Medications, watched not asked. What they take, whether they take it, and how they use the inhaler. Have them demonstrate it.
  6. 6
    Peak flow against their own best. Green is 80 to 100 percent, yellow is 50 to 79, red is under 50. The comparison is to their personal best, not to a population figure.

The silent chest

Read down the wheeze row and the severity column and notice what happens. The wheeze gets louder, then it disappears.

It disappears because nothing is moving. A distressed patient with a quiet chest is not improving; they are approaching respiratory failure. Never let the absence of wheezing reassure you when everything else looks worse.

Write this down

Blank paper. Three columns, mild, moderate, severe. Fill in speech, wheeze, saturation and peak flow from memory. Then write one sentence on why the severe column has no wheeze.

Bottom Line: Speech is a free severity score. Peak flow is judged against their own best. A silent chest in a struggling patient is worse than the loudest wheeze in the room.
07

When it stops responding

Status asthmaticus is a severe attack that does not improve on standard bronchodilators. It is an emergency, and the patient can die of it.

What it is
  • Severe bronchospasm that has not answered initial bronchodilator therapy
  • Distress that keeps worsening despite treatment
  • Real risk of respiratory failure, hypoxemia, acidosis and death
  • Needs aggressive treatment, usually ICU, and sometimes intubation

The findings that mean escalate now

FindingWhat it meansWhat you do
Silent chestObstruction so severe that no air is movingEmergency. Prepare for intubation
CyanosisSevere hypoxemia, saturation under 90 percentHigh-flow oxygen, prepare to escalate
Altered mental statusHypoxia or hypercapnia reaching the brainCall rapid response, prepare for intubation
Unable to speakToo breathless to get words outContinuous nebuliser and IV steroids

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Recognise Status Asthmaticus Early
Tripod position, one or two words per breath, sweating, agitation turning into lethargy, peak flow under 40 percent of their best, and a silent chest. The shift from agitated to sleepy is the one people miss; that is not the patient settling.

Emergency management

  1. 1
    Oxygen, generously. Target a saturation of 92 percent or above. A non-rebreather may be needed at first. These patients are not CO2 retainers, so the COPD caution does not apply.
  2. 2
    Continuous nebulised beta-agonist. Albuterol continuously at 10 to 15 mg an hour, or 2.5 to 5 mg every 20 minutes for three doses. Add ipratropium in severe cases.
  3. 3
    Systemic corticosteroids, early. IV methylprednisolone 125 mg or 1 to 2 mg/kg, or oral prednisone 40 to 60 mg. They take 4 to 6 hours to work, which is exactly why they go in now.
  4. 4
    Magnesium sulfate. 2 g IV over 20 minutes for a severe exacerbation. It relaxes smooth muscle. Watch for hypotension and flushing.
  5. 5
    Epinephrine if needed. 0.3 to 0.5 mg subcutaneously or intramuscularly in severe cases, and particularly if anaphylaxis is suspected.
  6. 6
    Prepare for intubation. If they are deteriorating despite all of it. Ketamine is often chosen for induction because it also bronchodilates.
Oxygen in asthma or in COPD

This pair catches people constantly, and the rule reverses between them.

  • Asthma: give oxygen freely, target 92 percent or above. There is no chronic CO2 retention, and the respiratory drive is normal.
  • COPD: low flow, 1 to 2 L a minute, target 88 to 92 percent, because a chronic retainer breathes on hypoxic drive.

In status asthmaticus the immediate threat is hypoxia. Treat it.

08

What you do first

Priority questions give you four defensible actions and ask which comes first. All four are things a nurse would do. That is the trick. You are being tested on ordering, not on correctness.

The ordering rules
  • Sit them up first. It costs nothing, needs no order, and helps immediately.
  • Bronchodilator before steroid. One opens the airway in minutes; the other takes hours.
  • Staying with the patient is an intervention. Panic raises oxygen demand and tightens the chest further.
  • A falling level of consciousness outranks every number on the monitor.
  • Check the technique before you escalate the prescription.
  1. 1
    Position and oxygenate. High Fowler's. Oxygen as prescribed. Continuous saturation monitoring during an exacerbation. Keep the room calm and the stimulation low.
  2. 2
    Give the drugs, and check they land. Bronchodilators and steroids as ordered. Watch the inhaler or nebuliser technique. Monitor for tachycardia, tremor and thrush.
  3. 3
    Watch for deterioration. Rising work of breathing, falling responsiveness, a chest going quiet, a saturation drifting down. Report it as it happens, not at the end of the shift.
  4. 4
    Bring the anxiety down. Stay with them and stay calm. Breathlessness causes fear and fear worsens breathlessness. Coach pursed-lip and diaphragmatic breathing.
  5. 5
    Keep them hydrated. 2 to 3 L a day unless contraindicated, to keep secretions thin. IV fluids may be needed in an acute attack. Track intake and output.
  6. 6
    Keep triggers out of the room. No strong perfumes or cleaning products. Clean air, dust-free surfaces, a HEPA filter if one is available.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Watch Them Use the Inhaler
Up to 90 percent of patients get the technique wrong, so bad technique is a more likely cause of uncontrolled asthma than an inadequate dose. Have them demonstrate rather than describe. The common errors are not exhaling first, breathing in too fast, not holding the breath, and skipping the spacer.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Every Patient Needs a Written Plan
A written action plan cuts emergency visits and admissions; verbal instructions do not. It lists the daily medications, their personal best peak flow, what to do in each zone, and who to call. Post a copy at home and another at work or school.
Trigger avoidance, in practice
  • Dust mites: covers on the pillow and mattress, bedding washed hot weekly, humidity down
  • Pet dander: pets out of the bedroom, HEPA filters, regular baths for the animal
  • Pollen: windows shut when counts are high, shower after being outside
  • Mold: fix the leaks, run the extractor fans, clean what is visible
  • Smoke: none in the house or the car, and none secondhand. Avoid wood fires
  • Exercise: SABA 15 minutes beforehand, warm up gradually, avoid cold dry air
When they call for help

Teach these six as the point at which they stop trying to manage it themselves.

  • Albuterol has not worked after three treatments
  • They can only get out single words
  • They are sitting forward on their arms to breathe
  • It is hard to breathe even sitting still
  • They are confused or getting drowsy
  • They look grey, blue, or are pouring sweat
Bottom Line: Written plan, demonstrated technique, known triggers, peak flow zones. Everything here happens on a day nobody clinical is present.
10

Children are not small adults

Asthma is the most common chronic disease of childhood. Two things change everything: a young child cannot tell you what is wrong, and a small airway closes faster.

What it looks like when they cannot say it

Infants and toddlers present through behaviour and feeding, not complaints.

  • Trouble feeding, poor weight gain, refusing to eat
  • Fussiness and irritability, then lethargy
  • Repeated colds and respiratory infections
  • Audible wheeze, or a cough that is worse at night
  • Retractions, subcostal, intercostal or suprasternal
  • Nasal flaring and grunting in infants
Why children crash faster

A small airway narrowed by the same amount of swelling loses a far greater share of its opening. Children also have less respiratory reserve to spend.

So a child in respiratory distress can reach failure quickly. Treat it as urgent, reassess often, and do not leave them alone.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Spacers Are Essential for Children
A child cannot coordinate press-and-breathe, so a spacer holds the dose suspended while they breathe normally. Under 4, use a mask covering nose and mouth, and get a good seal; a crying child gets almost none of the drug. Five or six breaths through the spacer per puff.
School and daycare
  • Written action plan filed with the school nurse and the teacher
  • Rescue inhaler accessible, and self-carried where the state allows it
  • Triggers documented, with PE modifications agreed
  • Emergency contacts current
  • Staff trained to recognise symptoms and give the rescue medication
  • A plan for field trips, so the medication travels with the child
Sport is not the enemy

Children with asthma can and should be physically active. Withdrawing them from sport is the wrong answer.

  • SABA 15 to 20 minutes before exercise if needed
  • A 10 to 15 minute warm-up helps prevent exercise-induced symptoms
  • Avoid cold dry air, high pollen days and poor air quality
  • Swimming is often well tolerated, because the air is warm and humid
  • Coaches and PE teachers need the action plan
  • The rescue inhaler is with them during the activity
Bottom Line: Under 5, a spacer is mandatory and a mask under 4. Smaller airways mean faster deterioration. Keep the child in sport, with a plan.
