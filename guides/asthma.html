Respiratory Anatomy & Physiology Review

Understanding normal airway anatomy and physiology is essential for comprehending how asthma disrupts breathing. Asthma primarily affects the lower airways, causing reversible airway obstruction.

Key Airway Structures Affected in Asthma

  • Bronchi: Large airways that branch from the trachea; contain cartilage for support and smooth muscle in walls
  • Bronchioles: Smaller airways without cartilage; highly susceptible to smooth muscle constriction
  • Smooth Muscle: Surrounds airways; contraction causes bronchoconstriction (narrowing)
  • Mucous Glands: Line airways; secrete mucus for trapping particles and humidification
  • Epithelial Lining: Contains ciliated cells that move mucus upward; becomes inflamed in asthma
Clinical Pearl: Asthma vs COPD Airway Changes

Asthma: Airway obstruction is REVERSIBLE - bronchodilators can open airways

COPD: Airway obstruction is largely IRREVERSIBLE - structural damage is permanent

This distinction is critical for treatment and prognosis!

Normal Airway Control

System Effect on Airways Clinical Relevance
Parasympathetic (Vagal) Bronchoconstriction, increased mucus Anticholinergics block this effect
Sympathetic (Beta-2) Bronchodilation, decreased mucus Beta-agonists stimulate this response
Inflammatory Mediators Bronchoconstriction, edema, mucus Corticosteroids reduce inflammation
Key Concept: Beta-2 Receptors

Beta-2 receptors are found in bronchial smooth muscle. When stimulated by medications like albuterol, they cause smooth muscle relaxation and bronchodilation. This is why short-acting beta-agonists (SABAs) are the first-line rescue medications for acute asthma symptoms.

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Airways are lined with smooth muscle and mucous glands. In asthma, chronic inflammation causes hyper-responsive airways that overreact to triggers with bronchospasm, edema, and excess mucus.

Asthma Pathophysiology

Asthma is a chronic inflammatory disorder of the airways characterized by reversible airflow obstruction, airway hyperresponsiveness, and chronic inflammation. Understanding the underlying pathophysiology helps guide treatment decisions.

Definition

Asthma is a chronic respiratory disease characterized by recurrent episodes of wheezing, breathlessness, chest tightness, and coughing, particularly at night or in the early morning. These episodes are associated with widespread but variable airflow obstruction that is often reversible either spontaneously or with treatment.

The Three Key Features of Asthma

What Happens in Asthma

1
Chronic Airway Inflammation

Triggered by allergens, irritants, or infections. Involves mast cells, eosinophils, T-lymphocytes, and other inflammatory cells. Releases histamine, leukotrienes, and prostaglandins that cause swelling and mucus production.

2
Bronchospasm (Bronchoconstriction)

Smooth muscle surrounding airways contracts in response to triggers. Causes rapid narrowing of airway lumen. Results in wheezing, shortness of breath, and chest tightness. Reversible with bronchodilators.

3
Airway Hyperresponsiveness

Airways become "twitchy" and overreact to triggers that don't affect normal airways. Even mild stimuli (cold air, exercise, smoke) can trigger bronchoconstriction. Increases with ongoing inflammation if untreated.

Memory Aid: Asthma Triad

The "I-B-H" of Asthma:

  • Inflammation - Chronic airway inflammation
  • Bronchospasm - Smooth muscle constriction
  • Hyperresponsiveness - Exaggerated airway reactivity

Early vs Late Phase Asthma Response

Early Phase (Minutes)

Timing

Occurs within 10-30 minutes of trigger exposure

What Happens

  • Mast cells degranulate
  • Histamine and leukotrienes released
  • Immediate bronchoconstriction
  • Responds to bronchodilators (SABA)
Late Phase (Hours)

Timing

Occurs 4-12 hours after trigger exposure

What Happens

  • Eosinophils and T-cells infiltrate
  • Ongoing inflammation
  • Mucus hypersecretion, edema
  • Requires corticosteroids to control
Critical Point: Airway Remodeling

Uncontrolled chronic inflammation leads to permanent airway changes! Over time, repeated inflammation causes structural changes including smooth muscle hypertrophy, subepithelial fibrosis, and goblet cell hyperplasia. This "airway remodeling" can make asthma irreversible and harder to treat. This is why controller medications to reduce inflammation are essential even when patients feel well.

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Asthma is reversible (unlike COPD). The early phase is bronchospasm (minutes), the late phase is inflammation (4â€“8 hours). This is why we treat with both bronchodilators AND anti-inflammatory agents.

Asthma Severity Classification

Asthma severity classification guides initial treatment decisions. Severity is determined by symptom frequency, nighttime awakenings, rescue inhaler use, activity limitation, and lung function before treatment is started.

Key Distinction: Severity vs Control

Severity = Intrinsic disease intensity (assessed BEFORE starting treatment)

Control = How well symptoms are managed WITH treatment

A patient can have severe asthma that is well-controlled, or mild asthma that is poorly controlled!

Rescue vs Controller Medications

Understanding the difference between rescue (reliever) and controller (maintenance) medications is essential for proper asthma management and patient education. Each type serves a distinct purpose in treatment.

Rescue (Reliever) Medications

Purpose

Quick relief of acute symptoms; used as needed

Key Characteristics

  • Fast onset (minutes)
  • Short duration (4-6 hours)
  • Treats bronchoconstriction
  • Does NOT treat inflammation
  • Overuse indicates poor control
Controller (Maintenance) Medications

Purpose

Long-term prevention; used daily regardless of symptoms

Key Characteristics

  • Slow onset (days to weeks)
  • Long-lasting protection
  • Reduces inflammation
  • Prevents airway remodeling
  • Must be taken consistently
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Rescue vs Controller - The Key Difference
Think of it like a fire analogy! A rescue inhaler (like albuterol) is like a fire extinguisher - it puts out the fire (bronchospasm) quickly but doesn't prevent future fires. A controller medication (like an inhaled corticosteroid) is like a fireproof coating - it prevents fires from starting by reducing the underlying inflammation. Patients need to understand: using only rescue inhalers is like constantly putting out fires without fixing the faulty wiring!

Critical Safety Alert: SABA Overuse

Using a rescue inhaler more than 2 days/week indicates poorly controlled asthma!

  • Needing a rescue inhaler >2 times/week (excluding pre-exercise use) = poor control
  • Using >1 canister per month is associated with increased risk of severe exacerbations
  • Overreliance on SABA without controller therapy increases mortality risk
  • If overusing: assess controller medication adherence, technique, and triggers

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
ICS is the Most Effective Controller
Inhaled corticosteroids (ICS) are the cornerstone of asthma treatment! They are the most effective medication for reducing airway inflammation, preventing exacerbations, and improving lung function. Key teaching points: ICS are different from systemic steroids - they work locally in the lungs with minimal systemic effects. Patients must use them daily even when feeling well. Effects aren't immediate - it takes days to weeks to see full benefit. Always rinse mouth after use to prevent thrush!
BLACK BOX WARNING: LABA Monotherapy

LABAs must NEVER be used alone in asthma - always combine with ICS!

LABA monotherapy increases the risk of asthma-related death. LABAs should only be used in combination with an inhaled corticosteroid in patients whose asthma is not adequately controlled on other asthma-controller medications.

Inhaler Technique Essentials

MDI (Metered-Dose Inhaler) Steps
  1. Shake inhaler well (5-10 seconds)
  2. Exhale fully away from inhaler
  3. Place mouthpiece in mouth with tight seal (or 1-2 inches away)
  4. Begin slow, deep inhalation while pressing canister
  5. Continue inhaling for 3-5 seconds
  6. Hold breath for 10 seconds (or as long as comfortable)
  7. Wait 1 minute between puffs if multiple doses needed
  8. Rinse mouth after ICS use

Use a spacer with MDIs for better drug delivery and reduced oral deposition!

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Quick-relief = SABA (albuterol). Long-term control = ICS Â± LABA. Always rinse mouth after ICS. Never use LABA alone (must be combined with ICS). Needing SABA > 2x/week = step up therapy.

Nursing Assessment

Thorough assessment of asthma patients is crucial for determining severity, recognizing exacerbations, and evaluating treatment effectiveness. Assessment includes subjective history and objective physical findings.

Comprehensive Asthma Assessment

Key Assessment Components

1
Respiratory Assessment

Rate, rhythm, depth, effort. Note wheezing (expiratory > inspiratory), prolonged expiratory phase, use of accessory muscles, nasal flaring, retractions. Measure SpO2 and peak flow.

2
Symptom Frequency

Daytime symptoms (how many days/week), nighttime awakenings, rescue inhaler use, activity limitations. Use standardized tools: ACT (Asthma Control Test) score.

3
Trigger Identification

Environmental exposures, allergens, irritants, infections, exercise, weather changes, medications (aspirin, beta-blockers), emotional stress. Assess home/work environment.

4
Medication Review

Current medications (rescue and controller), adherence, inhaler technique, understanding of each medication's purpose. Frequency of SABA use indicates control level.

5
Peak Flow Monitoring

Personal best established during good control. Compare current readings to personal best. Green (80-100%), Yellow (50-79%), Red (<50%) zones guide action.

DANGER SIGN: Silent Chest

Absence of wheezing in an asthma patient with respiratory distress is an ominous sign!

A "silent chest" indicates such severe airway obstruction that insufficient air is moving to produce wheezing. This is a sign of impending respiratory failure and requires immediate intervention. Do NOT be falsely reassured by absence of wheezing in a severely distressed patient!

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Wheezing is NOT the best indicator of severity â€” a silent chest means NO air movement and is more dangerous than loud wheezing. Monitor peak flow, SpO2, and ability to speak in full sentences.

Status Asthmaticus Emergency

Status asthmaticus is a severe, life-threatening asthma exacerbation that does not respond to standard bronchodilator therapy. It is a medical emergency requiring immediate intervention to prevent respiratory failure and death.

Status Asthmaticus = MEDICAL EMERGENCY

This is a life-threatening condition! Status asthmaticus is characterized by:

  • Severe bronchospasm unresponsive to initial bronchodilator therapy
  • Progressive respiratory distress despite treatment
  • Risk of respiratory failure, hypoxemia, acidosis, and death
  • Requires aggressive treatment, possible ICU admission, and may need intubation

Recognition of Status Asthmaticus

Silent chest (no wheezing) Severe obstruction - no air movement EMERGENCY - prepare for intubation
Cyanosis Severe hypoxemia (SpO2 <90%) High-flow O2, prepare for escalation
Altered mental status Hypoxia/hypercapnia affecting brain Call rapid response, prepare for intubation
Unable to speak Too breathless - severe distress Continuous nebulizer, IV steroids
Peak flow <25% predicted Severe airway obstruction Escalate care, may need ICU
Paradoxical chest movement Respiratory muscle fatigue Impending respiratory failure
Bradycardia Pre-arrest sign in respiratory distress EMERGENCY - imminent arrest
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Recognize Status Asthmaticus EARLY
Time is critical in status asthmaticus - early recognition saves lives! Watch for these red flags: patient sitting in tripod position, speaking only 1-2 words between breaths, diaphoresis, agitation progressing to lethargy, peak flow <40% of personal best, and most importantly - a silent chest (no wheezing despite severe distress). A silent chest means no air is moving and is a sign of impending respiratory arrest. Don't wait - escalate care immediately when you see these signs!

Emergency Management

Status Asthmaticus Treatment Protocol

1
Oxygen Therapy

High-flow oxygen to maintain SpO2 â‰¥92%. Unlike COPD, asthma patients do not have chronic CO2 retention - give oxygen liberally. May need non-rebreather mask initially.

2
Continuous Nebulized Beta-Agonists

Albuterol continuous nebulization (10-15 mg/hour) or frequent intermittent treatments (2.5-5 mg q20min x3). May add ipratropium for severe exacerbations.

3
Systemic Corticosteroids

IV methylprednisolone (125 mg or 1-2 mg/kg) or oral prednisone (40-60 mg) immediately. Reduces inflammation - onset in 4-6 hours. Critical for preventing late-phase response.

4
Magnesium Sulfate

IV magnesium sulfate (2 grams over 20 minutes) for severe exacerbations. Causes smooth muscle relaxation. Monitor for hypotension, flushing.

5
Epinephrine (if needed)

Subcutaneous or IM epinephrine (0.3-0.5 mg) for severe cases, especially if anaphylaxis suspected. Provides potent bronchodilation.

6
Prepare for Intubation

If patient deteriorating despite treatment, prepare for endotracheal intubation and mechanical ventilation. Use ketamine for induction (bronchodilator properties).

NCLEX Priority: Oxygen in Asthma vs COPD

Key Difference:

  • Asthma: Give high-flow oxygen freely! Asthma patients do not have chronic CO2 retention and rely on normal respiratory drive.
  • COPD: Use low-flow oxygen (1-2 L/min) cautiously to avoid suppressing hypoxic drive in chronic CO2 retainers.

In status asthmaticus, hypoxia is the immediate threat - treat it aggressively!

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Status asthmaticus is a medical emergency. Give continuous nebulized albuterol, IV corticosteroids, IV magnesium sulfate. Prepare for intubation if PaCO2 rises (sign of exhaustion).

Nursing Interventions

Nursing care for asthma patients focuses on symptom management, medication administration, trigger avoidance, and patient education to promote self-management and prevent exacerbations.

Priority Nursing Interventions

Key Interventions

1
Maintain Airway and Optimize Oxygenation

Position upright (high Fowler's). Administer oxygen as prescribed. Monitor SpO2 continuously during exacerbations. Assess respiratory status frequently. Keep calm environment to reduce oxygen demand.

2
Administer Medications as Prescribed

Give bronchodilators and corticosteroids as ordered. Ensure proper inhaler/nebulizer technique. Space controller medications appropriately. Monitor for side effects (tachycardia, tremors, thrush).

3
Monitor for Deterioration

Watch for signs of worsening: increased work of breathing, decreased responsiveness, silent chest, falling SpO2. Monitor peak flow if patient able. Report deterioration immediately.

4
Reduce Anxiety and Promote Calm Breathing

Stay calm and reassuring. Dyspnea causes anxiety which worsens symptoms. Coach pursed-lip and diaphragmatic breathing. Remove unnecessary stimulation. Stay with patient during acute episodes.

5
Ensure Adequate Hydration

Encourage fluid intake (2-3 L/day unless contraindicated) to help thin secretions. IV fluids may be needed during acute exacerbations. Monitor I&O.

6
Prevent Triggers in Hospital Environment

Minimize exposure to allergens and irritants. Avoid strong perfumes, cleaning products. Maintain clean air. Consider HEPA filter if available. Keep room dust-free.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Always Assess Inhaler Technique
Poor inhaler technique is a major cause of uncontrolled asthma! Studies show up to 90% of patients use inhalers incorrectly. Before stepping up therapy for "uncontrolled" asthma, always assess and correct inhaler technique. Have the patient demonstrate their technique - don't just ask if they know how. Common errors include: not exhaling before inhaling, inhaling too fast, not holding breath after, and not using a spacer with MDIs. Teach and re-teach at every visit!

Every Patient Needs a Written Action Plan
A written asthma action plan reduces ER visits and hospitalizations! Every asthma patient should have a personalized written action plan that includes: daily medications, personal best peak flow, green/yellow/red zone actions, and emergency contact information. Review the plan with the patient, post copies at home and work/school, and update it regularly. Tips for Trigger Avoidance
  • Dust mites: Dust-proof pillow/mattress covers, wash bedding in hot water weekly, reduce humidity
  • Pet dander: Keep pets out of bedroom, use HEPA filters, bathe pets regularly
  • Pollen: Keep windows closed during high pollen, shower after outdoor activities
  • Mold: Fix water leaks, use exhaust fans in bathrooms/kitchen, clean visible mold
  • Smoke: No smoking in home/car, avoid secondhand smoke, avoid wood fires
  • Exercise: Use SABA 15 minutes before exercise, warm up gradually, avoid cold dry air

"ASTHMA Emergency" - When to Call 911:

  • Speech limited to single words
  • Tripod positioning to breathe
  • Hard to breathe even at rest
  • Mental status changes (confusion, lethargy)
  • Appearance: cyanosis, grey, diaphoretic

Pediatric Considerations

Asthma is the most common chronic disease in children. Pediatric asthma presents unique challenges in assessment, diagnosis, and management due to developmental factors and age-related differences in anatomy and physiology.

Pediatric-Specific Assessment Challenges

Young children cannot verbalize symptoms! Infants and toddlers may present with:

  • Feeding difficulties, poor weight gain, or refusal to eat
  • Increased fussiness, irritability, or lethargy
  • Recurrent "colds" or respiratory infections
  • Audible wheezing or persistent cough (especially at night)
  • Retractions (subcostal, intercostal, suprasternal)
  • Nasal flaring and grunting in infants

Children Deteriorate Faster Than Adults!

Pediatric airways are smaller - severe obstruction develops more rapidly!

Children have less respiratory reserve than adults. A child in respiratory distress can progress to respiratory failure quickly. Always treat pediatric respiratory distress as urgent and reassess frequently. Never leave a child with respiratory distress unattended.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Spacers Are Essential for Children
ALWAYS use a spacer with MDIs in children! Children cannot coordinate the "press and breathe" technique required for MDIs. A valved holding chamber (spacer) allows the child to breathe normally while the medication stays suspended. For children under 4, use a spacer with a mask that covers nose and mouth. Ensure a good seal - a crying child won't get adequate medication delivery. Take 5-6 breaths through the spacer per puff.

School & Daycare Management

School Asthma Action Plan Essentials
  • Written action plan on file with school nurse and teacher
  • Rescue inhaler accessible at school (some states allow self-carry)
  • Triggers identified - PE modifications, allergen avoidance
  • Emergency contacts updated and readily available
  • Staff trained to recognize symptoms and administer rescue medication
  • Field trip plan - ensure medications accompany child
Exercise and Sports Participation

Children with asthma CAN and SHOULD participate in physical activities!

  • Pre-treat with SABA 15-20 minutes before exercise if needed
  • Warm-up period of 10-15 minutes helps prevent exercise-induced symptoms
  • Avoid triggers: cold/dry air, high pollen days, poor air quality
  • Swimming is often well-tolerated (warm, humid air)
  • Coaches and PE teachers should know the action plan
  • Child should have rescue inhaler available during activities

Severity classification guides initial treatment step; control guides adjustments

Silent chest in distress = EMERGENCY - no air moving, impending arrest

ICS is most effective controller; LABAs must NEVER be used alone

Written action plan with peak flow zones improves outcomes
