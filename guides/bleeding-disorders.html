Bleeding Disorders

Hemophilia (factor deficiency → joint bleeds) and immune thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP, platelet destruction → petechiae and purpura). Labs, factor replacement, bleeding precautions, and how to tell these apart from child abuse.

7 Sections 14 Quiz Questions ~12 Min Read

Learning Objectives

Differentiate hemophilia A and B by factor deficiency and inheritance pattern.
Describe the classic clinical findings of hemarthrosis and deep tissue bleeding.
Explain the immune mechanism of ITP and typical post-viral onset.
Interpret coagulation labs and differentiate bleeding disorders from abuse.
Apply bleeding precautions and emergency management of acute bleeds.
Teach families home care, activity modification, and when to seek help.

High-Yield Summary

Eight facts to remember about pediatric bleeding disorders.

  • Hemophilia = inherited clotting factor deficiency. Hemophilia A (factor VIII, ~80%) > Hemophilia B (factor IX, Christmas disease, ~20%). Both are X-linked recessive — almost always affects boys.
  • Hemophilia classic finding: hemarthrosis (bleeding into joints — knees, elbows, ankles). Causes chronic joint damage. Also deep tissue hematomas; NO petechiae.
  • Hemophilia labs: PROLONGED PTT, normal PT, normal platelet count, normal bleeding time. Confirmed by factor assay (factor VIII or IX level).
  • ITP = Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura — autoantibodies destroy platelets in the spleen. Typically follows a viral illness by 1–4 weeks in an otherwise healthy child.
  • ITP classic presentation: sudden onset petechiae (pinpoint, non-blanching) + purpura (larger purplish areas) + ecchymoses + mucosal bleeding in a well-appearing child. No hepatosplenomegaly or lymphadenopathy.
  • ITP labs: ISOLATED severe thrombocytopenia (often <20,000). CBC otherwise normal; PT/PTT normal. Most childhood ITP is self-limited and resolves within 6 months.
  • ITP first-line treatment: observation if mild; IVIG, anti-D immunoglobulin, or corticosteroids for significant bleeding; platelet transfusion ONLY for life-threatening bleeding (antibodies destroy transfused platelets quickly).
  • Always screen for abuse in children with bruising — but remember, a bleeding disorder can mimic child abuse. Bruising in unusual locations, symmetric patterns, or delayed presentation raises concern either way. Coag labs distinguish.

Bleeding Disorder Fundamentals

Primary vs. secondary hemostasis

Bleeding disorders in children fall into two main buckets based on which part of hemostasis fails:

  • Primary hemostasis — platelet function (initial plug formation). When platelets are deficient (ITP) or dysfunctional (von Willebrand), you see mucocutaneous bleeding: petechiae, purpura, epistaxis, gum bleeding, menorrhagia.
  • Secondary hemostasis — coagulation factor cascade (stable clot formation). When clotting factors are deficient (hemophilia), you see deep tissue bleeding: hemarthrosis, muscle hematomas, intracranial hemorrhage. Petechiae are NOT a feature of hemophilia.
Feature Platelet Problem (ITP, vWD) Factor Problem (Hemophilia)
Onset of bleeding Immediate after injury Delayed (hours later)
Bleeding sites Skin, mucous membranes (nose, gums, GI, menses) Deep tissue — joints, muscles, CNS
Classic finding Petechiae, purpura Hemarthrosis
Platelet count Low (or normal but dysfunctional) Normal
PT / PTT Normal Prolonged PTT, normal PT
NCLEX Point — Petechiae vs Hemarthrosis

A question stem that mentions petechiae and mucosal bleeding in a well-appearing toddler is almost always pointing to ITP. A question stem that mentions a swollen painful knee or elbow in a boy after a minor bump is pointing to hemophilia. Learn the pattern and half these questions become easy.

🎯 Bottom Line: Platelet problem = mucocutaneous (petechiae, purpura). Factor problem = deep (hemarthrosis, muscle). Hemophilia prolongs PTT; ITP has low platelets only.

Hemophilia (A & B)

Inherited factor deficiency, joint bleeds, and factor replacement

Hemophilia is an inherited bleeding disorder caused by deficiency of a clotting factor. The two major types differ by which factor is missing but behave clinically the same: recurrent deep-tissue bleeding, especially into joints.

Types of Hemophilia

Hemophilia A

~80%
Deficiency

Factor VIII deficiency.

Inheritance

X-linked recessive — mothers are usually carriers; almost all affected are boys.

Severity

Based on factor level: severe <1%, moderate 1–5%, mild 5–40%.

Hemophilia B

~20%
Deficiency

Factor IX deficiency ("Christmas disease").

Inheritance

X-linked recessive, same pattern as hemophilia A.

Severity

Similar clinical picture to hemophilia A.

Von Willebrand Disease

Most Common
Deficiency

vWF (holds factor VIII + helps platelets stick).

Inheritance

Autosomal dominant; boys and girls equally.

Presentation

Mucosal bleeding: epistaxis, easy bruising, menorrhagia. Usually milder than hemophilia.

Clinical Manifestations of Hemophilia

Hemarthrosis — spontaneous or post-trauma bleeding into a joint; knees, elbows, ankles most common. Warm, swollen, painful, limited range of motion.

Deep tissue hematomas — large, palpable bruises; may cause compartment syndrome.

Mucosal bleeding — mouth/tongue (after tooth loss), prolonged epistaxis, GI or GU bleeding.

Intracranial hemorrhage — rare but deadly; any head injury warrants immediate factor replacement and imaging.

Delayed bleeding — unlike platelet problems, hemophilia bleeds often start HOURS after injury.

Diagnosis often in infancy — prolonged bleeding after circumcision, easy bruising when starting to crawl.

Stages of a Joint Bleed

  1. Early (aura): child reports "tingling" or "bubbling" sensation in the joint — this is the child\u2019s signal to start factor NOW.
  2. Acute swelling: warm, swollen, stiff joint; often immobile. Pain is severe.
  3. Resolution: with early factor replacement + RICE, bleeding stops in 1–2 days.
  4. Chronic arthropathy: repeated bleeds destroy cartilage → permanent joint damage, early arthritis, sometimes disability.
The Child Knows the Joint First

Older children with hemophilia often recognize the aura of a joint bleed before swelling is visible. They\u2019ll say, "my knee feels funny." Believe them and start factor replacement immediately. Catching a bleed early dramatically reduces pain and damage.

The RICE Response (At Home or In Hospital)

  • Rest — immobilize; no weight-bearing on an affected joint until cleared.
  • Ice — cold pack to promote vasoconstriction and reduce swelling.
  • Compression — elastic wrap; NOT tight enough to impair circulation.
  • Elevation — above heart level.
  • PLUS factor replacement as soon as possible.
🎯 Bottom Line: Hemophilia A (VIII) / B (IX), X-linked recessive, boys. Hallmark = hemarthrosis. RICE + factor replacement. Head trauma = emergency.

Immune Thrombocytopenic Purpura (ITP)

Post-viral platelet destruction in an otherwise well child

ITP is an autoimmune disorder in which the body produces antiplatelet antibodies that tag platelets for destruction in the spleen. In children, ITP typically follows a common viral illness by 1–4 weeks and resolves spontaneously in most cases.

Pathophysiology in Plain Language

  1. Child has a viral illness (URI, GI, viral exanthem) or rarely a recent vaccine.
  2. The immune response produces antibodies. These antibodies cross-react with platelet surface antigens.
  3. Antibody-coated platelets are removed from circulation by the spleen → isolated thrombocytopenia.
  4. Bone marrow megakaryocytes are increased (trying to make more platelets), but destruction outpaces production.
  5. Most childhood ITP self-resolves within 6 months. Chronic (>12 months) is less common.

Classic Presentation

Petechiae — pinpoint, non-blanching, red-purple dots; typically on dependent areas (legs) and points of pressure.

Purpura — larger purplish patches of bleeding under the skin; widespread.

Ecchymoses (bruising) disproportionate to activity — including on non-trauma sites.

Mucosal bleeding — epistaxis, gum bleeding, oral blood blisters ("wet purpura"), menorrhagia in adolescents.

Well-appearing child — NO fever, weight loss, bone pain, or hepatosplenomegaly. These findings point AWAY from ITP toward leukemia.

Typical age: preschool to school-age; peak 2–5 years. Often a history of viral illness 1–4 weeks ago.

Rule Out Leukemia Before Calling It ITP

Isolated thrombocytopenia in a well child usually means ITP, but leukemia can present similarly. Red flags favoring leukemia: fever, weight loss, bone pain, hepatosplenomegaly, lymphadenopathy, anemia, or abnormal WBC. Many oncology clinics do a bone marrow biopsy before starting steroids to make sure it\u2019s not leukemia — steroids can mask leukemia findings.

🎯 Bottom Line: ITP = post-viral, antiplatelet antibodies, isolated thrombocytopenia, petechiae + purpura in a well child. Rule out leukemia. Often self-resolves.

Hemophilia-Specific Workup

  • Factor VIII and IX activity levels — confirms diagnosis and severity (severe <1%, moderate 1–5%, mild 5–40%).
  • Inhibitor screen — detects antibodies against infused factor in chronic patients.
  • Genetic testing for family planning and carrier detection in mothers/sisters.
  • Imaging — CT/MRI for head injury or suspected intracranial bleed; US for muscle hematomas.

ITP-Specific Workup

  • CBC with differential and peripheral smear — isolated thrombocytopenia with normal morphology.
  • Hx + physical looking for hepatosplenomegaly, lymph nodes, fever, bone pain — would argue against ITP.
  • Bone marrow biopsy — not always required; done before steroids in uncertain cases or atypical features.
  • Consider secondary causes: lupus, HIV, H. pylori, drug-induced.

Abuse Screen (When Bruising Is Unexplained)

Bleeding Disorders Can Mimic Child Abuse — and Vice Versa

Children with easy bruising, petechiae, or unexplained bleeding require BOTH a bleeding workup AND a careful abuse evaluation. Coag labs (PT, PTT, platelets) and a thorough history distinguish the two, but they aren\u2019t mutually exclusive. Red flags for abuse: bruises in unusual or symmetric patterns, patterned bruises (hand, belt, cord), injuries inconsistent with the history or developmental stage, injuries in a non-ambulatory infant ("those who don\u2019t cruise rarely bruise"), delayed care seeking. Report abuse concerns per mandatory reporting laws while completing the medical workup.

🎯 Bottom Line: Hemophilia = prolonged PTT + low factor VIII or IX. ITP = isolated low platelets. Always consider abuse with unexplained bruising \u2014 and vice versa.

Treatment Overview

Factor replacement, immune therapy, and bleeding control

Hemophilia Treatment

Factor VIII Concentrate
Hemophilia A

IV infusion; either recombinant or plasma-derived.

On-demand for active bleeds OR prophylactic 2–3x/week to prevent bleeds.

Factor IX Concentrate
Hemophilia B

IV infusion for hemophilia B.

Prophylaxis or on-demand; similar principles to factor VIII.

Emicizumab (Hemlibra)
Bispecific Antibody

Subcutaneous weekly injection; mimics factor VIII\u2019s role.

Massively reduces bleed frequency; used prophylactically in hemophilia A, including with inhibitors.

Desmopressin (DDAVP)
Vasopressin Analog

For mild hemophilia A and vWD; stimulates release of factor VIII / vWF.

Watch for hyponatremia and fluid overload.

Aminocaproic Acid / Tranexamic Acid
Antifibrinolytics

Adjunct for mouth/nose bleeds; prevents clot breakdown.

Oral or IV; avoid in GU bleeds (clot obstruction risk).

Factor First for Any Head Injury

A child with hemophilia who experiences even a minor head injury gets factor replacement first, then imaging. Intracranial hemorrhage is the leading cause of death in hemophilia — a few minutes to infuse factor could be life-saving. "Factor first, worry later" is a common hemophilia ED rule.

ITP Treatment

  1. Observation — if platelets are modestly decreased and no active bleeding, most children recover on their own. Activity restriction is key.
  2. IVIG (intravenous immunoglobulin) — raises platelets rapidly (hours to 1–2 days) by blocking macrophage Fc receptors. First-line for significant bleeding.
  3. Anti-D immunoglobulin (WinRho) — alternative to IVIG in Rh-positive, non-splenectomized patients; causes mild hemolysis that saturates splenic macrophages.
  4. Corticosteroids (prednisone, dexamethasone) — effective but side effects; often second-line. Rule out leukemia before starting.
  5. Platelet transfusion — reserved for life-threatening bleeding. Transfused platelets are destroyed rapidly by the same antibodies; give with IVIG/steroids.
  6. Splenectomy — for chronic, severe ITP not responding to medical therapy (rare in children).
  7. TPO receptor agonists (romiplostim, eltrombopag) — stimulate platelet production; used in chronic ITP.
NCLEX Point — Why Not Just Transfuse Platelets in ITP?

Because the same antibodies destroy transfused platelets within hours, routine platelet transfusion is NOT effective in ITP. Reserve it for life-threatening bleeding, and always give with IVIG or steroids to block destruction.

Medications to AVOID

Aspirin — impairs platelet function; avoid in both hemophilia and ITP.

NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) — impair platelets; also GI bleed risk.

IM injections — deep tissue bleeding risk in hemophilia; bleeding in ITP. Give all vaccines SubQ with firm pressure.

Rectal meds/procedures — mucosal injury causes bleeding.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Nosebleed First Aid in a Bleeding Disorder
Have the child lean slightly forward (NOT back — prevents swallowing blood), pinch the soft part of the nose firmly for at least 10 minutes without checking. Apply a cool compress to the bridge of the nose. If bleeding persists 15–20 minutes despite pressure, or the child becomes pale/faint, call the hemophilia center or go to the ED.
🎯 Bottom Line: Hemophilia = factor replacement (VIII or IX) + emicizumab prophylaxis; DDAVP for mild A/vWD. ITP = IVIG, anti-D, steroids; platelet transfusion only for life-threatening bleeds. Avoid ASA/NSAIDs/IM.

Nursing Priorities & Bleeding Precautions

Daily care and emergency management

Bleeding Precautions (Both Conditions)

Soft toothbrush, avoid flossing if platelets low; soft foods if mouth sore.

Electric razor for adolescents; no straight razors.

No rectal procedures, no IM injections (SubQ with pressure), no aspirin/NSAIDs.

Pressure 5–10 minutes after every venipuncture, finger stick, or injection.

Age-appropriate activity restriction — no contact sports; protective gear (helmets, pads) when permitted.

Frequent skin and mucous membrane checks — document petechiae, ecchymoses, bleeding.

Fall precautions — bed in low position, side rails as appropriate, declutter the room.

Monitor for internal bleeding — tachycardia, hypotension, pallor, abdominal pain, hematuria, melena.

Acute Bleed Management

  1. Apply direct pressure to any accessible bleeding site for 10+ minutes without peeking.
  2. Immobilize affected joint or limb in a position of comfort; elevate.
  3. Apply cold/ice pack to the area to promote vasoconstriction.
  4. Administer factor replacement or ITP therapy as ordered — get the IV started early for hemophilia.
  5. Assess pain — acetaminophen (NOT NSAIDs) or opioids for severe pain.
  6. Monitor vitals, LOC, output — early signs of significant blood loss include tachycardia and pallor; late signs include hypotension.
  7. Reassess the bleed regularly; notify provider for worsening symptoms.

Specific Hemophilia Situations

Head trauma → factor FIRST, then CT. Watch for ICP changes.

Joint bleed → RICE + factor; elevate; no weight-bearing until cleared.

Muscle hematoma (iliopsoas) → hip/abdominal pain; compartment syndrome risk.

Airway bleed (throat/tongue) → airway compromise; factor + ICU.

Hematuria → hydrate; avoid antifibrinolytics (clot obstruction); factor if significant.

Pre-procedure or surgery → pre-treat with factor; consult hematology.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Give Vaccines SubQ, Not IM
Children with hemophilia or active ITP should get routine vaccines subcutaneously (never IM) using the smallest gauge needle, followed by at least 5 minutes of firm pressure. This prevents deep muscle hematomas. Coordinate with the hemophilia clinic; some prefer vaccines given right after factor infusion.
🎯 Bottom Line: Soft brush + electric razor + no ASA/NSAIDs + SubQ shots + 10-min pressure. Head trauma = factor first. Monitor for internal bleeding.

Family Education

Home care, activity, and when to call

Home-Care Priorities

Medication plan — how/when to infuse factor at home, prophylaxis schedule (hemophilia), activity limits.

Recognize bleeds early — tingling joints (hemophilia), new petechiae/bruising (ITP).

Avoid ASA/NSAIDs — read labels; some cold/flu meds contain these.

Soft brush, electric razor, protective gear — routine daily habits.

MedicAlert bracelet identifying the condition.

Dental care — work with hematology-aware dentist; may need factor before procedures.

Activity Guidance

Encouraged Avoid or Limit
Swimming, biking (with helmet), walking, golf, doubles tennis, yoga Football, rugby, hockey, wrestling, boxing, MMA — high-impact contact sports
Age-appropriate play with supervision Climbing/trampolines for young kids with hemophilia
School-based 504 plan accommodations; PE modifications Skating/skateboarding without pads/helmet

When to Call the Hemophilia Center / Go to the ED

Any head injury, regardless of severity

Severe or worsening joint/muscle pain and swelling

Persistent bleeding >15–20 minutes despite pressure

Hematemesis, melena, hematuria

Sudden severe abdominal, back, chest, or neck pain

Altered mental status, severe headache, vision changes

Psychosocial & Support

  • Hemophilia centers offer multidisciplinary care — hematologist, nurse, PT, social work, dentist.
  • Camp programs (Hemophilia Foundation camps) help kids meet peers and build independence with self-infusion.
  • Siblings and carrier identification — genetic counseling for family planning; female carriers can have bleeding symptoms.
  • ITP families — usually self-limited; reassure about good prognosis while being vigilant during the acute period.
  • Insurance & financial — factor products are expensive; social work can navigate coverage and patient assistance programs.
Modern Therapy Has Transformed These Lives

In the 20th century, hemophilia often meant disability, joint destruction, and shortened lifespan. Today, with prophylactic factor replacement and newer therapies like emicizumab, many children have minimal bleeds and near-normal lives. Most childhood ITP resolves spontaneously. Your optimistic but realistic teaching supports long-term adherence and hope.

🎯 Bottom Line: Meds, activity modifications, protective gear, MedicAlert. Head injury / uncontrolled bleeding = ED. Modern therapy allows a largely normal life.
