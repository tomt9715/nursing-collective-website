Bleeding Disorders

Hemophilia and ITP look like one topic and behave like two. Deep bleeds or surface bleeds, factor first or image first, a disorder or abuse. This guide is those discriminations.

01

What actually gets you

Bleeding disorders feel like one topic, but they are not. Hemophilia and ITP bleed in different places, from different causes, and you treat them differently. Learn the splits and the questions get easy.

Points get lost here in three predictable ways, so read these first.

You made one "bleeding kid" bucket

Then a stem gives you a swollen knee, and another gives you pinpoint spots on the legs. You call both "bleeding" and freeze, but they are opposites. Hemophilia bleeds deep, into joints and muscle; ITP bleeds at the surface, as petechiae and purpura. A single axis splits them.

You waited for the scan

A boy with hemophilia hits his head and looks fine, so you order imaging and wait. That is the wrong order. In hemophilia, a head injury gets factor first, then the CT, because intracranial bleeding is the thing that kills these children.

You confused the purpura with abuse, or missed the abuse

Bruises and purpura raise both questions at once: a bleeding disorder can look like abuse, and abuse can hide behind "he bruises easily". The coag panel sorts most of it. Normal labs plus a patterned bruise points away from disease.

Bottom Line: Stop studying "bleeding disorders" as one thing. Study three splits: deep or surface, factor first or image first, a disorder or abuse.

The four things

For every disorder below, say four things with the book closed: the one-sentence definition, the clue you would actually see in a child, the priority nursing action, and the first-line treatment with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

03

Hemophilia: the deep bleeds

Discrimination axis: where does the blood go, deep or surface.

Hemophilia is an inherited factor deficiency, so the clot cascade cannot finish. Bleeding is deep and slow to start; think joints, muscle, and brain, not skin.

This is the pair students blur, so set it straight now.

Hemophilia: factor problem, deep
  • Hemarthrosis: warm, swollen, painful joint
  • Deep muscle hematomas
  • Intracranial hemorrhage after head injury
  • Bleeding starts hours after the injury
  • Platelet count normal; no petechiae

Who: almost always boys. Long PTT, normal platelets.

ITP: platelet problem, surface
  • Petechiae: pinpoint, non-blanching dots
  • Purpura and easy bruising
  • Gum and nose bleeding
  • Bleeding is immediate after injury
  • Platelet count low; coags normal

Who: a well child after a virus. Low platelets, normal PTT.

The axis that separates them

Deep or surface. Hemophilia bleeds into joints and muscle; the platelet count is normal and the PTT is long. ITP bleeds into skin and mucosa; the platelet count is low and the coags are normal. Petechiae are never hemophilia.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Deep or Surface
Swollen joint in a boy equals hemophilia. Pinpoint spots in a well child equals ITP. One is a factor problem, deep. The other is a platelet problem, at the surface. Sort the depth first and the stem answers itself.

Hemophilia A or B

Discrimination axis: which factor is missing.

A and B behave the same at the bedside: same joint bleeds, same head-injury rule, same X-linked inheritance. Only the missing factor differs.

Hemophilia A, about 80 percent

Factor VIII is deficient. This is classic hemophilia.

X-linked recessive. Mothers carry it; sons show it.

Severity tracks the factor level: severe under 1 percent, moderate 1 to 5 percent, mild 5 to 40 percent.

Hemophilia B, about 20 percent

Factor IX is deficient. Also called Christmas disease.

Same X-linked pattern as A.

Same clinical picture. You treat it with factor IX instead of VIII.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
A is 8, B is 9
Hemophilia A goes with factor VIII. Hemophilia B goes with factor IX. Line up the letters and the numbers: A, 8; B, 9. Von Willebrand is a third thing; it is the most common inherited bleeding disorder and it hits boys and girls alike.

The joint bleed, stage by stage

Recurrent joints bleed in a pattern; catch it early and you save the cartilage.

  1. 1
    The aura. The child says the joint feels "funny" or "tingly". Nothing shows yet; this is the moment to start factor.
  2. 2
    Acute swelling. The joint turns warm, swollen, and stiff. Pain is severe and movement stops.
  3. 3
    Resolution. With early factor plus RICE, the bleed settles in a day or two.
  4. 4
    Chronic arthropathy. Repeat bleeds wreck the cartilage. The result is early arthritis and lasting joint damage.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Believe the Aura
A child who has bled before knows the feeling before you see it. When he says the knee feels funny, believe him. Start factor and RICE right then. Do not wait for swelling. Early treatment aborts the bleed and spares the joint.
Bottom Line: Hemophilia is deep bleeding from a missing factor: A is VIII, B is IX, both X-linked and in boys. Hallmark is hemarthrosis. Treat the aura, not the swelling.
04

ITP: the surface bleeds

Immune thrombocytopenic purpura is an autoimmune problem. Antibodies coat the platelets and the spleen clears them, so the platelet count drops and the skin bleeds.

The story is almost always the same: a well child, a recent virus, sudden spots.

How it unfolds

  1. 1
    A virus comes first. A cold, a GI bug, a viral rash, sometimes a recent vaccine. It runs its course.
  2. 2
    Antibodies cross-react. The immune response makes antibodies that stick to platelet surfaces.
  3. 3
    The spleen removes them. Coated platelets are pulled from circulation. The count falls fast; the marrow is fine.
  4. 4
    Most of it self-resolves. Childhood ITP usually clears within 6 months. Past 12 months, call it chronic.

What you see

Points to ITP
  • Petechiae and purpura, sudden and widespread
  • Ecchymoses out of proportion to play
  • Gum bleeding, nosebleeds, oral blood blisters
  • A well child: no fever, no bone pain
  • Recent viral illness, one to four weeks back
Points away, toward leukemia
  • Fever, weight loss, night sweats
  • Bone pain
  • Enlarged liver, spleen, or lymph nodes
  • Anemia or an abnormal white count
  • More than platelets is off
Rule out leukemia before you call it ITP

Isolated low platelets in a well child usually is ITP. Leukemia can start the same way. Fever, bone pain, big liver or nodes, or anemia change the picture. Many teams do a marrow study before steroids, because steroids can mask leukemia.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Well Child, Post-Viral
The ITP child looks well apart from the spots. That is the tell. Add fever, bone pain, or a big spleen and you are no longer looking at simple ITP. Sick child with low platelets means work it up harder.
Bottom Line: ITP is post-viral platelet destruction in a well child: petechiae, purpura, isolated low platelets, normal coags. Rule out leukemia. Most of it clears on its own.

The axis that separates them

Long PTT with normal platelets is hemophilia; confirm with the factor VIII or IX level. Low platelets with normal coags is ITP. A long PTT that comes with a long bleeding time and heavy mucosal bleeding points to von Willebrand.

Confirming hemophilia

  • Factor VIII and IX activity levels confirm the diagnosis and grade severity: severe under 1 percent, moderate 1 to 5 percent, mild 5 to 40 percent.
  • Inhibitor screen looks for antibodies against infused factor.
  • Genetic testing supports carrier detection and family planning.
  • Imaging for a head injury or a deep muscle bleed; give factor first.

Confirming ITP

  • CBC with smear shows isolated low platelets with normal cells.
  • History and exam hunt for fever, bone pain, big liver, spleen, or nodes; any of those argues against ITP.
  • Marrow study is not always needed; do it before steroids when the picture is uncertain.
  • Secondary causes to consider: lupus, HIV, H. pylori, a drug.

A bleeding disorder or abuse

Discrimination axis: what the coag panel shows against what the injury looks like.

The two can look alike, so do both

Easy bruising and unexplained bleeding earn both a bleeding workup and an abuse evaluation. The coag panel and a careful history usually split them. Normal platelets, PT, PTT, and factor levels make a bleeding disorder unlikely.

Red flags for abuse: a patterned bruise such as a hand or belt mark, bruises in many stages of healing, injury that does not fit the story or the child's stage, bruising in a baby who cannot yet cruise, and a delay in seeking care. If you are concerned, report it. Nurses are mandatory reporters, and you keep the medical workup going at the same time.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Labs Sort It, Then You Report
Normal coags plus a patterned bruise plus a story that does not fit points to abuse, not disease. You do not have to be certain. Reasonable concern is the trigger. Report it and keep working the child up. Those who cannot cruise rarely bruise.
Bottom Line: Long PTT with low factor is hemophilia. Isolated low platelets is ITP. Long bleeding time with mucosal bleeding is von Willebrand. Unexplained bruising gets a workup and an abuse screen, both.
06

Treatment and the head-injury rule

Hemophilia gets the missing factor back; ITP gets the immune attack blocked. The drugs are different because the problems are different.

Hemophilia treatment

TreatmentUsed forWhat to know
Factor VIII concentrateHemophilia AIV; on-demand for bleeds or prophylaxis 2 to 3 times a week.
Factor IX concentrateHemophilia BIV; same on-demand or prophylaxis approach as factor VIII.
EmicizumabHemophilia A prophylaxisSubcutaneous weekly; mimics factor VIII and cuts bleeds sharply, even with inhibitors.
Desmopressin (DDAVP)Mild hemophilia A, von WillebrandReleases stored factor VIII and vWF. Watch for low sodium and fluid overload.
Aminocaproic / tranexamic acidMouth and nose bleedsPrevents clot breakdown. Avoid in urinary bleeding; the clot can block flow.
Factor first for any head injury

A child with hemophilia who bumps his head gets factor first, then imaging. Even a minor injury, even if he looks fine. Intracranial hemorrhage is the leading cause of death in hemophilia. The few minutes to infuse factor can save the child. Factor first, worry later.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Factor First, Then the Scan
When a stem lists four reasonable actions after a hemophilia head injury, the order is the test. Factor replacement comes before the CT. Not observation, not acetaminophen, not ice. Give factor, then image.

ITP treatment

  1. 1
    Observation. Mild counts and no active bleeding often just need watching and activity limits. Most children recover on their own.
  2. 2
    IVIG. Raises platelets within hours to a day or two by blocking splenic clearance. First-line for real bleeding.
  3. 3
    Anti-D immunoglobulin. An option in Rh-positive children with a spleen. It saturates splenic macrophages.
  4. 4
    Corticosteroids. They work but carry side effects. Rule out leukemia before you start them.
  5. 5
    Platelet transfusion. Only for life-threatening bleeding, given with IVIG or steroids. The antibodies destroy transfused platelets fast.
Why transfusing platelets rarely helps in ITP

The same antibodies that cleared the child's own platelets clear the donor ones just as fast. So a transfusion buys a short, small rise. Reserve it for a life-threatening bleed, and pair it with IVIG or steroids to slow the destruction.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Platelets Do Not Stick in ITP
A stem may offer platelet transfusion as the "obvious" fix for ITP. It is a trap for anything short of a life-threatening bleed. The antibodies eat the new platelets too. IVIG is the fast answer for serious bleeding.

Drugs you never give

  • Aspirin blocks platelet function. Avoid it in both disorders.
  • NSAIDs such as ibuprofen impair platelets and irritate the gut. Use acetaminophen instead.
  • IM injections cause deep bleeding. Give vaccines subcutaneously with firm pressure.
  • Rectal medications and procedures tear fragile mucosa and bleed.
Bottom Line: Hemophilia gets factor, VIII or IX, plus DDAVP for mild A and von Willebrand. ITP gets IVIG or steroids; transfuse only for life-threatening bleeds. Head injury gets factor first. Never give aspirin, NSAIDs, or IM shots.
07

What you do first

Priority questions hand you four safe-looking actions and ask which comes first. You are being tested on order, not on correctness. Here are the rules that decide it.

The ordering rules
  • After a hemophilia head injury, factor comes before imaging.
  • Airway beats everything. A throat or tongue bleed threatens the airway.
  • Direct pressure and factor start now; you do not wait for orders to hold pressure.
  • Stop the bleed before you treat the pain, and never with an NSAID.

Managing an acute bleed

  1. 1
    Direct pressure. Hold any reachable site firmly for 10 minutes without peeking.
  2. 2
    Immobilize and elevate. Rest the joint or limb in a position of comfort; raise it above heart level.
  3. 3
    Cold. Apply ice to the site to slow the bleed by vasoconstriction.
  4. 4
    Give the treatment. Factor for hemophilia, immune therapy for ITP. Get the IV in early.
  5. 5
    Watch for hidden blood loss. Tachycardia and pallor come early; hypotension comes late.

Bleeding precautions, both conditions

  • Soft toothbrush; skip flossing when platelets are low.
  • Electric razor for teens; no straight razors.
  • Pressure for 10 minutes after every stick or injection.
  • No IM injections, no aspirin, no NSAIDs, no rectal procedures.
  • Activity limits: no contact sports; helmets and pads when allowed.
  • Fall precautions: bed low, clutter cleared, path safe.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
SubQ, Not IM
These children get their vaccines subcutaneously, never IM, with the smallest needle and firm pressure after. An IM shot bleeds deep into muscle. Some clinics time the shot for right after a factor infusion.
Bottom Line: Pressure for 10 minutes, immobilize, ice, then give factor or immune therapy. Soft brush, electric razor, SubQ shots, no aspirin or NSAIDs. Head trauma means factor first; a throat bleed means airway first.
08

What families need to hear

Most of this care happens at home, and the teaching is what keeps the child safe between visits.

Home-care priorities

  • The medication plan: how and when to infuse factor at home, and the prophylaxis schedule.
  • Catch bleeds early: a tingling joint in hemophilia, new petechiae or bruising in ITP.
  • Read every label: aspirin and NSAIDs hide in cold and flu products.
  • Daily habits: soft brush, electric razor, protective gear.
  • A MedicAlert bracelet naming the condition.
  • Dental care with a team that knows the diagnosis; some procedures need factor first.

Activity guidance

EncourageAvoid or limit
Swimming, biking with a helmet, walking, golf, doubles tennis Football, hockey, wrestling, boxing; high-impact contact sports
Supervised, age-appropriate play Trampolines and climbing for young children with hemophilia
School 504 plan and PE modifications Skating or skateboarding without pads and a helmet

When to call the center or go to the ED

  • Any head injury, whatever it looks like.
  • Severe or worsening joint or muscle pain and swelling.
  • Bleeding past 15 to 20 minutes despite steady pressure.
  • Blood in vomit, stool, or urine.
  • Sudden severe belly, back, chest, or neck pain.
  • A bad headache, vision change, or altered behavior.
The prognosis is good, and you can say so

Hemophilia once meant disability and a short life. With prophylactic factor and drugs like emicizumab, many children now have few bleeds and full lives. Most childhood ITP clears on its own. Say this to families; realistic hope keeps them adherent.

Bottom Line: Teach the med plan, early bleed signs, label reading, protective gear, and a MedicAlert bracelet. Head injury or uncontrolled bleeding means the ED. Modern therapy allows a largely normal life.
