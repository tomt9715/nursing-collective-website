Understanding the Pleural Space

Before managing chest tubes, you must understand the pleural space and why maintaining negative pressure is essential for breathing. This foundation explains everything about how chest tubes work.

Normal Pleural Anatomy

  • Visceral Pleura: Thin membrane covering the lung surface
  • Parietal Pleura: Thin membrane lining the chest wall
  • Pleural Space: Potential space between the two layers containing a thin layer of serous fluid (~10-20 mL)
  • Pleural Fluid: Lubricates the pleural surfaces, allowing lungs to glide during breathing
Key Concept: Negative Pressure

The pleural space normally has negative pressure (about -4 to -8 cm H2O). This negative pressure:

  • Keeps the lungs inflated against the chest wall
  • Creates the suction that allows lungs to expand during inspiration
  • Is maintained by the balance between elastic recoil of the lungs and chest wall

When this negative pressure is lost (air or fluid enters), the lung collapses!

What Disrupts Normal Pleural Pressure?

Condition What Enters Pleural Space Result
Pneumothorax Air Lung collapse; loss of negative pressure
Hemothorax Blood Lung compression; hypovolemia
Pleural Effusion Fluid (serous, purulent, chylous) Lung compression; respiratory distress
Empyema Pus (infected fluid) Lung compression; sepsis risk
Tension Pneumothorax Air (one-way valve effect) Mediastinal shift; cardiovascular collapse
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Why Negative Pressure Matters
Think of the pleural space like a vacuum seal! Imagine two pieces of glass with a thin layer of water between them - they're nearly impossible to pull apart. That's how the visceral and parietal pleura work together. The negative pressure and thin fluid layer keep the lung "stuck" to the chest wall. When you breathe in and your chest expands, the lung HAS to expand with it because of this seal. But if air or fluid gets into that space, the seal breaks, the lung can fall away from the chest wall, and it collapses. The chest tube's job is to remove whatever broke the seal and restore that negative pressure.

Indications for Chest Tubes

Chest tubes are inserted to drain air, blood, or fluid from the pleural space and restore negative pressure. Understanding the indication helps you anticipate what you'll see in the drainage system.

Pneumothorax

Types

  • Spontaneous: No trauma; often tall, thin males; ruptured bleb
  • Traumatic: Penetrating or blunt chest injury
  • Iatrogenic: Post-procedure (central line, thoracentesis, biopsy)
  • Tension: EMERGENCY - one-way valve, mediastinal shift

Chest Tube Expectations

  • Air leak initially (bubbling in water seal)
  • Minimal fluid drainage
  • Placed anteriorly (2nd-3rd ICS)
Hemothorax

Causes

  • Trauma: Rib fractures, penetrating injury
  • Post-surgical: After thoracic surgery
  • Vascular: Aortic rupture, vessel injury
  • Malignancy: Tumor erosion

Chest Tube Expectations

  • Bloody drainage (may be large volume)
  • No air leak typically
  • Placed posteriorly/laterally (5th-6th ICS)

Other Indications

Indication Description What to Expect
Pleural Effusion Fluid accumulation (heart failure, cancer, infection) Serous or serosanguinous fluid; variable volume
Empyema Infected pleural fluid (pneumonia complication) Purulent, thick drainage; may need fibrinolytics
Chylothorax Lymphatic fluid leak (thoracic duct injury) Milky white drainage; high triglycerides
Post-Thoracic Surgery Prophylactic after lung resection, cardiac surgery Serosanguinous; air leak if lung resection
NCLEX Alert: Chest Tube Placement

Location matters!

  • Air rises: Pneumothorax tubes placed HIGH (2nd-3rd intercostal space, midclavicular)
  • Fluid sinks: Hemothorax/effusion tubes placed LOW (5th-6th intercostal space, midaxillary)

If the question asks about tube position, think about what's being drained!

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Pneumothorax (air), hemothorax (blood), pleural effusion (fluid), empyema (pus), post-thoracic surgery. Tension pneumothorax requires immediate needle decompression BEFORE chest tube.

Chest Drainage Systems

Modern chest drainage systems combine three chambers into one unit. Understanding each chamber's function is essential for troubleshooting and safe management.

The Three-Chamber System

Chamber Functions

1
Collection Chamber

Receives drainage from the pleural space. Calibrated to measure output. Monitor amount, color, and consistency of drainage. Located closest to the patient tubing.

2
Water Seal Chamber

Acts as a ONE-WAY VALVE. Allows air to exit pleural space but prevents air from entering. Contains 2 cm of water. This is where you assess for air leaks and tidaling!

3
Suction Control Chamber

Controls amount of negative pressure applied. Can be wet (water level) or dry (dial/regulator). Typically set at -20 cm H2O. Not always used - may be water seal only.

Wet vs Dry Suction Systems

Wet Suction

How It Works

  • Water level determines suction amount
  • Gentle bubbling when suction working
  • Fill to prescribed level (usually 20 cm)

Characteristics

  • Continuous gentle bubbling is NORMAL
  • Vigorous bubbling = suction too high
  • Water evaporates - check levels regularly
  • Quieter than you'd expect
Dry Suction

How It Works

  • Dial or regulator sets suction level
  • Bellows or indicator shows suction active
  • No water in suction chamber

Characteristics

  • Quieter operation
  • No evaporation concerns
  • Easier to set precise suction
  • Check bellows/indicator for function
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Water Seal is Your Safety Net
The water seal chamber is the most critical part of the system! It acts like a one-way valve: air can bubble OUT through the water, but air can't get back IN because the tube is submerged. This protects the patient even if suction fails or is disconnected. Key points: The water seal should ALWAYS have water (typically 2 cm). If the water seal chamber goes dry, air can enter the pleural space and collapse the lung! Never add water to the water seal beyond the fill line. The water seal is where you look for tidaling (normal) and air leaks (bubbling).

System Setup Quick Reference

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Three-chamber system: collection (drainage), water-seal (one-way valve), suction control. Water-seal chamber must have 2 cm water. Suction control maintains â€“20 cmH2O. Keep system upright and below chest.

Tidaling vs Air Leaks

Understanding what you're seeing in the water seal chamber is one of the most important chest tube skills. The difference between tidaling and an air leak determines your nursing actions.

Tidaling (NORMAL)

What You See

  • Water level rises and falls with breathing
  • Rises with inspiration (spontaneous breathing)
  • Falls with expiration
  • NO continuous bubbling

What It Means

  • System is patent and functioning
  • Chest tube is in pleural space
  • Normal pressure changes transmitted
  • This is GOOD - document and continue monitoring
Air Leak (ASSESS!)

What You See

  • Continuous bubbling in water seal
  • Bubbling with each breath (intermittent)
  • May be constant or only with coughing
  • Bubbling intensity may vary

What It Means

  • Air is entering the system somewhere
  • Could be from patient OR from system leak
  • Must determine source!
  • Expected post-op but should decrease over time
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Tidaling vs Air Leak - The Key Distinction
Tidaling = gentle rise and fall. Air leak = bubbles! Think of it this way: Tidaling is like watching a gentle wave in the water - the fluid level moves up and down with breathing but there are no bubbles. This shows the system is connected to the pleural space and working. An air leak produces actual bubbles in the water seal - you'll see them rising through the water. Intermittent bubbling with breathing can be normal after pneumothorax or lung surgery (air escaping from the lung). But continuous bubbling that doesn't change with breathing often means there's a leak in the system itself that needs to be found and fixed!

Troubleshooting Air Leaks

Finding the Source of an Air Leak

1
Check all connections - Ensure all tubing connections are tight and secure. This is the most common source of system leaks.
2
Inspect tubing - Look for cracks, holes, or damage in the tubing from the patient to the collection system.
3
Check insertion site - Examine the dressing and occlusive seal. Air can enter around the tube at the chest wall.
4
Momentarily occlude tubing - Briefly pinch the tubing close to the patient. If bubbling stops, leak is at patient (expected with pneumothorax). If it continues, leak is in the system.
5
Document and report - Note the size of the air leak (small, moderate, large), when it occurs (continuous vs with breathing/coughing), and any changes from baseline.
When to Be Concerned About Air Leaks
  • New air leak that wasn't present before
  • Increasing air leak that's getting larger over time
  • Air leak persisting >5-7 days post-operatively
  • Large continuous leak with respiratory distress
  • Air leak with subcutaneous emphysema (crepitus around site)

When Tidaling STOPS

NCLEX Priority: No Tidaling = Problem!

If tidaling stops, it means one of two things:

  • GOOD: The lung has fully re-expanded and the tube can potentially be removed
  • BAD: The system is obstructed (kinked tubing, clot, dependent loop)

Action: Check the system for obstruction first! Trace tubing from patient to collection system. If no obstruction found, notify provider - may need chest X-ray to confirm lung expansion before removal.

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Tidaling = water fluctuates with breathing = patent tube (NORMAL). Absence of tidaling = obstruction, kink, or full lung re-expansion. Continuous bubbling in water-seal = air leak â€” trace it from patient to system.

Systematic Assessment

Chest tube assessment should be performed at least every 4 hours (more frequently for unstable patients). A systematic approach ensures nothing is missed.

The DOPE Assessment

Memory Aid: DOPE

Use DOPE to remember your chest tube assessment:

  • Drainage - Amount, color, consistency
  • Occlusive dressing - Intact, dry, no air leak at site
  • Patency - No kinks, clots, or dependent loops; tidaling present
  • Equipment - Water seal level, suction setting, system upright and below chest

Complete Assessment Checklist

Assessment Components

1
Patient Assessment

Respiratory rate, oxygen saturation, breath sounds bilaterally, work of breathing, pain level, subcutaneous emphysema (crepitus)

2
Insertion Site

Dressing intact and occlusive, no drainage around tube, sutures intact, no signs of infection (redness, swelling, purulent drainage)

3
Tubing

No kinks or dependent loops, connections secure, tube secured to patient (taped), no clots visible in tubing

4
Drainage System

Upright and below chest level, water seal at 2 cm, suction at prescribed level, tidaling present (or document absence), note air leak if present

5
Drainage Output

Mark time and level on collection chamber, calculate hourly output for acute situations, note color and consistency changes

Drainage Characteristics

Drainage Type Appearance Common Causes Action
Serous Clear, straw-colored Pleural effusion, post-operative Monitor amount; expected finding
Serosanguinous Pink to light red Post-operative, trauma healing Normal post-op; monitor trends
Sanguinous (Bloody) Bright red Active bleeding, hemothorax Monitor closely; report if >100 mL/hr
Purulent Thick, yellow-green, cloudy Empyema, infection Notify provider; may need cultures
Chylous Milky white Thoracic duct injury Notify provider; dietary modification
Critical Drainage Amounts - Notify Provider!
  • >100 mL/hour of bloody drainage for 3+ consecutive hours
  • >200 mL immediately after insertion (massive hemothorax)
  • Sudden increase in drainage rate
  • Change in character (clear to bloody, serous to purulent)
  • No drainage when expected (may indicate obstruction)
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Assess every 1â€“2 hours: insertion site (subcutaneous emphysema, drainage), tubing (kinks, dependent loops), water-seal (tidaling, bubbling), drainage (amount, color, rate), respiratory status (bilateral breath sounds, SpO2).

When to Clamp Chest Tubes

This is one of the most commonly tested concepts and most frequently misunderstood aspects of chest tube care. The short answer: Almost never clamp a chest tube!

NEVER Clamp a Chest Tube If...

  • There is an active air leak - Clamping traps air in the pleural space, causing tension pneumothorax!
  • The patient has a pneumothorax - Same reason - risk of tension pneumothorax
  • During routine transport - Keep the system open; just keep it below chest level
  • To check for lung re-expansion - This is done by water seal trial, NOT clamping
  • When you're unsure - When in doubt, DON'T clamp!
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Golden Rule: Don't Clamp Air Leaks!
If there's an air leak, NEVER clamp the chest tube! Here's why this is so dangerous: If air is leaking from the lung into the pleural space and you clamp the tube, that air has nowhere to go. It keeps accumulating, pressure builds up, and you create a tension pneumothorax - a life-threatening emergency where the pressure shifts the mediastinum and compresses the heart. The whole point of the chest tube is to let that air ESCAPE. Remember: air leak + clamp = potential tension pneumothorax. This is a critical NCLEX concept!

The ONLY Times to Clamp

Acceptable Reasons to Clamp
  • Changing the drainage system - Brief clamping (seconds to minutes) while switching to new system. Have new system ready FIRST!
  • Locating source of air leak - Momentary clamping to determine if leak is from patient or system
  • Assessing for removal readiness - ONLY if ordered by provider, ONLY if no air leak present, and with close monitoring
  • Accidental disconnection - Clamp briefly while reconnecting or placing tube in sterile water

Key principle: Any clamping should be BRIEF and the patient should be monitored closely!

What to Do If the System Breaks or Disconnects

Emergency: System Disconnection

1
Don't panic! The water seal provides some protection even if suction is lost.
2
If tube disconnects from system: Submerge the end of the chest tube in a container of sterile water or saline (about 2 cm deep) to create a temporary water seal.
3
If no sterile water available: Brief clamping (use padded clamps) while obtaining new system - but only for moments!
4
Reconnect or replace system as quickly as possible. Never leave a chest tube clamped for extended periods.
5
Assess the patient immediately. Check respiratory status, breath sounds, oxygen saturation.
NCLEX Priority: Tube Comes Out!

If the chest tube is accidentally pulled out:

  • Immediately cover the site with a sterile occlusive dressing (petroleum gauze)
  • Tape on THREE sides only - this creates a flutter valve
  • The open side allows air to escape during expiration but seals during inspiration
  • Call for help, monitor patient closely, prepare for possible reinsertion
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: NEVER routinely clamp a chest tube â€” risk of tension pneumothorax. Only clamp briefly to locate an air leak, change the system, or as specifically ordered. If in doubt, DON'T clamp.

Complications & Emergencies

Recognizing and responding quickly to chest tube complications can be life-saving. Know the signs and immediate interventions.

Tension Pneumothorax

EMERGENCY: Tension Pneumothorax

This is a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate intervention!

Signs:

  • Severe respiratory distress, tachypnea
  • Tracheal deviation AWAY from affected side
  • Absent breath sounds on affected side
  • Hypotension, tachycardia
  • Jugular venous distension (JVD)
  • Cyanosis

Immediate Action: If chest tube is clamped - UNCLAMP IMMEDIATELY! Call for help. Prepare for needle decompression or chest tube insertion. This is a clinical diagnosis - don't wait for X-ray!

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Tension Pneumothorax - Act Fast!
Tension pneumothorax = obstructive shock from trapped air! Here's what's happening: Air enters the pleural space but can't escape (one-way valve effect). Pressure builds up, collapses the lung completely, then pushes the mediastinum to the opposite side. This compresses the heart and great vessels, causing cardiovascular collapse. Classic signs to remember: tracheal deviation (away from the affected side), absent breath sounds (affected side), JVD (blood can't return to heart), and hypotension. Treatment is IMMEDIATE decompression - this is the one time you might see a needle stuck in someone's chest (2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line).

Other Complications

Complication Signs Nursing Action
Subcutaneous Emphysema Crepitus (crackling under skin), swelling around insertion site, may extend to neck/face Mark borders with pen; monitor for spread; notify provider; check tube patency
Tube Obstruction Loss of tidaling, no drainage when expected, respiratory distress Check for kinks/clots; may need to milk tubing (if ordered); never irrigate without order
Tube Dislodgement Tube visibly out of chest, air leak at site, respiratory distress Cover site with occlusive dressing (3 sides); call for help; monitor vitals
Infection Fever, redness/swelling at site, purulent drainage, elevated WBC Maintain sterile technique; notify provider; may need antibiotics
Re-expansion Pulmonary Edema Cough, dyspnea, pink frothy sputum after lung re-expands (rare) Occurs with rapid drainage of large effusion; supportive care; O2, possible intubation

Milking vs Stripping Tubing

Current Evidence on Tube Manipulation

Traditional practice: "Strip" or "milk" chest tubes to prevent clots

Current evidence: Routine stripping is NOT recommended!

  • Stripping creates very high negative pressures (-400 cm H2O) that can damage lung tissue
  • Milking (gentle squeezing) creates less pressure but still controversial
  • Only perform if specifically ordered and clot obstruction is suspected
  • Better approach: Keep tubing free of dependent loops; may gently lift tubing to facilitate drainage
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Watch for tension pneumothorax (tracheal deviation, absent breath sounds, hypotension), infection at site, subcutaneous emphysema (crepitus), and re-expansion pulmonary edema (rapid drainage).

Chest Tube Removal

Chest tube removal is a provider procedure, but nurses play a key role in preparation, assistance, and post-removal monitoring.

Criteria for Removal

Criterion Typical Threshold
Drainage Amount <150-200 mL in 24 hours (varies by institution)
Air Leak No air leak for 24+ hours
Chest X-Ray Lung fully expanded; no residual pneumothorax or significant effusion
Clinical Status Stable respiratory status; no respiratory distress
Water Seal Trial May be placed on water seal (no suction) for several hours to assess tolerance before removal

Nursing Role in Removal

Before, During & After Removal

1
Pre-Procedure

Explain procedure to patient. Pre-medicate for pain as ordered (30 min before). Gather supplies: suture removal kit, occlusive dressing (petroleum gauze), tape. Position patient (usually sitting at edge of bed or supine).

2
During Procedure

Instruct patient to take deep breath, hold it, and bear down (Valsalva maneuver) OR exhale during removal - prevents air entry. Provider removes tube in one quick motion. Immediately apply occlusive dressing.

3
Post-Procedure

Monitor respiratory status closely for first several hours. Check dressing for drainage or air leak. Auscultate breath sounds. Watch for signs of recurrent pneumothorax. Chest X-ray usually obtained 1-2 hours post-removal.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Why Valsalva During Removal?
The Valsalva maneuver (or exhaling) during chest tube removal prevents air from entering the pleural space! When the tube is pulled out, there's a brief moment where the hole in the chest wall is open. If the patient inhales at that moment, negative pressure could suck air into the pleural space, causing a pneumothorax. By having the patient hold their breath and bear down (or exhale), intrathoracic pressure increases, pushing air OUT rather than allowing it in. The occlusive dressing is applied immediately after to seal the site. The dressing should be left in place for 48-72 hours.
Post-Removal Warning Signs - Notify Provider!
  • Increasing dyspnea or respiratory distress
  • Decreased oxygen saturation
  • Diminished breath sounds on affected side
  • Subcutaneous emphysema developing or worsening
  • Significant drainage from site
  • Tracheal deviation (tension pneumothorax)
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Remove when: air leak resolved, drainage minimal (< 150 mL/24h), lung re-expanded on CXR. Patient performs Valsalva or exhales during removal. Apply petroleum gauze dressing immediately.
