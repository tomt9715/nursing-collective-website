Chest Tubes

One chamber you actually watch, one movement that is normal and one that is not, and the single instruction that turns a chest tube into a tension pneumothorax.

Reading Time
18-22 minutes
Difficulty Level
Intermediate

The Nursing Collective

By the end, without notes

  • Tell tidaling from an air leak by what you see in the water seal
  • Say the three situations where clamping is allowed, and why every other one is dangerous
  • Say what tidaling stopping might mean, and which possibility you rule out first
  • State the immediate action for a disconnection and for a tube that has come out
  • Recognise a tension pneumothorax from four findings and act before the x-ray
01

What actually gets you

Chest tubes look mechanical. Three chambers, some water, a suction dial. Students learn the apparatus and then miss the questions anyway.

The questions are not about the apparatus. They are about what you do when something changes. Points get lost here in three predictable ways.

You reached for the clamp

Clamping feels like control. On a patient with an air leak it can kill them.

Air leaving the lung now has nowhere to go. It accumulates. Pressure builds. You have manufactured a tension pneumothorax.

You saw movement in the water seal and could not name it

Two different things happen in that chamber. The water level rising and falling is tidaling, and it is normal. Bubbles rising through the water are an air leak, and they need a source.

One means the system is working. The other means air is getting in somewhere. Confusing them means the wrong action every time.

You treated "no tidaling" as good news

Tidaling stopping means one of two opposite things. The lung has fully re-expanded, which is excellent. Or the system is obstructed, which is not.

You cannot tell from the chamber alone. Rule out the obstruction first, because that is the one that hurts the patient.

Bottom Line: Learn this as a set of decisions, not a diagram. What is moving in the water seal, what changed, and what do you do about it.
02

The four things

For every situation below, you should be able to say four things with the book closed: what you are looking at, what it means, the priority nursing action, and the mistake that makes it worse.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

What you see What it means Priority action The mistake
Tidaling Water rises and falls with breathing. The system is patent and in the pleural space. Document it and carry on. Calling it an air leak and chasing a problem that is not there.
Continuous bubbling Air is entering the system. From the patient, or from a leak in the equipment. Find the source. Work from the patient outward. Clamping the tube to make it stop.
Tidaling stops Either the lung re-expanded, or the tube is obstructed. Trace the tubing for kinks, clots and dependent loops first. Assuming the good version and documenting improvement.
Tube pulled out The pleural space is open to the air. Occlusive dressing taped on three sides, then call. Sealing all four sides, which turns it into a tension pneumothorax.
Copy this down

Write these four rows on one index card, by hand. Movement without bubbles is good. Bubbles need a source. No movement needs investigating. An open chest gets three sides of tape. That card is most of the exam.

03

Why the lung stays up

The pleural space is a potential space between two membranes. The visceral pleura covers the lung. The parietal pleura lines the chest wall. Between them sits 10 to 20 mL of serous fluid.

That space holds a negative pressure of about minus 4 to minus 8 cm H2O. The negative pressure is what keeps the lung expanded against the chest wall.

The one idea the whole topic rests on

Two sheets of glass with water between them will not pull apart. The pleura works the same way.

The seal is why the lung follows the chest wall outward on every breath. Let air or fluid in and the seal breaks. The lung falls away and collapses.

A chest tube removes whatever broke the seal. That is its entire job.

ConditionWhat got inWhat happens
PneumothoraxAirThe lung collapses as the negative pressure is lost
HemothoraxBloodThe lung is compressed, and the patient loses volume
Pleural effusionSerous, purulent or chylous fluidThe lung is compressed and breathing becomes difficult
EmpyemaPusCompression plus a source of sepsis
Tension pneumothoraxAir, with a one-way valveThe mediastinum shifts and circulation fails
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Seal Is the Whole Thing
The lung does not inflate itself. It is held open by the vacuum between two membranes. Anything that gets into that space breaks the vacuum. Every chest tube question comes back to restoring it.
Bottom Line: The pleural space holds a negative pressure that keeps the lung expanded. Air or fluid in that space collapses the lung. The tube takes it back out.
04

Air or fluid, high or low

Discrimination axis: air rises and fluid sinks, so the tube goes where the problem collects.

What is being drained tells you where the tube sits. It also tells you what to expect in the chamber.

Pneumothorax: air, so the tube goes high

Placed at the 2nd or 3rd intercostal space, midclavicular line.

Types:

  • Spontaneous. No trauma. Classically a tall thin young man with a ruptured bleb.
  • Traumatic. Penetrating or blunt chest injury.
  • Iatrogenic. After a central line, thoracentesis or biopsy.
  • Tension. An emergency. A one-way valve shifts the mediastinum.

Expect: an air leak at first, and very little fluid.

Hemothorax: blood, so the tube goes low

Placed at the 5th or 6th intercostal space, midaxillary line.

Causes:

  • Trauma. Rib fractures, penetrating injury.
  • Surgical. After thoracic surgery.
  • Vascular. Aortic rupture or vessel injury.
  • Malignancy. Tumour eroding into a vessel.

Expect: bloody drainage, sometimes a lot of it, and usually no air leak.

The rest of the list

IndicationWhat it isWhat the drainage looks like
Pleural effusion Fluid from heart failure, cancer or infection Serous or serosanguineous, variable volume
Empyema Infected pleural fluid, usually after pneumonia Thick and purulent. May need fibrinolytics
Chylothorax Lymph leaking from an injured thoracic duct Milky white, high in triglycerides
After thoracic surgery Placed prophylactically after resection or cardiac surgery Serosanguineous, with an air leak after a lung resection
Tension pneumothorax comes before the tube

A tension pneumothorax is decompressed with a needle. Second intercostal space, midclavicular line. It happens immediately.

The chest tube comes afterwards. Waiting for a tube in a patient whose mediastinum has already shifted is waiting too long.

Bottom Line: Air rises, so pneumothorax tubes go high. Fluid sinks, so hemothorax and effusion tubes go low. Tension pneumothorax gets a needle first.
05

Three chambers, one that matters

The unit at the bedside is three chambers in one box. You will be asked about the middle one. Far more than the other two.

  1. 1
    Collection chamber. Closest to the patient. It receives and measures the drainage. You watch amount, colour and consistency here.
  2. 2
    Water seal chamber. A one-way valve made of water. Air can bubble out through it; nothing can get back in, because the tube is submerged. It holds 2 cm of water. Tidaling and air leaks are both assessed here.
  3. 3
    Suction control chamber. Sets how much negative pressure is applied, usually minus 20 cm H2O. Wet systems use a water column; dry systems use a dial. Not every patient is on suction.
The water seal must never run dry

Two centimetres of water is what stands between the pleural space and the room. If that chamber dries out, air can travel back up the tube and collapse the lung.

It protects the patient even when suction fails or is disconnected. That is why water seal alone is safe. Do not fill above the line either.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Water Seal Is the Safety Net
Suction is optional. The water seal is not. Two centimetres of water lets air out and never lets it back. Check the level every shift, because water evaporates.

Wet and dry suction

Wet suction

The height of the water column sets the suction, usually 20 cm.

  • Gentle continuous bubbling here is normal and expected.
  • Vigorous bubbling means the wall suction is set too high.
  • The water evaporates, so the level needs checking.

Bubbling in the suction chamber is normal. Bubbling in the water seal is not the same thing.

Dry suction

A dial sets the level and a bellows or indicator confirms it is running.

  • No water in this chamber, so nothing evaporates.
  • Quieter, and easier to set precisely.
  • Check the indicator rather than listening for bubbles.

The absence of bubbling here tells you nothing is wrong.

The setup, as numbers
  • Water seal: 2 cm H2O.
  • Suction: minus 20 cm H2O in most orders.
  • Position: upright, and always below chest level.
  • Tubing: no kinks, no loops, no dependent coils.

Below chest level is not a preference. Raise the unit above the insertion site and drainage runs backward into the pleural space.

Bottom Line: Collection measures, water seal protects, suction controls. Two centimetres in the water seal, minus 20 on the suction, and the whole unit stays upright and below the chest.
06

Movement or bubbles

Discrimination axis: the water level moving, versus air passing through the water.

This is the most tested pair in the topic. Both happen in the same chamber and they mean opposite things.

Tidaling: normal

What you see: the water level rises and falls with each breath. Up on inspiration in a spontaneously breathing patient, down on expiration. No bubbles.

What it means: the tube is patent and sitting in the pleural space. Pressure changes are getting through.

What you do: document it and keep monitoring. This is the finding you want.

Air leak: assess it

What you see: actual bubbles rising through the water seal. It may be constant, or only on coughing.

What it means: air is entering the system. Either from the patient's lung, or from a break in the equipment.

What you do: find out which. That is the whole task.

The axis that separates them

Movement of water versus air through water.

Intermittent bubbling that comes and goes with the breath is often expected. It follows a pneumothorax or a lung resection. That is the patient's own air leaving, which is the point of the tube.

Continuous bubbling that does not change with the breath is different. That leak is usually in the equipment, not the patient.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Waves, Not Bubbles
Tidaling is a wave. An air leak is bubbles. If the level moves and nothing rises through it, the system is doing its job. If you can see bubbles, something is letting air in and you need to find out where.

Finding the leak

Work outward from the patient. The most common source is the least dramatic one.

  1. 1
    Check the connections. Every junction from patient to unit. Loose connections are the most common system leak.
  2. 2
    Inspect the tubing. Look for cracks, holes and damage along its whole length.
  3. 3
    Check the insertion site. Air can be drawn in around the tube if the dressing has lifted.
  4. 4
    Occlude briefly, close to the patient. If the bubbling stops, the leak is coming from the patient, which is expected with a pneumothorax. If it continues, the leak is downstream in the system. Momentary only.
  5. 5
    Document and report. Note the size, when it happens, and whether it has changed from the last shift. The trend is what the team acts on.
Air leaks that need reporting
  • A new leak that was not there before
  • A leak that is getting bigger
  • A leak still present 5 to 7 days after surgery
  • A large continuous leak with respiratory distress
  • A leak with subcutaneous emphysema, meaning crepitus around the site

When the tidaling stops

Two opposite meanings

The good one: the lung has fully re-expanded, and the tube may be ready to come out.

The bad one: the system is obstructed by a kink, a clot, or a dependent loop.

You cannot tell them apart by looking at the chamber. Trace the tubing from patient to unit first. If nothing is obstructed, notify the provider. Expect a chest x-ray to confirm the lung is up.

Rule out the dangerous one before you document the reassuring one.

Bottom Line: Tidaling is movement and it is normal. Bubbles are an air leak and need a source. No tidaling means obstruction until you have traced the tubing and proved otherwise.
07

Assessing it, in order

Assess at least every 4 hours, and more often if the patient is unstable. Work the same way every time so nothing is skipped.

DOPE
  • Drainage. Amount, colour, consistency.
  • Occlusive dressing. Intact, dry, sealed at the site.
  • Patency. No kinks, clots or dependent loops. Tidaling present.
  • Equipment. Water seal level, suction setting, unit upright and below the chest.
  1. 1
    The patient first. Respiratory rate, saturation, breath sounds on both sides, work of breathing, pain, and crepitus around the site.
  2. 2
    The insertion site. Dressing intact and occlusive, no drainage tracking around the tube, sutures intact, no redness or purulence.
  3. 3
    The tubing. No kinks, no dependent loops, connections secure, tube taped to the patient, no visible clots.
  4. 4
    The unit. Upright and below chest level, water seal at 2 cm, suction at the ordered level, tidaling present or its absence documented.
  5. 5
    The output. Mark the time and level on the chamber. Work out the hourly rate when the situation is acute. Note any change in colour.

Reading the drainage

TypeWhat it looks likeWhere it comes fromWhat you do
SerousClear, straw colouredPleural effusion, or routine post-opMonitor the volume. Expected.
SerosanguineousPink to light redPost-operative, healing traumaNormal after surgery. Watch the trend.
SanguineousBright redActive bleeding, hemothoraxMonitor closely. Report above 100 mL per hour.
PurulentThick, yellow-green, cloudyEmpyema or infectionNotify the provider. Cultures likely.
ChylousMilky whiteThoracic duct injuryNotify the provider. Diet is usually modified.

The expected progression after surgery is bloody, then pink, then straw coloured. Drainage that goes the other way is a finding.

Drainage that gets the provider called
  • More than 100 mL per hour of bloody drainage for 3 hours running
  • More than 200 mL immediately after insertion, which suggests a massive hemothorax
  • A sudden increase in the rate
  • A change in character, clear to bloody or serous to purulent
  • No drainage at all when you were expecting some, which points at obstruction
Bottom Line: Patient, site, tubing, unit, output. Every 4 hours, more if unstable. Over 100 mL per hour of blood for 3 hours gets reported, and so does no drainage at all.
08

Almost never clamp

This is the most tested decision in the topic. The answer is nearly always the same. Do not clamp.

Never clamp when
  • There is an active air leak. The trapped air becomes a tension pneumothorax.
  • The patient has a pneumothorax. Same mechanism, same result.
  • You are moving the patient. Leave the system open and keep it below chest level.
  • You want to test whether the lung stayed up. That is a water seal trial, not a clamp.
  • You are not certain. When in doubt, do not clamp.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Air Leak Plus Clamp Equals Tension
If air is leaking from the lung and you clamp the tube, the air has nowhere to go. It accumulates, the pressure shifts the mediastinum, and the heart is compressed. The tube exists to let that air out. Clamping closes the exit.

The three times you may

Acceptable, and briefly
  • Changing the drainage unit. Seconds, with the new system already open and ready.
  • Locating an air leak. A momentary occlusion to tell patient from system.
  • An accidental disconnection. Briefly, while you reconnect or set up sterile water.

A provider may also order a clamping trial before removal. Only when there is no air leak. Any clamp is brief, and the patient is watched throughout.

When the system comes apart

  1. 1
    Do not panic. The water seal is a physical valve. Losing suction is not the same as losing protection.
  2. 2
    If the tube disconnects from the unit, put the end into sterile water or saline, about 2 cm deep. You have just rebuilt the water seal.
  3. 3
    If there is no sterile water to hand, clamp briefly with padded clamps while you get a new system. Moments, not minutes.
  4. 4
    Reconnect or replace as fast as you can. Never leave a chest tube clamped and walk away.
  5. 5
    Assess the patient. Respiratory status, breath sounds, saturation. The equipment is fixed; now check the person.
If the tube comes out of the chest

Cover the site immediately with a sterile occlusive dressing, usually petroleum gauze.

Tape it on three sides only. That makes a flutter valve. Air can escape on expiration through the open side, and the dressing seals against inspiration.

Tape all four sides and you have built the one-way valve that causes a tension pneumothorax. Then call for help and prepare for reinsertion.

Bottom Line: Do not clamp. The exceptions are a system change, a momentary leak hunt, and a disconnection. Tube out means occlusive dressing taped on three sides.
09

What goes wrong

Tension pneumothorax

Discrimination axis: air trapped with no way out, so the pressure pushes the mediastinum across.

Four findings and an action
  • Tracheal deviation away from the affected side
  • Absent breath sounds on the affected side
  • Jugular venous distension, because blood cannot return to the heart
  • Hypotension with tachycardia

Add severe distress, tachypnea and cyanosis. This is a clinical diagnosis, so do not wait for an x-ray.

If the tube is clamped, unclamp it now. Call for help. Prepare for needle decompression at the 2nd intercostal space, midclavicular line.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
This Is Obstructive Shock
Trapped air collapses the lung, then pushes the mediastinum across and compresses the heart and great vessels. That is why the blood pressure falls and the neck veins fill. Treating it means letting the air out, immediately.

The rest

ComplicationWhat you findWhat you do
Subcutaneous emphysema Crepitus under the skin, swelling at the site, sometimes spreading to neck and face Mark the borders with a pen so spread can be measured. Check tube patency. Notify the provider.
Obstruction Tidaling stops, no drainage when you expected some, respiratory distress Look for kinks and clots. Milk the tubing only if ordered. Never irrigate without an order.
Dislodgement Tube visibly out, air leaking at the site, distress Occlusive dressing taped on three sides. Call for help. Monitor.
Infection Fever, redness and swelling at the site, purulent drainage, rising white count Sterile technique on every dressing change. Notify the provider.
Re-expansion pulmonary edema Cough, dyspnea and pink frothy sputum just after the lung comes back up. Rare. Happens when a large effusion is drained too fast. Supportive care, oxygen, sometimes intubation.
Do not strip the tubing

Stripping a chest tube was standard practice for decades. It is no longer recommended.

Stripping generates negative pressures around minus 400 cm H2O, which is enough to injure lung tissue. Milking, meaning gentle squeezing, generates less but is still debated.

Do it only if it is specifically ordered and a clot is suspected. The better routine is keeping the tubing free of dependent loops in the first place.

Bottom Line: Tension pneumothorax is tracheal deviation, absent breath sounds, JVD and hypotension, treated before the x-ray. Mark subcutaneous emphysema so you can measure it. Do not strip the tubing.
10

Taking it out

The provider removes the tube. Your job is three things. The preparation, the breath at the right moment, and the hours afterwards.

CriterionThreshold
DrainageUnder 150 to 200 mL in 24 hours, depending on the institution
Air leakNone for 24 hours or more
Chest x-rayLung fully expanded, no residual pneumothorax or significant effusion
Clinical statusStable respiratory status, no distress
Water seal trialOften placed on water seal without suction for some hours first, to see if the lung holds
  1. 1
    Before. Explain what will happen. Pre-medicate for pain about 30 minutes ahead. Gather the suture removal kit, petroleum gauze and tape. Position the patient, usually sitting at the edge of the bed or supine.
  2. 2
    During. Have the patient take a deep breath, hold it and bear down, which is the Valsalva manoeuvre. Exhaling works too. The provider pulls the tube in one movement and the occlusive dressing goes straight on.
  3. 3
    After. Watch the respiratory status closely for several hours. Check the dressing for drainage or a leak. Listen to breath sounds. A chest x-ray is usually taken 1 to 2 hours later.
Why the breath matters

Pulling the tube leaves the chest wall briefly open. Let the patient inhale in that instant and negative pressure draws air in. You have made a pneumothorax.

Valsalva raises intrathoracic pressure, so air is pushed out rather than sucked in. Exhaling does the same job.

The dressing goes on immediately and stays for 48 to 72 hours.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Never Let Them Inhale
Bear down or breathe out. Either raises the pressure inside the chest so air leaves through the hole instead of entering it. An inhale at that moment is how a routine removal becomes a pneumothorax.
After removal, report
  • Increasing dyspnea or respiratory distress
  • Falling oxygen saturation
  • Diminished breath sounds on the affected side
  • Subcutaneous emphysema appearing or spreading
  • Significant drainage from the site
  • Tracheal deviation, which means a tension pneumothorax
Bottom Line: Out when the leak has gone, drainage is under 150 mL a day and the lung is up on x-ray. Valsalva or exhale during the pull. Occlusive dressing on immediately.
