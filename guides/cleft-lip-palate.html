Cleft Lip & Palate

Congenital craniofacial differences that turn feeding into the first — and most important — nursing priority. Surgical staging, post-op positioning, and the long multi-disciplinary journey.

7 Sections 13 Quiz Questions ~10 Min Read

Learning Objectives

Differentiate cleft lip from cleft palate and recognize when both occur together.
Teach and implement specialized feeding techniques (ESSR, specialty nipples, upright positioning).
Apply the "Rule of 10s" to determine cleft lip surgical readiness.
Distinguish post-op positioning and precautions for cleft lip repair vs. cleft palate repair.
Anticipate ear, speech, and dental complications requiring multi-disciplinary follow-up.
Support families through the grief, feeding, and surgical journey with evidence-based teaching.

High-Yield Summary

Eight facts that cover nearly every Cleft Lip/Palate question.

  • Cleft lip (CL) forms ~5–6 weeks gestation; cleft palate (CP) forms ~7–12 weeks gestation. Can occur separately or together. Multifactorial cause: genetic + environmental (smoking, alcohol, anticonvulsants, folate deficiency).
  • Incidence: ~1 in 600–700 live births. CL ± palate is more common in boys; isolated CP is more common in girls.
  • Feeding is the #1 nursing priority. CL alone can often breastfeed with positioning. CP cannot create suction — requires specialized bottles (Haberman, Pigeon, Mead Johnson).
  • Feed upright (45–60 degrees), small frequent volumes, burp often (excessive air swallowing), allow ~30–45 min per feed. Expect some nasal regurgitation.
  • ESSR technique: Enlarge nipple (cross-cut), Stimulate suck, Swallow, Rest. Used for infants without the "Special Needs" feeder.
  • Cleft lip repair: "Rule of 10s" — 10 weeks old, 10 lb, Hgb 10 g/dL. Procedure: cheiloplasty (Millard rotation-advancement).
  • Cleft palate repair: typically 9–18 months, BEFORE speech develops. Procedure: palatoplasty.
  • Post-op positioning (the NCLEX classic): Cleft lip → supine or side-lying, NEVER prone (protect lip). Cleft palate → prone or side-lying (allow drainage, protect airway). Both use elbow restraints and nothing hard near the suture line.

Overview & Types

What happens in utero and what shows up at birth

Cleft lip and cleft palate are congenital craniofacial differences that result from incomplete fusion during early embryonic development. They can occur together or separately, on one side (unilateral) or both (bilateral), and range from a small notch to a complete opening into the nasal cavity.

Embryology in One Paragraph

Around 5–6 weeks gestation, the medial nasal and maxillary processes fuse to form the upper lip and primary palate. Failure to fuse → cleft lip. Around 7–12 weeks, the lateral palatal shelves fuse to form the secondary palate (hard and soft). Failure to fuse → cleft palate. The two processes happen independently, which is why they can occur together or separately.

Classification

  • Cleft lip only (~20% of cases) — defect of the upper lip, may extend into the nostril.
  • Cleft palate only (~30%) — opening in the hard and/or soft palate.
  • Cleft lip + palate together (~50%) — the most common combination.
  • Unilateral vs bilateral, partial vs complete.

Epidemiology & Causes

  • Incidence: ~1 in 600–700 live births worldwide.
  • Boys: cleft lip ± palate. Girls: isolated cleft palate.
  • Highest incidence: Asian and Native American populations. Lowest: African descent.
  • Risk factors: maternal smoking, alcohol, anticonvulsants (phenytoin, valproate), folate deficiency, family history.
  • Often isolated; some are part of genetic syndromes (Pierre Robin sequence, Van der Woude, Trisomy 13).
Cleft Lip Is Visible; Cleft Palate Is Often Missed

Cleft lip is obvious at birth and sometimes on prenatal ultrasound. Cleft palate is often missed visually — discovered only when the provider palpates the roof of the mouth with a gloved finger during the newborn exam. Always palpate the palate in every newborn and in any infant with unexplained feeding difficulties, nasal regurgitation, or recurrent otitis media.

🎯 Bottom Line: Cleft lip = visible fusion failure at 5–6 weeks. Cleft palate = hidden fusion failure at 7–12 weeks. Independent processes; can occur separately or together.

Feeding the Infant with a Cleft

The first and most important nursing priority

Every cleft baby needs to eat to grow to surgery — and feeding failure is the main reason they're readmitted. The key physiologic problem is the inability to create negative pressure (suction) when the palate is open. Without suction, standard bottles don't work. Specialized techniques and equipment bridge the gap.

Why Feeding Is Hard

Cleft palate can't create a vacuum for sucking — milk leaks back into the nose.

Air swallowing causes colic, reflux, and abdominal distension.

Fatigue during feeds leads to poor intake and poor weight gain.

Aspiration risk especially with nasal regurgitation.

Specialized Feeding Equipment

Device How It Works Best For
Haberman (Special Needs) Feeder Soft silicone nipple with one-way valve; caregiver squeezes to control flow Severe CP where infant can't generate suction
Pigeon Bottle Air vent system; infant compresses nipple to express milk Moderate CP; commonly used worldwide
Mead Johnson Cleft Palate Nurser Soft plastic bottle; caregiver squeezes to assist flow Moderate CP; low cost, widely available
Standard bottle with cross-cut nipple Enlarged opening allows milk flow with minimal sucking CL only; mild CP when specialty unavailable
Breast Infant compresses breast tissue against palate CL only, small CP with good latch — work with lactation consultant

The ESSR Feeding Technique

When specialized equipment isn't available or as a complement to bottle feeding, the ESSR method structures the feeding process and reduces fatigue:

Enlarge

Cross-cut or enlarge the nipple opening.

Stimulate

Stimulate the sucking reflex by touching the nipple to the lips.

Swallow

Allow the infant to swallow the milk; watch for fatigue.

Rest

Pause between swallows to let the infant recover.

General Feeding Guidelines

  • Position upright (45–60 degrees) during and after feeds — reduces nasal regurgitation and aspiration.
  • Small, frequent feeds (every 2–3 hours) — less fatiguing than large volumes.
  • Limit feed time to ~30–45 minutes to avoid exhaustion.
  • Burp frequently (every ½ oz to 1 oz) — air swallowing is excessive.
  • Direct the nipple toward the side with intact palate (away from the cleft).
  • Expect some nasal regurgitation — not aspiration; clean gently.
  • Monitor weight gain carefully — failure to thrive delays surgery.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Feeding Is the Path to Surgery
A cleft baby who can't feed can't meet the Rule of 10s. No weight gain = delayed surgery = longer duration of feeding problems, infection risk, and family stress. That's why feeding teaching happens in the first days of life — often involving lactation, speech, and a cleft specialist. Treat feeding coaching as clinically urgent.
🎯 Bottom Line: Cleft palate = no suction. Use specialty nipples + ESSR + upright positioning. Small frequent feeds, burp often. Feeding success drives surgical readiness.
3

Surgical Repair

Staging, the Rule of 10s, and what actually gets fixed

Cleft repair is staged: lip first, then palate. The lip repair is cosmetic and functional (feeding improves, appearance normalizes). The palate repair is primarily functional — done BEFORE speech develops so the child can form sounds correctly.

Cleft Lip Repair (Cheiloplasty)

  • When: typically 2–3 months of age when the infant meets the "Rule of 10s."
  • Procedure: Millard rotation-advancement technique (most common) — rotates and advances tissue flaps to reconstruct the lip.
  • Creates functional upper lip and improves feeding.
  • Scar is permanent but usually well-hidden; may be revised later.
The Rule of 10s

Before cleft lip repair, the infant should be:

• 10 weeks old (approx)
• 10 lb (5 kg) body weight
• Hemoglobin ≥ 10 g/dL

These criteria ensure the infant can tolerate anesthesia and the tissue is mature enough for good healing. Failure to meet the rule delays surgery.

Cleft Palate Repair (Palatoplasty)

  • When: typically 9–18 months of age — before speech develops (around 12–18 months).
  • Procedure: palatoplasty reconstructs the palate by bringing the two sides together and closing the opening. Techniques include Furlow double-opposing Z-plasty and the Von Langenbeck.
  • Goal: create a functional palate that allows normal speech, feeding, and Eustachian tube drainage.
  • Some children need revision surgeries later (speech-improving, dental, rhinoplasty, bone graft for alveolar cleft around age 7–10).

Pre-Op Preparation

  1. Optimize nutrition — weight gain is the #1 goal.
  2. Prevent infection — keep the infant well, treat any URI or otitis media before scheduling surgery.
  3. Lab work: CBC (check Hgb), type and screen, basic metabolic panel.
  4. Family teaching — expectations, post-op care, arm restraints, feeding changes.
  5. Bond-building photos before surgery — many families want pre-repair images.
🎯 Bottom Line: Cleft lip first (Rule of 10s, 2–3 months). Cleft palate second (9–18 months, before speech). Stage more revisions over childhood.
4

Post-Op Care: Lip vs. Palate

Two different repairs, two different positioning rules

The distinction between post-lip and post-palate care is among the most NCLEX-tested points in all of pediatrics. The suture lines are in different places, so the precautions are opposite. Learn them side-by-side.

Post-Cleft Lip Repair

Cheiloplasty
  • Position: supine or side-lying. NEVER prone — rubbing face on sheets disrupts lip suture line.
  • Protect the suture line: Logan bow (metal arch) may be taped over lip for stability.
  • Clean suture line with sterile water or saline after feeds.
  • Apply antibiotic ointment if ordered.
  • Elbow (no-no) restraints prevent the infant from touching the lip.
  • Feed with soft nipple, syringe, or spoon — avoid pressure on suture line.
  • No pacifiers, no straws.
  • Expect mild bleeding early post-op — monitor, do not disturb.
  • Pain management: scheduled acetaminophen ± opioid.

Post-Cleft Palate Repair

Palatoplasty
  • Position: prone or side-lying — allows drainage, prevents tongue from obstructing airway.
  • Watch the airway — post-op swelling can compromise breathing. Have suction and oral airway at the bedside.
  • NO hard objects in the mouth: no straws, utensils, pacifiers, tongue depressors, toothbrushes, or hard toys.
  • Soft/liquid diet for 7–10 days, advance slowly. Feed with a cup or the side of a spoon (not the tip).
  • Elbow restraints to prevent fingers in the mouth.
  • Clean the mouth with water after feeds; avoid commercial mouthwash.
  • Pain management: scheduled acetaminophen ± opioid; may need IV fluids if oral intake poor.
  • Monitor for bleeding from the surgical site — report excessive bloody drainage.
Airway Is the #1 Post-Palate Risk

Swelling at the back of the throat after palatoplasty can obstruct the airway. Signs: stridor, retractions, drooling, restlessness, cyanosis. Keep the infant in a position that promotes drainage (prone or side-lying), keep suction at the bedside, and be ready to call for emergent airway support. The child is NOT off the hook just because surgery went well.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Remembering Which Position Goes with Which Repair
Think of where the suture line is. Lip = outside (face). You can't lie face-down on the sheets, so SUPINE for lip. Palate = inside (mouth). You need drainage to protect the airway, so PRONE for palate. Wherever the stitches are, protect that surface by keeping it off the bed.

Shared Post-Op Priorities (Both Repairs)

  • Elbow restraints (no-no's) used for 10–14 days on both repairs — prevent touching the site.
  • Pain control: scheduled acetaminophen; opioid for breakthrough. Poor pain control = crying = stress on suture line.
  • Calm, quiet environment — infants cry less when comforted and held by parents.
  • Feeding support — specialized feeders adjusted to avoid trauma to the repair site.
  • Education for parents before discharge: wound care, signs of infection, feeding technique, restraint use.
🎯 Bottom Line: Lip = supine, protect outside suture, soft nipple, no pacifier. Palate = prone/side, protect inside suture, airway watch, no hard objects in mouth.

Associated Issues

Ear infections, speech, and dental — the ongoing care

Cleft repair isn't the finish line — it's the start of a long multi-disciplinary journey. Three systems deserve ongoing attention: ears, speech, and dentition.

Chronic otitis media — Eustachian tube dysfunction from abnormal palatal muscles causes fluid buildup. Most cleft palate children need tympanostomy (ear) tubes.

Hearing loss — conductive hearing loss from recurrent OM delays language. Audiology screening at every well-child visit.

Speech delays — velopharyngeal insufficiency causes hypernasal speech. Speech therapy starts early, often pre-palate repair with parent coaching.

Dental issues — missing, malformed, or extra teeth near the cleft; malocclusion. Pediatric dentist and orthodontist needed early.

Alveolar bone graft — usually performed ages 7–10 to close the gap in the upper jaw where the cleft was, allowing adult teeth to erupt properly.

Psychosocial adjustment — bullying, body image, parent guilt. Counseling, cleft team support groups (Smile Train, Operation Smile) are invaluable.

The Multi-Disciplinary Cleft Team

Modern cleft care is delivered by a team: plastic surgeon, pediatrician, ENT, audiologist, speech therapist, dentist, orthodontist, psychologist, social worker, nurse coordinator. Most children have visits coordinated at specialized cleft centers. As the nurse, you're often the glue — helping families navigate appointments, communicating between services, and reinforcing teaching.

🎯 Bottom Line: Expect ear infections (tubes), speech delays (therapy), dental issues (orthodontics), and bone graft around age 7–10. Team-based care for years, not weeks.

Family Education & Support

From delivery room to adolescence

Parents often feel overwhelmed, grieving, and guilty at the birth of a cleft baby. Nursing care extends far beyond feeding technique — it includes emotional support, practical skills, and connecting families with community resources.

Emotional Support

  • Acknowledge grief — families may describe "the baby we planned for is not the baby we got."
  • Encourage early bonding: skin-to-skin contact, cuddling, making eye contact.
  • Reassure that cleft is NOT caused by anything the mother did; maternal guilt is common and needs direct addressing.
  • Connect with support groups (Cleft Lip & Palate Association, local cleft center support circles, online communities).

Feeding Competence

  • Teach and have the parent return-demonstrate the feeding technique before discharge.
  • Review positioning (upright), pacing (ESSR), frequency, burping.
  • Review signs of aspiration (coughing, choking, color change) vs. nasal regurgitation (expected, not harmful).
  • Track weight at every well-child visit; FTT delays surgery.

Surgical Prep & Post-Op

  • Explain the Rule of 10s so parents understand why surgery is scheduled when it is.
  • Demonstrate elbow restraint application and reassure parents that they can remove them for supervised play.
  • Teach home wound care and red flags: bleeding, worsening swelling, fever, drainage, separation of suture line.
  • Schedule follow-ups: suture removal ~1 week post-lip; cleft clinic visits; speech, audiology, dental.

Long-Term Navigation

  • Set expectations: years of specialist visits, possible revision surgeries, orthodontic care.
  • Insurance and cost counseling — cleft care is expensive but usually well-covered by insurance and cleft charities.
  • School age: speech delays and appearance may affect peer interactions — coach parents on how to talk about cleft with their child and with teachers.
  • Reassure families that most children with cleft grow up with normal speech, appearance, and function.
Good News Worth Sharing

Modern cleft repair is highly successful. Children who undergo staged surgical repair with good team-based follow-up typically have near-normal speech, appearance, and quality of life. Scars fade with time and revision surgeries. Make sure families hear this — the first days are often the darkest, and hope is evidence-based.

🎯 Bottom Line: Bond, feed, teach, follow up. Validate grief, correct guilt, show outcomes. You are the cleft team's anchor for the family.
