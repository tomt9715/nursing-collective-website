Cleft Lip & Palate

Feeding is the first priority, not the cosmetics. Two surgeries, two opposite post-op positions, and the numbers that decide when the baby goes to the OR.

01

What actually gets you

Cleft lip and palate looks like a picture question. It is not. The exam tests what you do at the bedside, and points get lost in three predictable places. Read these first.

You treated it as a cosmetic problem

The lip is what you see. Feeding is what you fix. An open palate cannot make suction, so a normal bottle does not work.

The baby swallows air, tires out, and milk runs back through the nose. The immediate risk is poor intake and aspiration, not the way the lip looks.

You mixed up the two post-op rules

Lip repair and palate repair have opposite positioning. Lip goes supine, palate goes prone. Swap them and you disrupt the suture line you were protecting.

The rules about what can touch the mouth also differ. Learn them as a pair, not one at a time.

You forgot to look past the cleft

A cleft can travel with a syndrome. Pierre Robin sequence, Van der Woude, Trisomy 13 all show up here.

An isolated cleft palate can hide until someone palpates the roof of the mouth. Screen the whole infant, not just the visible lip.

Bottom Line: Study this as bedside care, not a diagnosis. Feed the baby, learn the two opposite post-op rules, and screen for what travels with the cleft.
02

The four things

Cleft care runs in four phases: feed the newborn, repair the lip, repair the palate, then protect the repair. For each phase you should be able to say four things with the book closed.

The goal, the cue you would see, the priority action, and the one thing you teach. If you cannot say all four, you do not know the phase yet.

Phase Goal in one sentence Cue in the scenario Priority action Key teaching point
Newborn feeding Get calories in so the baby grows to surgery. Milk out the nose, long tiring feeds, poor weight gain. Specialty bottle, upright, burp often. Weight gain is what gets the baby to the OR.
Lip repair Close the lip, usually around 2 to 3 months. The baby now meets the Rule of 10s. Confirm 10 weeks, 10 lb, Hgb 10. Feeding gets easier and the scar fades.
Palate repair Close the palate before speech, 9 to 18 months. The toddler is approaching first words. Guard the airway after surgery. Early closure protects normal speech sounds.
Post-op protection Keep hands and objects off the fresh suture line. A curious infant and a new repair. Elbow restraints plus the correct position. Lip supine, palate prone; no hard objects after palate.

Write these four phases on one index card, by hand. Feed, fix the lip, fix the palate, protect the repair. That card is the spine of the whole guide.

03

What the cleft is

A cleft is incomplete fusion during early development. It can be lip only, palate only, or both. It can be one side or both sides, a small notch or a full opening into the nose.

Two separate events

The lip and the palate fuse at different times. That is why they can happen apart or together.

  • Cleft lip comes from failed fusion around 5 to 6 weeks gestation.
  • Cleft palate comes from failed fusion around 7 to 12 weeks gestation.
  • The two processes are independent, so a baby can have one or both.

How the cases split

  • Cleft lip and palate together is the most common, about half of cases.
  • Cleft palate alone is roughly a third.
  • Cleft lip alone is about a fifth.
  • Each can be unilateral or bilateral, partial or complete.

Who and why

Cause is multifactorial: genes plus environment. Risk rises with maternal smoking, alcohol, anticonvulsants such as phenytoin and valproate, folate deficiency, and family history.

Cleft lip with or without palate leans male. Isolated cleft palate leans female. Rates run highest in Asian and Native American populations and lowest in those of African descent.

The lip you see, the palate you feel

Cleft lip is obvious at birth and often on prenatal ultrasound. Cleft palate hides. It is found when a gloved finger palpates the roof of the mouth.

Palpate the palate on every newborn. Do it again for any infant with unexplained feeding trouble, nasal regurgitation, or repeat ear infections.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Feel for the hidden cleft
An isolated cleft palate can pass a visual exam. A finger on the hard palate does not miss it. When a feeding problem has no obvious cause, palpate before you chase anything else.
Bottom Line: Lip fails at 5 to 6 weeks and you can see it. Palate fails at 7 to 12 weeks and you have to feel for it. Independent events, so they come apart or together.
04

Feeding comes first

This is the central nursing problem. Every cleft baby has to eat to grow to surgery, and feeding failure is the main reason they come back.

The core issue is suction. An open palate cannot make the vacuum a normal bottle needs. So milk leaks into the nose, the baby swallows air, and feeds drag on until the baby is too tired to finish.

The equipment that bridges the gap

Device How it works Best for
Haberman (Special Needs) feeder Soft silicone nipple with a one-way valve; the caregiver squeezes to control flow. Severe cleft palate with no suction at all.
Pigeon bottle Air-vent system; the infant compresses the nipple to express milk. Moderate cleft palate; used widely worldwide.
Mead Johnson cleft palate nurser Soft plastic bottle; the caregiver squeezes to assist flow. Moderate cleft palate; low cost, widely available.
Standard bottle, cross-cut nipple Enlarged opening lets milk flow with little sucking. Cleft lip only; mild palate when specialty gear is missing.
Breast The infant compresses breast tissue against the palate. Cleft lip only, small palate cleft with a good latch. Use a lactation consultant.

The ESSR sequence

When you do not have a special feeder, or alongside one, ESSR paces the feed and cuts fatigue. Run it in order.

  1. E
    Enlarge. Cross-cut or enlarge the nipple opening so milk flows with minimal effort.
  2. S
    Stimulate. Touch the nipple to the lips to trigger the suck reflex.
  3. S
    Swallow. Let the infant swallow the milk. Watch for tiring.
  4. R
    Rest. Pause between swallows so the infant can recover, then repeat.

How you run the feed

  • Hold the baby upright, 45 to 60 degrees, during and after the feed. This cuts nasal regurgitation and aspiration.
  • Give small, frequent feeds, about every 2 to 3 hours.
  • Cap the feed near 30 to 45 minutes. A longer feed just exhausts the baby.
  • Burp often, every half ounce to one ounce, because air swallowing is heavy.
  • Aim the nipple toward the intact side, away from the cleft.
  • Expect some nasal regurgitation. It is not aspiration. Clean it gently.
  • Track weight at every visit. Poor gain delays surgery.
Regurgitation or aspiration

Milk out the nose during a feed is expected. It is regurgitation through the cleft, and you clean it and move on.

Coughing, choking, or a color change is different. That is aspiration, and you stop, reposition, and reassess.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Feeding is the path to surgery
A baby who cannot feed cannot meet the Rule of 10s. No weight gain means delayed surgery, more feeding problems, and more family stress. That is why feeding teaching starts in the first days of life. Treat it as clinically urgent.
Bottom Line: No palate means no suction. Specialty nipple, ESSR, upright, small frequent feeds, burp often. Weight gain drives the whole timeline.
05

Surgery, in stages

Repair is staged. Lip first, then palate. The lip repair improves feeding and appearance. The palate repair is timed to protect speech.

Cleft lip repair (cheiloplasty)

  • When: usually 2 to 3 months, once the baby meets the Rule of 10s.
  • How: the Millard rotation-advancement technique rotates and advances tissue flaps to rebuild the lip.
  • It creates a working upper lip and makes feeding easier.
  • The scar is permanent but usually well hidden, and it can be revised later.
The Rule of 10s

Before lip repair, the infant should be about 10 weeks old, 10 lb (5 kg), and have a hemoglobin of 10 g/dL or higher.

All three protect the baby: enough size and blood to tolerate anesthesia, and mature enough tissue to heal. Miss one and surgery waits.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
10, 10, 10
Ten weeks old, ten pounds, hemoglobin ten. When a question hands you an infant's age, weight, and Hgb, it is asking whether the baby is cleared. All three have to pass.

Cleft palate repair (palatoplasty)

  • When: usually 9 to 18 months, before speech develops around 12 to 18 months.
  • How: the surgeon brings the two sides together and closes the opening. Techniques include the Furlow double-opposing Z-plasty and the Von Langenbeck.
  • The goal is a palate that allows normal speech, feeding, and ear drainage.
  • Later revisions are common: speech surgery, dental work, rhinoplasty, and an alveolar bone graft around age 7 to 10.

Getting ready for the OR

  1. 1
    Nutrition first. Weight gain is the top pre-op goal, because it is what clears the baby.
  2. 2
    Prevent infection. Treat any upper respiratory infection or otitis media before the surgery date.
  3. 3
    Draw labs. CBC to check hemoglobin, a type and screen, and a basic metabolic panel.
  4. 4
    Teach the family. Cover expectations, post-op care, arm restraints, and feeding changes.
  5. 5
    Offer photos. Many families want pre-repair pictures. Support that.
Bottom Line: Lip first, around 2 to 3 months, on the Rule of 10s. Palate second, 9 to 18 months, before speech. More revisions follow across childhood.
06

Post-op: lip versus palate

Discrimination axis: where is the suture line.

Two repairs, two suture lines, two sets of rules. Where the stitches sit decides the position and what may touch the mouth. Learn them side by side.

After lip repair: protect the outside
  • Position supine or side-lying. Never prone; a face on the sheets rubs the suture line.
  • A Logan bow, a taped metal arch, may steady the lip.
  • Clean the suture line with sterile water or saline after feeds.
  • Apply antibiotic ointment if ordered.
  • Elbow restraints keep hands off the lip.
  • Feed with a soft nipple, syringe, or spoon. No pressure on the repair.
  • No pacifiers and no straws.
  • Expect mild bleeding early. Monitor, do not disturb.
After palate repair: protect the airway
  • Position prone or side-lying so blood and saliva drain and the tongue stays clear.
  • Watch the airway. Keep suction and an oral airway at the bedside.
  • Nothing hard in the mouth: no straws, utensils, pacifiers, tongue depressors, toothbrushes, or hard toys.
  • Soft or liquid diet for 7 to 10 days. Use a cup or the side of a spoon, not the tip.
  • Elbow restraints keep fingers out of the mouth.
  • Clean the mouth with water after feeds. Avoid commercial mouthwash.
  • Give IV fluids if oral intake is poor.
  • Report excessive bloody drainage from the site.
Airway is the top palate risk

Swelling at the back of the throat can block the airway after palatoplasty. Watch for stridor, retractions, drooling, restlessness, or cyanosis.

Keep the infant positioned to drain, keep suction at the bedside, and be ready to call for airway support. A smooth surgery does not clear this risk.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Position by where the stitches are
Lip is outside, on the face. You cannot lie face-down on it, so supine for lip. Palate is inside, and it needs drainage to protect the airway, so prone for palate. Keep whichever surface holds the stitches off the bed.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Prone here is on purpose
Prone worries people because of safe-sleep teaching. After palate repair it is deliberate. It lets blood and saliva drain and keeps the tongue off a swollen airway. The airway wins.

What you do first after palate repair

All of these are correct. The question is order. Protect the airway before anything else.

  1. 1
    Position to drain. Prone or side-lying, so a swollen airway stays clear.
  2. 2
    Ready the airway gear. Suction and an oral airway within reach.
  3. 3
    Apply elbow restraints. Keep fingers and objects away from the repair.
  4. 4
    Control pain. Scheduled acetaminophen, opioid for breakthrough. Crying strains the suture line.
  5. 5
    Feed safely. Soft or liquid, cup or spoon side, nothing hard.

What both repairs share

  • Elbow restraints for both, about 10 to 14 days. Remove them for supervised play, then reapply.
  • Pain control on a schedule. Poor control means crying, and crying stresses the repair.
  • A calm environment. Held and comforted infants cry less.
  • Feeding support adjusted to spare the suture line.
  • Discharge teaching: wound care, infection signs, feeding, restraint use.
Bottom Line: Lip is supine, protect the outside, soft nipple, no pacifier. Palate is prone, protect the airway, and nothing hard goes in the mouth.
07

What follows for years

Surgery is not the finish line. It starts a long, team-based journey. Three systems need ongoing attention: ears, speech, and teeth.

Chronic ear infections. Abnormal palate muscles disrupt the Eustachian tube, so fluid builds up. Most cleft palate children need ear tubes.

Hearing loss. Repeat infections cause conductive loss that delays language. Screen audiology at every well-child visit.

Speech delay. Velopharyngeal insufficiency makes speech hypernasal. Speech therapy starts early, often before palate repair.

Dental problems. Missing, extra, or malformed teeth and malocclusion near the cleft. A pediatric dentist and orthodontist are needed early.

Alveolar bone graft. Done around age 7 to 10 to fill the gap in the upper jaw, so the permanent canines and incisors erupt into solid bone.

Psychosocial needs. Bullying, body image, and parent guilt are real. Counseling and support groups such as Smile Train and Operation Smile help.

The cleft team

Care runs through a team: plastic surgeon, pediatrician, ENT, audiologist, speech therapist, dentist, orthodontist, psychologist, social worker, and nurse coordinator.

You are often the glue. You help families navigate appointments, pass information between services, and reinforce teaching.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
One chain to remember
Cleft palate leads to Eustachian tube dysfunction, then to ear infections, then to conductive hearing loss, then to speech delay. When a question links a cleft to the ears, this chain is the answer.
Bottom Line: Expect ear tubes, speech therapy, orthodontics, and a bone graft near age 7 to 10. This is team care for years, not weeks.
08

Working with the family

Parents often arrive overwhelmed, grieving, and blaming themselves. Your care goes past feeding technique. It includes emotional support, real skills, and connection to resources.

Emotional support

  • Name the grief. Families may say the baby they planned for is not the baby they got.
  • Encourage early bonding: skin-to-skin, cuddling, eye contact.
  • Correct the guilt. The cleft is not something the mother caused. Say so directly.
  • Connect them with support groups and their cleft center circle.

Feeding competence

  • Teach the feed, then have the parent return-demonstrate it before discharge.
  • Review positioning, pacing with ESSR, frequency, and burping.
  • Separate aspiration signs, such as coughing or color change, from expected nasal regurgitation.
  • Track weight at every visit. Poor gain delays surgery.

Surgery and follow-up

  • Explain the Rule of 10s so parents understand the timing.
  • Show them how to apply the elbow restraints and remove them for supervised play.
  • Teach the red flags: bleeding, swelling, fever, drainage, a separating suture line.
  • Set up follow-up: suture removal about a week after lip repair, then cleft clinic, speech, audiology, and dental visits.
The outcome is good, and they should hear it

Modern staged repair works well. With good team follow-up, most children reach near-normal speech, appearance, and function.

Scars fade and revisions help. The first days are the hardest, so make sure the family hears the outlook early.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Validate, correct, then resource
When a parent blames herself, do three things in order. Acknowledge the feeling, correct the misconception that she caused it, then offer support. Do not shame and do not brush the feeling aside.
Bottom Line: Bond, feed, teach, follow up. Validate the grief, correct the guilt, and show the outcomes. You anchor the family to the team.

Key Takeaways

Feeding is the first priority. Open palate means no suction, so use a specialty bottle.

ESSR plus upright = enlarge, stimulate, swallow, rest, held at 45 to 60 degrees.

Rule of 10s = 10 weeks, 10 lb, Hgb 10 before lip repair.

Timing = lip at 2 to 3 months, palate at 9 to 18 months before speech.

Position = supine after lip, prone after palate. Opposite rules.

Long-term = ear tubes, speech therapy, orthodontics, bone graft.
