Respiratory Anatomy & Physiology Review

Understanding normal respiratory function is essential for comprehending how COPD disrupts breathing. The respiratory system is responsible for gas exchange - bringing oxygen into the body and removing carbon dioxide.

Key Respiratory Structures

  • Upper Airways: Nose, pharynx, larynx - warm, filter, and humidify air
  • Lower Airways: Trachea, bronchi, bronchioles - conduct air to gas exchange units
  • Alveoli: Tiny air sacs where gas exchange occurs (300 million in average adult)
  • Surfactant: Reduces surface tension, prevents alveolar collapse
Clinical Pearl: Gas Exchange Basics

Oxygen diffuses from alveoli → blood (high O2 in alveoli, low O2 in blood)

CO2 diffuses from blood → alveoli (high CO2 in blood, low CO2 in alveoli)

This simple diffusion depends on adequate ventilation AND perfusion!

Normal Respiratory Drive

Stimulus Location Normal Response
High CO2 (Primary) Central chemoreceptors (medulla) Increased respiratory rate and depth
Low O2 (Secondary) Peripheral chemoreceptors (carotid/aortic bodies) Stimulates breathing when O2 drops significantly
Low pH (Acidosis) Central and peripheral chemoreceptors Increased ventilation to "blow off" CO2
Key Concept for COPD

In healthy individuals, elevated CO2 is the primary stimulus for breathing. This is critical to understand because COPD patients develop chronic CO2 retention, which changes their respiratory drive!

Pulmonary Function Terms

Term Definition Clinical Relevance
FEV1 Forced expiratory volume in 1 second Reduced in COPD; used to stage severity
FVC Forced vital capacity (total exhaled after max inhale) May be normal or reduced
FEV1/FVC Ratio Percentage of FVC exhaled in first second < 70% confirms airflow obstruction
Residual Volume Air remaining after maximal exhalation Increased in COPD (air trapping)

COPD Pathophysiology

Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease (COPD) is a progressive, irreversible lung disease characterized by airflow limitation that is not fully reversible. It encompasses two main conditions: chronic bronchitis and emphysema, which often coexist.

Definition

COPD is a preventable and treatable disease characterized by persistent respiratory symptoms and airflow limitation due to airway and/or alveolar abnormalities, usually caused by significant exposure to noxious particles or gases.

Risk Factors

Risk Factor Details Impact
Cigarette Smoking #1 cause - 80-90% of cases Damages airways and alveoli; triggers inflammation
Occupational Exposures Dust, chemicals, fumes Coal miners, farmers, textile workers at risk
Air Pollution Indoor and outdoor pollutants Biomass fuel smoke significant in developing countries
Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Deficiency Genetic deficiency (1-2% of cases) Early-onset emphysema; test young patients
Recurrent Respiratory Infections Childhood infections May predispose to reduced lung function
Critical Point: Smoking Cessation

Smoking cessation is the ONLY intervention proven to slow COPD progression! It should be addressed at every patient encounter. Even in advanced disease, quitting provides benefits.

Pathophysiologic Changes

What Happens in COPD

1
Chronic Inflammation

Inhaled irritants trigger ongoing inflammatory response in airways and lung parenchyma. Inflammatory cells (neutrophils, macrophages) release proteases that damage tissue.

2
Airway Remodeling

Repeated injury leads to thickening of airway walls, mucus gland hyperplasia, and increased mucus production. Airways narrow and become obstructed.

3
Alveolar Destruction

Protease/antiprotease imbalance destroys alveolar walls. Loss of elastic recoil causes air trapping and hyperinflation. Surface area for gas exchange decreases.

4
Air Trapping

Loss of elastic recoil and small airway collapse during exhalation traps air in lungs. This increases residual volume and causes barrel chest appearance.

Memory Aid: COPD Changes

COPD = Can't Overcome Pulmonary Damage

  • Chronic inflammation
  • Obstructed airways
  • Parenchymal destruction
  • Decreased airflow

Chronic Bronchitis vs Emphysema

While COPD is an umbrella term, understanding the distinct features of chronic bronchitis and emphysema helps guide assessment and treatment. Most patients have features of both, but one may predominate.

Classic Patient Presentations

Blue Bloater = Chronic Bronchitis (cyanotic, edematous, productive cough)

Pink Puffer = Emphysema (thin, pursed-lip breathing, dyspneic, pink skin)

These are classic descriptions - remember that most real patients have mixed features!

Chronic Bronchitis

Definition

Productive cough for 3+ months in 2 consecutive years

Primary Problem

  • Airway inflammation and mucus hypersecretion
  • Mucus gland hyperplasia
  • Airway wall thickening
  • Impaired mucociliary clearance

Key Clinical Features

  • Chronic productive cough
  • Copious sputum production
  • Frequent respiratory infections
  • Cyanosis (hypoxemia)
  • Peripheral edema (cor pulmonale)
  • Overweight appearance
Emphysema

Definition

Permanent enlargement of airspaces distal to terminal bronchioles with wall destruction

Primary Problem

  • Alveolar destruction and loss of elastic recoil
  • Air trapping
  • Hyperinflation
  • Decreased surface area for gas exchange

Key Clinical Features

  • Severe dyspnea (main symptom)
  • Minimal cough/sputum
  • Barrel chest
  • Pursed-lip breathing
  • Accessory muscle use
  • Thin, cachectic appearance

Detailed Comparison

Feature Chronic Bronchitis ("Blue Bloater") Emphysema ("Pink Puffer")
Age of Onset 40-50 years 50-60 years
Primary Symptom Chronic productive cough Progressive dyspnea
Sputum Copious, purulent Minimal, mucoid
Body Habitus Overweight, edematous Thin, cachectic
Skin Color Cyanotic (blue) Pink (maintains oxygenation)
Chest Appearance Normal or slightly increased AP diameter Barrel chest (increased AP diameter)
Breath Sounds Rhonchi, wheezes Diminished, distant
CO2 Retention Significant (chronic hypercapnia) Minimal until late stage
Cor Pulmonale Common and early Late in disease
Respiratory Drive Hypoxic drive (relies on low O2) Usually maintains CO2 drive longer
Clinical Pearl: Why "Pink Puffer"?

Emphysema patients maintain near-normal oxygen levels through increased work of breathing (puffing). They use pursed-lip breathing and accessory muscles to create back-pressure that keeps airways open. This extreme effort keeps them pink but causes severe dyspnea and muscle wasting.

"Blue Bloater" Profile

Why "Blue"

  • Chronic hypoxemia from V/Q mismatch
  • Mucus plugging impairs gas exchange
  • Central cyanosis visible

Why "Bloater"

  • Right heart failure (cor pulmonale)
  • Peripheral edema
  • JVD present
"Pink Puffer" Profile

Why "Pink"

  • Maintains adequate oxygenation
  • Compensates with increased ventilation
  • No cyanosis until very late

Why "Puffer"

  • Pursed-lip breathing visible
  • Prolonged expiratory phase
  • Uses accessory muscles
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Blue Bloater vs Pink Puffer
Use these classic descriptions to quickly recognize COPD presentations! Chronic Bronchitis ("Blue Bloater"): These patients are cyanotic (blue) from poor gas exchange due to mucus plugging, and bloated from right heart failure (cor pulmonale) causing peripheral edema. They have a productive cough with lots of sputum. Emphysema ("Pink Puffer"): These patients work so hard to breathe (puffing) that they maintain near-normal oxygen levels (pink skin). They're thin from the caloric demands of breathing, have barrel chests, and use pursed-lip breathing.

Oxygen Therapy & CO2 Retention

Managing oxygen therapy in COPD patients requires understanding the shift in respiratory drive that occurs with chronic CO2 retention. Improper oxygen administration can lead to respiratory failure.

Critical Safety Concept

In COPD patients with chronic CO2 retention, HIGH-FLOW OXYGEN CAN SUPPRESS THE RESPIRATORY DRIVE AND CAUSE RESPIRATORY FAILURE!

These patients have lost their sensitivity to elevated CO2 and rely on LOW OXYGEN LEVELS (hypoxic drive) to stimulate breathing.

Understanding the Hypoxic Drive

The Respiratory Drive Shift

1
Normal Respiratory Drive

In healthy individuals, elevated CO2 (hypercapnia) is the primary stimulus for breathing. When CO2 rises, the brain signals to breathe faster/deeper.

2
Chronic CO2 Retention in COPD

COPD patients (especially chronic bronchitis) develop chronically elevated CO2 levels. The brain's chemoreceptors become "desensitized" to high CO2.

3
Shift to Hypoxic Drive

When CO2 drive is lost, the body switches to using low oxygen (hypoxemia) as the primary stimulus to breathe. Peripheral chemoreceptors in carotid/aortic bodies detect low O2.

4
Danger of High-Flow Oxygen

If you give high-flow O2, the hypoxic stimulus is removed. The patient may stop breathing adequately, leading to CO2 narcosis and respiratory arrest!

NCLEX Priority

Target SpO2 for COPD patients with chronic hypercapnia: 88-92%

Do NOT try to achieve 100% saturation - this can suppress their respiratory drive!

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Low-Flow First
Always start COPD patients on low-flow oxygen! For chronic CO2 retainers, use 1-2 liters per minute via nasal cannula. These patients rely on their hypoxic drive to breathe because their brains have become desensitized to high CO2 levels. If you give high-flow oxygen, you remove their only stimulus to breathe, which can cause them to stop breathing adequately. Target SpO2 of 88-92% - don't aim for 100%! A Venturi mask is ideal when you need precise FiO2 control.

Signs of CO2 Narcosis (Hypercapnic Respiratory Failure)

Warning Signs - Act Immediately!
  • Decreased level of consciousness (confusion → lethargy → unresponsive)
  • Headache (CO2 causes cerebral vasodilation)
  • Decreased respiratory rate or shallow breathing
  • Flushed, warm skin (vasodilation from CO2)
  • Asterixis (flapping tremor when wrists extended)
  • Paradoxical chest movement

Action: Reduce oxygen flow rate, assess airway, prepare for possible intubation, notify provider STAT!

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Watch for CO2 Narcosis
Know these warning signs of CO2 narcosis - it's a medical emergency! When COPD patients receive too much oxygen, they may develop confusion that progresses to lethargy and unresponsiveness. Look for: decreased level of consciousness, headache (from cerebral vasodilation), flushed, warm skin, shallow or decreased respirations, and asterixis (a flapping tremor when wrists are extended). If you see these signs: reduce oxygen flow immediately, assess the airway, notify the provider STAT, and prepare for possible intubation.
Clinical Pearl: ABG Interpretation

Chronic CO2 Retainer ABG Pattern:

  • pH: Normal or slightly low (7.35-7.38) - compensated
  • PaCO2: Elevated (50-70 mmHg is their "normal")
  • HCO3: Elevated (compensation by kidneys)
  • PaO2: Low (60-70 mmHg may be acceptable)

This is compensated respiratory acidosis - their kidneys have retained bicarbonate to normalize pH!

Nursing Assessment

Thorough respiratory assessment is crucial for monitoring COPD patients, detecting exacerbations early, and evaluating treatment effectiveness.

Comprehensive COPD Assessment

Key Assessment Components

1
Respiratory Assessment

Rate, rhythm, depth, effort. Note use of accessory muscles, pursed-lip breathing, tripod positioning. Auscultate for wheezes, rhonchi, diminished breath sounds. Measure SpO2.

2
Cough and Sputum

Frequency, productivity, sputum color/amount/consistency. Change from baseline may indicate infection. Clear → yellow/green suggests bacterial infection.

3
Physical Appearance

Barrel chest (increased AP diameter), cyanosis (central vs peripheral), clubbing of fingers, weight/nutritional status, JVD, peripheral edema (cor pulmonale).

4
Functional Status

Dyspnea scale (0-10), activity tolerance, ADL performance, sleep quality, impact on daily life. Use standardized tools (mMRC Dyspnea Scale, CAT score).

5
Mental Status

Level of consciousness, orientation, confusion, anxiety. Changes may indicate hypoxemia or CO2 retention. Baseline mental status important for comparison.

Signs of COPD Exacerbation

Recognize Early - Report Immediately!
  • Increased dyspnea beyond normal day-to-day variation
  • Increased sputum volume and/or purulence
  • Increased cough frequency or severity
  • Fever (may indicate infection)
  • Decreased activity tolerance
  • Confusion or altered mental status
  • Peripheral edema worsening

Key Diagnostic Indicators

Test Purpose COPD Indicators
Spirometry (PFTs) Diagnose and stage COPD FEV1/FVC < 70%; FEV1 determines stage
ABG Assess gas exchange Hypoxemia, hypercapnia, respiratory acidosis
Chest X-Ray Rule out other conditions Hyperinflation, flat diaphragm, bullae
Alpha-1 Antitrypsin Genetic testing Low levels indicate genetic cause
CBC Monitor for polycythemia Elevated Hgb/Hct (compensation for hypoxemia)
BNP Assess for cor pulmonale Elevated if right heart failure present
Clinical Pearl: GOLD Classification

COPD severity is staged by FEV1 (post-bronchodilator) according to GOLD guidelines:

  • GOLD 1 (Mild): FEV1 ≥ 80% predicted
  • GOLD 2 (Moderate): FEV1 50-79% predicted
  • GOLD 3 (Severe): FEV1 30-49% predicted
  • GOLD 4 (Very Severe): FEV1 < 30% predicted

Breathing Techniques

Teaching proper breathing techniques is a key nursing intervention for COPD patients. These techniques improve ventilation, reduce air trapping, and decrease the work of breathing.

Pursed-Lip Breathing (PLB)

Pursed-Lip Breathing Technique

This is the MOST important breathing technique for COPD patients. It creates back-pressure that keeps airways open during exhalation, preventing air trapping.

1
Relax - Have the patient sit comfortably with shoulders relaxed. Relaxation reduces oxygen demand.
2
Inhale slowly through the nose - Count "1-2" while inhaling. Nose breathing warms and humidifies air.
3
Purse lips - Like you're going to whistle or blow out a candle. This creates resistance.
4
Exhale slowly through pursed lips - Count "1-2-3-4" (twice as long as inhalation). Don't force the air out.
5
Repeat - Practice during activity and at rest. Use automatically when short of breath.
Key Teaching Points

The 1:2 Ratio: Exhalation should be TWICE as long as inhalation

  • Inhale: 2 counts
  • Exhale: 4 counts

Why it works: Creates positive pressure that prevents small airway collapse during exhalation, allowing trapped air to escape.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Pursed-Lip Breathing
This is THE most important breathing technique for COPD patients! Have the patient inhale slowly through the nose for 2 counts, then exhale through pursed lips (like blowing out a candle) for 4 counts. The 1:2 ratio is key. Why does this work? COPD causes airways to collapse during exhalation, trapping air. Pursed-lip breathing creates back-pressure that keeps those small airways open, allowing trapped air to escape. Teach patients to practice when they're calm so it becomes automatic when they're short of breath.

Diaphragmatic (Abdominal) Breathing

Diaphragmatic Breathing Technique

Strengthens the diaphragm and promotes more efficient breathing by reducing use of accessory muscles.

1
Position - Lie on back with knees bent, or sit comfortably. Place one hand on chest, one on abdomen.
2
Inhale through nose - Feel the abdomen rise (hand moves outward). Chest should remain relatively still.
3
Exhale through pursed lips - Feel the abdomen fall. Gently tighten abdominal muscles to push air out.
4
Practice 5-10 minutes - 3-4 times daily. Progress to using during activities.

Additional Breathing Strategies

Technique Purpose When to Use
Tripod Position Maximizes accessory muscle use Acute dyspnea; sit leaning forward on arms
Paced Breathing Coordinates breathing with activity During exertion (exhale on effort)
Controlled Coughing Clears secretions without exhaustion When mucus needs clearing
Huff Coughing Moves secretions with less energy Alternative to forceful coughing
Patient Teaching Tips
  • Practice when NOT short of breath - Learn technique during calm periods so it's automatic during distress
  • Use during activities - Exhale during the exertion phase (lifting, climbing stairs)
  • Stay relaxed - Tension increases oxygen demand
  • Don't rush - Slow, controlled breathing is more effective than rapid breathing
  • Use a mirror - Helps patients visualize proper technique

Nursing Interventions

Nursing care for COPD patients focuses on optimizing oxygenation, managing symptoms, preventing complications, and promoting self-management.

Priority Nursing Interventions

Key Interventions

1
Maintain Patent Airway

Position for optimal breathing (high Fowler's or tripod). Encourage effective coughing and deep breathing. Suction if unable to clear secretions. Ensure hydration to thin secretions.

2
Administer Oxygen Safely

Use LOW-FLOW oxygen (1-2 L/min NC or Venturi mask). Target SpO2 88-92%. Monitor closely for signs of CO2 retention. Titrate carefully based on patient response.

3
Administer Medications as Prescribed

Bronchodilators, corticosteroids, antibiotics for exacerbations. Ensure proper inhaler technique. Schedule bronchodilators before activities and meals.

4
Promote Energy Conservation

Plan activities with rest periods. Teach pacing techniques. Assist with ADLs as needed. Schedule care to allow recovery time.

5
Ensure Adequate Nutrition

Small, frequent meals (large meals compress diaphragm). High-protein, high-calorie diet for cachectic patients. Bronchodilator before meals to reduce dyspnea.

6
Prevent Infections

Hand hygiene, avoid crowds during flu season. Ensure vaccinations current (influenza annually, pneumococcal). Teach early recognition of infection signs.

Clinical Pearl: Nutrition in COPD

COPD patients with emphysema often have increased energy expenditure from the work of breathing, leading to weight loss and muscle wasting. Adequate nutrition is essential:

  • Small, frequent meals - Prevents abdominal distension that restricts breathing
  • High protein - Maintains respiratory muscle strength
  • Limit carbohydrates - Carb metabolism produces more CO2 than fat/protein
  • Rest before meals - Reduces dyspnea during eating

Pharmacological Management

Medication therapy for COPD focuses on bronchodilation, reducing inflammation, and treating exacerbations. Understanding drug classes and proper administration is essential.

Bronchodilators (Cornerstone of COPD Treatment)

Drug Class Examples Mechanism Nursing Considerations
SABA (Short-Acting Beta-Agonist) Albuterol (ProAir, Ventolin) Relaxes bronchial smooth muscle RESCUE inhaler; monitor HR, tremors; use before activity
SAMA (Short-Acting Muscarinic Antagonist) Ipratropium (Atrovent) Blocks acetylcholine in airways Less tachycardia than SABA; dry mouth; avoid in glaucoma
LABA (Long-Acting Beta-Agonist) Salmeterol, Formoterol Sustained bronchodilation MAINTENANCE only - NOT for acute rescue; twice daily
LAMA (Long-Acting Muscarinic Antagonist) Tiotropium (Spiriva) 24-hour bronchodilation Once daily; dry mouth; urinary retention in BPH
Combination LABA/LAMA Anoro Ellipta, Stiolto Dual bronchodilation More effective than single agent; one inhaler
Critical Safety Alert

NEVER use LABA alone in COPD patients who also have asthma! LABAs without inhaled corticosteroids increase asthma mortality risk. If patient has asthma-COPD overlap, use LABA + ICS combination.

Anti-Inflammatory Medications

Drug Class Examples Use in COPD Nursing Considerations
ICS (Inhaled Corticosteroids) Fluticasone, Budesonide Severe COPD with frequent exacerbations Rinse mouth after use; increased pneumonia risk
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Rinse After ICS
Always teach patients to rinse their mouth after using inhaled corticosteroids! ICS medications like fluticasone and budesonide can cause oral thrush (candidiasis) - a fungal infection that appears as white patches in the mouth. They can also cause hoarseness. After each dose, have the patient rinse their mouth thoroughly with water (swish and spit - don't swallow!) to remove any medication deposited in the oral cavity. Using a spacer with MDIs also helps reduce oral deposition.
Drug Class Examples Use in COPD Nursing Considerations
ICS/LABA Combination Advair, Symbicort, Breo Moderate-severe COPD with exacerbations NOT for rescue; rinse mouth; monitor for thrush
Systemic Corticosteroids Prednisone, Methylprednisolone Acute exacerbations (short course) 5-7 day burst; monitor glucose; don't stop abruptly
PDE-4 Inhibitor Roflumilast (Daliresp) Severe COPD with chronic bronchitis Reduce inflammation; GI side effects; weight loss

Other Important Medications

Medication Purpose Nursing Considerations
Antibiotics Bacterial exacerbations Give if purulent sputum + increased dyspnea/volume
Mucolytics (Guaifenesin) Thin secretions Increase fluid intake; limited evidence in COPD
Diuretics Cor pulmonale/edema Monitor electrolytes; may worsen metabolic alkalosis

Inhaler Teaching Checklist
  • Shake MDI before use (if applicable)
  • Prime new inhalers or if not used for several days
  • Exhale fully before inhaling medication
  • Seal lips around mouthpiece or use spacer
  • Inhale slowly and deeply while activating
  • Hold breath 10 seconds if possible
  • Wait 1 minute between puffs if multiple doses
  • Rinse mouth after ICS to prevent thrush
  • Clean inhaler weekly per manufacturer instructions
Memory Aid: Inhaler Order

When using multiple inhalers, use in this order:

  1. Short-acting bronchodilator FIRST (opens airways)
  2. Long-acting bronchodilator (if scheduled dose)
  3. Corticosteroid inhaler LAST (deposits better in open airways)

Remember: "Bronchodilators Before Steroids"

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Bronchodilators Before Steroids
When using multiple inhalers, the order matters! Always use bronchodilators before corticosteroids. The sequence is: SABA first (short-acting beta-agonist like albuterol) to quickly open the airways, then LABA (long-acting beta-agonist) if scheduled, then ICS last (inhaled corticosteroid). Why? Bronchodilators open the airways so the steroid can deposit deeper into the lungs where it's most effective. If you give the steroid first into constricted airways, it just sits in the larger airways and doesn't reach its target.
