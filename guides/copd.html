COPD

The one disease where giving more oxygen can stop a patient breathing. Learn why, and the rest of the topic follows from it.

  • Explain why 100 percent oxygen can stop a COPD patient breathing

01

What actually gets you

COPD is one of the few topics where the instinctive answer is the dangerous one. A patient is short of breath, so you turn the oxygen up. In this disease that can stop them breathing altogether.

Points get lost here in four predictable ways.

You treated the saturation instead of the patient

An 89 percent saturation looks wrong, so the instinct is to fix it. In a chronic CO2 retainer, 88 to 92 percent is the target and 100 percent is the danger.

Every oxygen question in this topic is built on that inversion.

You learned blue bloater and pink puffer as costumes

The colours are not decoration. Blue means the gas exchange failed. Pink means the patient is working hard enough to keep it going.

Once you know why each one is that colour, you never confuse them again, and you can reason out findings the guide never listed.

You gave the wrong inhaler in a crisis

Salmeterol takes too long to work and will not rescue anybody. Albuterol will.

Learn every inhaler as rescue or maintenance before you learn its name. The exam is testing the category, not the spelling.

You missed confusion as a respiratory finding

A COPD patient who becomes confused is not tired, and is not simply older. Rising CO2 changes the level of consciousness first.

Confusion in this patient is an airway finding until proven otherwise.

Bottom Line: Study this as three discriminations: bronchitis or emphysema, safe oxygen or too much, rescue drug or maintenance drug.
02

The four things

For each form and complication below, you should be able to say four things with the book closed: the one-sentence definition, the clue you would see in a patient, the priority nursing action, and the first-line drug with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Type Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line drug
Chronic bronchitis Airways inflamed and clogged with mucus, for 3 months a year across 2 years. Productive cough, cyanosis, peripheral edema, heavier build. Clear the secretions. Hydration, coughing, positioning. Bronchodilator. Antibiotics if the sputum turns purulent.
Emphysema Alveolar walls destroyed, so the lung loses its recoil and traps air. Severe dyspnea, barrel chest, thin, pursed lips, little sputum. Teach pursed-lip breathing and energy conservation. Long-acting bronchodilator. Small frequent meals alongside it.
Acute exacerbation A sustained worsening beyond the patient's usual day to day. More dyspnea, more sputum, and the sputum turned purulent. Assess, then bronchodilate. Watch the mental status. Short-acting bronchodilator, systemic steroid, antibiotic if purulent.
CO2 narcosis Carbon dioxide climbing until it suppresses consciousness and breathing. Confusion, headache, flushed warm skin, slow shallow breathing, asterixis. Turn the oxygen down, assess the airway, call the provider. No drug. The treatment is less oxygen, and possibly ventilation.

Four rows on one index card, by hand. Bronchitis clogs, emphysema destroys. Exacerbation is more, thicker, worse. Narcosis is confusion plus warm skin. That card is most of the exam.

03

How the airway closes

COPD is airflow limitation that does not fully reverse. Air goes in reasonably well. It cannot get back out.

Hold that one idea and most of the findings follow. Trapped air makes the chest barrel. Trapped air makes the diaphragm flat. Trapped air is why exhalation, not inhalation, is the problem.

What causes it

Risk factorDetailWhy it matters
Cigarette smokingThe leading cause, behind 80 to 90 percent of casesDamages airways and alveoli, and keeps the inflammation running
Occupational exposureDust, chemicals, fumesMiners, farmers and textile workers carry real risk
Air pollutionIndoor and outdoorBiomass fuel smoke matters a great deal in some countries
Alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiencyGenetic, 1 to 2 percent of casesEmphysema in a young patient. Test them
Repeated childhood infectionsEarly respiratory illnessMay cap the lung function a person ever reaches
The only thing that changes the trajectory

Stopping smoking is the only intervention shown to slow COPD progression. Everything else treats symptoms.

That is why it comes up at every visit, and why it is the answer to a surprising number of questions about long-term management. It helps even in advanced disease.

The four steps

  1. 1
    Chronic inflammation. Inhaled irritants keep an inflammatory response running. Neutrophils and macrophages arrive and release proteases that digest tissue.
  2. 2
    Airway remodelling. Repeated injury thickens the walls and multiplies the mucus glands. The airway gets narrower and wetter.
  3. 3
    Alveolar destruction. The protease and antiprotease balance tips, and alveolar walls break down. Elastic recoil is lost, and surface area goes with it.
  4. 4
    Air trapping. Without recoil, small airways collapse on exhalation and shut the air in. Residual volume climbs and the chest starts to barrel.

Steps 2 and 3 are the two diseases. Step 2 is chronic bronchitis. Step 3 is emphysema. Most patients have both, which is why the label is COPD rather than one or the other.

What normally makes you breathe

You need this before the oxygen section, because COPD changes it.

StimulusDetected whereNormal response
High CO2, the main driverCentral chemoreceptors in the medullaBreathe faster and deeper
Low O2, the backupPeripheral chemoreceptors in the carotid and aortic bodiesTriggers breathing once oxygen drops a long way
Low pHCentral and peripheral chemoreceptorsVentilate harder to blow off CO2

In a healthy person, CO2 is the main stimulus and oxygen is the spare. In a chronic retainer, those swap. That swap is the whole of section 05.

The numbers on the pulmonary function test

Bottom Line: Air goes in and cannot get out. Bronchitis clogs the airway, emphysema destroys the alveolus, and both trap air. FEV1 over FVC under 70 percent is the diagnosis.
04

Blue or pink

Discrimination axis: did the gas exchange fail, or is the patient still winning.

Both patients have COPD. One has failed to keep their oxygen up and shows it. The other is keeping it up by working extremely hard, and shows that instead.

Everything else on this page follows from that single difference.

Chronic bronchitis: the blue bloater

Definition: a productive cough for 3 months in each of 2 consecutive years.

The problem: airway inflammation, mucus gland overgrowth, thickened walls and cilia that no longer clear anything.

  • Chronic productive cough with plenty of sputum
  • Frequent respiratory infections
  • Cyanosis, because the gas exchange has failed
  • Peripheral edema and JVD from cor pulmonale
  • Heavier build
  • Rhonchi and wheeze on auscultation

Why blue: mucus plugs mismatch ventilation and perfusion, so the blood leaves the lung still unsaturated.

Why bloated: chronic hypoxia constricts the pulmonary vessels, the right heart fails against them, and fluid backs up into the body.

Emphysema: the pink puffer

Definition: permanent enlargement of the airspaces past the terminal bronchioles, with the walls destroyed.

The problem: alveolar destruction, lost elastic recoil, air trapping, hyperinflation, and far less surface for gas exchange.

  • Severe dyspnea, and that is the main complaint
  • Little cough and little sputum
  • Barrel chest from the trapped air
  • Pursed-lip breathing, done without being taught
  • Accessory muscle use
  • Diminished, distant breath sounds

Why pink: they are still oxygenating, by breathing hard enough to compensate.

Why puffing: that compensation is the pursed lips, the long exhalation and the accessory muscles. It works, and it is exhausting.

The axis that separates them

Ask what the patient looks like they are doing. Producing sputum and swelling, or working and wasting.

Blue and bloated means the compensation lost. Pink and thin means it is still holding, at enormous cost. The thinness is not incidental; breathing that hard burns calories all day.

Blue means the gas exchange failed; bloated means the right heart failed behind it. Pink means they are still oxygenating; puffing is how. The colour tells you whether compensation is holding. Most real patients sit somewhere between the two.
Write this down

Blank paper. Two columns, bronchitis and emphysema. Six findings each, from memory. Then write one sentence under each explaining why the patient is that colour.

Bottom Line: Blue and bloated is bronchitis with failed compensation. Pink and thin is emphysema still compensating. The last row of the table sets up the oxygen problem.
05

The oxygen problem

This is the section the whole guide exists for. It is the most tested idea in respiratory nursing, and the one where doing the obvious thing harms the patient.

Why the drive changes

  1. 1
    Normally, CO2 drives breathing. It rises, the medulla notices, and you breathe faster and deeper. Oxygen is only the backup sensor.
  2. 2
    In COPD, CO2 stays high all the time. Chronic bronchitis especially. The central chemoreceptors stop reacting to a level they now see constantly.
  3. 3
    The backup takes over. With the CO2 sensor deaf, low oxygen becomes the stimulus, sensed at the carotid and aortic bodies. This is the hypoxic drive.
  4. 4
    Now fill them with oxygen. The low oxygen goes away, and so does the only remaining signal to breathe. Ventilation falls, CO2 climbs, and the patient slides into narcosis.
The target, and why it is low

Target saturation for a COPD patient with chronic hypercapnia is 88 to 92 percent.

Not 95. Not 100. A saturation you would chase in anyone else is the thing that harms this patient, and 88 to 92 is enough to perfuse them while leaving the drive intact.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Low Flow First
Start a chronic retainer at 1 to 2 L per minute by nasal cannula and target 88 to 92 percent. Their only stimulus to breathe is the low oxygen, so removing it removes the breathing. A Venturi mask is the choice when you need the FiO2 exact.

The devices

DeviceFlowFiO2 deliveredUse in COPD
Nasal cannula1 to 2 L/min24 to 28 percentPreferred for stable COPD
Nasal cannula3 to 4 L/min32 to 36 percentUse with caution, and watch closely
Venturi maskVariable24 to 50 percent, preciselyIdeal when the FiO2 has to be exact
Simple mask5 to 10 L/min40 to 60 percentCaution. Not the preferred device here
Non-rebreather10 to 15 L/min80 to 100 percentAvoid, unless the situation is immediately life-threatening
Why Venturi is the precise one

A nasal cannula delivers an FiO2 that changes with how the patient breathes. Breathe fast and shallow and they entrain more room air; the actual concentration drifts.

A Venturi mask fixes the ratio of oxygen to entrained air mechanically, so the delivered FiO2 stays put whatever the patient does. In a patient where the exact number matters, that is the point.

CO2 narcosis

Six findings, and what you do
  • Falling level of consciousness. Confusion, then lethargy, then unresponsive
  • Headache, because CO2 dilates cerebral vessels
  • Slower or shallower breathing
  • Flushed, warm skin, from the same vasodilation
  • Asterixis, a flapping tremor when the wrists are extended
  • Paradoxical chest movement

What you do: turn the oxygen down first. Then assess the airway, call the provider, and prepare for possible intubation.

Turning the flow down is within your scope and takes seconds. Do it before you go looking for anyone.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Watch for CO2 Narcosis
Confusion, headache, flushed warm skin, shallow breathing and asterixis. The flushed skin is the giveaway, because a hypoxic patient would be pale or blue, not warm and pink. Reduce the oxygen, assess the airway, then notify.
What their normal ABG looks like

A chronic retainer does not have a normal gas, and you should not try to give them one.

  • pH normal or just low, 7.35 to 7.38, because it has been compensated
  • PaCO2 high, 50 to 70 mmHg, and that is their baseline
  • Bicarbonate high, because the kidneys retained it to hold the pH
  • PaO2 low, and 60 to 70 mmHg may be acceptable for them

This pattern is compensated respiratory acidosis. A normal pH with a high CO2 tells you this has been going on for a long time; an acidotic pH tells you something changed today.

Bottom Line: 1 to 2 L per minute, target 88 to 92 percent. High-flow oxygen removes the hypoxic drive, and the result is CO2 narcosis. Turn it down before you do anything else.
06

Assessment, in order

Assessment questions are rarely asking what you would check. They are asking what you would check first.

  1. 1
    Breathing. Rate, rhythm, depth, effort. Accessory muscles, pursed lips, tripod position. Auscultate for wheeze, rhonchi and diminished sounds. Then the saturation.
  2. 2
    Mental status. This is a respiratory assessment in COPD. Confusion or a falling level of consciousness means hypoxemia or CO2 retention until proven otherwise.
  3. 3
    Cough and sputum. How often, how productive, what colour, how much. A change from their baseline is the thing that matters. Clear turning yellow or green suggests bacterial infection.
  4. 4
    Appearance. Barrel chest, cyanosis central or peripheral, finger clubbing, weight and nutrition, JVD, peripheral edema.
  5. 5
    Function. Dyspnea on a 0 to 10 scale, activity tolerance, ability to manage daily activities, sleep. Standard tools include the mMRC dyspnea scale and the CAT score.

Step 2 sits that high on purpose. In most patients mental status is a neurological finding. In this one it is an early warning that the gas exchange is failing.

An exacerbation, in three findings

The classic triad is more dyspnea, more sputum, and sputum that has turned purulent. Two of the three usually means treatment.

Also report:

  • Increased cough frequency or severity
  • Fever, which points at infection
  • Falling activity tolerance
  • Confusion or any change in mental status
  • Worsening peripheral edema

The tests

TestWhy it is doneWhat COPD shows
SpirometryDiagnose and stageFEV1 over FVC under 70 percent. FEV1 sets the stage

GOLD staging

Severity is staged on FEV1 after a bronchodilator, as a percentage of predicted.

  • GOLD 1, mild: FEV1 80 percent or more
  • GOLD 2, moderate: FEV1 50 to 79 percent
  • GOLD 3, severe: FEV1 30 to 49 percent
  • GOLD 4, very severe: FEV1 under 30 percent

Note what stages it. The ratio makes the diagnosis; the FEV1 grades it.

Bottom Line: Breathing first, mental status second. More dyspnea, more sputum, purulent sputum is an exacerbation. The ratio diagnoses, the FEV1 stages.
07

Teaching the breathing

These are nursing interventions with no order required and no side effects, which is exactly why they show up as correct answers so often.

Pursed-lip breathing

The single most useful technique in this disease. The problem in COPD is airways collapsing on the way out, so you breathe out against resistance and hold them open.

  1. 1
    Relax. Sitting comfortably, shoulders down. Tension costs oxygen.
  2. 2
    In through the nose, for two counts. The nose warms and humidifies the air on the way past.
  3. 3
    Purse the lips. As though about to whistle, or blow out a candle. That is the resistance.
  4. 4
    Out through pursed lips, for four counts. Twice as long as the breath in. Do not force it.
  5. 5
    Repeat. Practise at rest and during activity, so it becomes automatic when the breathlessness arrives.
Why it works

The ratio is 1 to 2. In for two counts, out for four.

Pursed lips create back-pressure inside the airway on exhalation. That pressure splints the small airways open long enough for the trapped air to leave. It is a positive end-expiratory pressure the patient generates themselves.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Pursed-Lip Breathing
In through the nose for two, out through pursed lips for four. The back-pressure holds collapsing airways open so trapped air can escape. Teach it while the patient is calm; nobody learns a new technique mid-attack.

Diaphragmatic breathing

Strengthens the diaphragm and takes work away from the accessory muscles.

  1. 1
    Position. Lying with knees bent, or sitting comfortably. One hand on the chest, one on the abdomen.
  2. 2
    In through the nose. The abdominal hand should rise. The chest hand should barely move.
  3. 3
    Out through pursed lips. The abdomen falls. Tighten the abdominal muscles gently to help the air out.
  4. 4
    Practise 5 to 10 minutes, three or four times a day, then start using it during activity.

The rest of the toolkit

TechniqueWhat it doesWhen to use it
Tripod positionFixes the shoulder girdle so accessory muscles can pull on the chestAcute dyspnea. Sitting, leaning forward on the arms
Paced breathingMatches the breath to the movementDuring exertion. Breathe out on the effort
Controlled coughingClears secretions without exhausting the patientWhenever mucus needs moving
Huff coughingMoves secretions for less energyWhen a forceful cough is too tiring
How to teach it so it sticks
  • Practise when they are not breathless. A technique learned in distress is not learned
  • Use it during activity. Breathe out on the effort, whether lifting or climbing
  • Stay relaxed. Tension raises oxygen demand
  • Do not rush. Slow and controlled beats fast
  • Use a mirror. Seeing it makes it easier to copy
Write this down

Out loud, to nobody. Teach pursed-lip breathing in five sentences, with the counts. If you have to look at the count, you cannot teach it yet.

Bottom Line: In for two, out for four. Pursed lips make back-pressure that keeps airways open. Teach it calm so it is available in a crisis.
08

What you do first

Priority questions give you four defensible actions and ask which comes first. All four are things a nurse would do. That is the trick. You are being tested on ordering, not on correctness.

The ordering rules
  • Position beats everything. Sitting a patient up costs nothing, needs no order, and works immediately.
  • Open the airway before you oxygenate it. A bronchodilator into an open airway does more than oxygen into a shut one.
  • Turning oxygen down is an intervention. In suspected narcosis it is the first one.
  • Mental status change outranks a number. Confusion is a reason to act now, not to recheck in an hour.
  • Anything you can do without an order beats anything that needs one.

Standing interventions

  1. 1
    Keep the airway open. High Fowler's or tripod. Encourage effective coughing and deep breathing. Suction only if they cannot clear it themselves. Keep them hydrated so the secretions stay thin.
  2. 2
    Give oxygen safely. Low flow, 1 to 2 L per minute by cannula or a Venturi mask. Target 88 to 92 percent. Titrate on response and watch for retention.
  3. 3
    Give the drugs properly. Bronchodilators, steroids, antibiotics for exacerbations. Check the inhaler technique yourself. Schedule bronchodilators before activity and before meals.
  4. 4
    Conserve their energy. Plan activities around rest. Teach pacing. Help with daily activities where needed, and space care out so they can recover between things.
  5. 5
    Feed them. Small frequent meals, because a full stomach pushes on the diaphragm. High protein and high calorie for a wasted patient. Bronchodilator first so eating is not exhausting.
  6. 6
    Keep infection out. Hand hygiene, crowds avoided in flu season, influenza vaccine annually and pneumococcal vaccine up to date. Teach them what an early infection looks like.
Why nutrition is a respiratory intervention

Breathing this hard burns calories continuously, which is why emphysema patients waste. Weak respiratory muscles then make the breathing harder still.

  • Small frequent meals, because a distended abdomen restricts the diaphragm
  • High protein, to keep the respiratory muscles working
  • Limit carbohydrate, because metabolising it produces more CO2 than fat or protein
  • Rest before eating, so they are not too breathless to finish

What the patient needs to hear

TopicWhat you actually say
Stopping smokingThe only thing that slows the disease. Help is available, and medication can make it easier
MedicationsInhaler technique, which inhaler is rescue and which is maintenance, and never stopping suddenly
Breathing techniquesPursed-lip and diaphragmatic breathing, and when each one helps
Energy conservationPacing, rest periods, assistive devices, planning the day in advance
Infection preventionVaccines, hand hygiene, avoiding sick contacts, spotting symptoms early
When to callMore dyspnea, fever, sputum change, confusion, worsening swelling
Bottom Line: Sit them up, open the airway, then oxygenate carefully. Nutrition and energy conservation are respiratory interventions, not afterthoughts.
09

Rescue or maintenance

Discrimination axis: is this drug for right now, or for every day.

Sort every inhaler into one of those two boxes before you learn anything else about it. Give a maintenance drug in an emergency and nothing happens fast enough.

Bronchodilators

ClassExamplesRescue or maintenanceWhat you watch for
SABA, short-acting beta-agonist Albuterol Rescue. This is the emergency inhaler Tachycardia and tremor. Use before activity as well
SAMA, short-acting muscarinic antagonist Ipratropium Rescue, often paired with albuterol Less tachycardia than a SABA. Dry mouth. Avoid in glaucoma
LABA, long-acting beta-agonist Salmeterol, formoterol Maintenance only. Never for rescue Twice daily, on schedule, whether or not they feel breathless
LAMA, long-acting muscarinic antagonist Tiotropium Maintenance Once daily. Dry mouth. Urinary retention in prostatic hyperplasia
LABA with LAMA Anoro Ellipta, Stiolto Maintenance Two mechanisms in one inhaler, and better than either alone
Never a LABA alone in asthma

A long-acting beta-agonist without an inhaled corticosteroid raises asthma mortality.

So if a patient has asthma, or asthma and COPD together, the LABA is only ever given combined with an inhaled steroid. This one is worth memorising as a rule rather than reasoning out.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Rinse After ICS
Steroid left sitting in the mouth suppresses local immunity, and candida moves in. Swish and spit after every dose, and do not swallow it. A spacer also cuts how much lands in the mouth in the first place.

Using an inhaler properly

The checklist
  • Shake a metered dose inhaler before use
  • Prime a new inhaler, or one unused for several days
  • Breathe all the way out first
  • Seal the lips around the mouthpiece, or use a spacer
  • Breathe in slowly and deeply as you actuate it
  • Hold the breath for 10 seconds if they can
  • Wait a minute between puffs
  • Rinse the mouth after any inhaled steroid
  • Clean the inhaler weekly as the manufacturer directs
The order, when there is more than one

Short-acting bronchodilator first. Long-acting bronchodilator second. Inhaled steroid last.

The reason is deposition. A steroid fired into a narrowed airway lands in the large airways and stops there. Open the airway first and it reaches the small ones, which is where it needs to be.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Bronchodilators Before Steroids
SABA, then LABA, then the inhaled steroid. Open the road before you send the traffic down it. A steroid given into a constricted airway never reaches the part of the lung it was meant for.
Bottom Line: Albuterol rescues, salmeterol and tiotropium maintain. Rinse after any inhaled steroid, and bronchodilate before you steroid.
