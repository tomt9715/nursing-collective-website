Coronary Anatomy Review

Understanding coronary artery anatomy is fundamental to recognizing how coronary artery disease affects different regions of the heart and predicting clinical outcomes based on the location and extent of arterial occlusion.

The Coronary Circulation

The coronary arteries originate from the aortic root, just above the aortic valve cusps. They provide the myocardium with its oxygen and nutrient supply. The heart, despite making up only 0.5% of body weight, receives about 5% of cardiac output at rest.

Clinical Pearl: Coronary Perfusion Timing

Unlike other organs, the heart receives most of its blood supply during diastole. During systole, the contracting myocardium compresses the coronary vessels. This is why tachycardia is dangerous in CAD patients - shortened diastole means less time for coronary perfusion!

Major Coronary Arteries

Artery Area Supplied Clinical Significance
Left Main Coronary Branches into LAD and LCx "Widow maker" - occlusion is often fatal
Left Anterior Descending (LAD) Anterior LV wall, septum, bundle branches Most commonly affected in CAD; anterior MI
Left Circumflex (LCx) Lateral and posterior LV walls Lateral or posterior MI
Right Coronary Artery (RCA) Right ventricle, inferior LV, SA/AV nodes Inferior MI; can cause heart blocks

Collateral Circulation

When coronary arteries become gradually narrowed over time, the heart can develop collateral vessels - small interconnecting branches that provide alternative blood flow routes. This is why some patients with severe CAD may have fewer symptoms than expected.

Good to Know

Patients with chronic stable CAD often have better-developed collateral circulation than those with acute coronary events. This can partially protect the myocardium during periods of increased demand.

Pathophysiology of Coronary Artery Disease

Coronary artery disease is a chronic, progressive condition primarily caused by atherosclerosis - the buildup of fatty plaques within the arterial walls that narrow the vessel lumen and reduce blood flow to the myocardium.

The Atherosclerotic Process

Stages of Atherosclerosis Development

1
Endothelial Dysfunction

Risk factors (hypertension, smoking, diabetes, hyperlipidemia) damage the arterial endothelium, making it permeable to lipids and inflammatory cells.

2
Fatty Streak Formation

LDL cholesterol infiltrates the arterial wall and becomes oxidized. Macrophages engulf the oxidized LDL, becoming "foam cells" that accumulate as fatty streaks.

3
Fibrous Plaque Development

Smooth muscle cells migrate and proliferate, forming a fibrous cap over the lipid-rich core. The plaque progressively narrows the arterial lumen.

4
Complicated Lesion

Plaques may calcify, ulcerate, or rupture. Rupture exposes the thrombogenic core, triggering platelet aggregation and thrombus formation - leading to acute coronary syndrome.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Plaque Stability Matters
It's not just about the size of the plaque - it's about stability! A large, stable plaque with a thick fibrous cap may cause chronic stable angina but is less likely to cause an acute event. A smaller plaque with a thin, inflamed fibrous cap is "vulnerable" and can rupture suddenly, triggering a heart attack. This is why some patients have MI with relatively mild CAD, while others with severe stenosis remain stable for years. Statins help by stabilizing plaques, not just lowering cholesterol numbers.

Oxygen Supply vs Demand Imbalance

CAD symptoms occur when myocardial oxygen demand exceeds supply. Understanding this balance is key to both diagnosis and treatment.

Oxygen Supply Factors Oxygen Demand Factors
Coronary blood flow (vessel patency) Heart rate (faster = more demand)
Arterial oxygen content Myocardial contractility
Hemoglobin level Wall tension (preload/afterload)
Diastolic perfusion time Blood pressure (afterload)
NCLEX Tip: Treatment Rationale

CAD treatment targets both sides of the equation: increase supply (nitrates dilate coronary arteries, oxygen if hypoxic) and decrease demand (beta-blockers reduce HR and contractility, rest reduces workload). Remember: "More supply, less demand!"

Risk Factors for CAD

Identifying and modifying risk factors is essential for primary and secondary prevention of coronary artery disease. Risk factors are categorized as modifiable (changeable) and non-modifiable (unchangeable).

Non-Modifiable Risk Factors

Risk Factor Details
Age Men ≥45 years; Women ≥55 years (or premature menopause)
Sex Men have higher risk; women's risk increases after menopause
Family History First-degree relative with CAD: male <55 or female <65 years
Race/Ethnicity Higher rates in African Americans, South Asians, Hispanics

Modifiable Risk Factors

Risk Factor Target Goals Interventions
Hypertension BP <130/80 mmHg for most; <140/90 if lower risk DASH diet, sodium restriction, medications
Hyperlipidemia LDL <70 mg/dL (high risk); <100 mg/dL (moderate) Statins, lifestyle changes, diet modification
Diabetes Mellitus HbA1c <7% for most patients Glycemic control, SGLT2 inhibitors, GLP-1 agonists
Smoking Complete cessation Counseling, nicotine replacement, varenicline
Obesity BMI <25 kg/m²; waist <40" (M) or <35" (F) Caloric restriction, exercise, weight loss surgery
Physical Inactivity ≥150 min moderate exercise/week Cardiac rehabilitation, structured exercise program
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Risk Factor Modification is Powerful
Don't underestimate lifestyle changes! Smoking cessation reduces cardiovascular risk by 50% within just one year. Controlling blood pressure reduces stroke risk by 35-40% and heart attack risk by 20-25%. High-intensity statin therapy can reduce LDL by 50% and significantly lower cardiovascular events. When teaching patients, emphasize that these modifiable factors together account for the majority of CAD risk - and they have the power to change their trajectory!
Memory Aid: CAD Risk Factors

CARDIAC

  • Cholesterol (elevated LDL)
  • Age and family history
  • Raised blood pressure (hypertension)
  • Diabetes mellitus
  • Inactivity (sedentary lifestyle)
  • Abdominial obesity
  • Cigarettes (smoking)

Emerging Risk Factors

  • Elevated C-reactive protein (CRP): Marker of inflammation; associated with increased cardiovascular risk
  • Elevated homocysteine: Amino acid linked to endothelial damage
  • Lipoprotein(a): Genetic lipid abnormality that increases clotting risk
  • Metabolic syndrome: Cluster of risk factors (central obesity, elevated triglycerides, low HDL, hypertension, elevated fasting glucose)
  • Chronic kidney disease: Accelerates atherosclerosis
  • Obstructive sleep apnea: Associated with hypertension and arrhythmias

Stable vs Unstable Angina

Angina pectoris is chest pain or discomfort caused by myocardial ischemia. Distinguishing between stable and unstable angina is critical because they represent different points on the acute coronary syndrome spectrum and require different management approaches.

Stable Angina

Characteristics

  • Predictable pattern - occurs with same triggers
  • Triggered by exertion, stress, cold, or meals
  • Duration: 3-5 minutes (up to 15 min)
  • Relieved by rest and/or nitroglycerin
  • No EKG changes at rest
  • Troponin negative

Management

Outpatient management with lifestyle modification, medications (nitrates PRN, beta-blockers, aspirin, statins), and elective workup.

Unstable Angina

Characteristics

  • Unpredictable pattern - new, worsening, or at rest
  • May occur at rest or with minimal exertion
  • Duration: >20 minutes
  • Not fully relieved by rest or nitroglycerin
  • EKG may show ST depression or T wave changes
  • Troponin negative (positive = NSTEMI)

Management

Part of ACS! Requires hospitalization, continuous monitoring, anticoagulation, and urgent cardiology evaluation.

Critical Distinction

Unstable angina is an EMERGENCY. It indicates that a coronary plaque is "unstable" and at risk for complete occlusion, which would cause a myocardial infarction. Patients with unstable angina should be admitted and treated as acute coronary syndrome.

Remember: Unstable angina + positive troponin = NSTEMI (myocardial infarction)

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Recognize Changing Angina Patterns
A stable patient becoming unstable is a red flag! Watch for any of these changes in a patient's angina pattern: (1) angina occurring at rest when it previously only occurred with exertion, (2) angina triggered by less activity than before, (3) angina lasting longer than usual, (4) angina not responding to nitroglycerin as well as before, or (5) new-onset angina. Any of these patterns suggests progression to unstable angina and requires immediate evaluation. Teach your patients to recognize these warning signs and seek care promptly.

Classic Angina Presentation

  • Location: Substernal or left chest
  • Quality: Pressure, squeezing, tightness, heaviness ("elephant on chest")
  • Radiation: Left arm, jaw, neck, shoulder, back, or epigastrium
  • Associated symptoms: Dyspnea, diaphoresis, nausea, anxiety
  • Levine's sign: Patient places clenched fist over sternum when describing pain
NCLEX Tip: Atypical Presentations

Women, elderly, and diabetic patients often present atypically:

  • Fatigue or weakness instead of chest pain
  • Nausea, indigestion, or "heartburn"
  • Dyspnea without chest discomfort
  • Back or jaw pain only

Always maintain high suspicion in these populations!

Prinzmetal's (Variant) Angina

A special form of angina caused by coronary artery spasm rather than fixed atherosclerotic obstruction:

  • Occurs primarily at rest, often in early morning hours
  • Associated with ST elevation during episodes (transient)
  • More common in smokers and cocaine users
  • Treated with calcium channel blockers and nitrates (not beta-blockers, which can worsen spasm)

Diagnostic Testing for CAD

Multiple diagnostic modalities are used to detect CAD, assess severity, and guide treatment decisions. The choice of test depends on the patient's presentation, risk factors, and ability to exercise.

Resting Studies

Test What It Shows Nursing Considerations
12-Lead EKG Arrhythmias, ischemia, prior MI (Q waves) May be normal at rest in stable CAD; compare to baseline
Chest X-ray Heart size, pulmonary congestion, calcifications Cannot diagnose CAD directly; rules out other causes
Echocardiogram Wall motion abnormalities, ejection fraction, valve function Resting echo may miss ischemia; stress echo more sensitive
Lipid Panel LDL, HDL, triglycerides, total cholesterol Fasting preferred for accurate triglyceride level
BMP/CMP Renal function, electrolytes, glucose Important for medication dosing (especially contrast)

Stress Testing

Stress tests provoke myocardial ischemia to detect CAD. They increase myocardial oxygen demand (exercise or pharmacologic stress) while monitoring for ischemia via EKG or imaging.

Type Method Best Candidates
Exercise EKG (Treadmill) Patient exercises on treadmill while monitored Patients who can exercise with interpretable EKG
Exercise Echo Echo before and after exercise Patients who can exercise but have abnormal baseline EKG
Nuclear Stress Test Radioactive tracer images perfusion at rest/stress Patients with uninterpretable EKG; more sensitive
Pharmacologic Stress (Dobutamine) Drug increases HR/contractility Patients who cannot exercise; asthma (avoid adenosine)
Pharmacologic Stress (Adenosine/Regadenoson) Coronary vasodilator with nuclear imaging Patients who cannot exercise (no asthma/COPD)
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Stress Test Preparation
Patient preparation is crucial for accurate results! Instruct patients to: (1) avoid caffeine for 24-48 hours before adenosine/regadenoson tests (caffeine blocks the vasodilator effect), (2) hold beta-blockers if ordered (they limit heart rate response), (3) wear comfortable clothing and walking shoes, (4) fast for 4 hours before the test, and (5) bring their inhaler if they have asthma but notify the staff - adenosine can cause bronchospasm. A well-prepared patient means accurate results and fewer repeat tests!

Coronary CT Angiography (CCTA)

Non-invasive CT imaging of the coronary arteries with IV contrast:

  • Excellent for ruling out CAD in low-to-intermediate risk patients
  • High negative predictive value - if normal, CAD is very unlikely
  • Can visualize plaque burden and stenosis severity
  • Requires slow, regular heart rate (beta-blockers often given)
  • Involves contrast and radiation exposure
Contrast Precautions

Before any contrast study, verify:

  • Renal function: Hold metformin 48 hours post-contrast; adequate hydration
  • Allergies: Previous contrast reactions require premedication
  • Thyroid: Contrast can affect thyroid function in susceptible patients

Cardiac Catheterization

Cardiac catheterization with coronary angiography is the gold standard for diagnosing and assessing the severity of CAD. It directly visualizes the coronary arteries and can be combined with intervention (PCI) if significant stenosis is found.

Indications for Cardiac Catheterization

  • Acute coronary syndrome (STEMI, NSTEMI, unstable angina)
  • Positive or high-risk stress test results
  • Stable angina refractory to medical therapy
  • Heart failure with suspected CAD
  • Preoperative evaluation for non-cardiac surgery (selected patients)
  • Sudden cardiac arrest survivors

Pre-Procedure Nursing Care

Before Cardiac Catheterization

1
Verify Informed Consent

Ensure patient understands the procedure, risks (bleeding, contrast reaction, arrhythmia, MI, stroke, death), benefits, and alternatives.

2
NPO Status

Patient should be NPO for 6-8 hours before procedure. Clear liquids may be allowed up to 2 hours before.

3
Laboratory Assessment

Check CBC (platelets), BMP (creatinine for contrast), PT/INR, PTT. Type and screen if surgery possible.

4
Medication Review

Hold metformin day of and 48 hours after. Hold anticoagulants per protocol. May continue aspirin and statins.

5
IV Access and Allergy Assessment

Establish large-bore IV access. Document any contrast or shellfish allergies - may need steroid premedication.

6
Baseline Assessment

Document vital signs, peripheral pulses (especially at access site - femoral or radial), skin color, and neurovascular status of extremities.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Mark Those Pulses!
Always document baseline peripheral pulses before catheterization! Mark the location of pedal pulses (dorsalis pedis and posterior tibial) with a skin marker before the procedure. This makes post-procedure assessment much easier and allows for accurate comparison. If radial access is used, perform Allen's test to verify adequate ulnar collateral flow before the procedure. After the procedure, compare pulses to baseline - any decrease could indicate arterial occlusion or hematoma compression and requires immediate action.

Post-Procedure Nursing Care

Access Site Position/Activity Key Monitoring
Femoral Bed rest 2-6 hours; keep leg straight; HOB ≤30° Groin site, distal pulses, bleeding/hematoma every 15 min x4, then hourly
Radial Can sit up immediately; wrist band in place 2-4 hours Radial pulse, hand color/sensation, bleeding, TR band pressure
Post-Cath Complications to Watch For
  • Bleeding/Hematoma: Most common; hold pressure if active bleeding
  • Retroperitoneal bleed: Back/flank pain, hypotension, tachycardia - emergency!
  • Pseudoaneurysm: Pulsatile mass at access site with bruit
  • Arterial occlusion: Loss of pulse, pain, pallor, paresthesia distal to site
  • Contrast-induced nephropathy: Monitor creatinine at 24-48 hours; hydrate well
  • Arrhythmias: Keep on telemetry; have crash cart available

Medical Management of CAD

Medical therapy for CAD focuses on relieving symptoms, preventing disease progression, reducing cardiovascular events, and improving survival. The "ABCDE" approach helps organize comprehensive CAD management.

Memory Aid: ABCDE of CAD Management
  • Aspirin (antiplatelet therapy) + ACE inhibitors
  • Beta-blockers + Blood pressure control
  • Cholesterol management (statins) + Cigarette cessation
  • Diet and Diabetes management
  • Exercise

Pharmacological Therapy

Drug Class Examples Purpose Key Nursing Points
Antiplatelet Aspirin, clopidogrel, ticagrelor Prevent platelet aggregation and clot formation Watch for bleeding; aspirin 81 mg daily is standard
Beta-blockers Metoprolol, carvedilol, bisoprolol Reduce HR, BP, and myocardial oxygen demand Monitor HR (hold if <60), BP; do not stop abruptly
Statins Atorvastatin, rosuvastatin Lower LDL, stabilize plaques Monitor liver function; report muscle pain (rhabdomyolysis)
ACE Inhibitors Lisinopril, ramipril, enalapril Prevent remodeling, reduce afterload Monitor K+ and creatinine; watch for cough, angioedema
Nitrates Nitroglycerin, isosorbide Dilate coronary arteries, reduce preload SL NTG: 3 doses 5 min apart; call 911 if no relief. Headache common
Calcium Channel Blockers Amlodipine, diltiazem Reduce BP, prevent spasm (Prinzmetal's) Monitor HR with diltiazem/verapamil; ankle edema common
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Nitroglycerin Patient Teaching
Your NTG teaching can save a life! Teach patients: (1) Keep NTG tablets in the original dark glass container - light degrades them. (2) Replace every 6 months or if they don't cause the slight "fizzing" or burning sensation under the tongue. (3) For chest pain: sit or lie down, take one tablet sublingual, wait 5 minutes - if pain continues, take second tablet and wait 5 more minutes - if still having pain after third tablet (15 minutes total), call 911 immediately. The old "take 3 then call" rule is outdated - if pain isn't improving, emergency services should be activated earlier!

Lifestyle Modifications

  • Smoking cessation: Most important modifiable risk factor; reduces risk by 50% within 1 year
  • Diet: Mediterranean diet, DASH diet; limit saturated fats, increase omega-3s
  • Exercise: 150 minutes moderate aerobic activity per week; cardiac rehab referral
  • Weight management: Goal BMI <25; even 5-10% weight loss improves CV risk
  • Alcohol: Moderate consumption (1 drink/day women, 2/day men) or abstain
  • Stress management: Chronic stress contributes to HTN and unhealthy behaviors
Cardiac Rehabilitation

Cardiac rehab is a class I recommendation for all patients with CAD. It includes supervised exercise training, education, and counseling. Studies show 20-30% reduction in mortality with cardiac rehab participation, yet it remains underutilized. Advocate for referral!

Interventional & Surgical Treatment

When medical therapy is insufficient or CAD is severe, revascularization procedures restore blood flow to the ischemic myocardium. The two main approaches are percutaneous coronary intervention (PCI) and coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG).

PCI (Angioplasty + Stent)

Procedure

  • Catheter-based; less invasive
  • Balloon inflates to open stenosis
  • Stent placed to keep artery open
  • Short recovery (1-2 days)

Best Candidates

  • 1-2 vessel disease
  • Acute STEMI (primary PCI)
  • High surgical risk patients
CABG Surgery

Procedure

  • Open heart surgery
  • Grafts bypass blocked arteries
  • Uses LIMA, saphenous vein, or radial artery
  • Longer recovery (weeks-months)

Best Candidates

  • Left main disease (≥50% stenosis)
  • 3-vessel disease, especially with diabetes
  • Reduced ejection fraction

Post-PCI Nursing Care

  • Dual antiplatelet therapy (DAPT): Aspirin + P2Y12 inhibitor (clopidogrel, ticagrelor, prasugrel) for 6-12 months minimum
  • Access site care: Same as diagnostic cath (see catheterization section)
  • Monitor for stent thrombosis: Recurrent chest pain, EKG changes - emergency!
  • Education: Do NOT stop DAPT early without cardiology guidance - stent thrombosis can be fatal
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
DAPT Compliance is Life or Death
Stopping dual antiplatelet therapy early can be fatal! When a stent is placed, the metal is a foreign body that's highly thrombogenic until the endothelium covers it (6-12 months). Stopping clopidogrel/ticagrelor early dramatically increases the risk of acute stent thrombosis - essentially causing a heart attack at the stent site with mortality rates of 20-40%. Make sure every stent patient understands: NEVER stop your antiplatelet medications without talking to your cardiologist first - even for dental work or minor surgery. Coordination with all healthcare providers is essential.

Post-CABG Nursing Care

System Key Monitoring Points
Cardiac Continuous telemetry; watch for arrhythmias (especially A-fib), tamponade signs
Respiratory Ventilator weaning; incentive spirometry; prevent atelectasis and pneumonia
Neurologic Assess for stroke (pump-related emboli); delirium common in elderly
Renal Monitor I&O, creatinine; contrast and pump can cause AKI
Wounds Sternal incision, leg harvest site; watch for infection, dehiscence
Pain Adequate pain control essential for mobility and pulmonary toileting
Post-CABG Sternal Precautions

The sternum is wired together after surgery and takes 6-8 weeks to heal. Teach patients:

  • No lifting more than 5-10 pounds for 6-8 weeks
  • No pushing or pulling heavy objects
  • Hug a pillow when coughing to splint the incision
  • No driving for 4-6 weeks (airbag and steering wheel risk)
  • Report any clicking, popping, or drainage from sternal wound

Key Takeaways

Atherosclerosis is the underlying process in CAD - plaque buildup narrows coronary arteries over time.

Stable angina is predictable and relieved by rest/NTG; unstable angina is an emergency.

Major modifiable risk factors: HTN, hyperlipidemia, diabetes, smoking, obesity, inactivity.

Cardiac cath is gold standard for CAD diagnosis; careful pre/post care prevents complications.

ABCDE: Aspirin/ACEi, Beta-blockers/BP, Cholesterol/Cigarettes, Diet/Diabetes, Exercise.

Post-stent DAPT (aspirin + clopidogrel/ticagrelor) is essential - never stop early!

PCI for 1-2 vessel disease; CABG for left main or 3-vessel disease.

Patient education on risk factor modification and medication adherence is crucial for outcomes.
