High-Yield Summary — Review This in 60 Seconds

The must-know facts from this guide, distilled for quick review.

  • Type 1 = absolute insulin deficiency (autoimmune destruction of beta cells). Always requires exogenous insulin. Oral agents alone are NOT sufficient.
  • Type 2 = insulin resistance + relative deficiency. Managed with lifestyle changes, oral agents, injectables, and eventually insulin as disease progresses.
  • Metformin is first-line for Type 2 diabetes. Decreases hepatic glucose production. Hold before contrast dye procedures (risk of lactic acidosis). No hypoglycemia when used alone.
  • Insulin timing matters: Rapid-acting (lispro, aspart) = give with meals. Regular = give 30 min before meals. NPH = give consistently, never IV. Glargine = give once daily at same time, never mix.
  • Sulfonylureas (glipizide, glyburide) stimulate insulin release from the pancreas. They DO cause hypoglycemia and weight gain.
  • GLP-1 agonists (-tide drugs: semaglutide, liraglutide) enhance insulin secretion, slow gastric emptying, promote weight loss. Black box warning for thyroid C-cell tumors.
  • SGLT2 inhibitors (-gliflozin drugs: empagliflozin, dapagliflozin) block glucose reabsorption in kidneys. Watch for UTIs, genital infections, and euglycemic DKA.
  • Rule of 15 for hypoglycemia: Give 15g fast-acting carbs, recheck BG in 15 minutes, repeat if still below 70 mg/dL.
  • Never mix glargine (Lantus) or detemir (Levemir) with other insulins. NPH is the only insulin that is cloudy (roll, don't shake).
  • When mixing insulins: Draw up clear (Regular) before cloudy (NPH). Memory aid: "RN" = Regular before NPH.

Diabetes Overview & Drug Therapy Goals

Diabetes mellitus is a chronic metabolic disorder characterized by hyperglycemia resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both. Understanding the pathophysiology of Type 1 versus Type 2 diabetes is essential for choosing appropriate pharmacological therapy.

Type 1 Diabetes

Autoimmune Beta-Cell Destruction

  • Absolute insulin deficiency
  • Usually diagnosed in childhood/adolescence
  • Requires exogenous insulin for survival
  • Prone to diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)
  • Not preventable with lifestyle changes

Drug Therapy

Insulin only (basal-bolus regimen or insulin pump)

Type 2 Diabetes

Insulin Resistance + Relative Deficiency

  • Cells resist insulin action
  • Usually diagnosed in adults (rising in youth)
  • Managed with lifestyle + oral agents + injectables
  • Prone to hyperglycemic hyperosmolar state (HHS)
  • Modifiable risk factors: obesity, inactivity

Drug Therapy

Metformin first-line, then add agents as needed. May require insulin as disease progresses.

Glycemic Targets (ADA Guidelines)

HbA1c Goal
< 7% (most adults)
Fasting Glucose
80-130 mg/dL
Postprandial (2hr)
< 180 mg/dL
Hypoglycemia Threshold
< 70 mg/dL
Key Concept

HbA1c reflects average blood glucose over the past 2-3 months. It correlates with the lifespan of red blood cells. An HbA1c of 7% corresponds to an average glucose of approximately 154 mg/dL. Conditions that affect RBC turnover (hemolytic anemia, blood transfusions, sickle cell disease) can give falsely low or high results.

Bottom Line: Type 1 = no insulin production, always needs insulin. Type 2 = insulin resistance, starts with metformin, may progress to needing insulin. Target HbA1c < 7% for most adults.

Insulin Types & Administration

Insulin is the cornerstone of Type 1 diabetes management and is often needed in advanced Type 2 diabetes. Knowing the onset, peak, and duration of each type is critical for preventing hypoglycemia and maintaining glucose control.

Category Generic (Brand) Onset Peak Duration Key Point
Rapid-Acting Lispro (Humalog), Aspart (NovoLog), Glulisine (Apidra) 10-15 min 1-2 hr 3-5 hr Give with meals (or within 15 min)
Short-Acting Regular insulin (Humulin R, Novolin R) 30-60 min 2-4 hr 6-8 hr Give 30 min before meals. Only insulin for IV use.
Intermediate NPH (Humulin N, Novolin N) 1-2 hr 4-12 hr 18-24 hr Cloudy. Roll gently, never shake. Never give IV.
Long-Acting Glargine (Lantus, Basaglar), Detemir (Levemir) 1-2 hr No peak (flat) 24 hr Give once daily, same time. Never mix with other insulins.
Ultra Long-Acting Degludec (Tresiba) 1 hr No peak 42+ hr Flexible dosing time. Very stable basal coverage.
Memory Aid: Mixing Insulins

"RN" = Regular before NPH

When mixing insulins in one syringe, always draw up clear (Regular) before cloudy (NPH). This prevents contaminating the Regular vial with NPH particles.

  • Inject air into NPH vial first (do NOT withdraw)
  • Inject air into Regular vial, then draw up Regular
  • Then draw up NPH into the same syringe
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Peaks = Hypoglycemia Risk
The peak is when the insulin is working hardest — and when hypoglycemia risk is greatest. Rapid-acting peaks in 1-2 hours (watch right after meals), NPH peaks at 4-12 hours (watch for late afternoon or overnight lows), and long-acting insulins like glargine have no peak (lower hypo risk). Always ensure food is available at peak times. If a patient on NPH skips lunch, they are at high risk for hypoglycemia in the afternoon.

Insulin Administration Nursing Considerations

Critical Insulin Safety Points

1
Always Verify Blood Glucose Before Administration

Check BG before giving insulin. Hold and notify provider if BG is below the facility's threshold (commonly < 70 mg/dL). Follow sliding scale parameters carefully.

2
Rotate Injection Sites

Use abdomen (fastest absorption), upper arms, thighs, or buttocks. Rotate within the same region for consistency. Avoid injecting into areas that will be exercised (increases absorption rate).

3
Storage and Handling

Unopened insulin: refrigerate (36-46°F). In-use vials/pens: room temperature for 28 days (check specific product). Never freeze insulin. Discard if discolored, clumped, or expired.

4
High-Alert Medication

Insulin is an ISMP high-alert medication. Require independent double-check per facility policy. Use insulin-specific syringes only. Never abbreviate "units" as "U" (can be misread as "0").

Safety Alert: Sound-Alike Insulins

Humalog vs. Humulin and NovoLog vs. Novolin are commonly confused. Humalog and NovoLog are rapid-acting. Humulin and Novolin can be Regular (R) or NPH (N). Always verify the exact insulin type, not just the brand.

Bottom Line: Know onset/peak/duration for each insulin type. Regular is the only insulin given IV. Never mix glargine or detemir. Draw Regular before NPH ("RN"). Peak time = highest hypoglycemia risk.

Metformin (Biguanides)

Metformin (Glucophage) is the first-line drug for Type 2 diabetes and one of the most commonly prescribed medications worldwide. It works primarily by decreasing hepatic glucose production and improving insulin sensitivity in peripheral tissues.

Quick Reference: Metformin

Drug Class
Biguanide
Route
PO (with meals)
Typical Starting Dose
500 mg BID or 850 mg daily
Max Dose
2,550 mg/day

Mechanism of Action

  • Decreases hepatic glucose production (primary mechanism)
  • Increases insulin sensitivity in muscle and fat tissue
  • Decreases intestinal absorption of glucose
  • Does NOT stimulate insulin secretion from beta cells

Key Advantages

  • No hypoglycemia when used alone (does not increase insulin secretion)
  • Weight neutral or mild weight loss (unlike sulfonylureas/insulin)
  • Proven cardiovascular benefit
  • Inexpensive and well-established safety profile
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Metformin GI Side Effects
GI side effects are the #1 reason patients stop metformin. Nausea, diarrhea, and abdominal cramping are common when starting therapy. Teach patients to take it with food and to start at a low dose with gradual titration. The extended-release (ER) formulation causes significantly fewer GI side effects and can be a good alternative for patients who can't tolerate immediate-release.
Critical: Lactic Acidosis Risk

Lactic acidosis is rare but life-threatening. Know these contraindications and precautions:

  • Hold before IV contrast dye procedures (hold 48 hours before and after; restart only after renal function confirmed stable)
  • Contraindicated if eGFR < 30 mL/min (severe renal impairment)
  • Use caution with hepatic impairment, heart failure, excessive alcohol use
  • Signs of lactic acidosis: malaise, myalgia, respiratory distress, unusual fatigue, abdominal pain
Patient Teaching: Metformin
  • Take with meals to reduce GI side effects
  • Avoid excessive alcohol (increases lactic acidosis risk)
  • Report symptoms of lactic acidosis: unusual muscle pain, difficulty breathing, unusual fatigue
  • May cause vitamin B12 deficiency with long-term use — monitor levels
  • Inform all providers you take metformin before any imaging with contrast dye
Bottom Line: Metformin is first-line for T2DM. No hypoglycemia when used alone. Hold for contrast dye procedures. Contraindicated in severe renal disease. Take with food to minimize GI effects.

Sulfonylureas & Meglitinides

Both of these classes work by stimulating insulin secretion from pancreatic beta cells. Because they increase insulin release regardless of glucose levels, both carry a risk of hypoglycemia.

Sulfonylureas

Drug Generation Dosing Key Notes
Glipizide (Glucotrol) 2nd 5-20 mg/day, give 30 min before meals Shorter acting, preferred in elderly
Glyburide (DiaBeta, Micronase) 2nd 2.5-20 mg/day Longer acting, higher hypo risk. Avoid in renal impairment.
Glimepiride (Amaryl) 2nd 1-8 mg/day Once daily dosing. Lower hypo risk than glyburide.

Mechanism of Action

Sulfonylureas bind to receptors on pancreatic beta cells, closing potassium channels and triggering insulin release. They require functioning beta cells to work (ineffective in Type 1 diabetes).

Side Effects & Warnings
  • Hypoglycemia (most significant adverse effect — especially with glyburide, in elderly, or with skipped meals)
  • Weight gain (insulin secretion promotes fat storage)
  • Disulfiram-like reaction with alcohol (flushing, nausea)
  • Cross-allergy with sulfa drugs (use with caution in sulfa-allergic patients)

Meglitinides

Drug Dosing Key Notes
Repaglinide (Prandin) 0.5-4 mg before each meal Take within 30 min of meals. Skip dose if skip meal.
Nateglinide (Starlix) 60-120 mg before meals Faster onset, shorter duration than sulfonylureas.

Meglitinides have a similar mechanism to sulfonylureas but act faster and for a shorter duration. They target postprandial glucose spikes and are dosed with meals. If a meal is skipped, the dose is skipped.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Sulfonylurea Hypoglycemia
Sulfonylureas cause hypoglycemia because they stimulate insulin release regardless of blood glucose level. The risk is highest when patients skip meals, exercise more than usual, drink alcohol, or have renal impairment (drug accumulates). Teach patients to eat regular meals and always carry a fast-acting carbohydrate source. Glyburide has the highest hypoglycemia risk due to its long duration and active metabolites — glipizide or glimepiride is preferred in the elderly.
Clinical Pearl: "No Meal, No Pill"

For meglitinides (repaglinide, nateglinide), teach patients: "No meal, no pill." These drugs are taken before each meal to cover postprandial glucose. If the patient skips a meal, they skip the dose. This flexible dosing makes them useful for patients with irregular eating patterns.

Bottom Line: Sulfonylureas and meglitinides stimulate insulin release from beta cells. Both cause hypoglycemia and weight gain. Sulfonylureas are dosed daily; meglitinides are dosed with meals ("no meal, no pill").

TZDs & Alpha-Glucosidase Inhibitors

Thiazolidinediones (TZDs) — The "Insulin Sensitizers"

TZDs improve insulin sensitivity in muscle and adipose tissue by activating PPARgamma receptors. They do not increase insulin secretion and take 2-4 weeks for full effect.

Drug Dosing Key Notes
Pioglitazone (Actos) 15-45 mg daily May improve lipid profile. Bladder cancer risk with prolonged use.
Rosiglitazone (Avandia) 4-8 mg daily Restricted due to cardiovascular concerns. Rarely used.
TZD Warnings
  • Fluid retention and edema — can precipitate or worsen heart failure. Contraindicated in NYHA Class III-IV heart failure.
  • Weight gain (fluid retention + fat redistribution)
  • Increased fracture risk (especially in postmenopausal women)
  • Hepatotoxicity — monitor LFTs at baseline and periodically
  • Slow onset of action (2-4 weeks for effect, 8-12 weeks for full effect)

Alpha-Glucosidase Inhibitors

These drugs slow the breakdown and absorption of complex carbohydrates in the small intestine, reducing postprandial glucose spikes.

Drug Dosing Key Notes
Acarbose (Precose) 25-100 mg with first bite of each meal Must take with food to work. Monitor LFTs.
Miglitol (Glyset) 25-100 mg with first bite of each meal Similar to acarbose; better absorbed.
Clinical Pearl: Treating Hypoglycemia on AGIs

If a patient on an alpha-glucosidase inhibitor becomes hypoglycemic, give GLUCOSE (dextrose) tablets — not sucrose (table sugar)! These drugs block the breakdown of complex sugars, so sucrose, juice, and crackers will not be absorbed fast enough. Only pure glucose (dextrose) will work because it doesn't need to be broken down.

AGI Side Effects & Teaching
  • GI effects are very common: flatulence, bloating, abdominal discomfort, diarrhea
  • Start low and titrate slowly to minimize GI symptoms
  • Take with the first bite of each meal (must be present with food to work)
  • Minimal hypoglycemia risk when used alone
Bottom Line: TZDs improve insulin sensitivity but cause fluid retention (contraindicated in severe HF) and fracture risk. AGIs slow carb absorption — treat hypoglycemia with glucose tablets, NOT regular sugar.

DPP-4 Inhibitors & GLP-1 Receptor Agonists

These two classes are based on the incretin system. Incretins (like GLP-1) are gut hormones released after eating that stimulate insulin secretion and suppress glucagon in a glucose-dependent manner. This means they are less likely to cause hypoglycemia.

DPP-4 Inhibitors (The "-gliptins")

DPP-4 is the enzyme that breaks down incretins. By inhibiting DPP-4, these drugs prolong the action of natural GLP-1 and GIP, enhancing glucose-dependent insulin secretion.

Drug Dosing Key Notes
Sitagliptin (Januvia) 100 mg daily Dose adjust for renal impairment
Saxagliptin (Onglyza) 2.5-5 mg daily Heart failure risk — use with caution
Linagliptin (Tradjenta) 5 mg daily No renal dose adjustment needed (biliary excretion)
DPP-4 Inhibitor Advantages
  • Weight neutral (no significant weight gain or loss)
  • Low hypoglycemia risk (glucose-dependent mechanism)
  • Well tolerated with few side effects
  • Simple once-daily oral dosing
  • Watch for: joint pain (arthralgia), rare pancreatitis risk, upper respiratory infection symptoms

GLP-1 Receptor Agonists (The "-tide" Drugs)

GLP-1 agonists mimic the GLP-1 incretin hormone at pharmacologic levels, providing much stronger effects than DPP-4 inhibitors. They are injectable (except oral semaglutide) and have significant benefits beyond glucose control.

Drug Route/Frequency Key Notes
Liraglutide (Victoza) SubQ daily Also approved for cardiovascular risk reduction
Semaglutide (Ozempic) SubQ weekly Significant weight loss. Also available as oral (Rybelsus).
Dulaglutide (Trulicity) SubQ weekly CV benefit demonstrated. Single-use pen.
Exenatide (Byetta, Bydureon) SubQ twice daily or weekly (ER) First GLP-1 approved. Give Byetta within 60 min before meals.
GLP-1 Agonist Warnings
  • Black box warning: thyroid C-cell tumors (medullary thyroid carcinoma in animal studies). Contraindicated in patients with personal or family history of medullary thyroid cancer or MEN 2 syndrome.
  • GI side effects: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea (most common, usually improve over time)
  • Pancreatitis risk: Teach patients to report severe, persistent abdominal pain
  • Delayed gastric emptying — caution with gastroparesis
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
GLP-1 Triple Benefit
GLP-1 agonists offer three key benefits beyond glucose lowering: (1) Weight loss — they slow gastric emptying and reduce appetite, making them especially beneficial for overweight T2DM patients; (2) Cardiovascular protection — liraglutide, semaglutide, and dulaglutide have proven CV benefit; (3) Low hypoglycemia risk — they only stimulate insulin when glucose is elevated. The main trade-off is GI side effects (nausea) and the thyroid C-cell tumor black box warning.
DPP-4 vs. GLP-1: How to Remember the Difference

DPP-4 inhibitors = "protect" your own incretins (oral, mild effect, weight neutral)

GLP-1 agonists = "replace" with a superpowered version (injectable, stronger effect, weight loss)

Do NOT combine DPP-4 inhibitors with GLP-1 agonists — they work on the same pathway and there is no added benefit.

Bottom Line: DPP-4 inhibitors ("-gliptins") are well-tolerated oral agents with low hypo risk. GLP-1 agonists ("-tides") are injectable, promote weight loss, and have CV benefits but carry a black box warning for thyroid C-cell tumors. Don't combine the two classes.

SGLT2 Inhibitors

Sodium-glucose co-transporter 2 (SGLT2) inhibitors represent a unique class that works in the kidneys rather than the pancreas. They block glucose reabsorption in the proximal tubule, causing excess glucose to be excreted in the urine (glycosuria).

Drug Dosing Key Notes
Empagliflozin (Jardiance) 10-25 mg daily Proven CV and renal benefits. Also approved for HF.
Dapagliflozin (Farxiga) 5-10 mg daily Approved for HF and CKD regardless of diabetes status.
Canagliflozin (Invokana) 100-300 mg daily Associated with increased amputation risk (assess feet). CV benefit.
Memory Aid: SGLT2 = "Sugar Goes to Lavatory Too"

SGLT2 inhibitors cause the kidneys to dump glucose into the urine. This glucose-rich urine creates a perfect environment for infections. Remember: Sugar Goes to Lavatory Too — and that sugar feeds yeast and bacteria, causing UTIs and genital infections.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Euglycemic DKA Warning
SGLT2 inhibitors can cause DKA even with NORMAL blood glucose levels (euglycemic DKA). This is a dangerous trap because providers may not suspect DKA when the glucose reading looks normal. If a patient on an SGLT2 inhibitor presents with nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, or fruity breath, check for ketones even if blood glucose is below 250 mg/dL. Hold SGLT2 inhibitors before surgery (at least 3-4 days) and during acute illness.
SGLT2 Side Effects & Warnings
  • Genital yeast infections (glycosuria feeds candida — more common in women)
  • Urinary tract infections
  • Euglycemic DKA (DKA with normal glucose — check ketones if symptomatic)
  • Hypotension/dehydration (osmotic diuresis) — monitor BP, encourage hydration
  • Fournier's gangrene (rare necrotizing fasciitis of perineum — teach patients to report genital/perineal pain or tenderness)
  • Canagliflozin: increased risk of lower limb amputations (assess feet regularly)
SGLT2 Benefits Beyond Glucose
  • Cardiovascular protection (reduced hospitalizations for heart failure)
  • Renal protection (slows CKD progression)
  • Weight loss (losing calories through glycosuria)
  • Blood pressure reduction (mild diuretic effect)
  • Low hypoglycemia risk when used alone
Bottom Line: SGLT2 inhibitors ("-gliflozin") work in the kidneys to dump glucose in urine. Benefits include weight loss, CV protection, and renal protection. Watch for genital infections, UTIs, euglycemic DKA, and dehydration. Hold before surgery.

Hypoglycemia Management

Hypoglycemia (blood glucose < 70 mg/dL) is the most common and dangerous acute complication of diabetes medication therapy. Rapid recognition and treatment are critical to prevent seizures, loss of consciousness, and death.

Signs and Symptoms

Mild-Moderate (Adrenergic)
  • Tremors/shakiness
  • Sweating (diaphoresis)
  • Tachycardia (rapid heart rate)
  • Hunger
  • Anxiety, irritability
  • Pallor, tingling
Severe (Neuroglycopenic)
  • Confusion
  • Slurred speech
  • Seizures
  • Loss of consciousness
  • Behavioral changes
  • Visual disturbances
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Rule of 15
The Rule of 15 is the standard protocol for conscious hypoglycemia: Give 15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrate, wait 15 minutes, recheck blood glucose. Repeat if still below 70 mg/dL. Examples of 15g fast-acting carbs: 4 oz (1/2 cup) juice or regular soda, 3-4 glucose tablets, 1 tablespoon of honey or sugar. Once BG normalizes, follow with a complex carb and protein snack to prevent rebound. If the patient is unconscious, never give anything by mouth — administer IV dextrose (D50 in adults) or IM glucagon.

Hypoglycemia Treatment Protocol

1
Conscious Patient: Rule of 15

Give 15g fast-acting carbohydrate (4 oz juice, 3-4 glucose tabs). Recheck in 15 minutes. Repeat if BG still < 70 mg/dL. Follow with complex carb + protein snack.

2
Unconscious Patient: IV Dextrose or Glucagon

Never give oral glucose to an unconscious patient (aspiration risk). Give IV dextrose 50% (D50W) 25-50 mL push. If no IV access, give IM glucagon 1 mg. Position on side to protect airway.

3
Post-Recovery

Investigate the cause (skipped meal? too much insulin? increased activity?). Continue monitoring BG every 15-30 minutes until stable. Document and report the event. Educate patient on prevention.

Critical Alert: Hypoglycemia Unawareness

Some patients (especially those with long-standing diabetes or on beta-blockers) lose the early adrenergic warning signs (no sweating, no tremors, no tachycardia). They may progress directly to severe neuroglycopenic symptoms (confusion, seizures, unconsciousness). These patients need more frequent glucose monitoring and higher glucose targets.

Bottom Line: Rule of 15 for conscious hypo. IV D50 or IM glucagon for unconscious patients — never give oral glucose. Beta-blockers can mask hypoglycemia symptoms. Always investigate the cause after treatment.

Memory Aid: Which Drugs Cause Hypoglycemia?

Only three classes cause hypoglycemia when used alone:

  • Insulin
  • Sulfonylureas
  • Meglitinides

Remember: "ISM" — these three Increase inSulin secretion or replace it, so they cause hypo regardless of glucose levels. All other classes are glucose-dependent or work through other Mechanisms.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Hypo Risk Drugs
Most diabetes medications do NOT cause hypoglycemia when used alone. Only insulin, sulfonylureas, and meglitinides directly stimulate insulin release regardless of blood glucose level. All other classes (metformin, TZDs, DPP-4 inhibitors, GLP-1 agonists, SGLT2 inhibitors, AGIs) work through glucose-dependent mechanisms. However, ANY drug can contribute to hypoglycemia when combined with insulin or a sulfonylurea, so always assess the full medication list.

Drug Interactions to Watch

High-Yield Drug Interactions

1
Beta-Blockers + Insulin/Sulfonylureas

Beta-blockers mask tachycardia and tremors (adrenergic symptoms of hypoglycemia). Sweating is preserved. Patients may not recognize they are hypoglycemic until severe neuroglycopenic symptoms appear.

2
Corticosteroids + All Diabetes Meds

Corticosteroids cause hyperglycemia by increasing insulin resistance and gluconeogenesis. Patients on diabetes medications may need dose increases when starting steroids. Monitor glucose closely.

3
Alcohol + Metformin or Sulfonylureas

Alcohol increases lactic acidosis risk with metformin and increases hypoglycemia risk with sulfonylureas (inhibits gluconeogenesis). Advise moderation and never drink on an empty stomach.

4
ACE Inhibitors/ARBs + SGLT2 Inhibitors

Both classes can lower blood pressure and affect renal function. Monitor blood pressure and kidney function (BUN, creatinine, eGFR) when used together.

Bottom Line: Only insulin, sulfonylureas, and meglitinides cause hypoglycemia alone. Beta-blockers mask hypo symptoms. Steroids raise glucose. Always review the full med list for interactions.

Pediatric Considerations

Diabetes medication management in children and adolescents requires special considerations related to growth, development, and unique physiological factors.

Type 1 Diabetes in Children

Type 1 is the most common form of diabetes in children. Key pediatric considerations:

  • Insulin doses are weight-based (typically 0.5-1.0 units/kg/day total, adjusted by growth and puberty)
  • Insulin pumps and continuous glucose monitors (CGMs) are increasingly standard of care
  • Hypoglycemia may present differently: irritability, behavioral changes, difficulty concentrating in school
  • Young children cannot reliably report symptoms — educate parents and school staff on recognition
  • Adolescents in puberty need higher insulin doses due to growth hormone and sex hormone effects on insulin resistance
Type 2 Diabetes in Youth

T2DM is rising in children and adolescents, particularly those with obesity.

  • Metformin is the only oral agent FDA-approved for children (age 10+)
  • Insulin is the other approved medication for pediatric T2DM
  • Lifestyle modifications (diet, exercise) are emphasized alongside pharmacotherapy
  • GLP-1 agonists (liraglutide) recently approved for adolescents 10+ with T2DM
  • Other oral agents (sulfonylureas, SGLT2 inhibitors) are generally NOT approved for pediatric use
School Management & Family Education
  • Develop a Diabetes Medical Management Plan (DMMP) for school
  • School nurses and teachers need training on hypo/hyperglycemia recognition
  • Fast-acting glucose should be available in the classroom, gym, and cafeteria
  • Children should wear medical alert identification
  • Adolescents may struggle with medication adherence due to peer pressure and body image — provide developmentally appropriate education
Bottom Line: T1DM is most common in children; insulin is always required with weight-based dosing. For pediatric T2DM, metformin is first-line. Educate families AND school personnel. Hypoglycemia may present as behavioral changes in young children.
