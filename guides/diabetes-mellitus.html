High-Yield Summary — Review This in 60 Seconds

The must-know facts from this guide, distilled for quick review.

  • Type 1 = autoimmune beta-cell destruction = absolute insulin deficiency. Always requires insulin. Prone to DKA. Usually diagnosed in youth.
  • Type 2 = insulin resistance + relative deficiency. Managed with lifestyle + oral agents + injectables. Prone to HHS. Usually diagnosed in adults (rising in youth).
  • DKA: Glucose 250-600, pH < 7.35, ketones present, Kussmaul respirations, fruity breath. Treat with IV fluids + IV regular insulin + potassium replacement.
  • HHS: Glucose > 600 (often > 1000), NO significant ketones, severe dehydration, altered mental status. Treat with aggressive IV fluids first.
  • Insulin timing: Rapid (lispro/aspart) = with meals. Regular = 30 min before meals (only insulin for IV). NPH = cloudy, roll don't shake. Glargine = once daily, never mix.
  • Metformin is first-line for T2DM. No hypoglycemia alone. Hold for contrast dye. Contraindicated if eGFR < 30.
  • Hypoglycemia (< 70 mg/dL): Rule of 15 if conscious. IV D50 or IM glucagon if unconscious. Beta-blockers mask symptoms.
  • HbA1c < 7% for most adults. Reflects average glucose over 2-3 months. Each 1% change ≈ 28-30 mg/dL average glucose change.
  • Three P's of hyperglycemia: Polyuria, Polydipsia, Polyphagia. Plus fatigue and blurred vision.
  • Chronic complications: Retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy (microvascular). CAD, stroke, PVD (macrovascular). Annual eye/foot exams essential.

Pathophysiology: Type 1 vs Type 2

Diabetes mellitus is a group of metabolic disorders characterized by chronic hyperglycemia resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both. Understanding the fundamental differences between Type 1 and Type 2 guides all clinical decision-making.

Insulin: The Key Player

Insulin is a hormone produced by the beta cells of the pancreatic islets of Langerhans. Its primary function is to allow glucose to enter cells for energy. Without adequate insulin (or when cells resist it), glucose accumulates in the blood while cells starve.

Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus

Autoimmune Beta-Cell Destruction

  • Absolute insulin deficiency (no insulin produced)
  • Autoimmune destruction of beta cells
  • Usually diagnosed in childhood/adolescence
  • Sudden onset with classic "3 P's"
  • Requires exogenous insulin for survival
  • Prone to DKA (diabetic ketoacidosis)
  • Accounts for 5-10% of all diabetes
  • Often normal or underweight at diagnosis
Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus

Insulin Resistance + Relative Deficiency

  • Cells resist insulin action (insulin present but ineffective)
  • Progressive beta-cell dysfunction over time
  • Usually diagnosed in adults (rising in youth)
  • Gradual onset, often asymptomatic initially
  • Managed with lifestyle + oral agents ± insulin
  • Prone to HHS (hyperglycemic hyperosmolar state)
  • Accounts for 90-95% of all diabetes
  • Often overweight or obese at diagnosis
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Type 1 vs Type 2 Made Simple
Think of it like a lock and key: In Type 1, there are no keys (no insulin produced) — you must provide external keys (inject insulin). In Type 2, the keys exist but the locks are rusty (cells resist insulin) — you can oil the locks (sensitizers like metformin), make more keys (sulfonylureas), or eventually provide new keys (insulin). Type 1 = no keys. Type 2 = rusty locks.
Memory Aid: The Three P's of Hyperglycemia

Polyuria (excessive urination — osmotic diuresis from glucose in urine)

Polydipsia (excessive thirst — dehydration from fluid loss)

Polyphagia (excessive hunger — cells can't use glucose, body thinks it's starving)

Bottom Line: Type 1 = no insulin production (autoimmune), always needs insulin, prone to DKA. Type 2 = insulin resistance + declining production, starts with oral agents, prone to HHS. Type 2 is far more common (90-95%).

Key Concept: HbA1c

HbA1c reflects average blood glucose over the past 2-3 months (correlates with RBC lifespan). An HbA1c of 7% ≈ average glucose of 154 mg/dL. Conditions that affect RBC turnover (hemolytic anemia, blood transfusions, sickle cell disease) can give falsely low or high results. HbA1c does NOT require fasting.

Acute Complications: DKA vs HHS

These are life-threatening emergencies that require immediate recognition and intervention. Understanding the differences is essential for prioritizing treatment.

DKA (Diabetic Ketoacidosis)

Primarily Type 1 (can occur in T2)

  • Glucose: 250-600 mg/dL
  • pH < 7.35 (metabolic acidosis)
  • Ketones present (blood and urine)
  • Kussmaul respirations (deep, rapid)
  • Fruity/acetone breath
  • Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
  • Dehydration, warm/dry skin
  • Develops rapidly (hours to days)

Triggers

Missed insulin, illness/infection, new-onset T1DM, surgery, stress

HHS (Hyperglycemic Hyperosmolar State)

Primarily Type 2

  • Glucose: > 600 mg/dL (often > 1000)
  • pH normal or near-normal (> 7.30)
  • No significant ketones
  • No Kussmaul respirations
  • No fruity breath
  • Severe dehydration
  • Altered mental status (confusion → coma)
  • Develops slowly (days to weeks)

Triggers

Illness/infection, inadequate fluid intake, medications (steroids, thiazides), elderly

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
DKA vs HHS: The Key Distinction
The difference comes down to ketones. In DKA, there is NO insulin at all, so the body breaks down fat for energy, producing ketones that cause acidosis. In HHS, there IS some insulin (enough to prevent ketone formation but not enough to control glucose), so no acidosis occurs. Instead, glucose climbs extremely high, causing massive osmotic diuresis and dehydration. Remember: DKA = acidosis with ketones. HHS = extreme dehydration without ketones.

DKA Treatment Priorities

DKA Management Protocol

1
Aggressive IV Fluid Replacement

Start with 0.9% NS at 1-1.5 L/hr for the first hour. Switch to 0.45% NS when hemodynamically stable. Add dextrose (D5) when glucose reaches 250 mg/dL to prevent hypoglycemia while continuing insulin.

2
IV Regular Insulin Drip

Continuous infusion of regular insulin (only type for IV use). Goal: decrease glucose 50-75 mg/dL per hour. Do NOT drop too fast (risk of cerebral edema, especially in children).

3
Potassium Replacement

Critical: Serum K+ may appear normal or high on admission, but total body potassium is depleted. As acidosis is corrected and insulin is given, K+ shifts into cells and levels DROP rapidly. Monitor K+ every 1-2 hours. Hold insulin if K+ < 3.3 mEq/L until repleted.

4
Monitor & Correct Acidosis

Check ABGs frequently. Bicarbonate may be given if pH < 6.9 (severe acidosis). Resolution = glucose < 200 + pH > 7.30 + bicarb ≥ 15 + anion gap closed.

Critical Alert: Potassium in DKA

Potassium is the #1 killer in DKA management. Insulin drives potassium into cells. If you give insulin without replacing potassium, the patient can develop fatal hypokalemia and cardiac arrest. Always check K+ BEFORE starting insulin. If K+ < 3.3: hold insulin, replace K+ first. If K+ 3.3-5.3: give K+ with insulin. If K+ > 5.3: hold K+, recheck in 2 hours.

Clinical Pearl: HHS Treatment Difference

HHS treatment prioritizes aggressive fluid replacement FIRST (patients may be 8-12 liters deficit). Insulin is secondary because the extreme glucose level will drop significantly with rehydration alone. HHS patients are usually elderly and at higher risk for fluid overload — monitor heart and lung sounds closely.

Bottom Line: DKA = acidosis with ketones (Type 1), treat with fluids + insulin + K+ replacement. HHS = extreme hyperglycemia without ketones (Type 2), treat with aggressive fluids first. Always check K+ before starting insulin in DKA.

Chronic Complications

Prolonged hyperglycemia damages blood vessels throughout the body. Complications are classified as microvascular (small vessel damage) or macrovascular (large vessel damage). Tight glucose control significantly reduces these risks.

Microvascular Complications

Complication What Happens Nursing Priority
Retinopathy Damage to retinal blood vessels → microaneurysms, hemorrhages, vision loss Annual dilated eye exams. Report vision changes immediately.
Nephropathy Glomerular damage → proteinuria → progressive renal failure Annual urine albumin screening. Control BP (ACE-I/ARB are renoprotective). Monitor BUN/creatinine.
Neuropathy Nerve damage → numbness/tingling in extremities (peripheral), gastroparesis, orthostatic hypotension (autonomic) Daily foot inspections. Test sensation with monofilament. Proper footwear. Report new numbness or tingling.

Macrovascular Complications

  • Coronary artery disease (CAD): DM patients have 2-4x higher risk of heart disease. #1 cause of death in diabetes.
  • Cerebrovascular disease: Increased stroke risk. Control BP, glucose, and lipids.
  • Peripheral vascular disease (PVD): Poor circulation to extremities → delayed wound healing → risk for amputation.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Diabetic Foot Care is Life or Limb
Foot complications are the #1 cause of non-traumatic lower-limb amputations. Neuropathy means patients can't feel injuries, and PVD means wounds heal poorly. Teach patients: inspect feet DAILY (use a mirror for soles), wash with warm (not hot) water, moisturize but NOT between toes, wear properly fitting shoes (never go barefoot), cut toenails straight across, and report ANY break in skin immediately. A small blister can become a limb-threatening infection.
Bottom Line: Microvascular = eyes, kidneys, nerves. Macrovascular = heart, brain, peripheral vessels. Annual eye exams, urine albumin checks, and daily foot inspections are essential. Tight glucose AND BP control reduce all complications.

Insulin Therapy

Insulin is the cornerstone of Type 1 management and is often needed as Type 2 progresses. Knowing onset, peak, and duration for each type is critical for preventing hypoglycemia.

Category Generic (Brand) Onset Peak Duration Key Point
Rapid-Acting Lispro (Humalog), Aspart (NovoLog) 10-15 min 1-2 hr 3-5 hr Give with meals (within 15 min)
Short-Acting Regular (Humulin R, Novolin R) 30-60 min 2-4 hr 6-8 hr Give 30 min before meals. Only insulin for IV.
Intermediate NPH (Humulin N, Novolin N) 1-2 hr 4-12 hr 18-24 hr Cloudy. Roll gently, never shake. Never give IV.
Long-Acting Glargine (Lantus), Detemir (Levemir) 1-2 hr No peak 24 hr Give once daily at same time. Never mix.
Memory Aid: Mixing Insulins

"RN" = Regular before NPH. When mixing in one syringe: inject air into NPH vial first (don't withdraw), inject air into Regular vial and draw up Regular, THEN draw up NPH. Clear before cloudy.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Insulin Peak = Hypoglycemia Risk
The peak is when insulin works hardest and hypoglycemia risk is greatest. Rapid-acting peaks in 1-2 hours (watch right after meals). NPH peaks at 4-12 hours (watch for late afternoon or overnight lows). Long-acting insulins have no peak (lower hypo risk). Always ensure food is available at peak times. A patient on NPH who skips lunch is at high risk for afternoon hypoglycemia.
Safety Alert: Sound-Alike Insulins

Humalog vs. Humulin and NovoLog vs. Novolin are commonly confused. Humalog and NovoLog are rapid-acting. Humulin and Novolin can be R (Regular) or N (NPH). Always verify the exact insulin type, not just the brand name. Insulin is an ISMP high-alert medication — require independent double-check.

Bottom Line: Know onset/peak/duration. Regular is the only insulin for IV. Never mix glargine or detemir. Draw Regular before NPH ("RN"). Peak time = highest hypo risk. Rotate injection sites within the same region.

Oral & Injectable Agents

Type 2 diabetes is managed with a stepwise approach: lifestyle modifications first, then metformin, then add-on agents as needed. Understanding each class helps nurses anticipate side effects and provide targeted teaching.

Drug Class Key Drug(s) Mechanism Hypo Risk? Must-Know Nursing Point
Biguanide Metformin (Glucophage) Decreases hepatic glucose production No (alone) Hold for contrast dye. Monitor renal function. GI side effects common.
Sulfonylureas Glipizide, Glyburide, Glimepiride Stimulates insulin secretion from beta cells YES Causes hypoglycemia and weight gain. Take before meals.
Meglitinides Repaglinide (Prandin), Nateglinide (Starlix) Rapid, short insulin secretion YES "No meal, no pill." Take within 30 min of meals.
TZDs Pioglitazone (Actos) Increases insulin sensitivity No (alone) Contraindicated in HF (NYHA III-IV). Fluid retention, fracture risk.
DPP-4 Inhibitors Sitagliptin (Januvia), Linagliptin (Tradjenta) Prolongs natural incretin hormones No (alone) Well tolerated. Weight neutral. Watch for pancreatitis.
GLP-1 Agonists Semaglutide (Ozempic), Liraglutide (Victoza) Mimics GLP-1 incretin at high levels No (alone) Black box: thyroid C-cell tumors. Promotes weight loss. CV benefit.
SGLT2 Inhibitors Empagliflozin (Jardiance), Dapagliflozin (Farxiga) Blocks renal glucose reabsorption No (alone) Watch for UTIs, genital infections, euglycemic DKA. CV/renal benefit.
AGIs Acarbose (Precose) Slows carbohydrate absorption No (alone) GI effects (gas, bloating). Treat hypo with glucose tablets only.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Metformin: The First-Line Champion
Metformin is first-line for T2DM for good reason: no hypoglycemia when used alone, weight neutral/slight loss, cardiovascular benefit, inexpensive, and well-established. The main concerns are GI side effects (take with food, use ER formulation) and lactic acidosis risk (hold for contrast dye, contraindicated if eGFR < 30). Long-term use can cause B12 deficiency — monitor levels.
Which Drugs Cause Hypoglycemia Alone?

Only three classes cause hypoglycemia when used as monotherapy:

  • Insulin (replaces endogenous insulin)
  • Sulfonylureas (stimulate insulin release regardless of glucose)
  • Meglitinides (stimulate rapid insulin release)

All other classes work through glucose-dependent mechanisms. However, ANY drug can contribute to hypoglycemia when combined with insulin or a sulfonylurea.

Bottom Line: Metformin is first-line for T2DM. Only insulin, sulfonylureas, and meglitinides cause hypo alone. GLP-1 agonists and SGLT2 inhibitors offer weight loss + CV benefits. TZDs are contraindicated in severe HF. Treat hypo on acarbose with glucose tablets only.

Hypoglycemia Management

Hypoglycemia (BG < 70 mg/dL) is the most dangerous acute complication of diabetes treatment. Rapid recognition and intervention prevent seizures, loss of consciousness, and death.

Mild-Moderate (Adrenergic)
  • Tremors/shakiness
  • Sweating (diaphoresis)
  • Tachycardia
  • Hunger
  • Anxiety, irritability
  • Pallor, tingling
Severe (Neuroglycopenic)
  • Confusion
  • Slurred speech
  • Seizures
  • Loss of consciousness
  • Behavioral changes
  • Visual disturbances
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Rule of 15
Conscious patient: Give 15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrate (4 oz juice, 3-4 glucose tablets, 1 tbsp honey), wait 15 minutes, recheck BG. Repeat if still < 70. Follow with complex carb + protein snack. Unconscious patient: NEVER give anything by mouth (aspiration risk). Give IV dextrose 50% (D50W) 25-50 mL or IM glucagon 1 mg. Position on side to protect airway.
Hypoglycemia Unawareness

Patients on beta-blockers or with long-standing diabetes may lose early warning signs. Beta-blockers block tachycardia and tremors — only sweating remains. These patients may progress directly to confusion or seizures without warning. They need more frequent monitoring and higher glucose targets.

Bottom Line: Rule of 15 if conscious. IV D50 or IM glucagon if unconscious — never give oral glucose. Beta-blockers mask early warning signs. Always investigate the cause after treatment.

Nursing Management & Patient Teaching

Diabetes management is 95% self-care. The nurse's role is to empower patients with knowledge and skills for safe, effective daily management.

Sick Day Rules

Teach Patients These Sick Day Guidelines
  • NEVER stop taking insulin — illness increases glucose even if not eating (stress hormones)
  • Check blood glucose every 4 hours
  • Check urine ketones (Type 1) if BG > 240 mg/dL
  • Maintain fluid intake (small, frequent sips)
  • If unable to eat regular meals, replace with simple carbs (regular soda, popsicles, gelatin)
  • Call provider if: BG > 300 twice, moderate-large ketones, vomiting > 4 hours, fever persists
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Never Skip Insulin When Sick
This is the #1 sick day mistake that sends patients to the ED. Patients think: "I'm not eating, so I don't need insulin." But illness triggers counter-regulatory hormones (cortisol, glucagon, epinephrine) that dramatically raise blood glucose. Type 1 patients who stop insulin during illness can develop DKA within hours. Always reinforce: sick = MORE insulin need, not less. They may need to adjust the dose, but never stop it entirely without provider guidance.

Essential Patient Teaching Points

Core Self-Management Skills

1
Blood Glucose Monitoring

Teach technique: wash hands with warm water (avoids false readings from food residue), use side of fingertip (less pain), rotate sites. T1DM: check 4-6x/day minimum. T2DM on insulin: check as directed. CGMs are increasingly used.

2
Nutrition Management

Carbohydrate counting for insulin dosing. Consistent meal timing (especially on sulfonylureas/NPH). Focus on complex carbs, fiber, lean protein. Limit refined sugars and saturated fats. Refer to a diabetes educator/dietitian.

3
Exercise Guidelines

150 min/week moderate aerobic activity. Check BG before exercise: if < 100, eat a snack first; if > 250 with ketones, do not exercise. Carry fast-acting carbs. Exercise increases insulin sensitivity (lowers BG) — may need to reduce insulin dose.

4
Foot Care

Inspect feet daily. Wash with warm (not hot) water. Moisturize (not between toes). Wear proper shoes — never go barefoot. Cut nails straight across. Report any cuts, blisters, redness, or swelling immediately.

5
Medical Alert & Follow-up

Wear medical alert identification. Annual dilated eye exam, foot exam, urine albumin test. HbA1c every 3-6 months. Flu vaccine annually. Keep glucagon kit available and teach family to use it.

Bottom Line: Never stop insulin when sick — illness raises glucose. Teach blood glucose monitoring, carb counting, exercise precautions, foot care, and sick day rules. Diabetes management is self-care — education IS the treatment.

Pediatric Considerations

Type 1 Diabetes in Children

T1DM is the most common form in children. Key pediatric considerations:

  • Insulin doses are weight-based (0.5-1.0 units/kg/day, higher in puberty due to growth hormones)
  • Insulin pumps and CGMs are increasingly standard of care
  • Hypoglycemia may present as: irritability, behavioral changes, difficulty concentrating, nightmares (nocturnal hypo)
  • Young children cannot reliably report symptoms — educate parents AND school staff
  • DKA is the leading cause of death in children with T1DM — educate on sick day rules. Cerebral edema is a unique DKA complication in children (fluid/insulin must be corrected slowly)
Type 2 Diabetes in Youth

T2DM is rising in children/adolescents, especially with obesity.

  • Metformin is the only oral agent FDA-approved for children (age 10+)
  • Liraglutide (GLP-1) recently approved for adolescents 10+ with T2DM
  • Lifestyle modifications are heavily emphasized
  • Adolescents may struggle with adherence due to peer pressure and body image
  • Develop a Diabetes Medical Management Plan (DMMP) for school
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Cerebral Edema in Pediatric DKA
Cerebral edema is a life-threatening complication unique to pediatric DKA. It occurs when glucose and osmolality are corrected too quickly, causing fluid to shift into brain cells. Signs include headache, altered LOC, bradycardia, and hypertension (Cushing's triad). Prevention: correct glucose gradually (no more than 50-75 mg/dL per hour), avoid hypotonic fluids early, and don't drop sodium too fast. This is why pediatric DKA management requires ICU-level monitoring.
Bottom Line: T1DM is most common in children; always weight-based insulin dosing. Cerebral edema is a unique pediatric DKA risk — correct slowly. For T2DM in youth, metformin is first-line. School staff education is essential.
