01

What actually gets you

Diabetes is a big topic that reduces to a small number of decisions. Almost every question is a discrimination or a threshold, and the mistakes are predictable.

You started the insulin before you looked at the potassium

Insulin drives potassium into cells. In DKA the total body potassium is already depleted, even when the serum level reads normal or high.

Give insulin without replacing it and the level falls off a cliff. This is the single most dangerous mistake in the whole guide, and it is a favourite question.

You memorised insulin onsets and forgot the peak

The peak is the only part of that table that changes what you do. Peak is when the patient goes low, so peak is when food has to be there.

A patient on NPH who skips lunch is a question waiting to happen.

You gave something by mouth to a patient who could not swallow it

Hypoglycemia has two treatments. One assessment picks between them. Can this patient protect their airway.

Juice into an unresponsive patient is an aspiration, not a treatment.

You assumed every diabetes drug can cause a low

Only three classes do it alone. Everything else works in a glucose-dependent way, so it cannot push the level below normal by itself.

Knowing which three lets you answer a whole family of questions in one step.

Bottom Line: Study this as four discriminations: Type 1 or Type 2, DKA or HHS, conscious or not, and which drugs drop the glucose on their own.
02

The four things

For every entry below, you should be able to say four things with the book closed: the one-sentence definition, the clue you would see in a patient, the priority nursing action, and the first-line drug with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Entity Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line drug
Type 1 Autoimmune destruction of beta cells, so no insulin is produced at all. Young, thin, sudden onset, three P's, sometimes already in DKA. Insulin, and teach that it is never stopped. Insulin. There is no alternative; this is replacement, not treatment.
Type 2 Cells resist insulin, and beta-cell output slowly falls behind. Adult, often overweight, gradual, often found on a routine test. Lifestyle change plus a drug. Check the kidneys before choosing. Metformin. No hypoglycemia alone, and it is held for contrast dye.
DKA No insulin at all, so fat is burned for fuel and ketones acidify the blood. Glucose 250 to 600, pH under 7.35, Kussmaul breathing, fruity breath. Fluids, then insulin, and check the potassium before the insulin. IV regular insulin, with fluids and potassium alongside it.
HHS Some insulin is present, so no ketones form, but glucose climbs enormously. Glucose over 600, no ketones, profound dehydration, confusion. Fluids first, and a great deal of them. IV fluids lead. Insulin follows, because rehydration alone drops the glucose.
Hypoglycemia Glucose under 70 mg/dL, from too much drug, too little food, or too much activity. Shaky, sweaty, tachycardic. Then confused, then unresponsive. Check whether they can swallow safely. That decides everything. Conscious, 15 g of fast carbohydrate. Unconscious, IV D50 or IM glucagon.
Copy this down

Five rows on one index card, by hand. Type 1 has no insulin, Type 2 cannot use it. DKA has ketones, HHS does not. Under 70 is low, and conscious decides the route. That card is most of the exam.

03

Type 1 or Type 2

Discrimination axis: is there no insulin, or is there insulin that does not work.

Insulin comes from the beta cells of the pancreatic islets. Its job is to let glucose into cells. Without it working, glucose piles up in the blood while the cells starve.

That last phrase explains the classic presentation. The body thinks it is starving, because as far as the cells are concerned it is.

Type 1: there is no insulin
  • Autoimmune destruction of the beta cells
  • Absolute deficiency; none is produced
  • Usually diagnosed in childhood or adolescence
  • Sudden onset, often with the three P's
  • Insulin is required for survival, not for control
  • Prone to DKA
  • 5 to 10 percent of all diabetes
  • Normal weight or underweight at diagnosis
Type 2: the insulin does not work
  • Cells resist insulin that is present
  • Beta-cell function declines gradually on top of that
  • Usually diagnosed in adults, though rising in young people
  • Gradual onset, often silent for years
  • Lifestyle and oral agents first, insulin later if needed
  • Prone to HHS
  • 90 to 95 percent of all diabetes
  • Often overweight at diagnosis
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
No Keys or Rusty Locks
Type 1 has no keys, so you supply them from outside. Type 2 has keys but rusty locks, so you can oil the locks with metformin, make more keys with a sulfonylurea, or eventually supply keys directly. That is also why insulin can never be stopped in Type 1 and can sometimes be avoided in Type 2.
Why the three P's happen

They are not a list to memorise. They are three consequences of one problem.

  • Polyuria. Glucose spills into the urine and drags water out with it, by osmosis
  • Polydipsia. All that lost water makes the patient thirsty
  • Polyphagia. The cells cannot use glucose, so the body behaves as though it is starving

Fatigue and blurred vision come along with them.

Bottom Line: Type 1 makes none and always needs insulin, and goes into DKA. Type 2 cannot use what it makes, starts on metformin, and goes into HHS.

The numbers that diagnose it

Any one of these confirms diabetes. It has to be confirmed on a second day unless the hyperglycemia is unequivocal.

HbA1c
6.5 percent or more
Random glucose
200 mg/dL or more, with classic symptoms

Targets once they are diagnosed

Hypoglycemia

What HbA1c actually measures

Glucose sticks to hemoglobin, and a red cell lives about 3 months. So the HbA1c is an average of the last 2 to 3 months, and no single bad day moves it much.

An HbA1c of 7 percent is an average glucose around 154 mg/dL. Each 1 percent is worth roughly 28 to 30 mg/dL.

It needs no fasting. But anything that changes red cell turnover makes it unreliable. Hemolytic anemia, a recent transfusion, sickle cell disease.

Prediabetes

The warning band

Fasting glucose
100 to 125 mg/dL
HbA1c
5.7 to 6.4 percent
Two-hour OGTT
140 to 199 mg/dL

This band is reversible, and that is worth saying to patients plainly. Lose 7 percent of body weight. Do 150 minutes of moderate exercise a week. That cuts the risk of progressing to Type 2 by 58 percent.

Bottom Line: 126 fasting, 6.5 percent HbA1c, or 200 random with symptoms. Target under 7 percent. Prediabetes is 100 to 125, and it is reversible.
05

DKA or HHS

Discrimination axis: are there ketones.

Both are emergencies. They come from the same disease and they need different priorities, so the exam separates them constantly.

DKA, mostly Type 1
  • Glucose 250 to 600 mg/dL
  • pH under 7.35, a metabolic acidosis
  • Ketones in the blood and the urine
  • Kussmaul respirations, deep and rapid
  • Fruity or acetone breath
  • Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain
  • Dehydrated, with warm dry skin
  • Develops over hours to days

Triggers: missed insulin, illness or infection, new Type 1, surgery, stress.

HHS, mostly Type 2
  • Glucose over 600 mg/dL, often over 1000
  • pH normal or near normal, over 7.30
  • No significant ketones
  • No Kussmaul respirations
  • No fruity breath
  • Severe dehydration
  • Altered mental status, from confusion to coma
  • Develops over days to weeks

Triggers: illness or infection, not drinking enough, steroids or thiazides, and older age.

The axis that separates them

It comes down to whether any insulin is left.

In DKA there is none, so the body burns fat instead, and burning fat produces ketones, and ketones are acids. Hence the low pH, the Kussmaul breathing that blows off CO2 to compensate, and the fruity breath.

In HHS there is a little insulin. Not enough to control the glucose, but enough to stop fat breakdown. So no ketones, no acidosis, and instead the glucose climbs until the osmotic diuresis has dried the patient out completely.

DKA is an acid problem. HHS is a water problem.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
DKA vs HHS
DKA has ketones because there is no insulin at all. HHS has none because a little insulin remains. DKA is acidosis, HHS is dehydration, and that difference sets the treatment priority: insulin leads in DKA, fluid leads in HHS.

Treating DKA, in order

  1. 1
    Fluids, aggressively. 0.9% saline at 1 to 1.5 L an hour for the first hour. Change to 0.45% once they are hemodynamically stable. Add dextrose when the glucose reaches 250 mg/dL, so the insulin can keep running without going low.
  2. 2
    IV regular insulin, as an infusion. Regular is the only insulin that goes IV. Aim to bring the glucose down 50 to 75 mg/dL an hour. Faster risks cerebral edema, especially in children.
  3. 3
    Potassium. The serum level lies on admission. Total body potassium is depleted, and as the acidosis corrects and insulin goes in, it moves into cells and the level falls fast. Check every 1 to 2 hours.
  4. 4
    Watch the acidosis close. ABGs regularly. Bicarbonate only if the pH is under 6.9. It has resolved when glucose is under 200, pH is over 7.30, bicarbonate is 15 or more, and the anion gap has closed.
Potassium is what kills people in DKA

Not the glucose. Insulin pushes potassium into cells, so giving insulin to a depleted patient can produce fatal hypokalemia and cardiac arrest.

  • Potassium under 3.3 mEq/L: hold the insulin and replace potassium first
  • Potassium 3.3 to 5.3: give potassium alongside the insulin
  • Potassium over 5.3: hold the potassium, recheck in 2 hours

Check it before the drip starts. Every time.

Why HHS is treated differently

Fluid leads. These patients can be 8 to 12 litres down. Rehydration alone brings the glucose a long way toward normal, by dilution and by restoring renal clearance.

Insulin comes second here rather than first. HHS patients are usually older. Watch the heart and the lungs while you fill them. Fluid overload is a real risk.

Write this down

Blank paper. Two columns, DKA and HHS. Glucose, pH, ketones, breathing, mental status, and the first thing you hang. Twelve cells, from memory.

Bottom Line: Ketones separate them. DKA is acidosis and insulin leads; HHS is dehydration and fluid leads. Potassium goes before the insulin in both.
06

When it goes the other way

Discrimination axis: can this patient safely swallow.

Hypoglycemia is glucose under 70 mg/dL, and it is the most dangerous acute complication of treating diabetes. A high glucose damages a patient over years. A low one can do it in minutes.

Early: the adrenaline symptoms
  • Tremor and shakiness
  • Sweating
  • Tachycardia
  • Hunger
  • Anxiety and irritability
  • Pallor, tingling

These are the body's warning system firing. The patient can still act on them.

Late: the brain running out of fuel
  • Confusion
  • Slurred speech
  • Seizures
  • Loss of consciousness
  • Behavioural change
  • Visual disturbance

Now the patient cannot help themselves, and the route of treatment changes.

The axis that separates the treatment

Conscious and able to swallow: the rule of 15. Give 15 g of fast carbohydrate. That is 4 oz of juice, 3 to 4 glucose tablets, or a tablespoon of honey. Wait 15 minutes. Recheck. Repeat if still under 70. Then follow with a complex carbohydrate and protein so it does not fall again.

Not conscious: nothing by mouth, ever. IV dextrose 50 percent, 25 to 50 mL, or 1 mg of glucagon intramuscularly. Position them on their side to protect the airway.

You are not choosing between these on severity of the number. You are choosing on whether the airway is safe.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Rule of 15
Fifteen grams of fast carbohydrate, wait fifteen minutes, recheck, repeat if needed, then a snack with protein. If they cannot swallow safely, nothing goes in the mouth; IV D50 or IM glucagon instead, and turn them on their side.
The patient with no warning system

The early symptoms are adrenaline. A beta-blocker blocks adrenaline, so it blocks the tremor and the tachycardia and leaves only the sweating.

Long-standing diabetes does the same thing by blunting the response over years. Either way, they go from fine to confused with nothing in between. So they need closer monitoring and slightly higher targets.

Bottom Line: Under 70 is low. Conscious gets the rule of 15; unconscious gets D50 or glucagon and nothing by mouth. Beta-blockers remove the warning.
07

Onset, peak, duration

Three numbers per insulin, and only one of them changes your nursing care. The peak is when hypoglycemia happens, so the peak is when food has to be available.

CategoryExamplesOnsetPeakDurationWhat matters
Rapid-acting Lispro, aspart 10 to 15 min 1 to 2 hr 3 to 5 hr Give with the meal, within 15 minutes of it
Short-acting Regular 30 to 60 min 2 to 4 hr 6 to 8 hr 30 minutes before the meal. The only insulin that can go IV
Intermediate NPH 1 to 2 hr 4 to 12 hr 18 to 24 hr Cloudy. Roll it, never shake it. Never IV
Long-acting Glargine, detemir 1 to 2 hr No peak 24 hr Once daily at the same time. Never mixed with anything
Read the peak column, not the onset column

Rapid-acting peaks at 1 to 2 hours, so the low comes just after the meal. NPH peaks at 4 to 12 hours, so the low comes in the late afternoon or overnight. Long-acting has no peak at all, which is exactly why it carries the lowest risk.

That is the entire table reduced to one useful question: when will this patient go low, and will there be food.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Peak Is When They Go Low
Rapid peaks at 1 to 2 hours, NPH at 4 to 12, long-acting not at all. Match food to the peak and most hypoglycemia disappears. A patient on NPH who skips lunch is the classic question.
Mixing, and the order it goes in

Regular before NPH. Clear before cloudy. The letters R and N are in alphabetical order, which is the easiest way to keep it.

Air into the NPH vial first without withdrawing any. Then air into the Regular vial, and draw the Regular up. Then draw up the NPH.

The reason is contamination. If cloudy NPH gets into the Regular vial, you have turned a clear fast insulin into an unpredictable one.

The names that get confused

Humalog and Humulin. NovoLog and Novolin. One letter apart, and completely different drugs.

Humalog and NovoLog are rapid-acting. Humulin and Novolin come as R or N. Read the full name and the letter, not the brand at a glance. Insulin is a high-alert medication and gets an independent double check.

Bottom Line: The peak is the number that matters. Regular is the only IV insulin. Regular before NPH, clear before cloudy, and long-acting is never mixed.
08

The drugs, and which ones drop you

Discrimination axis: does this drug work regardless of the glucose level, or only when it is high.

Type 2 is managed stepwise. Lifestyle, then metformin, then something added on. What you need per class is the side effect and whether it can cause a low.

ClassExamplesWhat it doesHypo aloneWhat you watch for
BiguanideMetforminCuts glucose production by the liverNoHold for contrast dye. Check renal function. GI upset is common
SulfonylureasGlipizide, glyburide, glimepirideMakes beta cells release insulinYesHypoglycemia and weight gain. Taken before meals
MeglitinidesRepaglinide, nateglinideShort sharp insulin releaseYesNo meal, no pill. Taken within 30 minutes of eating
TZDsPioglitazoneImproves insulin sensitivityNoContraindicated in heart failure. Fluid retention and fracture risk
DPP-4 inhibitorsSitagliptin, linagliptinMakes the natural incretins last longerNoWell tolerated, weight neutral. Watch for pancreatitis
GLP-1 agonistsSemaglutide, liraglutideMimics an incretin at high concentrationNoBlack box for thyroid C-cell tumours. Causes weight loss. Cardiovascular benefit
SGLT2 inhibitorsEmpagliflozin, dapagliflozinStops the kidney reabsorbing glucoseNoUrinary and genital infections, and euglycemic DKA. Cardiac and renal benefit
Alpha-glucosidase inhibitorsAcarboseSlows carbohydrate absorption from the gutNoGas and bloating. Treat a low with glucose tablets only
Only three cause a low on their own
  • Insulin, because it replaces the hormone directly
  • Sulfonylureas, because they make the pancreas release insulin whatever the glucose is doing
  • Meglitinides, for the same reason, faster and shorter

Everything else acts only when glucose is high, so it runs out of effect before the level goes below normal. That is the whole rule, and it answers a family of questions in one step.

Any of them can still contribute to a low when combined with insulin or a sulfonylurea.

The acarbose exception

Acarbose blocks the enzyme that breaks disaccharides into glucose. So if a patient on acarbose goes low, table sugar and juice will not work; the sugar cannot be split.

They need glucose itself. Glucose tablets, or IV dextrose. This is a small fact that gets tested precisely because it looks like an exception until you know why.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Metformin First
No hypoglycemia alone, weight neutral, cardiovascular benefit, cheap and well understood. The concerns are GI upset, which improves with food and the extended-release form, and lactic acidosis, which is why it is held for contrast dye and avoided below an eGFR of 30. Long term, watch the B12.
Bottom Line: Metformin first. Only insulin, sulfonylureas and meglitinides cause a low alone. Hold metformin for contrast, avoid TZDs in heart failure, and treat a low on acarbose with glucose only.
09

What the sugar breaks

Years of high glucose damage blood vessels. Small vessels and large vessels fail differently, which is why the complications sort into two groups.

Small vessels

ComplicationWhat happensWhat you do about it
RetinopathyRetinal vessels are damaged, producing microaneurysms, hemorrhages and vision lossDilated eye exam annually. Report vision change straight away
NephropathyGlomerular damage leads to protein in the urine, then progressive renal failureAnnual urine albumin. Control the blood pressure, with an ACE inhibitor or ARB for renal protection. Track BUN and creatinine
NeuropathyPeripheral nerves go numb and tingle. Autonomic nerves cause gastroparesis and orthostatic hypotensionDaily foot inspection. Monofilament testing. Proper shoes. Report new numbness

Large vessels

  • Coronary artery disease. Two to four times the risk, and the leading cause of death in diabetes.
  • Cerebrovascular disease. Higher stroke risk. Control pressure, glucose and lipids together.
  • Peripheral vascular disease. Poor circulation, wounds that will not heal, and the road to amputation.
Why the foot is where these two meet

Neuropathy means the patient cannot feel an injury. Peripheral vascular disease means the injury will not heal. Put those together and a blister nobody noticed becomes an infection that costs a limb.

Foot complications are the leading cause of non-traumatic lower limb amputation. Daily inspection is not a soft recommendation.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Foot Care Is Limb Care
Inspect daily, using a mirror for the soles. Wash in warm water, never hot, because they cannot feel hot. Moisturise, but not between the toes, where damp skin breaks down. Shoes always, never barefoot. Nails cut straight across. Any break in the skin gets reported the same day.
Bottom Line: Small vessels take the eyes, kidneys and nerves. Large vessels take the heart, brain and legs. Annual eye exam, annual urine albumin, daily foot check.
10

What you do first, and what you teach

Diabetes is managed almost entirely by the patient, on days you are not there. So teaching is not the soft part of this topic; it is the intervention.

The ordering rules
  • Treat a low before you investigate it. Hypoglycemia damages the brain in minutes; hyperglycemia takes hours.
  • Check the potassium before the insulin drip, every time.
  • Assess whether they can swallow before you give anything by mouth.
  • Fluid comes before insulin in HHS. Insulin leads in DKA, but only after the potassium is known.
  • Watch technique rather than asking about it, for injections and for meters alike.

Sick day rules

Never stop the insulin

This is the mistake that fills emergency departments. The patient reasons that they are not eating, so they do not need insulin.

But illness releases cortisol, glucagon and epinephrine, and all three raise glucose. Being sick means needing more insulin, not less, and a Type 1 patient who stops can be in DKA within hours.

  • Never stop insulin, though the dose may need adjusting
  • Check the glucose every 4 hours
  • Check urine ketones if the glucose is over 240 mg/dL
  • Keep fluids going, in small frequent sips
  • If meals are impossible, use simple carbohydrate instead: regular soda, ice lollies, gelatin
  • Call the provider for glucose over 300 twice, moderate or large ketones, vomiting past 4 hours, or a persistent fever
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Sick Means More Insulin, Not Less
Stress hormones raise glucose whether or not the patient is eating, so illness increases the insulin requirement. Check glucose every 4 hours, check ketones above 240, and keep fluids going. Stopping insulin because they cannot eat is how Type 1 patients arrive in DKA.

What they have to be able to do

  1. 1
    Check their own glucose. Wash hands in warm water first, because food residue gives a false high. Use the side of the fingertip, which hurts less, and rotate. Type 1 checks 4 to 6 times a day at minimum. Continuous monitors are increasingly the norm.
  2. 2
    Handle food. Carbohydrate counting for insulin dosing. Consistent meal timing matters most on a sulfonylurea or NPH. Complex carbohydrate, fibre, lean protein. Refer to a diabetes educator or dietitian.
  3. 3
    Exercise safely. 150 minutes a week. Check before starting: under 100, eat something first; over 250 with ketones, do not exercise at all. Carry fast carbohydrate. Exercise raises insulin sensitivity, so the dose may need to come down.
  4. 4
    Look after their feet. Daily inspection, warm water, moisturiser but not between the toes, shoes that fit, nails straight across, and report any break in the skin.
  5. 5
    Keep the system around them working. Medical alert identification. Annual eye exam, foot exam and urine albumin. HbA1c every 3 to 6 months. Influenza vaccine yearly. A glucagon kit at home, and somebody trained to use it.
Bottom Line: Never stop insulin when sick. Treat a low before you explain it. Everything else in this section is teaching, and the teaching is the treatment.
11

Children are not small adults

Type 1 in children

Type 1 is the common form in childhood.

  • Insulin is dosed by weight, 0.5 to 1.0 units per kg per day, and rises in puberty because growth hormone opposes insulin
  • Pumps and continuous monitors are increasingly standard
  • A low may look like irritability, a behaviour change, trouble concentrating, or nightmares if it happens overnight
  • Young children cannot reliably report symptoms, so parents and school staff need teaching too
  • DKA is the leading cause of death in children with Type 1, which makes sick day rules a safety intervention
Type 2 in young people
  • Rising with obesity, and no longer unusual
  • Metformin is the only oral agent approved for children, from age 10
  • Liraglutide is approved for adolescents from 10 with Type 2
  • Lifestyle work carries more of the load than it does in adults
  • Adherence is harder in adolescence, for reasons of peer pressure and body image rather than understanding
  • A Diabetes Medical Management Plan goes to the school
Cerebral edema

This complication of DKA is specific to children, and it is why paediatric DKA is corrected slowly.

Bring the glucose and the osmolality down too fast and water moves into brain cells. The signs are headache, a falling level of consciousness, bradycardia and hypertension.

  • Drop the glucose no faster than 50 to 75 mg/dL an hour
  • Avoid hypotonic fluids early
  • Do not let the sodium fall quickly

Bradycardia with a rising blood pressure in a child being treated for DKA is not an improvement. It is raised intracranial pressure.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Cerebral Edema in Pediatric DKA
Correcting too fast pulls water into brain cells. Glucose down no more than 50 to 75 mg/dL an hour, no hypotonic fluids early, and no rapid sodium shift. Headache with a falling level of consciousness, bradycardia and hypertension is the warning, and it needs ICU-level monitoring.
Bottom Line: Weight-based insulin, rising in puberty. Metformin from age 10 in Type 2. Cerebral edema is the paediatric DKA risk, so correct slowly.
