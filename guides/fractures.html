High-Yield Summary Review This in 60 Seconds

The must-know facts from this guide, distilled for quick review.

  • Fracture = break in bone continuity. Open (compound) = bone pierces skin (infection risk!). Closed (simple) = skin intact.
  • 5 P's of neurovascular assessment: Pain, Pulse, Pallor, Paresthesia, Paralysis. Check distal to injury site. Compare bilaterally. Absent pulses = emergency!
  • Compartment syndrome = surgical emergency! Pressure builds in fascial compartment compromises circulation tissue death within 46 hours. Pain OUT OF PROPORTION to injury + pain with passive stretch = classic signs.
  • Compartment syndrome treatment: FASCIOTOMY (surgical release of fascia). Do NOT elevate (reduces arterial flow), do NOT apply ice, do NOT give meds that mask pain. Remove constrictive dressings/cast IMMEDIATELY.
  • Fat embolism syndrome: Occurs 2472 hours post-fracture (especially long bone/pelvis). Triad: respiratory distress + petechial rash (chest/axilla) + mental status changes. NCLEX loves this!
  • Fracture healing stages: Hematoma (13 days) Granulation (314 days) Callus formation (26 weeks) Ossification (3 weeks6 months) Remodeling (up to 1 year).
  • Cast care: Keep dry, check for hot spots (infection), petal rough edges, NEVER insert objects to scratch. Report: foul odor, drainage, numbness/tingling, increased pain.
  • Traction: Maintain alignment, keep weights hanging freely (never rest on bed/floor), assess pin sites for infection (skeletal traction).
  • Hip fractures: Elderly + fall + shortened/externally rotated leg = think hip fracture. High risk for DVT, PE, and fat embolism.
  • Common mistake: Applying ice and elevating a limb with suspected compartment syndrome. This WORSENS ischemia ice and elevation reduce already-compromised blood flow.

Bone Anatomy & Physiology

Understanding bone structure for fracture management

Bones are dynamic, living organs that provide structural support, protect vital organs, produce blood cells, and store minerals. Understanding bone anatomy is essential for recognizing fracture patterns and promoting optimal healing.

Bone Structure

Periosteum
Outer protective layer
  • Dense fibrous membrane covering bone surface
  • Contains blood vessels and nerves
  • Essential for bone healing and repair
  • Attachment point for tendons and ligaments
Cortical (Compact) Bone
Dense outer layer
  • Makes up 80% of skeletal mass
  • Provides strength and rigidity
  • Found in shafts of long bones (diaphysis)
  • Organized in Haversian systems
Cancellous (Spongy) Bone
Inner trabecular network
  • Honeycomb-like structure (trabeculae)
  • Found at bone ends (epiphysis)
  • Contains red marrow for blood cell production
  • Absorbs shock and stress
Bone Marrow
Blood cell production center
  • Red marrow: Produces RBCs, WBCs, platelets
  • Yellow marrow: Fat storage (adults)
  • Located in medullary cavity
  • Can release fat emboli with long bone fractures

Long Bone Regions

Region Location Characteristics Clinical Significance
Epiphysis Ends of bone Mostly spongy bone, covered by articular cartilage Fractures affect joint function
Metaphysis Between epiphysis and diaphysis Contains growth plate (physis) in children Growth plate injuries can affect development
Diaphysis Shaft (middle portion) Thick cortical bone, medullary cavity Most common site for fractures

Bone Blood Supply

Adequate blood supply is critical for fracture healing. Blood reaches bone through multiple pathways:

Blood Supply to Bone

Nutrient artery: Main blood supply, enters through nutrient foramen. Periosteal vessels: Supply outer third of cortex. Metaphyseal/Epiphyseal vessels: Supply bone ends. Disruption of blood supply leads to delayed healing or avascular necrosis (AVN).

🎯 Bottom Line: Bones provide structure, protection, and mineral storage. Periosteum contains blood vessels and nerves (that's why fractures hurt). Bone heals itself, but alignment and immobilization are essential for proper healing.
2

Fracture Types & Classification

Identifying fracture patterns for appropriate treatment

A fracture is a break or disruption in bone continuity. Fractures are classified by their pattern, displacement, and whether they communicate with the external environment.

Open vs. Closed Fractures

Open (Compound) Fracture
Bone pierces skin - EMERGENCY
  • Bone fragments protrude through skin
  • High risk of infection (osteomyelitis)
  • Requires surgical debridement
  • IV antibiotics within 1 hour
  • Tetanus prophylaxis required
Closed (Simple) Fracture
Skin remains intact
  • Skin not broken by bone fragments
  • Lower infection risk
  • May still have significant soft tissue injury
  • Can often be managed conservatively
  • May still require surgery for alignment

Fracture Patterns

Closed (Simple) Skin remains intact; bone does not break through
Open (Compound) Bone pierces skin; high infection risk - EMERGENCY
Transverse Straight break perpendicular to bone axis; direct force injury
Spiral Twisting break wrapping around bone; rotational force (skiing, falls)
Comminuted Multiple fragments (3+ pieces); high-energy trauma; longest healing
Impacted Fragments driven into each other; FOOSH injuries (Fall On Outstretched Hand)
Greenstick Incomplete break (one side only); pediatric patients only
Oblique Diagonal break at an angle to bone axis; twisting or angular force

Displacement Classification

Classification Description Treatment Approach
Nondisplaced Bone fragments remain in anatomical alignment Often conservative (cast/splint)
Displaced Fragments have moved from normal position May require reduction and fixation
Angulated Fragments tilted at an angle to each other Reduction needed for proper alignment
Rotated Distal fragment twisted on longitudinal axis Surgical correction usually needed
NCLEX Alert: Fracture Classification

Remember: OPEN = EMERGENCY. Open fractures require IV antibiotics within 1 hour, surgical debridement, and tetanus prophylaxis. Comminuted fractures have the longest healing time due to multiple fragments and disrupted blood supply.

🎯 Bottom Line: Open fractures are surgical emergencies (infection risk cover with sterile saline-moistened dressing). Greenstick = kids. Pathological = cancer weakened bone. Comminuted = multiple fragments (difficult to repair).

Bone Healing Process

The four stages of fracture repair

Bone healing (osteogenesis) is a remarkable process that can restore bone to its original strength. Understanding these stages helps nurses anticipate patient needs and recognize delayed healing.

Fracture Healing Timeline
Stage 1: Hematoma Formation
0-3 days
Blood vessels rupture at fracture site, forming a hematoma (blood clot). This provides a framework for healing. Inflammatory response begins with WBCs removing debris. Patient experiences pain, swelling, and warmth at site.
Stage 2: Fibrocartilaginous Callus
3 days - 2 weeks
Fibroblasts and chondroblasts migrate to site. Granulation tissue and cartilage form a soft callus bridging the fracture gap. New blood vessels develop (angiogenesis). This soft callus is fragile and must be protected from stress.
Stage 3: Bony Callus Formation
2-6 weeks
Osteoblasts deposit calcium, converting soft callus to hard, bony callus (woven bone). The fracture site becomes rigid but is still weaker than mature bone. X-rays show visible callus formation. Progressive weight bearing may begin.
Stage 4: Bone Remodeling
6 weeks - 1+ year
Osteoclasts resorb excess bone while osteoblasts deposit lamellar (mature) bone along stress lines. Bone gradually returns to original shape and strength. This process continues for months to years. Weight bearing stimulates proper remodeling.

Factors Affecting Bone Healing

Factors That Promote Healing
Optimize these for best outcomes
  • Good blood supply to fracture site
  • Proper immobilization
  • Close approximation of fragments
  • Adequate nutrition (protein, calcium, vitamin D)
  • Younger age
  • Weight-bearing exercise (per orders)
Factors That Delay Healing
Address these to prevent complications
  • Inadequate immobilization
  • Infection
  • Poor blood supply (smoking, diabetes, PVD)
  • Malnutrition (especially protein deficiency)
  • Corticosteroid use
  • Advanced age
  • Excessive bone gap or movement
Nutrition for Bone Healing

Protein: 1.5-2 g/kg/day for tissue repair. Calcium: 1000-1200 mg/day. Vitamin D: 600-800 IU/day for calcium absorption. Vitamin C: For collagen synthesis. Encourage dairy, leafy greens, lean meats, and sunlight exposure.

🎯 Bottom Line: Bone healing takes weeks to months. Hematoma granulation soft callus hard callus remodeling. Promote healing with adequate protein, calcium, vitamin D, immobilization, and blood supply.

Neurovascular Assessment

The 5 P's: Critical monitoring for limb viability

Neurovascular assessment is a critical nursing skill for patients with fractures, casts, or any extremity injury. The "5 P's" provide a systematic approach to detect compromised circulation or nerve function early, preventing permanent damage.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The 5 P's Save Limbs
Memorize this: Pain, Pallor, Pulselessness, Paresthesia, Paralysis! These are your early warning signs that blood flow or nerve function is compromised. Always assess the injured limb AND compare to the uninjured side. Pain out of proportion to injury (especially with passive stretch) is often the FIRST sign of compartment syndrome - don't wait for the other P's to appear!

The 5 P's of Neurovascular Assessment

P
Pain
Assess quality, location, severity (0-10 scale)
ALERT: Pain with passive stretch or out of proportion to injury
P
Pallor
Check skin color; compare to unaffected limb
ALERT: Pale, white, or cyanotic skin
P
Pulselessness
Palpate distal pulses; assess capillary refill
ALERT: Weak/absent pulse; cap refill >3 sec
P
Paresthesia
Ask about numbness, tingling, "pins & needles"
ALERT: New numbness or abnormal sensations
P
Paralysis
Test ability to move fingers/toes
ALERT: Weakness or inability to move (LATE sign!)

Neurovascular Assessment Technique

Assessment How to Assess Normal Finding Report Immediately
Pain Ask patient to rate pain; passively stretch fingers/toes Pain manageable with medication; no increase with stretch Severe pain, especially with passive stretch
Pulses Palpate radial/ulnar (arm) or dorsalis pedis/posterior tibial (leg) Strong, easily palpable pulse bilaterally Weak, thready, or absent pulse
Color Compare to unaffected limb; check nail beds Pink, warm, matches uninjured side Pale, white, mottled, or cyanotic
Temperature Use back of hand to compare both limbs Warm, equal bilaterally Cool or cold to touch
Capillary Refill Press nail bed for 2-3 seconds, release, count seconds to pink <3 seconds >3 seconds (sluggish refill)
Sensation Light touch; ask about numbness/tingling Intact sensation, no paresthesias Numbness, tingling, decreased sensation
Movement Ask patient to wiggle fingers/toes; test grip/push strength Full active ROM, good strength Weakness or inability to move
Assessment Frequency

Immediately post-injury/surgery: Every 15-30 minutes. First 24-48 hours: Every 1-2 hours. After stabilization: Every 4-8 hours. Any change: Notify provider immediately and increase frequency.

🎯 Bottom Line: Always assess the 5 P's distal to injury: Pain (increasing = bad sign), Pulse, Pallor, Paresthesia, Paralysis. Compare bilaterally. Any change in neurovascular status requires IMMEDIATE notification.

Compartment Syndrome

Recognizing a limb-threatening emergency

Compartment syndrome occurs when pressure within a closed muscle compartment increases to a level that compromises blood flow and tissue perfusion. Without emergency intervention, permanent nerve damage and muscle death occur within 6-8 hours.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Compartment Syndrome = EMERGENCY
This is a surgical emergency - don't wait to report it! The #1 early sign is pain out of proportion to the injury, especially pain that increases when you passively stretch the muscle (like extending the fingers for a forearm compartment syndrome). If your patient's pain suddenly gets worse despite medication, or if they scream when you gently move their fingers, call the surgeon NOW. Irreversible damage starts in 6 hours!
Compartment Syndrome - Act Fast!
Irreversible damage in 6-8 hours

The 6 P's of Compartment Syndrome: Pain (especially with passive stretch), Pressure (tense compartment), Paresthesia, Pallor, Pulselessness, Paralysis. Note: Pulses may still be present early - don't be falsely reassured!

1
Remove or bivalve cast/dressing immediately
2
Notify surgeon STAT
3
Keep limb at heart level (not elevated!)
4
Prepare for emergent fasciotomy

Understanding Compartment Syndrome

What Causes It?
Pressure builds in muscle compartment
  • Internal: Bleeding, edema from fracture
  • External: Tight cast, circumferential dressing
  • Common with: Tibial fractures, forearm fractures
  • Also from: Crush injuries, burns, reperfusion
What Happens?
Tissue damage cascade
  • Pressure exceeds capillary perfusion pressure
  • Muscle and nerve ischemia begins
  • Cell death releases more fluid more swelling
  • Vicious cycle of increasing pressure
  • Without fasciotomy: Volkmann's contracture, amputation

Early vs. Late Signs

Early Signs (Act NOW!) Late Signs (Damage Occurring)
Pain out of proportion to injury Paralysis (unable to move)
Pain with passive stretch of muscles Pulselessness (very late sign)
Tense, swollen compartment Pallor, cool skin
Paresthesias (numbness, tingling) Complete sensory loss
Increasing analgesic requirements Muscle necrosis
NCLEX Alert: Compartment Syndrome Priority

Key point: Pain with PASSIVE stretch is the earliest and most reliable sign! If the NCLEX asks about the first sign of compartment syndrome, choose pain (especially with passive movement) over paralysis or pulselessness - those are LATE signs. Also remember: Do NOT elevate the limb above heart level - this decreases arterial pressure and worsens ischemia.

🎯 Bottom Line: Pain out of proportion + pain with passive stretch = compartment syndrome until proven otherwise. This is a surgical emergency fasciotomy within 46 hours or permanent damage occurs. Do NOT elevate, ice, or mask pain.

Nursing Interventions

Evidence-based care for fracture patients

Immediate Post-Fracture Care

Immobilization
  • Immobilize fracture in position found until x-ray
  • Splint joints above and below fracture site
  • Apply traction only if ordered
  • Handle injured extremity gently
  • Support with pillows or positioning devices
RICE Protocol
  • Rest - Limit movement of injured area
  • Ice - 20 minutes on, 20 off (protect skin)
  • Compression - Elastic bandage if appropriate
  • Elevation - Above heart level to reduce edema
  • Note: Do NOT elevate if compartment syndrome suspected
Pain Management
  • Assess pain using appropriate scale (0-10)
  • Administer analgesics as ordered
  • Ice and elevation for swelling/pain
  • Position for comfort
  • Report uncontrolled pain - may indicate compartment syndrome
Monitoring
  • Neurovascular checks per facility protocol
  • Vital signs (watch for shock with blood loss)
  • Monitor for fat embolism (long bone fractures)
  • Assess surgical site for bleeding/drainage
  • Document and report changes immediately
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Pain Paradox
Unrelieved pain is NEVER "just normal fracture pain"! Here's the paradox: we want to manage pain, but increasing pain that doesn't respond to medication is a red flag. If your patient says "the pain is worse" despite adequate analgesia, don't just give more meds - assess for compartment syndrome! Pain that breaks through medication, especially pain with passive movement, needs immediate investigation. Trust your patient when they say something is wrong.

Cast Care

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Cast Care Essentials
The #1 rule of cast care: NEVER put anything inside the cast! Patients love to scratch with coat hangers, pencils, or rulers - this damages skin and creates wounds that get infected. Teach them to use a hair dryer on cool setting for itching. Also, plaster casts take 24-72 hours to fully dry - handle with palms only (no fingertips!) to prevent dents that cause pressure injuries. And remember: if a fiberglass cast gets wet, use a hair dryer on LOW heat to dry it thoroughly.
Cast Type Drying Time Key Nursing Considerations
Plaster 24-72 hours Handle with palms only; avoid denting. Keep uncovered to dry. Heavier but molds better.
Fiberglass 30 minutes Water-resistant (not waterproof). Lighter. Can scratch skin. Dry thoroughly if wet.

Patient Education: Cast Care

DO
Essential care instructions
  • Elevate above heart level when resting
  • Wiggle fingers/toes frequently
  • Apply ice in bag over cast for swelling
  • Keep cast clean and dry
  • Report increased pain, numbness, or drainage
  • Inspect skin at cast edges daily
DON'T
Avoid these hazards
  • Put objects inside cast to scratch
  • Get cast wet (unless waterproof)
  • Bear weight until approved
  • Apply powder or lotion under cast
  • Ignore foul odor from cast
  • Trim or modify the cast yourself

Weight Bearing Status

Weight Bearing Progression
NWB
Non-Weight Bearing
No weight on affected leg. Use crutches/walker.
TTWB
Toe Touch
Toe touches for balance only. No actual weight.
PWB
Partial Weight
Specific % of body weight (e.g., 25-50%).
WBAT
As Tolerated
Full weight as comfortable. Normal gait goal.
🎯 Bottom Line: Immobilize fractures (splint before transport), maintain alignment, perform neurovascular checks q1-2h post-reduction/surgery, manage pain, prevent infection (open fractures), and start early mobilization when cleared.

Complications

Early recognition and nursing management

Fracture complications can be immediate, early, or delayed. Vigilant nursing assessment is essential for early detection and intervention.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Watch for Fat Embolism
Fat embolism syndrome (FES) typically occurs 24-72 hours after a long bone fracture. Watch for the classic triad: respiratory distress (the fat travels to the lungs first), neurologic changes (confusion, restlessness - fat crosses to brain), and petechiae (tiny red/purple spots, especially on chest, axillae, and conjunctivae). The petechiae are a late sign but very specific. Any patient with a femur or pelvic fracture who suddenly becomes confused and short of breath needs immediate evaluation!

Major Complications by Timing

Immediate (Hours After Injury)
Compartment Syndrome
  • Pain out of proportion/with passive stretch
  • Tense, swollen compartment
  • Paresthesias progressing to paralysis
Emergency fasciotomy within 6 hours
Fat Embolism Syndrome
  • Dyspnea, tachypnea, hypoxia
  • Confusion, restlessness, altered LOC
  • Petechiae (chest, axillae, conjunctivae)
Supportive care, O2, immobilization
Early (Days to Weeks)
Infection (Osteomyelitis)
  • Fever, chills, malaise
  • Wound erythema, drainage, odor
  • Elevated WBC, ESR, CRP
IV antibiotics, surgical debridement
Deep Vein Thrombosis
  • Unilateral leg swelling, warmth
  • Calf pain (Homans' sign - unreliable)
  • Can lead to PE (chest pain, dyspnea)
Anticoagulation, SCDs, early mobility
Late (Weeks to Months)
Delayed/Nonunion
  • Fracture not healing in expected time
  • Continued pain and instability
  • Risk factors: smoking, infection, malnutrition
Bone stimulator, surgical intervention
Avascular Necrosis
  • Bone death from disrupted blood supply
  • Common in femoral neck, scaphoid fractures
  • Persistent pain despite healing
Surgery, joint replacement if severe

Prevention Strategies

Prevent Fat Embolism
  • Early immobilization of fracture
  • Gentle handling during transport
  • Adequate hydration
  • Early surgical fixation when indicated
  • Monitor high-risk patients closely (long bone, pelvis)
Prevent DVT/PE
  • Early mobilization when safe
  • Sequential compression devices (SCDs)
  • Anticoagulant prophylaxis as ordered
  • Hydration
  • Ankle pumps and exercises
Prevent Infection
  • Sterile technique for wound care
  • Monitor surgical sites for drainage
  • Administer prophylactic antibiotics as ordered
  • Keep pin sites clean (external fixation)
  • Patient education on signs of infection
NCLEX Alert: Fat Embolism vs. Pulmonary Embolism

Fat embolism: Occurs 24-72 hours after long bone fracture. Classic triad = respiratory distress + neurologic changes + petechiae. Pulmonary embolism: From DVT, sudden onset chest pain, dyspnea, hemoptysis. On NCLEX, petechiae + confusion + fracture = think fat embolism!

🎯 Bottom Line: Fat embolism (2472h post long-bone fracture petechiae + dyspnea + confusion), DVT/PE, infection (open fractures), nonunion/malunion, and avascular necrosis. Fat embolism is an NCLEX favorite!

8 Pediatric Considerations

Pediatric fractures differ significantly from adult fractures due to the presence of growth plates, more porous and flexible bones, and different injury mechanisms. Children also heal faster but require careful monitoring to prevent growth disturbances.

Growth Plate (Physis) Injuries Are Unique to Children!

The growth plate is the weakest part of the pediatric skeleton. Injuries here can affect bone growth and lead to limb length discrepancies or angular deformities. Always suspect growth plate involvement in pediatric fractures near joints!

Salter-Harris Classification

The Salter-Harris classification describes growth plate fractures and helps predict prognosis:

Type Description Prognosis
Type I Fracture through physis only (growth plate separation) Excellent - rarely affects growth
Type II Fracture through physis and metaphysis (most common - 75%) Excellent - rarely affects growth
Type III Fracture through physis and epiphysis (into joint) Good with proper reduction - risk of growth disturbance
Type IV Fracture through metaphysis, physis, and epiphysis Higher risk of growth arrest - often needs surgery
Type V Crush injury to physis (compression) Poor - high risk of growth arrest (often diagnosed retrospectively)
Memory Aid: "SALTR" for Salter-Harris Types

Straight across (Type I - physis only) Above (Type II - metaphysis) Lower (Type III - epiphysis) Through all (Type IV) Rammed/cRushed (Type V)

Pediatric-Specific Fracture Types

Fracture Type Description Nursing Considerations
Greenstick Incomplete fracture - bone bends and cracks on one side only Common in children due to bone flexibility; may need complete break for proper alignment
Buckle (Torus) Compression fracture causing bulging of cortex Stable fracture; usually treated with splint/cast; excellent healing
Plastic Deformation (Bowing) Bone bends without visible fracture line May not show on X-ray initially; can cause functional problems if not corrected
Supracondylar Humerus Fracture above elbow - most common elbow fracture in children High risk for neurovascular injury! Assess radial pulse, median nerve function frequently
Non-Accidental Trauma (NAT) - Child Abuse Screening

Certain fracture patterns raise suspicion for child abuse:

  • Fractures in non-ambulatory infants (<1 year, not yet walking)
  • Multiple fractures in various stages of healing
  • Spiral fractures of long bones in non-walking children
  • Rib fractures (especially posterior) without major trauma history
  • Metaphyseal "corner" or "bucket handle" fractures
  • History inconsistent with injury pattern or developmental stage

Nurses are mandatory reporters! Document objectively. Report suspicions to appropriate authorities.

Pediatric Treatment Considerations

Consideration Pediatric Difference
Healing Time Faster than adults - children's bones heal in approximately half the time
Remodeling Greater remodeling capacity - some angulation may correct with growth (especially in younger children, near growth plates)
Cast Management Monitor for compartment syndrome; children may not verbalize pain well; watch for excessive crying, inconsolability
Pain Assessment Use age-appropriate pain scales (FLACC for infants/toddlers, Wong-Baker faces for preschool/school age)
Immobilization Keep immobilization time short when possible - children develop stiffness less but may struggle with compliance
Activity Restrictions Difficult to enforce in active children; educate parents on supervision; no contact sports until cleared
Assessing Pain in Young Children

Children may not say "my arm hurts." Watch for behavioral cues:

  • Guarding or protecting the injured area
  • Refusing to use the limb (pseudoparalysis)
  • Inconsolable crying, especially with movement
  • Facial grimacing
  • Changes in eating, sleeping, or activity level
Parent/Caregiver Education

Cast care: Keep dry, don't insert objects, watch for increased pain or swelling. Neurovascular checks: Teach parents to check color, warmth, movement, and sensation of fingers/toes. Return immediately if: Increased swelling, numbness/tingling, color changes, uncontrolled pain, or foul odor from cast. Activity: Supervise closely, avoid rough play, no swimming or bathing with cast.

🎯 Bottom Line: Children's bones are more porous and bend before breaking (greenstick fractures). Growth plate (physis) injuries can affect future bone growth Salter-Harris classification. Children heal faster than adults.
