GI Bleeding

One ligament decides which scope the patient gets. One lab value will reassure you while they bleed. And the heart rate moves long before the blood pressure does.

The Nursing Collective

By the end, without notes

  • Say upper or lower from the stool, and name the exception that breaks the rule
  • Explain why a normal first hemoglobin does not mean the patient is fine
  • Put the first hour in order, and defend the first action
  • State the transfusion trigger and say why more blood is not better

01

What actually gets you

GI bleeding is a topic where the reassuring findings are the dangerous ones. The blood pressure is normal. The hemoglobin is normal. The patient is talking to you.

Points get lost here in three predictable ways.

You believed the first hemoglobin

In acute bleeding the patient loses red cells and plasma together. Same proportion, so the concentration does not change at first.

The number only falls once fluid moves back in. That takes 24 to 72 hours. A normal hemoglobin in an unstable patient rules out nothing.

You waited for the blood pressure to fall

Blood pressure is a late sign. It holds until roughly 30 percent of the volume is gone. Compensation is doing its job.

The heart rate moves first. A rising pulse in someone with melena is the finding. Even with a textbook pressure.

You matched the stool colour to a scope

Black is upper and red is lower, usually. But a brisk upper bleed moves through fast. The blood never gets digested, so it arrives bright red.

So the unstable patient with bright red blood is probably an upper bleed. Stool colour is a clue. Hemodynamics overrule it.

Bottom Line: Assess the circulation, not the lab value. Heart rate before blood pressure. And when the stool colour and the vital signs disagree, believe the vital signs.
02

The four things

For every bleed below, you should be able to say four things with the book closed: where it comes from, the clue you would actually see in a patient, the priority nursing action, and the first-line treatment with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Bleed Where it comes from Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line treatment
Peptic ulcer Stomach or duodenum. The commonest upper bleed. Coffee-ground emesis or melena. NSAID use in the history. Two large-bore IVs and a type and crossmatch. High-dose IV pantoprazole. Raising the pH lets the clot hold.
Esophageal varices Collateral veins from portal hypertension, on top of cirrhosis. Large volume hematemesis in a patient with liver disease. Airway first, because they can drown in it. Octreotide, prophylactic ceftriaxone, then urgent banding.
Diverticulosis Colon. The commonest lower bleed. Painless bright red blood per rectum, often in an older adult. Assess hemodynamics before assuming it is minor. Resuscitate, then colonoscopy. Most stop on their own.
Mallory-Weiss tear The gastroesophageal junction, torn by forceful vomiting. Retching first, then blood. The order matters. Assess the volume. Most are self-limiting. Supportive care. Endoscopic therapy if it will not stop.
Copy this down

Write these four rows on one index card, by hand. Ulcer gets a PPI. Varices get octreotide. Diverticulosis gets time. Vomiting before blood means a tear. That card is most of the exam.

03

One ligament divides everything

The GI tract splits at the ligament of Treitz, the suspensory ligament of the duodenum. It marks the junction between duodenum and jejunum.

Above it is an upper bleed, and the patient gets an EGD. Below it is a lower bleed, and the patient gets a colonoscopy. That is why this landmark matters.

WhereWhat bleeds there
EsophagusVarices from portal hypertension, Mallory-Weiss tears, esophagitis, cancer
StomachPeptic ulcer disease, gastritis, gastric cancer, Dieulafoy lesions, angiodysplasia
DuodenumDuodenal ulcers, the single commonest upper bleed. A posterior ulcer can erode into the gastroduodenal artery.
Ligament of TreitzThe dividing line itself
Jejunum and ileumRare, about 5 percent. Angiodysplasia, Crohn's, tumours, Meckel's diverticulum

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Line That Picks the Scope
Above the ligament of Treitz means an EGD. Below it means a colonoscopy. Everything else in this guide, the stool colour, the workup, the drugs, follows from which side of that line the blood is coming from.

Why the blood supply matters

The GI tract takes about 25 percent of cardiac output at rest. After a meal it is nearer 35. That is a lot of blood passing a surface that can ulcerate.

  • Portal hypertension. Above a portal pressure of 12 mmHg, collateral vessels open and become esophageal and gastric varices.
  • End arteries. GI arteries have limited collateral flow, so an occlusion causes ischemia rather than being compensated for.
  • Watershed areas. The splenic flexure and the rectosigmoid junction sit between two supplies, so they are the first to become ischemic.
  • Diverticula. They form where vessels penetrate the muscularis, which is why diverticular bleeding is arterial and can be brisk.
Bottom Line: The ligament of Treitz divides upper from lower, and that division picks the scope. Diverticular bleeding is arterial because diverticula form on vessels.
04

Upper or lower

Discrimination axis: how long the blood spent being digested.

That single idea explains every stool finding in this section. Blood that sits in the gut turns black. Blood that races through stays red.

Upper: above the ligament

Commonest causes:

  • Peptic ulcer disease, 40 to 50 percent. The leading cause.
  • Esophageal and gastric varices, from portal hypertension.
  • Erosive gastritis, from NSAIDs or alcohol.
  • Mallory-Weiss tear, from forceful vomiting.
  • Malignancy of the stomach or esophagus.

What you see: melena, which is black tarry foul-smelling stool. Hematemesis, bright red or coffee-ground. A positive NG aspirate.

Melena needs at least 50 to 100 mL of blood, and 8 hours in the gut.

Lower: below the ligament

Commonest causes:

  • Diverticulosis, 30 to 40 percent. The leading cause.
  • Angiodysplasia, meaning arteriovenous malformations.
  • Colorectal cancer and polyps.
  • Hemorrhoids, the commonest benign cause.
  • Inflammatory bowel disease.

What you see: hematochezia, bright red blood per rectum, mixed through or coating the stool. Maroon stool suggests a more proximal colonic source.

Usually painless in diverticulosis. Pain suggests inflammatory bowel disease or ischemia.

Melena or hematochezia

FeatureMelenaHematochezia
AppearanceBlack, tarry, stickyBright red or maroon
SmellDistinctively foul, because the blood is digestedOrdinary stool smell
Usual sourceUpper GI, proximal to the jejunumLower GI, colon or rectum
Volume needed50 to 100 mL minimumVariable

The exception that matters

A brisk upper GI bleed can present as bright red blood per rectum. The blood moves through too fast to be oxidised, so it never turns black.

The clue is the patient, not the stool. Bright red blood plus hemodynamic instability points at an upper source. Instability means a large volume bleed, and large bleeds are usually upper.

Those patients get an NG tube or an upper endoscopy first, ahead of the colonoscopy.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Unstable and Red Means Look Up
Bright red blood per rectum in a stable patient is usually the colon. Bright red blood in a hypotensive tachycardic patient is usually a fast upper bleed. Scope the top first in that patient.
Bottom Line: Black means it sat and got digested, so upper. Red means it moved fast or started low. Instability breaks the rule and sends you upward.
05

How sick are they

The whole first assessment is one question. Is this patient hemodynamically stable. The unit, the timing of the scope and the transfusion all follow from the answer.

FindingWhat it means
HematemesisVomiting blood. Bright red means active; coffee-ground means digested. Either way, upper.
Melena or hematocheziaBlack tarry stool or bright red blood. This localises the source.
TachycardiaOver 100 bpm. The earliest sign of hypovolemia, and it can be masked by a beta-blocker.
HypotensionSystolic under 90 mmHg, or a drop of more than 20. That means over 30 percent of volume is gone.

Stable

  • No orthostatic change
  • Alert and orientated
  • Urine output over 0.5 mL/kg/hr
  • Warm, well perfused extremities

Plan: admit to the floor, endoscopy within 24 hours.

Unstable
  • Heart rate over 100, or systolic under 90
  • Positive orthostatic change
  • Altered mental status, confusion
  • Oliguria, under 0.5 mL/kg/hr
  • Cool, clammy, mottled extremities

Plan: ICU, emergent endoscopy, massive transfusion protocol on standby.

Read down the blood pressure column. It stays normal through class II. A litre and a half is already gone. That is why heart rate comes first.

Why the first hemoglobin lies

Whole blood leaves the body. Red cells and plasma go together, in the ratio they were already in.

So the concentration does not change, and hemoglobin measures concentration. It falls only once fluid shifts back in, from the tissues and from your IV bag. That takes 24 to 72 hours.

A normal hemoglobin in an unstable patient means the bleeding is recent. It does not mean it is small. Trend it every 6 hours and treat the patient in front of you.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Concentration Does Not Change
Losing whole blood does not dilute what is left. The hemoglobin only drops when fluid comes back in, over 24 to 72 hours. Assess the heart rate, the pressure and the mental status instead of the first lab value.

06

Finding the source

Endoscopy is the cornerstone. It finds the bleeding and treats it in one procedure. Everything else is what you reach for when endoscopy cannot answer the question.

When the scope happens

PatientTiming
Stable, low riskWithin 24 hours
High-risk featuresWithin 12 hours
Suspected variceal bleedWithin 12 hours
Hemodynamically unstableEmergently, after resuscitation

Note the last row. Resuscitation comes before the scope. An unstable patient sedated for endoscopy is a patient who arrests during endoscopy.

The two scopes

EGD, for upper bleeds

When: suspected upper bleed, meaning hematemesis, coffee-ground emesis, melena, or a positive NG aspirate.

How good: 92 to 98 percent sensitive for finding the source.

What it can do: injection therapy, thermal coagulation, hemoclips, and band ligation for varices.

Before: nothing by mouth, IV access, type and screen, resuscitation. Consider erythromycin to empty the stomach.

After: watch for rebleeding with 6-hourly hemoglobin, and for aspiration and perforation.

Colonoscopy, for lower bleeds

When: suspected lower bleed, after the patient is stabilised.

Timing: within 24 hours, after bowel prep where possible.

The problem: active bleeding obscures the view. An urgent scope may have to go without a full prep.

What it can do: clips, thermal coagulation, injection, argon plasma coagulation.

How good: 74 to 100 percent with an adequate prep, and lower while bleeding is active.

When the scope is not enough

TestWhat it detectsWhen you use it
CT angiography Active bleeding over 0.3 to 0.5 mL/min Non-invasive. When endoscopy is inconclusive, or the patient is too unstable for it. It can guide an angiographic intervention.
Tagged red cell scan Bleeding as slow as 0.1 mL/min The most sensitive option, useful for intermittent bleeding. It takes time, so not in an emergency.
Angiography Active bleeding over 0.5 to 1 mL/min Invasive, but it allows embolisation. Reserved for massive bleeding or failed endoscopy.
Capsule endoscopy Small bowel lesions A swallowed camera, for obscure bleeding when both scopes were negative. Not for acute bleeding.
Reading an NG aspirate
  • Bloody or coffee-ground. Confirms an upper bleed.
  • Clear. Does not rule an upper bleed out. The source may be past the pylorus, or the bleeding may be intermittent.
  • Bilious and not bloody. Now an upper bleed is unlikely, because you have sampled past the pylorus and found nothing.

The asymmetry is the point. A positive result proves something; a negative result proves very little. NG tubes are not routinely placed in every GI bleed.

Bottom Line: EGD for upper, colonoscopy for lower, and both diagnose and treat. Resuscitate before you scope. A clear NG aspirate rules nothing out.
07

What you do first

Priority questions give you four defensible actions and ask which comes first. All four are things a nurse would do. You are being tested on ordering.

The ordering rules
  • Airway beats circulation when the patient is vomiting blood into it.
  • Circulation beats diagnosis. Resuscitate, then scope.
  • Access beats fluids. You cannot resuscitate through a 22 gauge in the hand.
  • The culture of this topic is drawing labs before the first intervention, not after.
  • Assessment beats the lab value. A normal hemoglobin does not change the plan.
  1. 1
    Airway. Check level of consciousness and gag reflex. Massive hematemesis with a reduced conscious level means intubation to protect the airway. Position on the side if they are vomiting blood.
  2. 2
    Two large-bore IVs, 18 gauge or bigger. Start crystalloid, normal saline or lactated Ringer's. Aim to restore perfusion: systolic over 90 and heart rate under 100.
  3. 3
    Labs and the blood bank. Type and crossmatch 2 to 6 units, CBC, metabolic panel, coagulation studies, liver function. Send them urgently, and have blood available before the procedure.
  4. 4
    Monitoring. Cardiac monitor, pulse oximetry, blood pressure every 15 minutes while unstable. A urinary catheter for accurate hourly output, aiming above 0.5 mL/kg/hr.
  5. 5
    Nothing by mouth, in preparation for endoscopy. Clear liquids can resume after a successful procedure if there is no rebleeding.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Urine Output Is Real-Time Perfusion
The kidneys are among the first organs to lose perfusion when cardiac output falls. So urine output tells you whether your resuscitation is working, right now, without waiting for a lab. Over 0.5 mL/kg/hr, which is about 35 mL an hour in a 70 kg adult.

Blood products

ProductWhen you give itWhat you are aiming for
Packed red cells Hemoglobin under 7 g/dL, or under 8 to 9 with coronary disease or symptoms Hemoglobin 7 to 9 g/dL. The restrictive strategy.
Fresh frozen plasma INR over 1.5 to 2.0 with active bleeding INR under 1.5
Platelets Platelets under 50,000 with active bleeding Platelets over 50,000
Massive transfusion protocol More than 10 units of red cells expected in 24 hours A 1 to 1 to 1 ratio of red cells, plasma and platelets
Why less blood is better

A restrictive strategy means transfusing at a hemoglobin of 7. It produces better outcomes than a liberal one.

The mechanism is clearest in varices. Transfusing raises the circulating volume. That raises portal pressure. Higher portal pressure makes the varices bleed harder. You can transfuse a patient into a worse bleed.

The exception is coronary artery disease or symptomatic anemia, where 8 to 9 is used instead. Follow the local protocol.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
More Blood Can Mean More Bleeding
In variceal bleeding, transfusion raises volume, volume raises portal pressure, and portal pressure is what is driving the bleed. The trigger is 7 g/dL, and that is a ceiling as much as a floor.

What you monitor

  • Vital signs. Every 15 minutes while unstable, hourly when stable. Watch the trend, not the single reading.
  • Serial hemoglobin, every 6 hours during active bleeding. Remember the first one lags.
  • Intake and output, strictly, including every blood product.
  • Stool. Colour, consistency, frequency, estimated volume.
  • Neurological status. A change here means perfusion is failing.
Rebleeding: call the provider
  • Fresh hematemesis, or increasing melena or hematochezia
  • Hemoglobin falling more than 2 g/dL despite transfusion
  • New tachycardia or hypotension
  • Rising transfusion requirement
  • A change in mental status

Four situations that change the plan

  • Variceal bleeding. Start octreotide. Give prophylactic ceftriaxone. Prepare for urgent EGD with band ligation, and have balloon tamponade available.
  • Anticoagulation. Warfarin is reversed with vitamin K plus fresh frozen plasma or 4-factor PCC. For DOACs, idarucizumab reverses dabigatran and andexanet reverses the factor Xa inhibitors.
  • Cirrhosis. Blood in the gut is a protein load, and protein becomes ammonia. Start lactulose and watch the mental status for hepatic encephalopathy.
  • Beta-blockers. They blunt the tachycardia you are relying on. A normal heart rate in a beta-blocked patient means less than it looks like.
Bottom Line: Airway, two large-bore IVs, crystalloid, crossmatch, nothing by mouth, then the scope. Transfuse at 7. For varices, octreotide and antibiotics before the bands.
08

The drugs

Discrimination axis: an ulcer bleed gets acid suppression; a variceal bleed gets pressure reduction.

Two bleeds, two mechanisms, two drugs. Get that pair right and most of the pharmacology follows.

Proton pump inhibitors, for ulcers

DrugDoseWhat you need to know
Pantoprazole 80 mg IV bolus, then 8 mg/hr infusion for 72 hours High dose for high-risk ulcer appearances. It stabilises the clot.
Esomeprazole 80 mg IV bolus, then 8 mg/hr infusion The alternative, on the same regimen.
Oral PPI 40 to 80 mg twice daily after endoscopy The step down from IV. Continue for 6 to 8 weeks.
Why the dose is so high

Gastric acid dissolves clots. A clot over an ulcer sits in the one environment designed to break it down.

Raising gastric pH above 6 lets the clot hold. That is what reduces rebleeding. Ordinary dosing does not get the pH that high, or keep it there.

High-dose IV is for high-risk ulcers: active bleeding, a visible vessel, or an adherent clot. Low-risk ulcers may only need oral.

Vasoactive drugs, for varices

DrugDoseHow it works, and what you watch
Octreotide 50 mcg IV bolus, then 50 mcg/hr for 3 to 5 days Reduces splanchnic blood flow, which drops portal pressure. First-line. Monitor glucose, since it can push it either way.
Vasopressin 0.2 to 0.4 units/min IV A potent splanchnic vasoconstrictor. It also constricts the coronaries, so it is given with nitroglycerin. Largely replaced by octreotide.
Terlipressin 2 mg IV every 4 hours initially A vasopressin analogue with fewer side effects. Used in Europe, not available in the US.

The rest

DrugWhyWhat you need to know
Erythromycin 250 mg IV, 30 to 120 minutes before EGD Used as a prokinetic, not an antibiotic. It empties the stomach so the endoscopist can see.
Ceftriaxone 1 g IV daily for 7 days in cirrhosis Prophylaxis in any cirrhotic with a GI bleed. It reduces infection and mortality.
Lactulose 30 to 45 mL orally or by NG every hour until a bowel movement Prevents hepatic encephalopathy from the protein load of blood in the gut.
Vitamin K 10 mg IV or orally For warfarin reversal. It takes 6 to 24 hours, so pair it with plasma when reversal is needed now.
Tranexamic acid 1 g IV An antifibrinolytic. The HALT-IT trial found no mortality benefit in GI bleeding, so it is not routinely recommended.
What you stop
  • Anticoagulants. Warfarin, heparin, DOACs. Reverse them if the bleeding is life-threatening.
  • Antiplatelets. Aspirin, clopidogrel. Hold them, unless there is a cardiac stent placed within 30 days.
  • NSAIDs. A major cause of the ulcer in the first place.
  • Beta-blockers. Note that they mask the tachycardia. Do not stop them abruptly.

Every one of these was prescribed for a reason. You are trading a bleeding risk for a clotting risk. That is a team decision, not a nursing one.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Stopping Is Also a Risk
A patient on apixaban has atrial fibrillation. A patient on clopidogrel may have a fresh stent. Holding the drug stops one problem and starts another, so the recent stent is the detail that changes the answer.
Bottom Line: Ulcers get high-dose IV PPI, because acid dissolves clots. Varices get octreotide and ceftriaxone, because pressure drives the bleed. Hold the blood thinners, with the team.
