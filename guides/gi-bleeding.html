The must-know facts from this guide, distilled for quick review.

  • Upper GI bleed (above ligament of Treitz) = hematemesis (vomiting blood), coffee-ground emesis, melena (black tarry stool). Lower GI bleed = hematochezia (bright red blood per rectum).
  • Most common causes: Upper = peptic ulcer disease (PUD), esophageal varices. Lower = diverticulosis, colorectal cancer, hemorrhoids.
  • Esophageal varices (from portal hypertension/cirrhosis) are the most DANGEROUS upper GI bleeds â€” massive hemorrhage risk. Treat with octreotide, band ligation, balloon tamponade (Blakemore tube).
  • Melena = upper GI bleed (blood digested by gastric acid = black/tarry). Hematochezia = usually lower GI bleed, BUT massive upper GI bleed can also cause it.
  • Priority assessment: Hemodynamic status first! HR, BP, orthostatic changes. Tachycardia is often the FIRST sign of significant blood loss.
  • Two large-bore IVs (16â€“18 gauge) immediately. Fluid resuscitate with crystalloids, then blood products. Type and crossmatch STAT.
  • NGT lavage: Confirms upper GI source. Coffee-ground or bloody return = upper GI bleed. Clear or bilious return = bleeding below the pylorus.
  • EGD (esophagogastroduodenoscopy) = gold standard for upper GI bleed diagnosis AND treatment. Colonoscopy for lower GI bleeds.
  • PPIs (IV pantoprazole/esomeprazole) reduce rebleeding risk from peptic ulcers. Often given as continuous infusion post-EGD.
  • Common mistake: Focusing on Hgb/Hct early in acute bleeding â€” these values DON'T drop immediately because plasma and RBCs are lost proportionally. Serial values over 6â€“24 hours show the true picture.

GI Tract Anatomy & Blood Supply

Understanding the anatomical division

The gastrointestinal tract is divided into upper and lower portions by the Ligament of Treitz (suspensory ligament of the duodenum). This anatomical landmark is critical for classifying GI bleeding and guiding diagnostic and treatment approaches.

The GI Tract Journey

Esophagus

Upper GI tract begins here. Common bleeding sources: varices (portal hypertension), Mallory-Weiss tears, esophagitis, esophageal cancer.

Stomach

Most common source of UGIB. Causes: peptic ulcer disease (PUD), gastritis, gastric cancer, Dieulafoy lesions, angiodysplasia.

Duodenum

Duodenal ulcers are the #1 cause of UGIB. Posterior ulcers can erode into the gastroduodenal artery causing massive hemorrhage.

Ligament of Treitz

THE DIVIDING LINE! Above = Upper GI bleed. Below = Lower GI bleed. This landmark determines classification, workup, and management.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Ligament of Treitz
This anatomical landmark is THE dividing line for GI bleeding classification! The Ligament of Treitz (also called the suspensory ligament of the duodenum) marks the junction between the duodenum and jejunum. Bleeding above this point is Upper GI Bleeding (UGIB) - typically presenting with melena (black tarry stool) or hematemesis. Bleeding below is Lower GI Bleeding (LGIB) - typically presenting with hematochezia (bright red blood). This classification determines whether the patient needs an upper endoscopy (EGD) or colonoscopy.

Jejunum & Ileum

Small bowel bleeding is rare (5% of GI bleeds). Causes: angiodysplasia, Crohn's disease, tumors, Meckel's diverticulum.

Colon

Most common site of LGIB. Causes: diverticulosis (#1), angiodysplasia, colorectal cancer, hemorrhoids, IBD.

Rectum & Anus

Common sources of bright red blood: hemorrhoids (internal/external), anal fissures, rectal cancer, radiation proctitis.

Clinical Pearl: Blood Supply

The GI tract receives approximately 25% of cardiac output at rest, increasing to 35% after meals. The celiac trunk supplies the upper GI tract (foregut), the superior mesenteric artery (SMA) supplies the midgut (small bowel to mid-transverse colon), and the inferior mesenteric artery (IMA) supplies the hindgut (distal colon to rectum).

Key Anatomical Considerations

  • Portal Hypertension: Causes esophageal/gastric varices via collateral circulation when portal pressure exceeds 12 mmHg
  • Arterial Supply: GI arteries are end arteries with limited collateral flowâ€”occlusion causes ischemia
  • Watershed Areas: Splenic flexure and rectosigmoid junction are vulnerable to ischemia
  • Diverticula: Form at weak points where blood vessels penetrate the muscularis, explaining why diverticular bleeding is arterial

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: The ligament of Treitz is the dividing line â€” above it = upper GI, below it = lower GI. Know the GI anatomy because the location determines the presentation, workup, and treatment approach.

Upper vs Lower GI Bleeding

Key differences in presentation and causes

Distinguishing between upper and lower GI bleeding is essential because diagnostic workup and management differ significantly. The stool characteristics, clinical presentation, and common causes help guide this differentiation.

Upper GI Bleeding

Above the Ligament of Treitz
Common Causes
  • Peptic Ulcer Disease (40-50%) - #1 cause
  • Esophageal/Gastric Varices - portal HTN
  • Erosive Gastritis - NSAIDs, alcohol
  • Mallory-Weiss Tear - forceful vomiting
  • Malignancy - gastric/esophageal cancer
Stool Characteristics
  • Melena - black, tarry, foul-smelling stool
  • Requires >50-100 mL blood in GI tract
  • Blood oxidized by gastric acid and bacteria
Other Signs
  • Hematemesis (vomiting blood - bright red or coffee-ground)
  • Coffee-ground emesis (partially digested blood)
  • Positive NG aspirate for blood

Lower GI Bleeding

Below the Ligament of Treitz
Common Causes
  • Diverticulosis (30-40%) - #1 cause
  • Angiodysplasia - arteriovenous malformations
  • Colorectal Cancer/Polyps - occult or overt
  • Hemorrhoids - most common benign cause
  • IBD - Crohn's, ulcerative colitis
Stool Characteristics
  • Hematochezia - bright red blood per rectum
  • Blood mixed with or coating stool
  • Maroon stool suggests proximal colonic source
Other Signs
  • Usually painless (diverticulosis)
  • Abdominal pain with IBD, ischemia
  • Change in bowel habits may suggest malignancy
Critical: Brisk UGIB Can Cause Hematochezia!

A massive upper GI bleed can present with bright red blood per rectum (hematochezia) because blood moves quickly through the GI tract without time for oxidation. If a patient has hematochezia with hemodynamic instability, consider UGIB and place an NG tube or perform upper endoscopy first!

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Massive UGIB Exception
Don't assume bright red blood always means lower GI bleeding! When there's a brisk upper GI bleed, blood moves so quickly through the intestines that it doesn't have time to be digested and turn black. The result? Hematochezia (bright red blood per rectum) - which you'd normally associate with LGIB. The key clue: if the patient is hemodynamically unstable (hypotensive, tachycardic) with BRBPR, think UGIB first! These patients need an upper endoscopy before colonoscopy. The instability suggests massive bleeding, which is more common from upper sources.

Melena vs Hematochezia: Key Differences

Characteristic Melena Hematochezia
Appearance Black, tarry, sticky Bright red, maroon
Odor Distinctively foul (digested blood) Typical stool odor
Typical Source Upper GI (proximal to jejunum) Lower GI (colon, rectum)
Blood Volume Required 50-100 mL minimum Variable
Transit Time 8+ hours in GI tract Rapid transit or distal source
Exception Right-sided colonic bleeding (slow transit) Massive UGIB (rapid transit)
NCLEX Alert: Stool Color Clues

Melena = Upper GI (usually). Hematochezia = Lower GI (usually). But remember: massive UGIB can cause hematochezia, and a slow-bleeding right colon lesion can cause melena. Always assess hemodynamic status and consider the clinical picture!

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Upper GI bleeds (PUD, varices, Mallory-Weiss) are more common and more dangerous. Lower GI bleeds (diverticulosis, cancer) are usually less acute but still require evaluation. Varices = highest mortality.

Assessment & Hemodynamic Status

Recognizing severity and instability

The initial assessment of a GI bleed patient focuses on hemodynamic stability. The severity of bleeding determines the urgency of intervention, resuscitation needs, and timing of endoscopy.

Signs & Symptoms of GI Bleeding

Hematemesis

Vomiting blood - bright red (active) or coffee-ground (digested). Indicates UGIB.

Melena/Hematochezia

Black tarry stool or bright red blood per rectum. Key to localizing source.

Tachycardia

HR > 100 bpm. Early sign of hypovolemia. May be masked by beta-blockers.

Hypotension

SBP < 90 mmHg or drop > 20 mmHg. Indicates significant blood loss (>30%).

Orthostatic Changes

Dizziness on standing. HR increase >20 or SBP drop >20 = positive.

Pallor & Diaphoresis

Pale skin, cool extremities, sweating. Signs of hypovolemic shock.

Hemodynamic Stability Assessment

Hemodynamically Stable

  • Normal vital signs (HR < 100, SBP > 100)
  • No orthostatic hypotension
  • Alert and oriented
  • Adequate urine output (> 0.5 mL/kg/hr)
  • Warm, well-perfused extremities
  • Management: Admit to floor, endoscopy within 24 hrs

Hemodynamically Unstable

  • Tachycardia (HR > 100) or hypotension (SBP < 90)
  • Positive orthostatic changes
  • Altered mental status, confusion
  • Oliguria (< 0.5 mL/kg/hr)
  • Cool, clammy, mottled extremities
  • Management: ICU, emergent endoscopy, massive transfusion protocol

Estimating Blood Loss

Class I < 15% (< 750 mL) < 100 Normal Slightly anxious
Class II 15-30% (750-1500 mL) 100-120 Normal Mildly anxious
Class III 30-40% (1500-2000 mL) 120-140 Decreased Anxious, confused

Clinical Pearl: Initial Hemoglobin Can Be Misleading!

In acute GI bleeding, the initial hemoglobin may be normal because both red cells and plasma are lost proportionally. Hemodilution from fluid resuscitation and equilibration takes 24-72 hours. A "normal" initial Hgb in an unstable patient does NOT rule out significant bleeding!

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Initial Hgb Misleading
Don't be fooled by a "normal" hemoglobin in acute bleeding! When someone loses blood, they lose both red cells AND plasma in equal proportions - so the concentration (hemoglobin) initially stays the same. It's only after hemodilution occurs (from IV fluid resuscitation and the body pulling fluid from tissues) that the hemoglobin drops. This equilibration takes 24-72 hours. A patient can be bleeding significantly with a normal initial Hgb. Always assess hemodynamic status (heart rate, blood pressure, mental status) rather than relying on the first lab value.

Risk Stratification: Glasgow-Blatchford Score

The Glasgow-Blatchford Score predicts the need for intervention (transfusion, endoscopy, surgery) in UGIB. A score of 0 identifies very low-risk patients who may be candidates for outpatient management.

Diagnostic Procedures

Endoscopy and imaging modalities

The diagnostic approach to GI bleeding aims to identify the bleeding source and provide therapeutic intervention when possible. Endoscopy is the cornerstone of diagnosis and treatment for both upper and lower GI bleeding.

Endoscopy Timing Guidelines

Stable, Low Risk
Within 24 hours
High Risk Features
Within 12 hours
Hemodynamically Unstable
Emergent (after resuscitation)
Suspected Variceal Bleed
Within 12 hours

Upper GI Endoscopy (EGD)

Aspect Details
Indication Suspected UGIB (hematemesis, coffee-ground emesis, melena, positive NG aspirate)
Sensitivity 92-98% for identifying bleeding source
Therapeutic Options Injection therapy, thermal coagulation, hemoclips, band ligation (varices)
Pre-Procedure NPO, IV access, type & screen, resuscitation, consider erythromycin (gastric emptying)
Post-Procedure Monitor for rebleeding (H/H q6h), aspiration, perforation

Colonoscopy

Aspect Details
Indication Suspected LGIB (hematochezia) after stabilization
Timing Within 24 hours of presentation (after bowel prep if possible)
Challenge Active bleeding obscures visualization; may need urgent/emergent scope without full prep
Therapeutic Options Clips, thermal coagulation, injection, APC (argon plasma coagulation)
Diagnostic Yield 74-100% with adequate prep; lower with active bleeding

Other Diagnostic Modalities

CT Angiography

Non-invasive; detects active bleeding > 0.3-0.5 mL/min. Useful when endoscopy is inconclusive or patient is too unstable. Can guide angiographic intervention.

Tagged RBC Scan

Nuclear medicine study. Detects bleeding rates as low as 0.1 mL/min. Useful for intermittent bleeding. Takes time; not for emergencies.

Angiography

Invasive; requires active bleeding > 0.5-1 mL/min. Allows therapeutic embolization. Reserve for massive bleeding or failed endoscopy.

Capsule Endoscopy

Swallowed camera for small bowel evaluation. Used for obscure GI bleeding when EGD and colonoscopy are negative. Not for acute bleeding.

NCLEX Alert: NG Tube in GI Bleeding

An NG tube aspirate can help differentiate UGIB from LGIB:

  • Bloody or coffee-ground aspirate: Confirms UGIB
  • Clear aspirate: Does NOT rule out UGIB (bleeding may be beyond pylorus or intermittent)
  • Bilious, non-bloody aspirate: Makes UGIB unlikely

NG tubes are NOT routinely placed in all GI bleedsâ€”discuss with the team.

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: EGD is the gold standard for upper GI bleeds (both diagnostic and therapeutic). Colonoscopy for lower GI. NGT lavage localizes the source. Type and crossmatch, CBC, coagulation studies, BMP, lactate for all GI bleeds.

Priority Nursing Interventions

Evidence-based management

Nursing care for GI bleeding focuses on rapid assessment, hemodynamic stabilization, close monitoring, and preparation for diagnostic/therapeutic procedures. The first hours are critical.

Immediate Priorities: ABCs

Airway Protection

Assess LOC and gag reflex. Massive hematemesis with altered LOC = intubate for airway protection. Position patient on side if vomiting blood.

IV Access & Resuscitation

Two large-bore IVs (18G or larger). Start crystalloid resuscitation (NS or LR). Goal: restore perfusion, SBP > 90, HR < 100.

Labs & Blood Bank

Type & crossmatch (2-6 units), CBC, BMP, coags (PT/INR, PTT), LFTs. Send labs STAT. Have blood available before procedure.

Continuous Monitoring

Cardiac monitor, pulse ox, BP q15min if unstable. Foley catheter for strict I&O. Goal UOP > 0.5 mL/kg/hr.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Goal UOP
Urine output is your best real-time indicator of organ perfusion! In a bleeding patient, aim for urine output greater than 0.5 mL/kg/hr (for a 70kg patient, that's about 35 mL/hour or more). Why is this important? The kidneys are very sensitive to blood flow - when cardiac output drops from blood loss, the kidneys are among the first organs to show decreased perfusion. If urine output drops below this threshold (oliguria), it signals inadequate resuscitation. Place a Foley catheter for accurate hourly monitoring and document strictly. Trending urine output helps guide fluid and blood product administration.

Blood Product Administration

Product Trigger/Indication Goal
PRBCs Hgb < 7 g/dL (or < 8-9 if CAD, symptomatic) Hgb 7-9 g/dL (restrictive strategy preferred)
Fresh Frozen Plasma INR > 1.5-2.0, active bleeding INR < 1.5
Platelets Plt < 50,000 with active bleeding Plt > 50,000
Massive Transfusion Protocol Expected > 10 units PRBCs in 24h 1:1:1 ratio (PRBC:FFP:Plt)
Clinical Pearl: Restrictive Transfusion Strategy

Studies show that a restrictive transfusion strategy (Hgb trigger 7 g/dL) is associated with improved outcomes in GI bleeding compared to liberal transfusion. Over-transfusion in variceal bleeding can increase portal pressure and worsen bleeding.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Restrictive Transfusion
Less is more when it comes to blood transfusions in GI bleeding! Research shows that a restrictive transfusion strategy (transfusing when hemoglobin drops below 7 g/dL) actually leads to better patient outcomes than giving blood more liberally. Why? Over-transfusion can increase blood volume and, in patients with variceal bleeding from liver cirrhosis, this raises portal pressure and can make the bleeding worse. Exception: patients with coronary artery disease or symptomatic anemia may need a higher threshold (8-9 g/dL). Always follow your institution's protocol.

Monitoring & Documentation

  • Vital signs: q15min if unstable, q1h when stable. Note trends.
  • Serial H/H: q6h for active bleeding; remember initial values may be misleading
  • I&O: Strict monitoring. Track all blood product administration.
  • Stool output: Document color, consistency, frequency, estimated volume
  • Neuro checks: Monitor for changes indicating worsening perfusion
  • Signs of rebleeding: Fresh hematemesis, increasing melena/hematochezia, dropping H/H, hemodynamic changes
Signs of Rebleeding - Notify MD Immediately!
  • Fresh hematemesis or increased melena/hematochezia
  • Drop in Hgb > 2 g/dL despite transfusion
  • Hemodynamic instability (tachycardia, hypotension)
  • Increased transfusion requirements
  • Change in mental status

Special Considerations

Variceal Bleeding

Start octreotide infusion. Prophylactic antibiotics (ceftriaxone). Prepare for emergent EGD with band ligation. Have balloon tamponade ready.

Anticoagulation Reversal

Warfarin: Vitamin K + FFP or 4-factor PCC. DOACs: consider reversal agents (idarucizumab for dabigatran, andexanet for Xa inhibitors).

NPO Status

Keep patient NPO in preparation for endoscopy. Clear liquids may resume after successful procedure if no rebleeding.

Hepatic Encephalopathy Prevention

In cirrhotics: blood in GI tract = protein load = ammonia. Start lactulose. Monitor mental status closely.

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: ABCs first, then 2 large-bore IVs, aggressive fluid resuscitation, blood transfusion (crossmatched preferred, O-neg if emergent), NPO status, and prepare for endoscopy. For varices: octreotide + emergency EGD with banding.

Pharmacological Management

Key medications in GI bleeding

Proton Pump Inhibitors (PPIs)

Drug Dose Key Points
Pantoprazole (Protonix) 80 mg IV bolus, then 8 mg/hr infusion x 72h High-dose for high-risk ulcer stigmata; promotes clot stability
Esomeprazole (Nexium) 80 mg IV bolus, then 8 mg/hr infusion Alternative to pantoprazole; same high-dose regimen
Oral PPI 40-80 mg BID after endoscopy Transition to oral after initial IV therapy; continue 6-8 weeks
NCLEX Alert: Why High-Dose PPI?

Gastric acid dissolves blood clots. Raising gastric pH > 6 with high-dose PPI stabilizes clots and reduces rebleeding. High-dose IV PPI is indicated for high-risk ulcers (active bleeding, visible vessel, adherent clot). Low-risk ulcers may only need oral PPI.

Vasoactive Medications (for Variceal Bleeding)

Drug Dose Mechanism & Nursing Considerations
Octreotide (Sandostatin) 50 mcg IV bolus, then 50 mcg/hr infusion x 3-5 days Decreases splanchnic blood flow, reduces portal pressure. First-line for variceal bleed. Monitor glucose (can cause hyper/hypoglycemia).
Vasopressin 0.2-0.4 units/min IV Potent splanchnic vasoconstrictor. Causes coronary vasoconstrictionâ€”use with nitroglycerin. Largely replaced by octreotide.
Terlipressin 2 mg IV q4h initially Vasopressin analog with fewer side effects. Not available in US. Used in Europe.

Other Important Medications

Drug Use in GI Bleeding Nursing Considerations
Erythromycin 250 mg IV 30-120 min before EGD Prokinetic - improves gastric emptying and visualization. Give if significant blood in stomach.
Antibiotics (cirrhosis) Ceftriaxone 1g IV daily x 7 days Prophylaxis in cirrhotic patients with GI bleed. Reduces infections and mortality.
Lactulose 30-45 mL PO/NG q1h until bowel movement Prevents hepatic encephalopathy from blood protein load in cirrhotics.
Vitamin K 10 mg IV/PO For warfarin reversal. Takes 6-24 hours to work; use with FFP for immediate reversal.
Tranexamic Acid (TXA) 1g IV Antifibrinolytic. Recent trials (HALT-IT) showed no mortality benefit in GI bleeding; not routinely recommended.

Medications to Hold or Avoid

Stop or Hold These Medications!
  • Anticoagulants: Warfarin, heparin, DOACs - reverse if life-threatening bleeding
  • Antiplatelet agents: Aspirin, clopidogrel - hold unless cardiac stent within 30 days
  • NSAIDs: Ibuprofen, naproxen - major risk factor for peptic ulcer bleeding
  • Beta-blockers: May mask tachycardia response to hypovolemia (don't stop abruptly)

Always weigh bleeding risk vs. thrombotic risk with the medical team!

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Hold Anticoagulants
Stop the blood thinners - but it's not always simple! In active GI bleeding, hold anticoagulants (warfarin, heparin, DOACs like rivaroxaban, apixaban) and antiplatelet agents (aspirin, clopidogrel). For life-threatening bleeding, consider reversal: Vitamin K + FFP (or 4-factor PCC) for warfarin, idarucizumab for dabigatran, andexanet alfa for Xa inhibitors. But here's the tricky part: these patients are often on blood thinners for a reason (atrial fibrillation, mechanical valve, recent stent). You're trading bleeding risk for clotting risk. Always discuss with the medical team to weigh the risks - especially if the patient had a cardiac stent within 30 days.

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: PPIs (IV pantoprazole) for peptic ulcer bleeds. Octreotide (Sandostatin) for variceal bleeds (reduces portal pressure). Vasopressin for refractory bleeding. Vitamin K and FFP to correct coagulopathy.
