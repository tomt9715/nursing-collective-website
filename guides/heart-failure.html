Heart Failure

Four look-alike pairs, twelve numbers that decide your action, and how to order four reasonable answers when the question asks which comes first.

Reading Time
15-20 minutes
Difficulty Level
Intermediate
Last Updated

The Nursing Collective

By the end, without notes

  • Say which side failed from the findings alone, and name the axis that told you
  • Explain why a normal ejection fraction does not rule out heart failure
  • Put four reasonable nursing actions in the right order and defend the first one
  • State which drugs raise potassium and which lower it, and what you hold
  • Separate digoxin toxicity from worsening failure using two findings
01

What actually gets you

Heart failure is not a hard topic. It is a topic built out of four look-alike pairs, and the pairs are where the questions live. Learn the pairs and the rest follows.

Points get lost here in three predictable ways. Read these before you read anything else.

You learned the signs as one list

Then a scenario gives you crackles, orthopnea and flat neck veins, and you cannot say which side failed. The signs are not one list. They are two lists, and the scenario is telling you which one it wants.

You assumed a normal EF rules out heart failure

It does not. Roughly half of heart failure patients have a normal ejection fraction. Their heart squeezes fine; it will not fill. If you treat normal EF as a green light, you will miss every HFpEF question on the exam.

You knew the drugs and missed the potassium

Your patient takes furosemide and lisinopril. One drives potassium down. One drives it up. The question is never "what does lisinopril do"; it is which lab you check and what you hold.

Bottom Line: Stop studying heart failure as a disease. Study it as four discriminations: left or right, squeeze or fill, low potassium or high, toxicity or worsening disease.
02

The four things

For every form of heart failure below, you should be able to say four things with the book closed: the one-sentence definition, the clue you would actually see in a patient, the priority nursing action, and the first-line drug with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Type Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line drug
Left-sided The left ventricle cannot move blood forward, so it backs up into the lungs. Crackles, orthopnea, pink frothy sputum, three pillows to sleep. Sit them up, then oxygen. Furosemide. Watch potassium falling.
Right-sided The right ventricle cannot move blood forward, so it backs up into the body. Peripheral edema, JVD, hepatomegaly, weight gain. Daily weight, strict intake and output. Furosemide. Same potassium watch.
HFrEF (systolic) The ventricle is weak and cannot squeeze; EF under 40 percent. Low EF on the echo, dilated heart. Reduce workload before adding volume. ACE inhibitor plus beta-blocker. Start low, go slow.
HFpEF (diastolic) The ventricle is stiff and cannot fill; EF 50 percent or higher. Normal EF with real heart failure symptoms. Control blood pressure and rate to protect filling time. Treat the cause, usually hypertension. Diuretics for symptoms only.
Copy this down

Write these four rows on one index card, by hand. Left backs into the lungs. Right backs into the body. Under 40 cannot squeeze. Over 50 cannot fill. That card is most of the exam.

03

How the heart fails

Heart failure means the heart cannot deliver enough blood to meet the body's demand. It is a pump problem, not a plumbing problem. Blood is not lost; it backs up behind the chamber that failed.

That single idea explains every sign you have to memorise. Find the failing chamber and the symptoms tell themselves.

The compensation trap

When output drops, the body compensates. Three mechanisms fire, and all three help briefly and hurt eventually.

MechanismWhat it doesWhy it backfires
Sympathetic activationRaises heart rate and contractilityFaster rate means less filling time and more oxygen demand
RAAS activationRetains sodium and water to raise volumeMore volume for a pump that already cannot handle it
Ventricular remodellingMuscle thickens or the chamber dilatesA thicker wall fills worse; a stretched one squeezes worse

This is why the drugs look backwards at first. Beta-blockers slow a heart that is already struggling. ACE inhibitors block the system holding the blood pressure up. Both work because they stop the compensation from destroying the heart.

Bottom Line: Every compensatory mechanism buys time and costs muscle. Treatment interrupts compensation on purpose.
04

Left or right

Discrimination axis: where does the blood back up.

This is the pair that shows up most. You do not need two symptom lists memorised in order. You need one question: which way does the blood go when this chamber quits.

Left-sided: backs into the lungs
  • Crackles on auscultation
  • Dyspnea, orthopnea, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea
  • Pink frothy sputum
  • S3 gallop
  • Cough, worse lying flat

Most common cause: hypertension, coronary artery disease, myocardial infarction, valve disease.

Right-sided: backs into the body
  • Peripheral edema, sacral if bedridden
  • Jugular vein distension
  • Hepatomegaly, hepatojugular reflux
  • Weight gain and ascites
  • Nausea from gut congestion

Most common cause: left-sided failure. After that, pulmonary hypertension and COPD.

The axis that separates them

Lungs or body. Left equals lungs. Right equals rest. If the scenario gives you a wet chest and dry ankles, it is left. Swollen ankles and clear lungs, it is right. Both, it is biventricular, and the most common reason the right side failed is that the left side failed first.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Left = Lungs
Left equals Lungs. Right equals Rest of the body. Two words, and the scenario sorts itself. When a question hands you findings from both sides, ask which set came first; that is usually what it is testing.
Write this down

Blank paper. Two columns, left and right. Fill in five findings each without looking. Then check. The ones you missed are the ones the exam will use.

05

Squeeze or fill

Discrimination axis: is the problem emptying or filling.

Left and right tell you where the blood went. This pair tells you what the muscle is doing wrong. Ejection fraction is the number that separates them.

HFrEF, systolic: cannot squeeze

EF under 40 percent. The ventricle is weak and dilated. It fills normally and then fails to eject.

Think of a stretched balloon that will not snap back.

Treatment: ACE inhibitor or ARB, beta-blocker, aldosterone antagonist. These are survival drugs, not comfort drugs.

HFpEF, diastolic: cannot fill

EF 50 percent or higher. The ventricle is stiff and thick. It ejects a normal fraction of a volume that was too small to begin with.

Think of a balloon so thick you cannot inflate it.

Treatment: control blood pressure and heart rate. Slower rate means longer filling time. Diuretics relieve symptoms but do not fix the stiffness.

The normal EF trap

A patient with an EF of 55 percent can be in genuine heart failure. Normal ejection fraction does not mean a normal heart. It means the fraction is normal; the volume being ejected may be small because the stiff ventricle never filled.

When a question gives you classic heart failure symptoms and a normal EF, it is testing exactly this. The answer is HFpEF, not "no heart failure".

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Normal EF Trap
Ejection fraction is a percentage, not a volume. A stiff ventricle that holds very little can still eject a normal percentage of it. Normal EF plus real symptoms equals HFpEF.
06

Assessment, in order

Assessment questions are rarely asking what you would check. They are asking what you would check first. Order matters more than completeness.

  1. 1
    Airway and breathing. Auscultate for crackles. Note rate, effort, saturation, and any pink frothy sputum. A patient who cannot breathe does not need a weight first.
  2. 2
    Perfusion. Mental status, skin temperature, capillary refill, urine output. Confusion in a heart failure patient is a perfusion finding, not a psychiatric one.
  3. 3
    Cardiovascular. Heart sounds for an S3 gallop, rate and rhythm, blood pressure, JVD assessed with the head at 45 degrees.
  4. 4
    Fluid status. Daily weight, edema graded 1+ to 4+, strict intake and output, sacral edema if the patient is bedridden.
  5. 5
    Function. Activity tolerance, fatigue, dyspnea on exertion, ability to manage daily activities.
The weight rule

Report a gain of more than 2 pounds in 24 hours, or more than 5 pounds in a week. That is the threshold, and it is a common exam number.

Weight is the earliest reliable sign of fluid retention. Visible edema needs several litres before it appears; the scale catches it first.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Daily Weights
Same time, same scale, same clothing, after voiding and before eating. Change the conditions and the number means nothing. Teach it exactly that way and the patient teaching questions answer themselves.

07

What you do first

Priority questions give you four defensible actions and ask which comes first. All four are things a nurse would do. That is the trick. You are being tested on ordering, not on correctness.

The ordering rules
  • Breathing beats fluid. A patient in respiratory distress gets positioned and oxygenated before anyone weighs them.
  • What you can do now beats what needs an order. Raising the head of the bed needs no prescription.
  • Assessment beats intervention, unless the airway is failing.
  • Least invasive first. Position, then oxygen, then drugs, then machines.

Acute decompensation

For acute exacerbation the sequence is LMNOP. It is worth knowing because it is already in priority order.

  1. L
    Lasix. IV loop diuretic. Expect urine output to increase within about 30 minutes of an IV dose. Watch potassium falling.
  2. M
    Morphine. Reduces preload and eases the work of breathing. It also settles the anxiety that is driving the respiratory rate up.
  3. N
    Nitrates. Venous dilation drops preload, so less volume returns to a ventricle that cannot handle it.
  4. O
    Oxygen. Target a saturation above 94 percent. Escalate to BiPAP if the work of breathing does not settle.
  5. P
    Position. High Fowler's, 45 to 90 degrees. Legs dangling if the blood pressure allows. This one is free and immediate, so in a "what first" question it often wins.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
LMNOP for Acute HF
LMNOP lists the drugs first because it spells a word, not because it ranks them. In a question asking what to do first, position and oxygen usually come before the diuretic; you can do both immediately and neither needs an order.

Ongoing management

InterventionTargetWhy
Sodium restriction2 to 3 g per daySodium holds water in the vessels

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Prevent Valsalva
Straining against a closed glottis drops cardiac output sharply, and a failing heart has no reserve to spare. Stool softeners are given to heart failure patients before constipation happens, not after.
08

Medications and the potassium problem

You do not need to recite mechanisms. You need to know what each drug does to potassium, what you monitor, and what makes you hold it.

ClassExamplesEffect on K+What you monitor or hold for
ACE inhibitorsLisinopril, enalaprilRaisesDry cough, angioedema. Hold if potassium is above 5.5.
ARBsLosartan, valsartanRaisesUsed when the ACE cough will not settle. Same potassium watch.
Beta-blockersCarvedilol, metoprolol succinateNeutralHeart rate and blood pressure. Start low, go slow, never stop abruptly.
Loop diureticsFurosemide, bumetanideLowersPotassium, hearing, morning dosing so they are not up all night.
Aldosterone antagonistsSpironolactone, eplerenoneRaisesPotassium. Spironolactone can cause gynecomastia.
DigoxinDigoxinNeutral, but low K+ causes toxicityNarrow range 0.5 to 2.0. Hold if heart rate is under 60.
ARNISacubitril with valsartanRaisesNever give with an ACE inhibitor. Requires a 36 hour washout.
The axis that separates them

Loops push potassium down. ACE inhibitors, ARBs, ARNIs and aldosterone antagonists push it up. Most heart failure patients take drugs from both groups at once, which is why the potassium sits in the middle of so many questions.

Low potassium is what makes digoxin toxic at a normal level. That is the link the exam wants.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Watch Potassium
Loops drop it. ACE inhibitors, ARBs and aldosterone antagonists raise it. Hold the ACE inhibitor above 5.5. And remember that a low potassium turns a therapeutic digoxin level into a toxic one.

Digoxin toxicity or worsening failure

Discrimination axis: both cause fatigue and nausea. Two findings separate them.

Points to digoxin toxicity
  • Yellow or green halos, blurred vision
  • Bradycardia, heart block
  • Confusion out of proportion to the rest
  • Potassium is low
Points to worsening heart failure
  • Weight is climbing
  • Crackles, increasing dyspnea
  • Edema worsening
  • Heart rate is up, not down
The two that decide it

Visual changes and heart rate direction. Halos and a slow pulse mean toxicity. Rising weight and a fast pulse mean the failure is progressing. Hold digoxin if the heart rate is under 60 and tell the provider.

Say these four out loud

Pick any form of heart failure. Without looking, say the one-sentence definition, the clue you would see in a patient, the priority action, and the first-line drug with its teaching point.

If you can do that for all four forms, you are ready. If you cannot, you know exactly which section to reopen.

Bottom Line: You will not be asked what heart failure is. You will be given a patient and four reasonable actions, and asked which comes first. Study the discriminations and the ordering, in that order.

Key Takeaways

Left-sided HF = Pulmonary symptoms (dyspnea, crackles, orthopnea)

Right-sided HF = Systemic symptoms (edema, JVD, hepatomegaly)

HFrEF = Weak pump, EF < 40% (systolic dysfunction)

HFpEF = Stiff heart, EF ≥ 50% (diastolic dysfunction)

Daily weights are the most sensitive indicator of fluid status

ACE-I + Beta-blocker = Cornerstone therapy for HFrEF
