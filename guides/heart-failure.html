Cardiac Anatomy & Physiology Review

Before understanding heart failure, it's essential to review normal cardiac function. The heart is a four-chambered muscular pump responsible for circulating blood throughout the body.

The Heart's Chambers and Blood Flow

Clinical Pearl: Blood Flow Pathway

Remember the pathway: Body → Right Atrium → Right Ventricle → Lungs → Left Atrium → Left Ventricle → Body

  • Right Side of Heart: Receives deoxygenated blood from the body (via superior/inferior vena cava) and pumps it to the lungs for oxygenation
  • Left Side of Heart: Receives oxygenated blood from the lungs (via pulmonary veins) and pumps it to the systemic circulation
  • Left Ventricle: The primary "workhorse" - has the thickest muscular wall because it must generate enough pressure to pump blood to the entire body

Key Cardiac Function Concepts

Term Definition Normal Value
Cardiac Output (CO) Amount of blood pumped by the heart per minute (HR x SV) 4-8 L/min
Stroke Volume (SV) Amount of blood ejected with each heartbeat 60-100 mL/beat
Ejection Fraction (EF) Percentage of blood pumped out with each contraction 55-70%
Preload Volume of blood in ventricles at end of diastole (stretch) Assessed via CVP/PAWP
Afterload Resistance the heart must overcome to eject blood Assessed via SVR
Contractility Force of myocardial contraction (independent of preload) Intrinsic property
Memory Aid: Determinants of Cardiac Output

CO = HR x SV

Stroke Volume is determined by: P-A-C

  • Preload (venous return)
  • Afterload (resistance)
  • Contractility (pump strength)

Pathophysiology of Heart Failure

Heart failure (HF) occurs when the heart cannot pump enough blood to meet the body's metabolic demands. It is NOT a disease itself, but a clinical syndrome resulting from structural or functional cardiac problems.

Definition

Heart failure is the inability of the heart to pump sufficient blood to meet the body's oxygen and nutrient needs, leading to inadequate tissue perfusion and congestion.

Common Causes of Heart Failure

Category Examples Mechanism
Coronary Artery Disease MI, Ischemia Myocardial damage reduces contractility
Hypertension Chronic HTN Increased afterload causes LV hypertrophy
Valvular Disease Stenosis, Regurgitation Abnormal flow patterns increase workload
Cardiomyopathy Dilated, Hypertrophic Structural changes impair function
Arrhythmias A-fib, Tachycardia Inefficient filling or ejection

Compensatory Mechanisms

When cardiac output drops, the body activates several compensatory mechanisms. Initially helpful, these become harmful over time:

Body's Compensatory Responses

1
Sympathetic Nervous System Activation

Increases heart rate and contractility. Problem: Increases myocardial oxygen demand and causes vasoconstriction (increased afterload).

2
RAAS Activation (Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone)

Retains sodium and water to increase preload. Problem: Leads to fluid overload, edema, and increased cardiac workload.

3
Ventricular Remodeling

Heart muscle hypertrophies (thickens) or dilates. Problem: Eventually leads to decreased efficiency and worsening failure.

4
Natriuretic Peptide Release (BNP)

Promotes vasodilation and diuresis. Benefit: Counteracts fluid retention. Used as diagnostic marker (elevated BNP indicates HF).

Critical Understanding

Compensatory mechanisms maintain cardiac output short-term but cause long-term damage. This is why we use ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, and diuretics - to block these harmful compensatory responses!

Left-Sided vs Right-Sided Heart Failure

The symptoms of heart failure depend on which side of the heart is failing. Understanding this distinction is crucial for assessment and NCLEX questions.

NCLEX Key Concept

Think: "Where does the blood back up?"

  • Left heart fails → Blood backs up into the LUNGS (pulmonary congestion)
  • Right heart fails → Blood backs up into the BODY (systemic congestion)
Left-Sided Heart Failure

Primary Symptoms (Pulmonary)

  • Dyspnea (shortness of breath)
  • Orthopnea (difficulty breathing lying flat)
  • Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnea (PND)
  • Crackles/Rales on lung auscultation
  • Cough (may be pink, frothy sputum)
  • S3 heart sound (ventricular gallop)
  • Fatigue and decreased exercise tolerance
  • Pulmonary edema (severe cases)

Most Common Cause

Hypertension, CAD, MI, Aortic/Mitral valve disease

Right-Sided Heart Failure

Primary Symptoms (Systemic)

  • Peripheral edema (feet, ankles, legs)
  • Jugular Venous Distension (JVD)
  • Hepatomegaly (enlarged liver)
  • Ascites (abdominal fluid)
  • Weight gain (fluid retention)
  • Nausea/Anorexia (GI congestion)
  • Hepatojugular reflux
  • Dependent edema (sacral if bedridden)

Most Common Cause

Left-sided HF (most common!), Pulmonary HTN, COPD, PE

Memory Aid: Left vs Right Symptoms

Left = Lung symptoms (dyspnea, crackles, orthopnea)

Right = Rest of body symptoms (edema, JVD, hepatomegaly)

Quick Assessment Reference

Orthopnea Assessment
How many pillows to sleep?
JVD Assessment
Elevate HOB 45°, measure distension
Edema Assessment
Check ankles, sacrum; grade 1+ to 4+
Daily Weight
Same time, same scale, same clothes

Systolic vs Diastolic Dysfunction

Heart failure can also be classified by the type of ventricular dysfunction. This classification is based on ejection fraction (EF) and guides treatment decisions.

Systolic Dysfunction (HFrEF)

Heart Failure with Reduced Ejection Fraction

  • EF < 40% (reduced)
  • Problem: Heart can't CONTRACT effectively
  • The ventricle is weak and dilated
  • Poor "pump" function
  • Decreased cardiac output

Common Causes

  • Post-MI (damaged muscle)
  • Dilated cardiomyopathy
  • Chronic volume overload

Key Treatments

ACE-I/ARB, Beta-blockers, Aldosterone antagonists, Diuretics

Diastolic Dysfunction (HFpEF)

Heart Failure with Preserved Ejection Fraction

  • EF ≥ 50% (normal/preserved)
  • Problem: Heart can't RELAX effectively
  • The ventricle is stiff and hypertrophied
  • Impaired "filling" function
  • Decreased stroke volume despite normal EF

Common Causes

  • Chronic hypertension
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy
  • Aortic stenosis

Key Treatments

Blood pressure control, Diuretics for symptoms, Rate control

Clinical Pearl: Understanding EF

A patient with HFpEF (preserved EF) still has heart failure symptoms! Don't be fooled by a "normal" EF - their heart fills poorly during diastole, so even though the percentage ejected is normal, the actual volume pumped is reduced.

Feature Systolic (HFrEF) Diastolic (HFpEF)
Ejection Fraction < 40% (reduced) ≥ 50% (preserved)
Ventricular Problem Weak contraction Impaired relaxation
Ventricular Appearance Dilated, thin walls Stiff, thick walls
Primary Issue Pumping failure Filling failure
Typical Patient Post-MI, younger Elderly, HTN, female
BNP Levels Very elevated Mildly-moderately elevated

Nursing Assessment

Thorough assessment is critical for detecting worsening heart failure and evaluating treatment effectiveness.

Comprehensive HF Assessment

Key Assessment Components

1
Respiratory Assessment

Auscultate lungs for crackles/rales (indicates pulmonary congestion). Assess respiratory rate, effort, and oxygen saturation. Note presence of cough or pink frothy sputum.

2
Cardiovascular Assessment

Auscultate heart sounds (S3 gallop indicates volume overload). Assess heart rate, rhythm, and blood pressure. Check for JVD at 45° elevation.

3
Fluid Status Assessment

Daily weights (same time, scale, clothing). Assess for peripheral edema (grade 1+ to 4+). Monitor I&O strictly. Check for sacral edema in bedridden patients.

4
Perfusion Assessment

Assess mental status and level of consciousness. Check skin color, temperature, capillary refill. Monitor urine output (decreased indicates poor perfusion).

5
Functional Status

Assess activity tolerance and fatigue level. Note dyspnea with activities. Evaluate ability to perform ADLs.

Critical Alert: Weight Gain

Report weight gain of 2+ lbs (1 kg) in 24 hours or 5+ lbs (2.3 kg) in 1 week!

Rapid weight gain indicates fluid retention and worsening heart failure. This may require medication adjustment or hospitalization.

Key Diagnostic Indicators

Test Purpose HF Indicators
BNP / NT-proBNP Biomarker for ventricular stretch BNP > 100 pg/mL suggests HF
Echocardiogram Assess structure and EF Reduced EF, wall motion abnormalities
Chest X-Ray Detect pulmonary congestion Cardiomegaly, pulmonary edema
EKG Rhythm and ischemia LVH, arrhythmias, prior MI
Basic Metabolic Panel Renal function, electrolytes Elevated creatinine, low Na+ (dilutional)

Priority Nursing Interventions

Nursing interventions for heart failure focus on reducing cardiac workload, managing fluid balance, and optimizing oxygenation.

NCLEX Priority Framework

For acute HF exacerbation, remember: LMNOP

  • Lasix (diuretics)
  • Morphine (reduces preload and anxiety)
  • Nitrates (reduce preload)
  • Oxygen
  • Position (high Fowler's)

Priority Interventions for Fluid Overload

Managing Fluid Volume Excess

1
Position for Optimal Breathing

Elevate HOB 30-45° (high Fowler's) to reduce venous return and improve lung expansion. Dangle legs over bedside to decrease preload if not hypotensive.

2
Administer Oxygen as Prescribed

Apply supplemental O2 to maintain SpO2 > 94%. Monitor for changes in respiratory status. May require BiPAP or intubation in severe cases.

3
Administer Diuretics as Ordered

IV loop diuretics (furosemide) are first-line for acute decompensation. Monitor urine output (expect increased output within 30 min of IV dose). Watch for hypokalemia!

4
Strict I&O and Daily Weights

Maintain accurate intake and output records. Daily weights are the most sensitive indicator of fluid status. Goal: net negative fluid balance during diuresis.

5
Sodium and Fluid Restriction

Limit sodium to 2-3 g/day. Fluid restriction (1.5-2 L/day) may be ordered for severe HF. Educate patient on reading food labels.

6
Monitor Electrolytes

Diuretics can cause hypokalemia, hyponatremia, hypomagnesemia. Monitor labs and replace as needed. Potassium-sparing diuretics may be added.

Reducing Cardiac Workload

  • Activity restriction: Bed rest during acute phase, then gradual activity progression
  • Cluster nursing care: Provide rest periods between activities
  • Reduce anxiety: Calm environment, explanations, anxiolytics if needed
  • Maintain adequate oxygenation: Reduces cardiac strain
  • Prevent complications: DVT prophylaxis, skin care, prevent constipation (avoid Valsalva)
Clinical Pearl: The Valsalva Maneuver

Straining during bowel movements (Valsalva) can cause dangerous drops in cardiac output. Administer stool softeners prophylactically to all HF patients!

Pharmacological Management

Medication therapy for heart failure aims to reduce symptoms, slow disease progression, and improve survival. Understanding drug classes and nursing considerations is essential.

Drug Class Examples Mechanism Nursing Considerations
ACE Inhibitors Lisinopril, Enalapril Block RAAS, reduce preload/afterload Monitor for cough, hyperkalemia, angioedema. Hold if K+ > 5.5
ARBs Losartan, Valsartan Block angiotensin II receptors Alternative if ACE-I cough. Same K+ monitoring.
Beta-Blockers Carvedilol, Metoprolol succinate Reduce HR, protect myocardium Start low, go slow. Monitor HR, BP. Don't stop abruptly!
Loop Diuretics Furosemide (Lasix), Bumetanide Remove excess fluid Monitor K+, give AM dose, watch for ototoxicity
Aldosterone Antagonists Spironolactone, Eplerenone Block aldosterone, spare K+ Risk of hyperkalemia. Spironolactone may cause gynecomastia.
Digoxin Digoxin (Lanoxin) Increases contractility, slows HR Narrow therapeutic range (0.5-2.0). Monitor for toxicity!
ARNI Sacubitril/Valsartan (Entresto) Enhances natriuretic peptides + ARB Do NOT give with ACE-I (36hr washout). Superior to ACE-I alone.
Digoxin Toxicity Signs

Know the classic signs of digoxin toxicity:

  • GI: Nausea, vomiting, anorexia
  • Visual: Yellow-green halos, blurred vision
  • Cardiac: Bradycardia, heart blocks, dysrhythmias
  • CNS: Fatigue, confusion, weakness

Hold digoxin if HR < 60 bpm and notify provider!

Patient Education Points
  • Weigh yourself daily and report gains > 2 lbs/day or 5 lbs/week
  • Follow sodium restriction (read labels!)
  • Take medications as prescribed - don't skip doses
  • Report worsening dyspnea, increased edema, or chest pain
  • Avoid NSAIDs (cause fluid retention and reduce ACE-I effectiveness)
  • Get annual flu vaccine and pneumonia vaccine

Key Takeaways

Left-sided HF = Pulmonary symptoms (dyspnea, crackles, orthopnea)

Right-sided HF = Systemic symptoms (edema, JVD, hepatomegaly)

HFrEF = Weak pump, EF < 40% (systolic dysfunction)

HFpEF = Stiff heart, EF ≥ 50% (diastolic dysfunction)

Daily weights are the most sensitive indicator of fluid status

ACE-I + Beta-blocker = Cornerstone therapy for HFrEF
