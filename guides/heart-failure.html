Cardiac Anatomy & Physiology Review

Before understanding heart failure, it's essential to review normal cardiac function. The heart is a four-chambered muscular pump responsible for circulating blood throughout the body.

The Heart's Chambers and Blood Flow

Clinical Pearl: Blood Flow Pathway

Remember the pathway: Body ‚Üí Right Atrium ‚Üí Right Ventricle ‚Üí Lungs ‚Üí Left Atrium ‚Üí Left Ventricle ‚Üí Body

  • Right Side of Heart: Receives deoxygenated blood from the body (via superior/inferior vena cava) and pumps it to the lungs for oxygenation
  • Left Side of Heart: Receives oxygenated blood from the lungs (via pulmonary veins) and pumps it to the systemic circulation
  • Left Ventricle: The primary "workhorse" - has the thickest muscular wall because it must generate enough pressure to pump blood to the entire body

Key Cardiac Function Concepts

Term Definition Normal Value
Cardiac Output (CO) Amount of blood pumped by the heart per minute (HR x SV) 4-8 L/min
Stroke Volume (SV) Amount of blood ejected with each heartbeat 60-100 mL/beat
Ejection Fraction (EF) Percentage of blood pumped out with each contraction 55-70%
Preload Volume of blood in ventricles at end of diastole (stretch) Assessed via CVP/PAWP
Afterload Resistance the heart must overcome to eject blood Assessed via SVR
Contractility Force of myocardial contraction (independent of preload) Intrinsic property
Memory Aid: Determinants of Cardiac Output

CO = HR x SV

Stroke Volume is determined by: P-A-C

  • Preload (venous return)
  • Afterload (resistance)
  • Contractility (pump strength)
üéØ Bottom Line: Cardiac output = HR √ó SV. The left ventricle is the workhorse ‚Äî when it fails, blood backs up into the lungs (left) or the body (right).

Pathophysiology of Heart Failure

Heart failure (HF) occurs when the heart cannot pump enough blood to meet the body's metabolic demands. It is NOT a disease itself, but a clinical syndrome resulting from structural or functional cardiac problems.

Definition

Heart failure is the inability of the heart to pump sufficient blood to meet the body's oxygen and nutrient needs, leading to inadequate tissue perfusion and congestion.

Common Causes of Heart Failure

Category Examples Mechanism
Coronary Artery Disease MI, Ischemia Myocardial damage reduces contractility
Hypertension Chronic HTN Increased afterload causes LV hypertrophy
Valvular Disease Stenosis, Regurgitation Abnormal flow patterns increase workload
Cardiomyopathy Dilated, Hypertrophic Structural changes impair function
Arrhythmias A-fib, Tachycardia Inefficient filling or ejection

Compensatory Mechanisms

When cardiac output drops, the body activates several compensatory mechanisms. Initially helpful, these become harmful over time:

Body's Compensatory Responses

1
Sympathetic Nervous System Activation

Increases heart rate and contractility. Problem: Increases myocardial oxygen demand and causes vasoconstriction (increased afterload).

2
RAAS Activation (Renin-Angiotensin-Aldosterone)

Retains sodium and water to increase preload. Problem: Leads to fluid overload, edema, and increased cardiac workload.

3
Ventricular Remodeling

Heart muscle hypertrophies (thickens) or dilates. Problem: Eventually leads to decreased efficiency and worsening failure.

4
Natriuretic Peptide Release (BNP)

Promotes vasodilation and diuresis. Benefit: Counteracts fluid retention. Used as diagnostic marker (elevated BNP indicates HF).

Critical Understanding

Compensatory mechanisms maintain cardiac output short-term but cause long-term damage. This is why we use ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, and diuretics - to block these harmful compensatory responses!

üéØ Bottom Line: Heart failure is a syndrome, not a disease. Compensatory mechanisms (SNS, RAAS, remodeling) help short-term but cause long-term damage ‚Äî that's why we use ACE inhibitors, beta-blockers, and diuretics.

Left-Sided vs Right-Sided Heart Failure

The symptoms of heart failure depend on which side of the heart is failing. Understanding this distinction is crucial for assessment.

Key Concept

Think: "Where does the blood back up?"

  • Left heart fails ‚Üí Blood backs up into the LUNGS (pulmonary congestion)
  • Right heart fails ‚Üí Blood backs up into the BODY (systemic congestion)
Left-Sided Heart Failure

Primary Symptoms (Pulmonary)

  • Dyspnea (shortness of breath)
  • Orthopnea (difficulty breathing lying flat)
  • Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnea (PND)
  • Crackles/Rales on lung auscultation
  • Cough (may be pink, frothy sputum)
  • S3 heart sound (ventricular gallop)
  • Fatigue and decreased exercise tolerance
  • Pulmonary edema (severe cases)

Most Common Cause

Hypertension, CAD, MI, Aortic/Mitral valve disease

Right-Sided Heart Failure

Primary Symptoms (Systemic)

  • Peripheral edema (feet, ankles, legs)
  • Jugular Venous Distension (JVD)
  • Hepatomegaly (enlarged liver)
  • Ascites (abdominal fluid)
  • Weight gain (fluid retention)
  • Nausea/Anorexia (GI congestion)
  • Hepatojugular reflux
  • Dependent edema (sacral if bedridden)

Most Common Cause

Left-sided HF (most common!), Pulmonary HTN, COPD, PE

Memory Aid: Left vs Right Symptoms

Left = Lung symptoms (dyspnea, crackles, orthopnea)

Right = Rest of body symptoms (edema, JVD, hepatomegaly)

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Left = Lungs
Remember which symptoms go with which side! When the left side of the heart fails, blood backs up into the lungs, causing pulmonary symptoms like dyspnea (shortness of breath), crackles, and orthopnea (needing to sit up to breathe). When the right side fails, blood backs up into the body, causing systemic symptoms like peripheral edema, jugular vein distension (JVD), and an enlarged liver.

Systolic vs Diastolic Dysfunction

Heart failure can also be classified by the type of ventricular dysfunction. This classification is based on ejection fraction (EF) and guides treatment decisions.

Systolic Dysfunction (HFrEF)

Heart Failure with Reduced Ejection Fraction

  • EF < 40% (reduced)
  • Problem: Heart can't CONTRACT effectively
  • The ventricle is weak and dilated
  • Poor "pump" function
  • Decreased cardiac output

Common Causes

  • Post-MI (damaged muscle)
  • Dilated cardiomyopathy
  • Chronic volume overload

Key Treatments

ACE-I/ARB, Beta-blockers, Aldosterone antagonists, Diuretics

Diastolic Dysfunction (HFpEF)

Heart Failure with Preserved Ejection Fraction

  • EF ‚â• 50% (normal/preserved)
  • Problem: Heart can't RELAX effectively
  • The ventricle is stiff and hypertrophied
  • Impaired "filling" function
  • Decreased stroke volume despite normal EF

Common Causes

  • Chronic hypertension
  • Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy
  • Aortic stenosis

Key Treatments

Blood pressure control, Diuretics for symptoms, Rate control

Clinical Pearl: Understanding EF

A patient with HFpEF (preserved EF) still has heart failure symptoms! Don't be fooled by a "normal" EF - their heart fills poorly during diastole, so even though the percentage ejected is normal, the actual volume pumped is reduced.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Normal EF Trap
Don't be fooled by a "normal" ejection fraction! Patients with HFpEF (Heart Failure with preserved Ejection Fraction) have an EF of 50% or higher, which sounds normal. But their heart is stiff and doesn't relax properly during diastole, so it can't fill with enough blood. Even though they eject a normal percentage, the actual volume of blood pumped is reduced. These patients still have heart failure symptoms and need treatment. This is especially common in elderly patients with chronic hypertension.
Feature Systolic (HFrEF) Diastolic (HFpEF)
Ejection Fraction < 40% (reduced) ‚â• 50% (preserved)
Ventricular Problem Weak contraction Impaired relaxation
Ventricular Appearance Dilated, thin walls Stiff, thick walls
Primary Issue Pumping failure Filling failure
Typical Patient Post-MI, younger Elderly, HTN, female
BNP Levels Very elevated Mildly-moderately elevated
üéØ Bottom Line: HFrEF (EF < 40%) = weak pump that can't contract. HFpEF (EF ‚â• 50%) = stiff heart that can't relax. Don't be fooled by a "normal" EF ‚Äî the patient can still have heart failure.

Nursing Assessment

Thorough assessment is critical for detecting worsening heart failure and evaluating treatment effectiveness.

Comprehensive HF Assessment

Key Assessment Components

1
Respiratory Assessment

Auscultate lungs for crackles/rales (indicates pulmonary congestion). Assess respiratory rate, effort, and oxygen saturation. Note presence of cough or pink frothy sputum.

2
Cardiovascular Assessment

Auscultate heart sounds (S3 gallop indicates volume overload). Assess heart rate, rhythm, and blood pressure. Check for JVD at 45¬∞ elevation.

3
Fluid Status Assessment

Daily weights (same time, scale, clothing). Assess for peripheral edema (grade 1+ to 4+). Monitor I&O strictly. Check for sacral edema in bedridden patients.

4
Perfusion Assessment

Assess mental status and level of consciousness. Check skin color, temperature, capillary refill. Monitor urine output (decreased indicates poor perfusion).

5
Functional Status

Assess activity tolerance and fatigue level. Note dyspnea with activities. Evaluate ability to perform ADLs.

Critical Alert: Weight Gain

Report weight gain of 2+ lbs (1 kg) in 24 hours or 5+ lbs (2.3 kg) in 1 week!

Rapid weight gain indicates fluid retention and worsening heart failure. This may require medication adjustment or hospitalization.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Daily Weights
Daily weight is the most sensitive indicator of fluid status! Teach patients to weigh themselves at the same time each morning (after voiding, before eating), using the same scale, wearing similar clothing. A gain of 2+ pounds in one day or 5+ pounds in one week signals fluid retention and should be reported immediately. This is more reliable than waiting for visible edema to appear.

Key Diagnostic Indicators

Test Purpose HF Indicators
BNP / NT-proBNP Biomarker for ventricular stretch BNP > 100 pg/mL suggests HF
Echocardiogram Assess structure and EF Reduced EF, wall motion abnormalities
Chest X-Ray Detect pulmonary congestion Cardiomegaly, pulmonary edema
EKG Rhythm and ischemia LVH, arrhythmias, prior MI
Basic Metabolic Panel Renal function, electrolytes Elevated creatinine, low Na+ (dilutional)
üéØ Bottom Line: Daily weights are the most sensitive indicator of fluid status. Report weight gain > 2 lbs/day or 5 lbs/week. BNP > 100 pg/mL supports HF diagnosis.

Priority Nursing Interventions

Nursing interventions for heart failure focus on reducing cardiac workload, managing fluid balance, and optimizing oxygenation.

Priority Framework

For acute HF exacerbation, remember: LMNOP

  • Lasix (diuretics)
  • Morphine (reduces preload and anxiety)
  • Nitrates (reduce preload)
  • Oxygen
  • Position (high Fowler's)
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
LMNOP for Acute HF
Use LMNOP to remember priority interventions for acute heart failure decompensation! Lasix (furosemide) removes excess fluid; Morphine reduces preload and relieves anxiety; Nitrates (like nitroglycerin) dilate veins to reduce preload; Oxygen supports tissue oxygenation; Position (high Fowler's at 45-90¬∞) decreases venous return and improves breathing. These interventions work together to reduce the heart's workload quickly.

Priority Interventions for Fluid Overload

Managing Fluid Volume Excess

1
Position for Optimal Breathing

Elevate HOB 30-45¬∞ (high Fowler's) to reduce venous return and improve lung expansion. Dangle legs over bedside to decrease preload if not hypotensive.

2
Administer Oxygen as Prescribed

Apply supplemental O2 to maintain SpO2 > 94%. Monitor for changes in respiratory status. May require BiPAP or intubation in severe cases.

3
Administer Diuretics as Ordered

IV loop diuretics (furosemide) are first-line for acute decompensation. Monitor urine output (expect increased output within 30 min of IV dose). Watch for hypokalemia!

4
Strict I&O and Daily Weights

Maintain accurate intake and output records. Daily weights are the most sensitive indicator of fluid status. Goal: net negative fluid balance during diuresis.

5
Sodium and Fluid Restriction

Limit sodium to 2-3 g/day. Fluid restriction (1.5-2 L/day) may be ordered for severe HF. Educate patient on reading food labels.

6
Monitor Electrolytes

Diuretics can cause hypokalemia, hyponatremia, hypomagnesemia. Monitor labs and replace as needed. Potassium-sparing diuretics may be added.

Reducing Cardiac Workload

  • Activity restriction: Bed rest during acute phase, then gradual activity progression
  • Cluster nursing care: Provide rest periods between activities
  • Reduce anxiety: Calm environment, explanations, anxiolytics if needed
  • Maintain adequate oxygenation: Reduces cardiac strain
  • Prevent complications: DVT prophylaxis, skin care, prevent constipation (avoid Valsalva)
Clinical Pearl: The Valsalva Maneuver

Straining during bowel movements (Valsalva) can cause dangerous drops in cardiac output. Administer stool softeners prophylactically to all HF patients!

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Prevent Valsalva
Straining during bowel movements is dangerous for heart failure patients! The Valsalva maneuver (bearing down) causes a sudden drop in cardiac output and can trigger arrhythmias. Always give stool softeners prophylactically (like docusate sodium) to all HF patients to prevent constipation. Encourage adequate fluid intake (within restrictions), fiber, and activity as tolerated. This simple intervention can prevent serious cardiac events.
üéØ Bottom Line: For acute decompensation, think LMNOP: Lasix, Morphine, Nitrates, Oxygen, Position. Give stool softeners to ALL HF patients to prevent Valsalva.

Pharmacological Management

Medication therapy for heart failure aims to reduce symptoms, slow disease progression, and improve survival. Understanding drug classes and nursing considerations is essential.

Drug Class Examples Mechanism Nursing Considerations
ACE Inhibitors Lisinopril, Enalapril Block RAAS, reduce preload/afterload Monitor for cough, hyperkalemia, angioedema. Hold if K+ > 5.5
ARBs Losartan, Valsartan Block angiotensin II receptors Alternative if ACE-I cough. Same K+ monitoring.
Beta-Blockers Carvedilol, Metoprolol succinate Reduce HR, protect myocardium Start low, go slow. Monitor HR, BP. Don't stop abruptly!
Loop Diuretics Furosemide (Lasix), Bumetanide Remove excess fluid Monitor K+, give AM dose, watch for ototoxicity
Aldosterone Antagonists Spironolactone, Eplerenone Block aldosterone, spare K+ Risk of hyperkalemia. Spironolactone may cause gynecomastia.
Digoxin Digoxin (Lanoxin) Increases contractility, slows HR Narrow therapeutic range (0.5-2.0). Monitor for toxicity!
ARNI Sacubitril/Valsartan (Entresto) Enhances natriuretic peptides + ARB Do NOT give with ACE-I (36hr washout). Superior to ACE-I alone.
Digoxin Toxicity Signs

Know the classic signs of digoxin toxicity:

  • GI: Nausea, vomiting, anorexia
  • Visual: Yellow-green halos, blurred vision
  • Cardiac: Bradycardia, heart blocks, dysrhythmias
  • CNS: Fatigue, confusion, weakness

Hold digoxin if HR < 60 bpm and notify provider!

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Watch Potassium
Heart failure medications have opposite effects on potassium! Loop diuretics (like furosemide/Lasix) cause hypokalemia (low potassium) because they increase potassium excretion in urine. ACE inhibitors (like lisinopril) and ARBs cause hyperkalemia (high potassium) by reducing aldosterone. Since HF patients often take both types, monitor potassium levels closely. Hold ACE inhibitors if K+ is greater than 5.5 mEq/L and notify the provider. Low potassium increases the risk of digoxin toxicity.
Patient Education Points
  • Weigh yourself daily and report gains > 2 lbs/day or 5 lbs/week
  • Follow sodium restriction (read labels!)
  • Take medications as prescribed - don't skip doses
  • Report worsening dyspnea, increased edema, or chest pain
  • Avoid NSAIDs (cause fluid retention and reduce ACE-I effectiveness)
  • Get annual flu vaccine and pneumonia vaccine
üéØ Bottom Line: ACE-I + beta-blocker = cornerstone therapy. Loop diuretics cause low K‚Å∫, ACE-I/ARBs cause high K‚Å∫ ‚Äî monitor potassium closely when patients take both. Never combine Entresto with an ACE-I.

1 Single Best Answer Application

A nurse is caring for a patient admitted with heart failure. The patient reports increasing shortness of breath when lying flat and needing three pillows to sleep. Lung auscultation reveals bilateral crackles. Jugular veins are flat, and there is no peripheral edema. Which type of heart failure is this patient most likely experiencing?

  1. A. Right-sided heart failure
  2. B. Left-sided heart failure
  3. C. Biventricular heart failure
  4. D. Cor pulmonale

Correct Answer: B

Why B is correct:

Left-sided heart failure causes blood to back up into the pulmonary system. Orthopnea (needing pillows to sleep upright), dyspnea, and bilateral crackles are hallmark pulmonary congestion signs. The absence of JVD and peripheral edema rules out significant right-sided involvement.

Why the others are wrong:

A. Right-sided HF presents with systemic congestion ‚Äî JVD, peripheral edema, hepatomegaly ‚Äî which this patient lacks.

C. Biventricular failure would include both pulmonary AND systemic signs. This patient has no right-sided symptoms.

D. Cor pulmonale is right-sided HF caused by pulmonary disease. It would present with JVD and edema, not primarily pulmonary symptoms.

Test-Taking Tip

Remember: Left = Lung symptoms. Right = Rest of the body. Match the symptoms to the side.

2 Priority Analysis

A patient with chronic heart failure calls the nurse hotline and reports a weight gain of 3 pounds since yesterday. The patient denies chest pain or severe shortness of breath but notes mild ankle swelling. What should the nurse instruct the patient to do FIRST?

  1. A. Go to the emergency department immediately
  2. B. Restrict fluid intake to 1 liter for the rest of the day
  3. C. Contact the health care provider to report the weight gain
  4. D. Elevate the legs and reweigh in the morning

Correct Answer: C

Why C is correct:

A weight gain of more than 2 lbs (1 kg) in 24 hours indicates fluid retention and worsening heart failure. The provider needs to be notified so diuretic dosing or other management can be adjusted promptly. This is within the "report immediately" threshold.

Why the others are wrong:

A. The patient has no acute distress (no chest pain, no severe dyspnea). The ED is not indicated for gradual fluid retention without emergency symptoms.

B. While fluid restriction is part of HF management, the nurse should not independently change the fluid restriction without a provider order. The weight gain threshold has been met and requires provider notification.

D. Waiting until morning delays intervention. A 3-lb gain in 24 hours exceeds the 2-lb threshold and requires same-day provider communication.

Test-Taking Tip

Know the weight thresholds: gain of >2 lbs/day OR >5 lbs/week = notify provider. When the NCLEX asks what to do "first," look for the answer that addresses the immediate clinical concern without overreacting or underreacting.

3 Select All That Apply Application

A patient is admitted with acute decompensated heart failure. The nurse anticipates which of the following interventions? Select all that apply.

  1. A. Place the patient in high Fowler's position
  2. B. Administer IV furosemide (Lasix) as ordered
  3. C. Encourage high-sodium foods to increase blood volume
  4. D. Administer supplemental oxygen to maintain SpO2 > 94%
  5. E. Administer nitroglycerin as ordered to reduce preload
  6. F. Encourage ambulation to improve circulation

Correct Answers: A, B, D, E

Why each answer is correct or incorrect:

A. CORRECT ‚Äî High Fowler's position reduces venous return (preload) and improves lung expansion. This is the "P" in the LMNOP mnemonic (Position).

B. CORRECT ‚Äî IV loop diuretics are the first-line treatment for acute fluid overload. IV furosemide removes excess fluid rapidly.

C. INCORRECT ‚Äî Sodium restriction (2-3 g/day) is essential in HF. High-sodium foods worsen fluid retention and congestion.

D. CORRECT ‚Äî Oxygen therapy addresses hypoxia from pulmonary congestion. The "O" in LMNOP.

E. CORRECT ‚Äî Nitroglycerin reduces preload through venodilation, decreasing pulmonary congestion. The "N" in LMNOP.

F. INCORRECT ‚Äî During acute decompensation, activity increases myocardial oxygen demand. Bed rest with clustered care is appropriate during the acute phase.

Test-Taking Tip

For acute decompensated HF, remember LMNOP: Lasix, Morphine, Nitrates, Oxygen, Position. Every correct answer in this question maps to a letter in the mnemonic.

4 Single Best Answer Knowledge

A patient with heart failure has an echocardiogram showing an ejection fraction of 30%. The patient is experiencing fatigue and dyspnea on exertion. Which classification of heart failure does this patient have?

  1. A. Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF)
  2. B. Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF)
  3. C. Heart failure with mildly reduced ejection fraction (HFmrEF)
  4. D. Compensated heart failure

Correct Answer: B

Why B is correct:

HFrEF (systolic dysfunction) is defined by an EF < 40%. An EF of 30% means the ventricle cannot contract effectively ‚Äî it is weak and dilated. This is the "reduced" category.

Why the others are wrong:

A. HFpEF has an EF ‚â• 50%. The heart contracts normally but cannot relax and fill properly (diastolic dysfunction).

C. HFmrEF (midrange) has an EF of 41-49%. This patient's EF of 30% is below that range.

D. "Compensated heart failure" describes a functional status (symptoms controlled), not an EF classification. This patient has active symptoms.

Test-Taking Tip

Know the EF cutoffs: <40% = HFrEF (reduced), 41-49% = HFmrEF (midrange), ‚â•50% = HFpEF (preserved). Don't be tricked by a "normal" EF ‚Äî HFpEF patients still have heart failure!

5 Single Best Answer Analysis

A patient with HFrEF is taking furosemide 40 mg daily and lisinopril 10 mg daily. Today's lab results show: Potassium 3.2 mEq/L, BUN 28 mg/dL, Creatinine 1.4 mg/dL. Digoxin level is 1.8 ng/mL. Which lab value is the nurse MOST concerned about?

  1. A. BUN of 28 mg/dL
  2. B. Creatinine of 1.4 mg/dL
  3. C. Potassium of 3.2 mEq/L
  4. D. Digoxin level of 1.8 ng/mL

Correct Answer: C

Why C is correct:

A potassium of 3.2 mEq/L is critically concerning because this patient is also taking digoxin. Hypokalemia significantly increases the risk of digoxin toxicity, which can cause life-threatening arrhythmias. The furosemide (loop diuretic) is causing potassium loss, and the low K+ makes digoxin bind more effectively to cardiac cells, amplifying its toxic effects.

Why the others are wrong:

A. BUN of 28 is slightly elevated but not immediately dangerous. It may reflect mild dehydration from diuretic therapy.

B. Creatinine of 1.4 is mildly elevated and worth monitoring, but not the most urgent concern given the digoxin-potassium interaction.

D. Digoxin of 1.8 ng/mL is within the therapeutic range (0.5-2.0 ng/mL), but combined with hypokalemia, even a therapeutic level can produce toxicity.

Test-Taking Tip

When a patient takes digoxin, ALWAYS look at the potassium level first. Low potassium + digoxin = toxicity risk, even when the digoxin level appears "normal." This is a classic NCLEX trap.

6 Select All That Apply Application

A nurse is providing discharge teaching to a patient with chronic heart failure. Which statements by the patient indicate correct understanding? Select all that apply.

  1. A. "I will weigh myself every morning before breakfast using the same scale."
  2. B. "I should call my doctor if I gain more than 5 pounds in one day."
  3. C. "I need to avoid taking ibuprofen for my arthritis pain."
  4. D. "I can use salt substitutes freely since I need to limit sodium."
  5. E. "I should take my furosemide in the morning to avoid nighttime urination."

Correct Answers: A, C, E

Why each answer is correct or incorrect:

A. CORRECT ‚Äî Daily weights at the same time, on the same scale, in similar clothing is the most sensitive indicator of fluid retention. Morning before breakfast is the standard timing.

B. INCORRECT ‚Äî The threshold is 2 lbs in one day or 5 lbs in one week, not 5 lbs in one day. A 5-lb gain in a single day would be a medical emergency.

C. CORRECT ‚Äî NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) cause sodium and water retention AND reduce the effectiveness of ACE inhibitors and diuretics. HF patients should avoid them.

D. INCORRECT ‚Äî Many salt substitutes contain potassium chloride. Patients taking ACE inhibitors or ARBs are already at risk for hyperkalemia, so using potassium-based salt substitutes could push K+ to dangerous levels.

E. CORRECT ‚Äî Taking diuretics in the morning prevents nocturia and promotes daytime diuresis when the patient is active and near the bathroom.

Test-Taking Tip

For SATA questions, evaluate each option independently as true or false. Don't let one "obviously correct" answer make you rush through the others. The salt substitute trap (potassium + ACE inhibitor) is a high-yield NCLEX concept.

7 Priority Analysis

A nurse is caring for a patient with heart failure who is receiving IV furosemide. The patient suddenly becomes confused, reports muscle cramps, and the cardiac monitor shows a new irregular rhythm with prominent U waves. What should the nurse do FIRST?

  1. A. Administer a second dose of furosemide
  2. B. Obtain a stat potassium level
  3. C. Perform a 12-lead ECG
  4. D. Notify the rapid response team

Correct Answer: B

Why B is correct:

The clinical picture ‚Äî confusion, muscle cramps, new irregular rhythm, and U waves on the monitor ‚Äî is classic hypokalemia. The IV furosemide is causing excessive potassium loss. Obtaining a stat potassium level confirms the suspected electrolyte imbalance and guides the urgency of replacement. This is a "collect data" before "intervene" situation.

Why the others are wrong:

A. Giving more furosemide would worsen potassium depletion. This is the opposite of what's needed.

C. A 12-lead ECG would provide more information about the arrhythmia but does not address the underlying electrolyte problem. The telemetry already shows the rhythm change.

D. The rapid response team may be needed, but the nurse should first collect the critical data (potassium level) that will guide the team's interventions. The patient is not in immediate cardiac arrest.

Test-Taking Tip

U waves = think hyoUkalemia. When the NCLEX presents symptoms + a likely cause, the first step is usually to confirm with a diagnostic test before escalating care.

8 Single Best Answer Knowledge

A patient with HFrEF is being started on carvedilol (Coreg). The nurse knows that the provider prescribed a very low initial dose with plans to increase gradually over several weeks. Which statement BEST explains this approach?

  1. A. Beta-blockers can initially worsen heart failure symptoms before providing long-term benefit
  2. B. The patient must first demonstrate tolerance to ACE inhibitors before starting a beta-blocker
  3. C. High doses of beta-blockers cause irreversible liver damage in heart failure patients
  4. D. Carvedilol must be titrated to match the patient's digoxin level

Correct Answer: A

Why A is correct:

Beta-blockers reduce heart rate and contractility, which can initially worsen HF symptoms (increased fatigue, fluid retention, hypotension). However, long-term use improves survival by reducing myocardial remodeling and oxygen demand. The "start low, go slow" principle is critical ‚Äî doses are increased every 2 weeks as tolerated.

Why the others are wrong:

B. While ACE inhibitors are often started first, the slow beta-blocker titration is due to the drug's own hemodynamic effects, not a prerequisite relationship with ACE inhibitors.

C. Beta-blockers do not cause irreversible liver damage. Hepatotoxicity is rare and not the reason for slow titration.

D. Carvedilol dosing is independent of digoxin levels. They are separate medications with different titration guidelines.

Test-Taking Tip

"Start low, go slow" for beta-blockers in HF. They improve long-term survival but can transiently worsen symptoms. Also remember: never stop beta-blockers abruptly ‚Äî taper gradually to avoid rebound tachycardia.
