Joint Anatomy & Physiology

Understanding the structures replaced in arthroplasty

Total joint replacement (arthroplasty) involves removing damaged joint surfaces and replacing them with prosthetic components. Understanding normal anatomy helps nurses anticipate complications and provide appropriate care.

Hip Joint Anatomy

Acetabulum
Socket of the hip joint
  • Cup-shaped cavity in the pelvis
  • Lined with articular cartilage
  • Replaced with metal/ceramic cup
  • Polyethylene liner provides cushioning
Femoral Head
Ball of the hip joint
  • Round head of the femur bone
  • Articulates with acetabulum
  • Replaced with metal or ceramic ball
  • Attached to femoral stem component
Femoral Neck
Connection point - common fracture site
  • Connects femoral head to shaft
  • Common site for hip fractures
  • Contains blood supply to femoral head
  • Removed during total hip replacement
Joint Capsule
Stabilizing structures
  • Fibrous capsule surrounds joint
  • Ligaments provide stability
  • Cut during surgery - must heal
  • Why hip precautions are critical

Knee Joint Anatomy

Femoral Condyles
End of the thigh bone
  • Two rounded surfaces at femur end
  • Articulate with tibia
  • Replaced with metal femoral component
  • Smooth surface allows gliding motion
Tibial Plateau
Top of the shin bone
  • Flat surface at top of tibia
  • Covered with articular cartilage
  • Replaced with metal tibial tray
  • Polyethylene insert sits on top
Patella (Kneecap)
Sesamoid bone
  • Protects anterior knee joint
  • Glides in trochlear groove
  • May or may not be resurfaced
  • Patellar resurfacing = plastic button
Ligaments
Stability structures
  • ACL/PCL: Cruciate ligaments
  • MCL/LCL: Collateral ligaments
  • PCL often sacrificed in TKR
  • Collaterals usually preserved
Prosthetic Components

Joint replacements use combinations of metal alloys (cobalt-chrome, titanium), ceramic, and polyethylene (plastic). Components may be cemented or press-fit (cementless). Cemented prostheses allow immediate weight bearing; cementless require bone ingrowth and may have initial weight-bearing restrictions.

Indications & Types of Arthroplasty

When and why joint replacement is performed

Total joint replacement is considered when conservative treatments fail to relieve pain and restore function. It is one of the most successful orthopedic procedures, with excellent outcomes for most patients.

Indications for Surgery

Primary Indications Description Notes
Osteoarthritis (OA) Degenerative "wear and tear" arthritis Most common indication (>90%)
Rheumatoid Arthritis Autoimmune inflammatory arthritis May affect multiple joints
Avascular Necrosis Bone death from loss of blood supply Common in femoral head
Hip Fracture Femoral neck fracture in elderly Hemiarthroplasty or total hip
Failed Previous Surgery Revision of prior arthroplasty More complex surgery

Types of Procedures

Hip Arthroplasty
  • Total Hip (THA): Replaces both femoral head and acetabulum
  • Hemiarthroplasty: Replaces femoral head only (for fractures)
  • Hip Resurfacing: Caps femoral head instead of removing
  • Approaches: Posterior, anterior, lateral
  • Average lifespan: 15-25 years
Knee Arthroplasty
  • Total Knee (TKA): Replaces all 3 compartments
  • Partial (Unicompartmental): One compartment only
  • Patellofemoral: Just under kneecap
  • Standard midline approach
  • Average lifespan: 15-20 years
NCLEX Alert: Hemiarthroplasty vs Total Hip

Hemiarthroplasty replaces only the femoral head and is used for hip fractures when the acetabulum is healthy. Total hip arthroplasty (THA) replaces both the ball and socket, used for arthritis or when both surfaces are damaged. Both require hip precautions!

Preoperative Care

Preparing patients for successful surgery

Thorough preoperative preparation improves outcomes, reduces complications, and facilitates early recovery. Many facilities use "joint camp" or prehabilitation programs.

Preoperative Assessment

Cardiac Clearance
Cardiovascular assessment
  • ECG for patients over 50
  • Stress test if indicated
  • Optimize heart failure, hypertension
  • Hold certain cardiac medications
Laboratory Tests
Baseline values
  • CBC - baseline hemoglobin
  • BMP - kidney function
  • Coagulation studies (PT/INR, PTT)
  • Type and screen for blood
Medication Management
What to hold/continue
  • STOP: Anticoagulants, NSAIDs, aspirin
  • STOP: Metformin (day of surgery)
  • CONTINUE: Most cardiac meds with sip
  • Follow surgeon/anesthesia instructions
Infection Prevention
Reduce surgical site infection
  • MRSA nasal screening
  • Chlorhexidine (CHG) shower night before
  • Dental clearance (no active infection)
  • Treat any skin infections first

Patient Education Topics

Topic Key Teaching Points
Hip Precautions Explain restrictions before surgery so patient knows what to expect
Pain Management Multimodal approach; realistic expectations (pain is expected but managed)
Mobility Goals Walking same day or POD 1; importance of PT/OT participation
DVT Prevention Blood thinner importance; compression devices; ankle pumps
Discharge Planning Home setup (raised toilet seat, grab bars); who will help at home
Home Preparation Checklist

Arrange for: Raised toilet seat, shower chair/bench, grab bars, reacher/grabber, long-handled shoehorn, sock aid, hip kit for hip patients. Remove throw rugs and tripping hazards. Prepare meals in advance. Arrange for help with pet care, household tasks.

Hip Precautions

Preventing dislocation after hip replacement

Hip precautions are movement restrictions that prevent dislocation while the joint capsule heals. The specific precautions depend on the surgical approach used. Precautions are typically maintained for 6-12 weeks.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Big Three Hip Rules
For posterior approach hips, remember: Don't Bend, Don't Cross, Don't Twist! These three rules prevent the ball from popping out of the socket. (1) Don't bend hip past 90 degrees - no leaning forward in chair, no bending to pick things up. (2) Don't cross legs or ankles - use a pillow between legs when sleeping. (3) Don't twist/rotate inward - keep toes pointed up or slightly outward. Teach patients "nose over toes" - if bending, their nose shouldn't go past their toes!
Posterior Approach Hip Precautions
Most common approach - CRITICAL to prevent dislocation
No Flexion > 90
Don't bend hip more than 90 degrees. No bending forward at waist. Use raised toilet seat, reacher.
No Adduction
Don't cross legs or ankles. Keep pillow between legs when side-lying or sleeping.
No Internal Rotation
Don't turn toes inward. Keep toes pointed up or slightly outward at all times.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Posterior vs Anterior Approach
The surgical approach changes which movements are dangerous! Posterior approach (most common) cuts through the back muscles, so the hip can dislocate backward - that's why you avoid flexion, adduction, and internal rotation. Anterior approach goes through the front, so precautions are opposite - avoid extension, abduction, and external rotation. Always check the operative report or ask the surgeon which approach was used before teaching precautions!

Approach-Specific Precautions

Most Common

Posterior Approach

  • Avoid: Flexion > 90 degrees
  • Avoid: Adduction (crossing midline)
  • Avoid: Internal rotation
  • Incision on back/side of hip
  • Higher dislocation risk (2-3%)
  • Precautions typically 6-12 weeks
Growing Trend

Anterior Approach

  • Avoid: Extension (leg behind body)
  • Avoid: Abduction (leg out to side)
  • Avoid: External rotation
  • Incision on front of hip
  • Lower dislocation risk (~1%)
  • Often fewer/shorter precautions

Positioning & Activities

Activity Safe Technique (Posterior Approach)
Sitting Use firm, elevated chairs. Keep hips higher than knees. Don't lean forward.
Toileting Use raised toilet seat (adds 4-6 inches). Don't lean forward when wiping.
Sleeping Sleep on back or non-operative side with pillow between legs. Avoid side-lying on operative side initially.
Dressing Use reacher, long-handled shoehorn, sock aid. Dress operative leg first, undress it last.
Bathing Use shower chair. Use long-handled sponge for lower body. No tub baths until cleared.
Transfers Pivot on non-operative leg. Keep operative leg extended when transitioning. Don't twist.
Signs of Hip Dislocation

Sudden severe pain in hip/groin, leg appears shortened, leg rotated inward or outward (depending on dislocation direction), unable to bear weight, visible deformity. This is an emergency! Keep patient still, do not attempt to reduce, notify surgeon immediately.

Postoperative Nursing Care

Evidence-based care for optimal recovery

Postoperative care focuses on pain management, preventing complications, and achieving early mobility. Many patients go home within 1-2 days using enhanced recovery protocols.

Immediate Post-Op Assessment (PACU)

Hemodynamic Status
Monitor for blood loss
  • VS every 15 min until stable
  • Watch for hypotension (blood loss)
  • Monitor drain output if present
  • Hemoglobin check POD 1
Neurovascular Checks
Assess operative limb
  • Color, temperature, capillary refill
  • Pulses (dorsalis pedis, posterior tibial)
  • Sensation and movement
  • Compare to non-operative limb
Pain Assessment
Multimodal management
  • Assess pain level (0-10 scale)
  • Nerve block effectiveness
  • Administer scheduled medications
  • Position for comfort
Respiratory
Prevent atelectasis
  • O2 as needed post-anesthesia
  • Incentive spirometry every 1-2 hours
  • Cough and deep breathe
  • Elevate HOB when stable

Pain Management

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Get Them Moving Early
Early mobility is the #1 thing you can do to prevent complications! Most joint replacement patients should be up and walking on POD 0 or POD 1. Pre-medicate for pain 30-45 minutes before PT. Make sure the nerve block hasn't made the leg too weak - if they can't feel or move their foot, wait. Use a gait belt, have the patient dangle first to prevent orthostatic hypotension, and remember: falling is worse than waiting another hour for the block to wear off.
Regional Anesthesia
  • Peripheral nerve blocks (femoral, adductor canal)
  • Spinal anesthesia with intrathecal morphine
  • Local infiltration around joint
  • Provides 12-24+ hours of relief
  • Monitor for motor weakness (fall risk!)
Scheduled Medications
  • Acetaminophen 1g every 6-8 hours
  • NSAIDs (if not contraindicated)
  • Gabapentin/pregabalin for neuropathic pain
  • Opioids for breakthrough only
  • Muscle relaxants PRN
Non-Pharmacologic
  • Ice/cryotherapy (20 min on/off)
  • Elevation to reduce swelling
  • Positioning for comfort
  • Relaxation techniques
  • Distraction (TV, music, visitors)
NCLEX Alert: Multimodal Analgesia

Multimodal analgesia uses multiple medication classes targeting different pain pathways. This approach provides better pain control with fewer opioid side effects (nausea, constipation, respiratory depression). Schedule non-opioids (acetaminophen, NSAIDs) around the clock; use opioids for breakthrough only.

DVT Prevention

Venous thromboembolism prophylaxis

Joint replacement patients are at high risk for deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE). Aggressive prophylaxis is essential and continues after discharge.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
DVT Prevention is Critical
Without prophylaxis, up to 50% of joint replacement patients would develop DVT! Your job: make sure those SCDs are actually ON (not just in the room), verify the patient took their blood thinner, and get them moving. The triad that causes clots (Virchow's triad): stasis (lying in bed), endothelial injury (surgery), hypercoagulability (body's response to surgery). We can't prevent the last two, but YOU can fight stasis with early ambulation and compression!
DVT Prevention Strategies
Anticoagulation
Enoxaparin, rivaroxaban, apixaban, or aspirin for 10-35 days
SCDs
Sequential compression devices while in bed; remove only for ambulation
Early Mobility
Ambulate day of surgery or POD 1; frequent position changes
Ankle Pumps
Dorsiflexion/plantarflexion exercises every hour while awake
Compression Stockings
TED hose or graduated compression; wear during day
Hydration
Adequate fluids to prevent hemoconcentration

Anticoagulation Options

Medication Dosing Duration Nursing Considerations
Enoxaparin (Lovenox) 40 mg SQ daily or 30 mg SQ q12h 10-35 days Teach self-injection; rotate sites
Rivaroxaban (Xarelto) 10 mg PO daily 12 days (TKA), 35 days (THA) Take with food; no routine monitoring
Apixaban (Eliquis) 2.5 mg PO BID 12 days (TKA), 35 days (THA) No routine monitoring; renal dosing
Aspirin 81-325 mg PO daily 35 days Low bleeding risk; may be used in low-risk patients
Warfarin (Coumadin) Adjusted to INR 2-3 10-35 days Requires INR monitoring; dietary education
Signs of Pulmonary Embolism

Sudden onset: Dyspnea, chest pain (pleuritic), tachypnea, tachycardia, hypoxia, anxiety/restlessness, cough (may have hemoptysis). This is a medical emergency! Call rapid response, obtain ECG/ABG, anticipate CT angiography and anticoagulation.

Mobility & Rehabilitation

Progressive recovery milestones

Early and progressive mobility is essential for recovery. Most patients participate in physical therapy starting the day of surgery or POD 1 and continue outpatient therapy for 6-12 weeks.

Recovery Milestones
Day 0
Day of Surgery
Bed Mobility & Sitting
Ankle pumps, quad sets, gluteal sets. Dangle at bedside. May attempt standing/pivoting to chair with max assist. Assess for orthostatic hypotension.
Day 1
POD 1
First Ambulation
Walk in room/hallway with walker and assist. Transfer bed-to-chair independently. Continue exercises. Assess weight-bearing status per surgeon orders.
Day 2
POD 2
Increased Distance & Stairs
Walk 100+ feet with walker. Practice stairs if going home. May discharge home or to rehab facility. Independent with transfers.
Wk 2-6
Weeks 2-6
Outpatient Therapy
Progress to cane or no device. Increase ROM and strength. Continue hip precautions. Drive when off narcotics (usually 2-4 weeks).
3-6 mo
3-6 Months
Full Recovery
Return to most activities. Hip precautions usually discontinued at 6-12 weeks. Full strength and ROM. Low-impact exercise resumed.

Weight Bearing Status

Status Abbreviation Description
Weight Bearing As Tolerated WBAT Most common for cemented prostheses. Full weight as comfortable.
Toe Touch Weight Bearing TTWB Toe touches for balance only; no actual weight through leg.
Partial Weight Bearing PWB Specific percentage (e.g., 50%) of body weight on leg.
Non-Weight Bearing NWB No weight on operative leg (rare for joint replacement).
Safe Ambulation Tips

Pre-medicate 30-45 min before PT. Use gait belt for all initial ambulation. Dangle first to check for orthostatic hypotension. Check nerve block status - if leg is still numb/weak, delay ambulation. Walker first, progress to cane, then no device.

Complications

Recognition and prevention of adverse events

While joint replacement is highly successful, complications can occur. Early recognition and intervention are essential for optimal outcomes.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Watch for Infection
Prosthetic joint infection (PJI) is one of the most devastating complications! Early signs: persistent fever, increasing pain (especially at rest or at night), wound drainage beyond POD 3-5, redness spreading from incision. The scary part? Infection can occur years later from bacteria traveling through the bloodstream (dental work, UTI, skin infection). That's why joint replacement patients need prophylactic antibiotics before dental procedures for life!
Hip Dislocation
Risk highest first 6-12 weeks
  • Sudden severe hip/groin pain
  • Leg appears shortened
  • Leg rotated abnormally
  • Unable to bear weight
  • Treatment: Closed/open reduction
Prosthetic Joint Infection
Early (<3 mo) or late (>3 mo)
  • Fever, chills, malaise
  • Wound erythema, warmth, drainage
  • Increasing pain (especially at rest)
  • Elevated WBC, ESR, CRP
  • Treatment: Antibiotics, possible revision
DVT / Pulmonary Embolism
Risk highest 2-10 days post-op
  • Unilateral leg swelling, warmth, pain
  • PE: sudden dyspnea, chest pain, tachycardia
  • Hypoxia, anxiety
  • Prevention: Anticoagulation, SCDs, early mobility
Periprosthetic Fracture
Intraop or post-op
  • Sudden pain with activity
  • Inability to bear weight
  • History of fall or trauma
  • Risk: osteoporosis, revision surgery
  • Treatment: Fixation or revision
Leg Length Discrepancy
Noticed after surgery
  • Feeling one leg is longer/shorter
  • Limp or gait abnormality
  • Hip or back pain
  • May improve as muscles strengthen
  • Treatment: Shoe lift; rarely revision
Prosthesis Loosening
Years after surgery
  • Gradually increasing pain
  • Pain with weight bearing
  • Start-up pain (first steps after sitting)
  • Treatment: Revision surgery

Discharge Teaching

Discharge Teaching Checklist
Demonstrate hip precautions (if applicable) and verify understanding
Review anticoagulation medication, dose, duration, and signs of bleeding
Teach signs of DVT and PE - when to seek emergency care
Review wound care: keep dry, watch for infection signs
Confirm follow-up appointments: surgeon, PT/OT
Verify home setup: raised toilet seat, grab bars, walker
Review pain management plan and medication schedule
Educate on lifelong antibiotic prophylaxis before dental procedures
NCLEX Alert: Priority After Joint Replacement

The priority nursing interventions after joint replacement are: (1) Prevent DVT/PE - anticoagulation, SCDs, early mobility; (2) Prevent dislocation - hip precautions, proper positioning; (3) Manage pain - multimodal approach; (4) Prevent infection - sterile wound care, monitor for signs. On NCLEX, if asked about immediate post-op priority, think safety (DVT, dislocation) before comfort (pain).

Key Points to Remember

Posterior approach hip precautions: No flexion >90, no adduction, no internal rotation (6-12 weeks)
DVT prevention triad: Anticoagulation + SCDs + Early ambulation (continue anticoag 10-35 days)
First ambulation: POD 0 or 1; pre-medicate for pain; check nerve block status; use gait belt
Multimodal pain management: Scheduled acetaminophen + NSAIDs; opioids for breakthrough only
Hip dislocation signs: Severe pain, shortened leg, abnormal rotation - surgical emergency
Infection prevention: CHG shower pre-op; sterile wound care; antibiotics before dental work FOR LIFE
Weight bearing: Usually WBAT for cemented prostheses; confirm with surgeon order
Full recovery: 3-6 months; most patients report significant improvement in pain and function
