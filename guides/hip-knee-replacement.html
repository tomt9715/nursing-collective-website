Hip & Knee Replacement

One decision the whole exam hangs on, two post-op emergencies you cannot miss, and how to read a weight-bearing order the answer depends on.

Reading Time
15-20 minutes
Difficulty Level
Intermediate
Last Updated
July 2026
The Nursing Collective

By the end, without notes

  • Match hip precautions to the surgical approach, and name what each one forbids
  • Read a dislocation off the leg's position, and say what you do before touching it
  • Catch a DVT and a PE early, and say why you never walk a suspected DVT
  • Read a weight-bearing order and say what the patient may and may not do
  • Take a knee replacement from day zero to the flexion it needs at discharge
01

What actually gets you

This topic is not hard to understand. It is easy to answer wrong under pressure. The points leak out in three predictable places.

Read these before anything else.

You memorized one list of hip precautions

There is no single list. The precautions depend on the surgical approach. A posterior approach forbids flexion past 90 degrees, adduction past midline, and internal rotation, and it uses an abduction pillow. An anterior approach restricts extension and external rotation instead. Memorize one list and the question that names the other approach eats your point.

You missed one of the two post-op killers

Two things can kill this patient. The first is dislocation: sudden pain, a shortened externally rotated leg, a lump you can feel, loss of function. The second is venous thromboembolism, a DVT that throws a PE. Miss either and the vignette is not a comfort question anymore.

You did not check the weight-bearing status

The safe answer often depends on it. WBAT, TTWB, PWB and NWB each permit a different thing. So does the mobility plan and the block. If you walk a patient without knowing what they are cleared for, you guessed.

Bottom Line: Stop studying this as a surgery. Study it as four calls: which approach, is this a dislocation, is this a clot, what is this leg allowed to do.
02

The four things

Four decision points carry this whole topic. For each one you should be able to say the rule, the clue, the priority action, and the teaching point. Say them with the book closed.

If you cannot, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Decision point The rule or goal The clue in the scenario Priority action Teaching point
Hip precautions Posterior: no flexion past 90 degrees, no crossing midline, no internal rotation. Which approach the operative report names. Match the precautions to the approach, not to memory. Abduction pillow, raised toilet seat, reachers and a sock aid.
DVT prevention Anticoagulant plus SCDs plus early walking. A warm, swollen, tender calf on one side. Notify, hold ambulation, expect a duplex ultrasound. Keep the SCDs on, confirm the blood thinner, get them up.
Dislocation Catch it fast; it is an emergency. Sudden pain, a shortened externally rotated leg, a lump, loss of function. Immobilize as found, do not reduce it, call the surgeon. You never realign a prosthetic hip yourself.
Mobility Up on the day of surgery or day one. The weight-bearing order and the nerve block. Pre-medicate, dangle, then walk with a walker. Walker first; good leg up, operative leg down.
Copy this down

Write these four rows on one index card, by hand. Approach sets the precautions. A one-sided warm calf is a clot. A shortened rotated leg is a dislocation. The order tells you how to walk them. That card is most of the exam.

03

Hip precautions, by approach

Discrimination axis: which side was cut, posterior or anterior.

Hip precautions are movement limits that protect the healing capsule. They prevent the ball from leaving the socket. They run for about 6 to 12 weeks. The approach decides which movements are dangerous.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Big Three, posterior
For a posterior hip: do not bend past 90 degrees, do not cross, do not turn the toes in. Teach nose over toes. If they bend, the nose should not pass the toes. An abduction pillow in bed keeps the legs apart.
Posterior approach: most common
  • No flexion past 90 degrees
  • No adduction past midline, no crossing legs
  • No internal rotation, toes stay up or slightly out
  • Incision on the back or side of the hip
  • Higher dislocation rate, about 2 to 3 percent

Protects: the posterior capsule that was opened.

Anterior approach: growing trend
  • No hyperextension, no leg behind the body
  • No external rotation, no duck-footed leg
  • Avoid abduction, no leg far out to the side
  • Incision on the front of the hip
  • Lower dislocation rate, about 1 percent

Protects: the anterior capsule that was opened.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Approach decides the rules
Posterior means do not push the hip backward: no flexion, no adduction, no internal rotation. Anterior means do not push it forward: no extension, no external rotation. Check the operative report or ask the surgeon before you teach precautions.

Everyday activities, posterior approach

ActivitySafe technique
SittingFirm, elevated chair. Hips higher than knees. Do not lean forward.
ToiletingRaised toilet seat, adds 4 to 6 inches. Do not lean forward to wipe.
SleepingOn the back or non-operative side. Abduction pillow between the legs.
DressingReacher, long-handled shoehorn, sock aid. Operative leg dressed first, undressed last.
BathingShower chair, long-handled sponge. No tub baths until cleared.
TransfersPivot on the non-operative leg. Keep the operative leg extended. Do not twist.
Signs of dislocation

Sudden severe hip or groin pain. The leg looks shortened and externally rotated. There may be a lump. The patient cannot bear weight or move the leg.

This is an emergency. Keep the patient still. Do not try to reduce it. Notify the surgeon.

Bottom Line: Read the approach first. Posterior: no flexion past 90 degrees, no crossing midline, no internal rotation, abduction pillow in bed. Anterior: no extension, no external rotation. Both run 6 to 12 weeks.
04

DVT prevention

Joint replacement is one of the highest-risk surgeries for a clot. A DVT can throw a PE, and PE is the leading cause of death after these operations. Prophylaxis is not optional.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Fight the stasis
Virchow named three causes: stasis, vessel injury, and a hypercoagulable state. Surgery hands you the last two. You fight the first. Get the SCDs actually on, confirm the blood thinner went in, and get the patient moving.

What prevention looks like

  1. 1
    Anticoagulation. Enoxaparin, rivaroxaban, apixaban, aspirin or warfarin. It continues after discharge, roughly 10 to 35 days.
  2. 2
    Sequential compression devices. On while in bed. Remove only to ambulate, then put them back.
  3. 3
    Early ambulation. Up on the day of surgery or day one. Change position often.
  4. 4
    Ankle pumps and hydration. Dorsiflex and plantarflex every hour awake. Keep fluids up. Graduated compression stockings during the day.

Anticoagulation options

When it is already a clot

Suspect a DVT when one leg is warm, swollen, tender, and heavy. A calf more than 3 cm larger than the other side is a red flag. Do not walk this patient. Bed rest until it is ruled out or treated.

Homans sign is unreliable, skip it. D-dimer rises after any surgery, so it does not confirm much here. The duplex ultrasound is the definitive test.

When it becomes a PE

Sudden dyspnea. Pleuritic chest pain. Tachypnea and tachycardia. Falling oxygen. Anxiety, maybe a cough. This is an emergency.

Sit them up, put on high-flow oxygen, call a rapid response. Do not start diagnostics or dose heparin on your own first. CT pulmonary angiography confirms it after you stabilize.

Bottom Line: Prophylaxis is a drug plus SCDs plus early walking. A warm swollen one-sided calf means stop walking and notify. Sudden dyspnea after surgery means PE until proven otherwise; oxygen and rapid response first.
05

Complications to recognize

These surgeries usually go well. The exam lives in the ones that do not. Learn to name each complication from its signs.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Infection can come back
A prosthetic joint infection is one of the worst outcomes. Early signs: persistent fever, pain at rest or at night, drainage past day 3 to 5, spreading redness. It can also seed years later from dental work, a UTI, or a skin infection. That is why these patients take antibiotics before dental procedures for life.
Hip dislocation
  • Sudden severe hip or groin pain
  • Leg shortened and externally rotated
  • A palpable lump, loss of function
  • Cannot bear weight

Do: immobilize as found, do not reduce, call the surgeon. Reduction is done under sedation.

Prosthetic joint infection
  • Fever, chills, malaise
  • Wound redness, warmth, drainage
  • Increasing pain, worse at rest
  • Rising WBC, ESR, CRP

Do: antibiotics, sometimes revision or implant removal. It may be early, under 3 months, or late.

VTE and PE
  • One warm, swollen, tender leg
  • PE: sudden dyspnea, chest pain
  • Tachycardia, low oxygen, anxiety
  • Highest risk about 2 to 10 days out

Do: prevent it, then treat fast. Oxygen and rapid response for a PE.

Neurovascular compromise
  • Check the five Ps of the operative leg
  • Pain, pulse, pallor, paresthesia, paralysis
  • Compare to the other leg
  • A dislocated head can press the sciatic nerve

Do: assess often, report a new deficit. Numbness or a lost pulse is not normal.

Two more to know

Fat embolism
  • Marrow fat enters the circulation
  • Respiratory distress and hypoxia
  • Confusion or altered mental status
  • A petechial rash, often on the chest

When: usually the first days after long-bone or joint surgery.

Fracture and loosening
  • Periprosthetic fracture: sudden pain, cannot bear weight, often a fall
  • Loosening: gradual pain years later, start-up pain
  • Leg length feels off, causing a limp

Do: fixation or revision surgery, or a shoe lift for a length difference.

Dental antibiotics, for life

The prosthesis is a permanent foreign body. Bacteria can seed it through the blood for life, so the risk never ends. Before invasive dental work, the patient takes amoxicillin 2 g by mouth 1 hour ahead, or clindamycin if penicillin-allergic.

Bottom Line: Learn each by its signs. A rotated shortened leg is dislocation. A one-sided warm calf is a clot. Fever with rest pain is infection. Sort them fast and the priority answers itself.
06

Postoperative care

Post-op care is pain control, watching for complications, and getting the patient moving. Many go home in a day or two. Order your assessments; do the highest-stakes ones first.

What you assess, and how often

  1. 1
    Airway and breathing. Oxygen after anesthesia as needed. Incentive spirometry every 1 to 2 hours. Cough and deep breathe. Elevate the head of bed once stable.
  2. 2
    Hemodynamics. Vital signs every 15 minutes until stable. Watch for hypotension from blood loss. Check the drain output. Recheck hemoglobin on day one.
  3. 3
    Neurovascular checks. Every 1 to 2 hours. Color, temperature, capillary refill, dorsalis pedis and posterior tibial pulses, sensation, movement. Compare bilaterally.
  4. 4
    Wound and dressing. Drainage should be sanguineous, then serosanguineous. Report heavy bleeding, spreading warmth or redness, pus, or wound separation.
  5. 5
    Pain. Rate it and reassess after you treat it. Check the nerve block. A leg too numb to move is a fall risk.

Pain control is multimodal

Multimodal means several drug classes hitting different pathways. It controls pain with fewer opioid side effects, so less nausea, constipation and respiratory depression. Schedule the non-opioids around the clock. Save opioids for breakthrough.

Weight-bearing status, and who does what

The weight-bearing order shapes every transfer. Cemented joints usually allow immediate weight; cementless ones may be restricted while bone grows in. For a knee, a CPM machine may start at 30 to 40 degrees and advance toward 90. It does not replace physical therapy.

Delegation matters here. Assessment, teaching, and evaluation stay with the RN. A UAP can help with meals, hygiene and mobility once you have assessed and given clear precautions.

Weight-bearing shorthand

WBAT: full weight as tolerated, common for cemented joints. TTWB: toe touches for balance only. PWB: a set percentage, often 50 percent. NWB: no weight at all, rare here. Confirm the order before you stand the patient.

Bottom Line: Breathing and bleeding first, then the leg. Neurovascular checks every 1 to 2 hours. Pain is multimodal, non-opioids scheduled. Never move the patient without the weight-bearing order.
07

Mobility and rehab

Early movement drives recovery. Physical therapy usually starts the day of surgery or day one, then continues for 6 to 12 weeks. Priority questions here are about the safe order of a first walk.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Get them up, safely
Early mobility prevents the most complications. Pre-medicate 30 to 45 minutes before therapy. Check the block; if the foot cannot feel or move, wait. Use a gait belt, dangle first for orthostatic changes. A fall is worse than waiting an hour.

The safe order of a first walk

  1. 1
    Pre-medicate. Give the analgesic about 30 minutes before. Take baseline vitals. A guarded, painful patient will not participate.
  2. 2
    Set the abduction pillow. Before any position change, keep the legs apart. This stops a posterior hip from crossing midline during the move.
  3. 3
    Dangle and assess. Sit at the edge with the operative leg extended. Check for orthostatic hypotension. Anesthesia and blood loss make it common.
  4. 4
    Stand with a walker. Bear weight as the order allows. Cue the precautions out loud as they rise.
  5. 5
    Walk a short distance. A first goal is often 10 to 50 feet. Return safely. Document distance, weight-bearing, and tolerance.

The recovery timeline

WhenWhat happens
Day 0Ankle pumps, quad sets, gluteal sets. Dangle. Knee CPM may start at 30 to 40 degrees.
Day 1First ambulation with a walker. Transfer bed to chair. Weight-bearing as ordered.
Day 2Walk 100 or more feet. Practice stairs if going home. Discharge or rehab.
Weeks 2 to 6Progress to a cane, then no device. Drive when off narcotics, about 2 to 4 weeks.
3 to 6 monthsReturn to most activity. Precautions usually stop at 6 to 12 weeks.
Stairs and the knee goal

On stairs: good leg up, operative leg down. Say it as good up, bad down. For a knee replacement, aim for 90 degrees of flexion by discharge and about 120 degrees by 3 months. Ninety degrees is what stairs and a car seat need.

Bottom Line: Pre-medicate, pillow, dangle, stand, walk, in that order. Walker first, then cane. Good leg up, bad leg down. A knee needs 90 degrees before it goes home.

Key Takeaways
