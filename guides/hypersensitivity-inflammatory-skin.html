Hypersensitivity & Inflammatory Skin

Atopic dermatitis, contact dermatitis, diaper dermatitis, urticaria, and the Stevens-Johnson spectrum. From daily eczema management to the derm emergencies.

6 Sections 14 Quiz Questions ~12 Min Read

Learning Objectives

Identify atopic dermatitis and apply the daily management ladder (emollients, topical steroids, triggers).
Differentiate contact dermatitis from atopic dermatitis and from irritant diaper dermatitis.
Recognize urticaria and know when hives escalate to anaphylaxis.
Identify Stevens-Johnson syndrome and toxic epidermal necrolysis early and initiate the right escalation.
Administer antihistamines, corticosteroids, and epinephrine safely.
Coach families on trigger avoidance, "soak and seal" bathing, and when to seek urgent care.

High-Yield Summary

Eight facts that cover the hypersensitivity spectrum.

  • Atopic dermatitis (eczema) is part of the atopic triad (eczema + asthma + allergic rhinitis). The "itch that rashes" — scratching makes it worse.
  • Location clues: infants get eczema on cheeks, scalp, extensor surfaces. Older children: flexural surfaces (antecubital, popliteal, neck, wrists).
  • Eczema management ladder: moisturize daily + "soak and seal" bathing, topical steroids for flares (lowest potency that works), calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus) for steroid-sparing, antihistamines for sleep, avoid triggers.
  • Contact dermatitis: cell-mediated (Type IV) reaction. Poison ivy/oak/sumac (urushiol), nickel, latex, fragrances. Pattern matches contact (linear streaks). Treat with topical steroids ± oral steroids if widespread.
  • Irritant diaper dermatitis: from urine/stool contact. Spares the skin folds (affects convex surfaces). Treat with barrier cream, frequent changes, air time. If no improvement in 2–3 days, consider candidal superinfection.
  • Urticaria (hives): wheals that appear and disappear within 24 hours at a given site, move around. Triggers: foods (peanuts, eggs, milk, nuts, shellfish), meds, viral, insect. H1 antihistamine first-line.
  • Anaphylaxis red flags: hives PLUS respiratory (wheeze, stridor, dyspnea), CV (hypotension), or GI involvement. IM epinephrine IMMEDIATELY — 0.15 mg <30 kg, 0.3 mg ≥30 kg into vastus lateralis.
  • SJS / TEN: severe medication-induced mucocutaneous reaction. Positive Nikolsky sign (skin sloughs with lateral pressure), painful mucosal erosions, skin detachment. ICU/burn unit care; stop the offending drug immediately.

Atopic Dermatitis (Eczema)

The chronic itchy disease with a family history of allergy

Atopic Dermatitis

Chronic
Who gets it

Starts in early infancy; affects up to 20% of children. Part of the atopic triad with asthma and allergic rhinitis. Strong genetic and immunologic component (filaggrin mutation).

Classic Findings
  • Intensely itchy red, dry, scaly patches
  • Infants: cheeks, scalp, extensor surfaces (diaper area often spared)
  • Older children: flexural surfaces (antecubital, popliteal, neck, wrists, ankles)
  • Chronic: thickened, leathery skin (lichenification) from chronic scratching
  • May see allergic shiners (dark circles), Dennie-Morgan lines under eyes
Triggers
  • Dry skin, cold weather
  • Irritants: soaps, detergents, wool, synthetic fabrics, fragrances
  • Allergens: dust mites, pet dander, pollen, food (less common trigger in older kids)
  • Heat, sweating, stress
  • Secondary infections (Staph, HSV)

Daily Management

Prevention
The "Soak and Seal" Method
  • Short (5–10 min) lukewarm bath daily
  • Mild, fragrance-free cleanser (avoid regular soap)
  • Pat dry (don't rub)
  • Apply moisturizer within 3 minutes of patting dry — traps moisture
  • Thick emollient (petrolatum-based) preferred over lotion
  • Reapply moisturizer 2–3 times daily
Lifestyle
  • Cotton, loose clothing — avoid wool and synthetics
  • Short fingernails; cotton gloves at night to prevent scratching
  • Fragrance-free laundry detergent, double-rinse cycle
  • Humidifier in winter; avoid overheated rooms
  • Identify and avoid specific triggers

Treatment Ladder

Stepwise
Flare Control
  • Topical corticosteroids — low potency (hydrocortisone 1%) for face, higher potency (triamcinolone) for body
  • BID × 1–2 weeks; taper as skin clears
  • Topical calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus) for steroid-sparing, especially on face/folds
  • Sedating antihistamines (diphenhydramine, hydroxyzine) at night for itch/sleep
  • Wet wrap therapy for severe flares
Severe Disease
  • Phototherapy (UV)
  • Oral immunosuppressants (methotrexate, cyclosporine)
  • Dupilumab (biologic) for refractory moderate-severe

Secondary Infection

Red Flag
Watch For
  • Honey-colored crusts (secondary Staph impetigo)
  • Weeping, yellow drainage, new tenderness
  • Eczema herpeticum — HSV infection of eczematous skin: painful vesicles in clusters, fever, may have ocular or systemic involvement
  • Cellulitis: warm red spreading border, fever
Treatment
  • Bacterial: topical mupirocin or oral antibiotic
  • Eczema herpeticum = acyclovir IV + emergency ophthalmology if eyes involved
  • Continue eczema care during and after infection treatment
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Moisturize Within 3 Minutes of the Bath
The "soak and seal" method works because wet skin absorbs water during the bath, and moisturizer applied right after seals that water in. Wait too long (even 5 minutes) and the water evaporates; the moisturizer goes on dry skin and does far less. Teach families this timing explicitly — it's the single most effective home intervention for mild-to-moderate eczema.
Eczema Herpeticum Is an Emergency

A child with known eczema who develops painful clustered vesicles, fever, or rapidly spreading lesions may have eczema herpeticum — disseminated HSV on eczematous skin. Requires IV acyclovir immediately. If near the eyes, emergent ophthalmology referral to prevent corneal scarring and blindness.

🎯 Bottom Line: Eczema is chronic. Daily moisturize, bath then seal, steroids for flares, avoid triggers, protect nails. Watch for Staph impetigo and eczema herpeticum.

Contact & Diaper Dermatitis

Pattern matters: the rash is where the trigger touched

Allergic Contact Dermatitis

Type IV
Cause

Cell-mediated (delayed-type IV) hypersensitivity reaction to an antigen. Reaction appears 24–72 hours after exposure in sensitized individuals.

Common Triggers
  • Poison ivy/oak/sumac (urushiol oil) — classic linear streaks
  • Nickel (jewelry, belt buckles, snaps)
  • Latex (gloves, balloons, medical equipment)
  • Fragrances, dyes, cosmetics, preservatives
  • Topical medications (neomycin, bacitracin)
Appearance
  • Very itchy, red, edematous papules/vesicles
  • Pattern matches contact — linear in poison ivy, ring under a watchband
  • Acute: weeping and oozing; chronic: dry, scaly, lichenified
Treatment
  • Remove / avoid offender (wash off urushiol ASAP if exposed)
  • Cool compresses, Burow's (aluminum acetate) solution
  • Topical corticosteroids (medium-to-high potency)
  • Oral corticosteroids for extensive or facial/genital involvement
  • Antihistamines for itch

Irritant Diaper Dermatitis

Common
Cause

Skin irritation from prolonged contact with urine and stool, friction, and moisture. Not an allergic reaction — a chemical irritation.

Classic Appearance
  • Red, shiny skin on convex surfaces (buttocks, tops of thighs, pubic area)
  • SPARES the skin folds — key distinguishing feature from candida
  • Tender; infant may cry with diaper changes
Treatment & Prevention
  • Frequent diaper changes — every 2 hours or whenever soiled
  • Air time — expose skin to air without diaper for short periods
  • Barrier creams: zinc oxide or petrolatum; apply generously with each change
  • Gentle cleansing with water; avoid wipes with fragrance/alcohol
  • If not improving in 2–3 days: consider candida superinfection (add nystatin or clotrimazole)
NCLEX: Irritant vs. Candida Diaper Rash

Irritant diaper dermatitis: affects convex surfaces, spares the folds, no satellite lesions → barrier cream and frequent changes.
Candida diaper dermatitis: involves skin folds, satellite lesions, bright red → nystatin or clotrimazole cream.
The fold involvement is the key differentiator on exam questions.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
"Leaves of Three, Let Them Be"
Urushiol oil from poison ivy/oak/sumac penetrates skin within minutes. Washing the area with soap and water within 15 minutes of exposure can prevent the reaction entirely. After 30 minutes it's usually too late. The rash itself is NOT contagious — but the oil on clothing, shoes, and pet fur CAN transfer. Wash all contaminated items.
🎯 Bottom Line: Contact dermatitis = pattern matches exposure; remove trigger + topical steroid. Irritant diaper = spares folds, barrier cream fixes it. Candida = folds + satellites, needs antifungal.

Urticaria & Anaphylaxis

From hives to airway emergency

Urticaria (Hives)

IgE-Mediated
Appearance
  • Wheals (hives) — raised, red, edematous, intensely itchy
  • Size variable — small papules to large plaques
  • Migratory — individual lesions appear and disappear within 24 hours, but new ones continue to emerge
  • May be accompanied by angioedema (deeper swelling of lips, face, hands)
Common Triggers
  • Foods: peanuts, tree nuts, eggs, milk, shellfish, wheat, soy
  • Medications: penicillins, sulfonamides, NSAIDs, vaccines
  • Viral infections — #1 cause in children
  • Insect stings, latex
  • Physical: cold, heat, pressure, exercise
Acute vs. Chronic
  • Acute urticaria: <6 weeks; usually viral or identifiable trigger
  • Chronic urticaria: ≥6 weeks; often idiopathic or autoimmune
Treatment
  • H1 antihistamines first-line: cetirizine or loratadine (non-sedating) or diphenhydramine (sedating)
  • H2 antihistamines (famotidine) may be added for refractory
  • Oral corticosteroids for severe/widespread
  • Identify and avoid trigger
  • Allergy referral for recurrent or chronic cases

Anaphylaxis

Emergency
Definition

Acute, life-threatening IgE-mediated systemic allergic reaction. Can occur within minutes to 1 hour after exposure.

Signs & Symptoms (Any TWO Systems)
  • Skin: hives, angioedema, flushing, itching
  • Respiratory: wheezing, stridor, dyspnea, cough, hoarseness, throat tightness
  • Cardiovascular: hypotension, tachycardia, syncope
  • GI: vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain
  • Sense of impending doom
Treatment
  1. IM epinephrine IMMEDIATELY — vastus lateralis (outer thigh)
    • <30 kg: 0.15 mg (EpiPen Jr)
    • ≥30 kg: 0.3 mg (EpiPen)
    • May repeat every 5–15 min
  2. Call 911 / rapid response
  3. Airway management, oxygen, position (supine with legs elevated unless vomiting/dyspneic → seated)
  4. IV fluids for hypotension
  5. H1 + H2 antihistamines, corticosteroids (adjunct, NOT first-line)
  6. Bronchodilators for wheezing
  7. Observation 4–6 hours for biphasic reaction
Epinephrine Is First — Not Antihistamines

In anaphylaxis, IM epinephrine is the ONLY life-saving first-line treatment. Antihistamines and corticosteroids are adjuncts — they don't reverse airway or cardiovascular collapse. Delay in giving epinephrine is the #1 cause of fatal anaphylaxis. Teach families with auto-injectors: "If in doubt, give it." Over-treating is not dangerous; under-treating is.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Biphasic Anaphylaxis — Watch for Round Two
Up to 20% of anaphylaxis cases have a biphasic reaction — symptoms return 4–12 hours after the initial resolution, without re-exposure. That's why even a child who responds beautifully to one dose of epinephrine needs observation for 4–6 hours minimum. Always discharge with an EpiPen prescription and action plan.
🎯 Bottom Line: Hives = H1 antihistamine. Hives + respiratory/CV/GI = anaphylaxis = IM epi (vastus lateralis) IMMEDIATELY. Watch 4–6 hours for biphasic.

Erythema Multiforme, SJS & TEN

A spectrum from mild to potentially fatal

Erythema multiforme (EM), Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS), and toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN) are on a spectrum of hypersensitivity reactions. EM is usually benign; SJS and TEN are dermatologic emergencies with significant mortality.

Erythema Multiforme (EM)

Usually Benign
Appearance

Target / "bull's eye" lesions — central dusky zone surrounded by a paler ring and an outer red ring. On palms, soles, and extensor extremities.

Triggers
  • HSV (most common) — can cause recurrent EM
  • Mycoplasma pneumoniae
  • Less often: medications
EM Minor vs. Major
  • Minor: skin only, self-limiting, resolves in 2–3 weeks
  • Major: mucosal involvement (mouth most commonly); more serious
Treatment
  • Supportive; treat underlying trigger (acyclovir if HSV)
  • Antihistamines for itch; topical steroids; pain control
  • Mouth care if oral lesions

SJS / TEN

Emergency
Definitions
  • SJS: <10% BSA skin detachment
  • SJS/TEN overlap: 10–30% BSA
  • TEN: >30% BSA; mortality 30–50%
Presentation
  • Prodrome: fever, malaise, sore throat, cough (1–3 days)
  • Rash: dusky red macules, purpuric spots, vesicles, bullae — progresses rapidly
  • Painful mucosal erosions in mouth, eyes, and genitals
  • Positive Nikolsky sign — lateral pressure causes epidermis to slough
  • Skin pain out of proportion to appearance
Common Triggers
  • Medications: anticonvulsants (carbamazepine, lamotrigine, phenytoin), allopurinol, sulfonamides, NSAIDs, some antibiotics
  • Infections: Mycoplasma pneumoniae
Treatment
  • Immediate discontinuation of offending drug
  • ICU or burn unit admission
  • IV fluid replacement, electrolyte correction, nutritional support
  • Wound care like burns — sterile handling, dressings
  • Ophthalmology (eye involvement is common and can blind)
  • Oral/mouth care for painful erosions
  • IVIG sometimes; corticosteroids controversial
Always Review the Med List

If a child develops a widespread painful rash with mucosal involvement within 1–8 weeks of starting a new medication, think SJS. Common culprits: anticonvulsants (carbamazepine, lamotrigine, phenytoin), sulfonamide antibiotics, allopurinol, NSAIDs. The offending drug must be stopped IMMEDIATELY — continuing it escalates to TEN and death.

Nikolsky Sign — The Hands-On Diagnostic Clue

Apply gentle lateral pressure to apparently normal-looking skin near a lesion. If the epidermis sloughs off, Nikolsky is positive — highly suggestive of SJS/TEN. Positive Nikolsky + mucosal involvement + recent new medication = SJS/TEN until proven otherwise. Urgent dermatology consult.

🎯 Bottom Line: EM = target lesions, usually HSV, self-limiting. SJS/TEN = mucosal erosions + Nikolsky+, often drug-induced, ICU/burn unit care, stop the drug NOW.

Medications at a Glance

What to reach for — and what to reach for FIRST
Emollients / Moisturizers
Eczema Daily Care

Fragrance-free, thick (petrolatum-based). Apply within 3 min of bath, reapply 2–3× daily.

CeraVe, Vanicream, Eucerin, plain petroleum jelly.

Topical Corticosteroids
Eczema & Contact

Low potency (hydrocortisone 1%) for face, folds, infants.

Medium potency (triamcinolone 0.1%) for body, limbs.

Apply BID during flares × 1–2 weeks; taper as skin clears.

Long-term use → skin atrophy, striae.

Topical Calcineurin Inhibitors
Steroid-Sparing

Tacrolimus (Protopic) or pimecrolimus (Elidel).

Good for face and folds where long-term steroids risk atrophy.

Mild burning for first few days is common.

H1 Antihistamines
Urticaria & Itch

Non-sedating: cetirizine (Zyrtec), loratadine (Claritin) — daytime.

Sedating: diphenhydramine (Benadryl), hydroxyzine (Atarax) — nighttime itch.

First-line for hives.

Oral Corticosteroids
Severe Inflammation

Prednisone or prednisolone for severe contact dermatitis, widespread eczema flare, severe urticaria.

Short course 5–7 days; taper if longer.

Watch for mood changes, BG elevation, GI upset.

Epinephrine (IM)
Anaphylaxis · First-Line

IM into vastus lateralis (outer thigh).

0.15 mg if <30 kg (EpiPen Jr); 0.3 mg if ≥30 kg (EpiPen).

Repeat every 5–15 min if needed. ALWAYS first, antihistamines second.

Barrier Creams (Zinc Oxide)
Diaper Rash

Desitin, Triple Paste, A+D ointment.

Apply generously with each diaper change to protect skin from urine/stool.

Dupilumab (Biologic)
Refractory Eczema

IL-4/IL-13 inhibitor. Injection every 2 weeks.

For moderate-severe atopic dermatitis refractory to topicals.

Approved down to age 6 months.

Acyclovir
HSV-Triggered EM / Eczema Herpeticum

Oral for recurrent EM from HSV; IV for eczema herpeticum.

Adequate hydration to prevent nephrotoxicity.

🎯 Bottom Line: Moisturize + steroid for eczema. Antihistamine for hives. EPI first for anaphylaxis. Stop the drug for SJS/TEN.

Family Education & Prevention

The daily habits and the emergency response

Eczema Daily Care

  1. Bath daily for 5–10 min in lukewarm water; use mild fragrance-free cleanser.
  2. Pat dry (don't rub) and moisturize within 3 minutes.
  3. Moisturize thickly, 2–3 times per day; use plain petrolatum or fragrance-free cream.
  4. Use topical steroid on active red/itchy patches only, BID during flare; stop when clear.
  5. Keep fingernails short; cotton gloves or mittens at night to prevent scratching.
  6. Identify and avoid triggers: wool, synthetics, fragrances, heat, stress, known allergens.
  7. Call provider for: honey-colored crusts, fever, painful clustered vesicles, or spreading red area.

Allergy Avoidance & Auto-Injector Use

Know the allergen — food label reading, ingredient awareness, avoidance at school/parties.

Carry two EpiPens at all times — one may be needed, biphasic reactions may require a second dose.

Practice with a trainer — teach all caregivers, including daycare/school staff.

Medical alert bracelet — identifies allergy in case of emergency.

Action plan — written plan at school; review annually.

If in doubt, give the EpiPen — over-treating anaphylaxis is not dangerous; under-treating is.

When to Call or Go to the ED

Throat tightness, wheezing, stridor — use EpiPen and call 911.

Facial or lip swelling, difficulty breathing after medication or food exposure.

Widespread rash with mucosal erosions (mouth sores, eye redness) — rule out SJS.

Skin sloughs with pressure or large blisters forming.

Painful clustered vesicles on eczematous skin — possible eczema herpeticum.

Honey-colored crusts, fever, warmth, spreading redness — possible Staph infection.

Normalize the Skin Journey

Children with chronic skin conditions often feel self-conscious and frustrated. Acknowledge the emotional toll — not just the itch, but the stares, the comments, the missed sleep. Reassure families that many children outgrow atopic dermatitis by adolescence or adulthood, and that daily care prevents most flares. Empower older children to participate in their own skin care routine; self-efficacy is part of the treatment.

🎯 Bottom Line: Moisturize daily + steroid for flares. Carry two EpiPens. Know the red flags: anaphylaxis, SJS, eczema herpeticum. Empower the child.
