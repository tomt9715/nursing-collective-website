Hypersensitivity & Inflammatory Skin

Four hypersensitivity types, two look-alike rashes, and the one drug reaction that is a burn-unit emergency. How to place a reaction and know what you do next.

Reading Time
15-20 minutes
Difficulty Level
Intermediate
Last Updated
July 2026
The Nursing Collective

By the end, without notes

  • Place a reaction in the right hypersensitivity type from the scenario alone
  • Tell atopic dermatitis from contact dermatitis, and say what each one changes
  • Say when hives are just hives and when they are anaphylaxis
  • Spot early SJS in a rash that looks minor, and name the drug link
  • State the topical steroid rule: which potency, where, and how long
  • Give epinephrine, moisturizer, and barrier care without looking them up
01

What actually gets you

Skin questions are not about naming a rash. They are about placing it. Place the reaction in the right hypersensitivity type. Match the rash to what you do next. The rest follows.

Points get lost here in three predictable ways. Read these before anything else.

You blurred the four hypersensitivity types

So a scenario hands you a reaction and you cannot place it. Type I is immediate and IgE-driven. Anaphylaxis and urticaria live there. Type II is cytotoxic. Type III is immune-complex. Type IV is delayed and T-cell driven. Contact dermatitis lives there. If you cannot place it, you cannot predict what it does.

You treated every itchy rash the same

Atopic dermatitis and contact dermatitis both itch. They need different actions. Atopic is chronic; you repair the barrier and moisturize daily. Contact follows an exposure; you find the trigger and remove it. Same itch, different job.

You read SJS as a bad rash

It is not a bad rash. It is a dermatologic emergency. A drug triggers it. The mucous membranes erode. The skin sloughs. You stop the drug and treat the child like a burn. Miss it and it climbs toward death.

Bottom Line: Do not memorize rashes. Learn three splits. Which hypersensitivity type. Atopic or contact. A bad rash or SJS.

The four things

For every condition below, say four things with the book closed. The one-sentence definition. The clue you would see in a patient. The priority action. The first-line treatment with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Condition Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line treatment and teaching
Atopic dermatitis A chronic, itchy, barrier-defect rash that runs with allergy. Itchy, dry, scaly patches on the flexural folds; a child with asthma or hay fever. Repair the barrier: moisturize daily. Emollient plus a topical steroid on flares. Moisturize within 3 minutes of the bath.
Contact dermatitis A delayed reaction where the antigen touched the skin. An itchy rash whose shape matches the exposure; linear streaks after a hike. Find the trigger and remove it. Topical steroid; oral steroid if widespread. Wash urushiol off fast.
Urticaria Wheals that rise, itch, and move within 24 hours. Welts that come and go, often after a virus or a food. Check for airway and circulation involvement first. An H1 antihistamine. Watch for the hives that turn into anaphylaxis.
SJS / TEN A drug-driven reaction that erodes mucosa and sloughs skin. A painful rash with mouth and eye sores, weeks into a new drug. Stop the offending drug now. ICU or burn-unit care; treat the skin like a burn.

03

The four hypersensitivity types

Discrimination axis: how fast, and by what mechanism.

Four types of hypersensitivity exist. Two carry most of this guide. Type I is fast. Type IV is slow. That one split does most of the work.

Type Mechanism Timing after exposure Where you meet it
Type I Immediate, IgE-mediated Minutes to 1 hour Anaphylaxis, urticaria, the atopic child
Type II Cytotoxic, antibody against cells Variable Transfusion reactions, hemolysis
Type III Immune-complex deposition Hours to days Serum sickness, lupus, glomerulonephritis
Type IV Delayed, T-cell mediated 24 to 72 hours Contact dermatitis, TB skin test, SJS / TEN
The axis that separates them

Speed and mechanism. Type I fires within minutes and rides on IgE. Think anaphylaxis and hives. Type IV takes a day or two and rides on T-cells. Think poison ivy. Fast points to Type I. Delayed points to Type IV.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Fast is Type I, Slow is Type IV
A reaction in minutes is Type I and IgE-mediated; epinephrine is on the table. A reaction a day or two later is Type IV and T-cell mediated; a topical steroid and trigger removal is the job. Speed sorts most scenarios before you read another word.
04

Atopic dermatitis

The itch that rashes.

Atopic dermatitis is eczema. The child scratches, and scratching makes it worse. It is chronic. It starts in early infancy. It affects up to 20 percent of children.

It travels with allergy. It is part of the atopic triad. Eczema, asthma, and allergic rhinitis run together. A filaggrin mutation weakens the skin barrier, so the skin dries and lets irritants in.

Location dates the child. Infants show it on the cheeks, scalp, and extensor surfaces. The diaper area is usually spared. Older children show it on flexural surfaces. Look in the antecubital and popliteal folds, the neck, the wrists, and the ankles.

Classic findings

  • Intensely itchy red, dry, scaly patches
  • Thickened, leathery skin (lichenification) from chronic scratching
  • Allergic shiners (dark circles) and Dennie-Morgan lines under the eyes

Triggers

  • Dry skin and cold weather
  • Irritants: soaps, detergents, wool, synthetic fabrics, fragrances
  • Allergens: dust mites, pet dander, pollen, and food (a less common trigger in older kids)
  • Heat, sweating, and stress
  • Secondary infection with Staph or HSV
Soak and seal Daily
  • Short lukewarm bath, 5 to 10 minutes daily
  • Mild, fragrance-free cleanser; avoid regular soap
  • Pat dry, do not rub
  • Apply moisturizer within 3 minutes to trap the water
  • Thick emollient (petrolatum-based) over lotion
  • Reapply moisturizer 2 to 3 times a day
  • Cotton loose clothing; short nails; cotton gloves at night
  • Fragrance-free detergent, double-rinse; humidifier in winter
Treatment ladder Stepwise
  • Topical steroid on flares: low potency (hydrocortisone 1%) for the face, higher (triamcinolone) for the body
  • Apply BID for 1 to 2 weeks; taper as the skin clears
  • Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus) spare steroids on the face and folds
  • Sedating antihistamines (diphenhydramine, hydroxyzine) at night for itch and sleep
  • Wet wrap therapy for severe flares
Severe disease Refractory
  • Phototherapy (UV)
  • Oral immunosuppressants (methotrexate, cyclosporine)
  • Dupilumab, a biologic, for refractory moderate-to-severe disease
Watch for infection Red flag
  • Honey-colored crusts point to secondary Staph impetigo; treat with mupirocin or an oral antibiotic
  • Weeping, yellow drainage, or new tenderness
  • Eczema herpeticum: painful clustered vesicles, fever, and possible ocular or systemic spread
  • Cellulitis: a warm red spreading border with fever
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Moisturize Within 3 Minutes of the Bath
Wet skin absorbs water during the bath, and moisturizer applied right after seals that water in. Wait even 5 minutes and the water evaporates, so the cream goes on dry skin and does far less. Teach families this timing on its own; it is the single most effective home step for mild-to-moderate eczema.
Eczema herpeticum is an emergency

A child with known eczema who develops painful clustered vesicles, fever, or rapidly spreading lesions may have eczema herpeticum. This is disseminated HSV on eczematous skin. It needs IV acyclovir at once. Near the eyes, get emergent ophthalmology to prevent corneal scarring and blindness. Continue the eczema care during and after treatment.

Bottom Line: Eczema is chronic. Moisturize daily, seal after the bath, steroid the flares, avoid triggers, protect the nails. Watch for Staph impetigo and eczema herpeticum.
05

Contact and diaper dermatitis

Discrimination axis: chronic and flexural, or exposure-patterned.

Contact dermatitis and atopic dermatitis both itch. The difference is the story. Atopic is chronic and follows the folds. Contact follows an exposure. The rash sits where the trigger touched.

Atopic dermatitis Chronic
  • Chronic and recurring, with a family history of allergy
  • Sits on the flexural folds in older children
  • The job is barrier repair: moisturize daily
Contact dermatitis Type IV
  • Tied to a single exposure, delayed by 24 to 72 hours
  • The shape matches the contact, like linear streaks
  • The job is trigger removal: find it and stop it
The axis that separates them

The pattern and the story. A chronic itch in the folds, with asthma or hay fever nearby, is atopic. An itch whose shape traces an exposure is contact. That difference changes the action. Atopic repairs the barrier. Contact removes the trigger.

The triggers and the rash

Allergic contact dermatitis Type IV

A cell-mediated, delayed Type IV reaction to an antigen. It appears 24 to 72 hours after exposure in a sensitized child.

Common triggers:

  • Poison ivy, oak, and sumac (urushiol oil), the classic linear streaks
  • Nickel (jewelry, belt buckles, snaps)
  • Latex (gloves, balloons, medical equipment)
  • Fragrances, dyes, cosmetics, preservatives
  • Topical medications (neomycin, bacitracin)

Appearance: very itchy red edematous papules and vesicles. The pattern matches the contact, linear in poison ivy or a ring under a watchband. Acute lesions weep; chronic ones go dry and lichenified.

Treatment: remove or avoid the offender and wash urushiol off fast. Cool compresses and Burow's (aluminum acetate) solution. Topical steroids at medium-to-high potency. Oral steroids for extensive or facial or genital involvement. Antihistamines for itch.

Irritant diaper dermatitis Common

Skin irritation from prolonged contact with urine and stool, plus friction and moisture. It is chemical irritation, not an allergic reaction.

Appearance: red, shiny skin on the convex surfaces (buttocks, tops of the thighs, pubic area). It spares the skin folds. The infant is tender and may cry with diaper changes.

Treatment:

  • Frequent changes, every 2 hours or whenever soiled
  • Air time with the diaper off for short periods
  • Zinc oxide or petrolatum barrier cream, applied generously each change
  • Gentle cleansing with water; avoid fragrance or alcohol wipes
  • If no improvement in 2 to 3 days, consider candidal superinfection and add nystatin or clotrimazole
Irritant or candida diaper rash

Irritant diaper dermatitis sits on the convex surfaces, spares the folds, and has no satellite lesions; barrier cream and frequent changes fix it. Candidal diaper dermatitis involves the skin folds, has satellite lesions, and is bright red; it needs nystatin or clotrimazole. The fold involvement is the differentiator.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Leaves of Three, Let Them Be
Urushiol oil penetrates skin within minutes. Washing with soap and water within 15 minutes can prevent the reaction entirely; after 30 minutes it is usually too late. The rash itself is not contagious, but the oil on clothing, shoes, and pet fur can transfer. Wash every contaminated item.
Bottom Line: Contact dermatitis follows the exposure; remove the trigger and add a topical steroid. Irritant diaper rash spares the folds; barrier cream fixes it. Candida hits the folds with satellites and needs an antifungal.
06

Urticaria and anaphylaxis

From hives to an airway emergency.

Urticaria is hives. Wheals rise, itch, and move. A single welt fades within 24 hours. New ones keep coming. That migration is the tell.

Hives on their own get an antihistamine. The moment a second body system joins in, you are in anaphylaxis, and the plan changes entirely.

Urticaria (hives) Type I

Appearance: raised, red, edematous, intensely itchy wheals. Size ranges from small papules to large plaques. Individual lesions come and go within 24 hours while new ones emerge. Angioedema (deeper swelling of the lips, face, hands) may join in.

Triggers: foods (peanuts, tree nuts, eggs, milk, shellfish, wheat, soy); medications (penicillins, sulfonamides, NSAIDs, vaccines); viral infection, the number one cause in children; insect stings and latex; physical cold, heat, pressure, and exercise.

Acute or chronic: acute is under 6 weeks and usually viral or a clear trigger. Chronic is 6 weeks or more and often idiopathic or autoimmune.

Treatment: an H1 antihistamine first, cetirizine or loratadine by day, diphenhydramine at night. An H2 antihistamine (famotidine) may be added for refractory cases. Oral steroids for severe or widespread hives. Identify the trigger; refer recurrent or chronic cases to allergy.

Anaphylaxis Emergency

An acute, life-threatening, IgE-mediated systemic reaction. It can start within minutes to 1 hour of exposure.

Any two systems:

  • Skin: hives, angioedema, flushing, itching
  • Respiratory: wheezing, stridor, dyspnea, cough, hoarseness, throat tightness
  • Cardiovascular: hypotension, tachycardia, syncope
  • GI: vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain
  • A sense of impending doom

Treatment, in order: IM epinephrine at once into the vastus lateralis, 0.15 mg if <30 kg (EpiPen Jr) or 0.3 mg if ≥30 kg (EpiPen), repeat every 5 to 15 minutes. Call 911 or the rapid response. Manage the airway, give oxygen, and position supine with the legs raised unless vomiting or dyspneic, then seated. IV fluids for hypotension. H1 and H2 antihistamines and corticosteroids are adjuncts, not first-line. Bronchodilators for wheezing. Observe 4 to 6 hours for a biphasic reaction.

Epinephrine is first, not antihistamines

In anaphylaxis, IM epinephrine is the only life-saving first-line treatment. Antihistamines and corticosteroids are adjuncts; they do not reverse airway or cardiovascular collapse. Delay in giving epinephrine is the leading cause of fatal anaphylaxis. Teach families with auto-injectors to give it if in doubt. Over-treating is not dangerous; under-treating is.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Biphasic Anaphylaxis, Watch for Round Two
Up to 20 percent of anaphylaxis cases have a biphasic reaction; symptoms return 4 to 12 hours after the first resolution, without re-exposure. That is why a child who responds beautifully to one dose still needs observation for 4 to 6 hours minimum. Always discharge with an EpiPen prescription and an action plan.
Itchy and moving, or painful and fixed

Hives itch, move, and fade within a day. Early SJS does the opposite. It is painful, it stays put, and it comes with mouth or eye sores and a new drug. When a rash hurts more than it itches, stop calling it hives and look at the next section.

Bottom Line: Hives alone get an H1 antihistamine. Hives plus respiratory, cardiovascular, or GI signs is anaphylaxis; give IM epinephrine into the vastus lateralis at once. Then watch 4 to 6 hours for biphasic.
07

Erythema multiforme, SJS, and TEN

A spectrum from mild to potentially fatal.

Erythema multiforme, SJS, and TEN sit on one spectrum. EM is usually benign. SJS and TEN are emergencies. The mucous membranes and the drug list tell you which one you are looking at.

Hives (urticaria) Benign
  • Itchy, not painful
  • Wheals move and fade within 24 hours
  • No mucosal erosion
  • An H1 antihistamine is the job
Early SJS Emergency
  • Painful, out of proportion to how it looks
  • Dusky, fixed lesions that spread
  • Mouth, eye, or genital erosions
  • A new drug started 1 to 8 weeks ago
The axis that separates them

Pain, mucosa, and a drug. Hives itch and move. Early SJS hurts, stays, and erodes the mucous membranes, and it follows a new medication. Any one of those three should pull you off the hives track.

Erythema multiforme Usually benign

Appearance: target or bull's-eye lesions, a central dusky zone with a paler ring and an outer red ring. They sit on the palms, soles, and extensor extremities.

Triggers: HSV, the most common, which can cause recurrent EM; Mycoplasma pneumoniae; and less often, medications.

Minor or major: minor is skin only, self-limiting, and resolves in 2 to 3 weeks. Major adds mucosal involvement, most often the mouth, and is more serious.

Treatment: supportive; treat the trigger with acyclovir if HSV. Antihistamines for itch, topical steroids, pain control, and mouth care for oral lesions.

SJS / TEN Emergency

Definitions by skin detachment:

  • SJS: <10% of body surface area
  • SJS and TEN overlap: 10 to 30%
  • TEN: >30%, with mortality of 30 to 50 percent

Presentation: a prodrome of fever, malaise, sore throat, and cough for 1 to 3 days. Then dusky red macules, purpuric spots, vesicles, and bullae that progress rapidly. Painful mucosal erosions of the mouth, eyes, and genitals. A positive Nikolsky sign. Skin pain out of proportion to the appearance.

Triggers: medications, above all anticonvulsants (carbamazepine, lamotrigine, phenytoin), allopurinol, sulfonamides, NSAIDs, and some antibiotics. Also Mycoplasma pneumoniae.

Treatment: stop the offending drug immediately. Admit to ICU or a burn unit. Replace IV fluid, correct electrolytes, and support nutrition. Give wound care like a burn with sterile handling and dressings. Involve ophthalmology, since eye involvement is common and can blind. Give oral and mouth care. IVIG is sometimes used; corticosteroids are controversial.

Always review the med list

If a child develops a widespread painful rash with mucosal involvement within 1 to 8 weeks of a new medication, think SJS. Common culprits are anticonvulsants (carbamazepine, lamotrigine, phenytoin), sulfonamide antibiotics, allopurinol, and NSAIDs. Stop the offending drug at once. Continuing it escalates toward TEN and death.

Nikolsky sign, the hands-on clue

Apply gentle lateral pressure to normal-looking skin near a lesion. If the epidermis sloughs off, the Nikolsky sign is positive, which strongly suggests SJS or TEN. Positive Nikolsky plus mucosal involvement plus a recent new medication is SJS until proven otherwise. Get an urgent dermatology consult.

Bottom Line: EM is target lesions, usually HSV, self-limiting. SJS and TEN are mucosal erosions plus a positive Nikolsky, often drug-induced; ICU or burn-unit care, and stop the drug now.
08

What you do first

Each condition has a first move. Match the move to the reaction. These are in priority order, from the airway emergency down to daily care.

  1. 1
    Anaphylaxis: IM epinephrine. Into the vastus lateralis. 0.15 mg if <30 kg, 0.3 mg if ≥30 kg. Repeat every 5 to 15 minutes. This comes before antihistamines and steroids.
  2. 2
    SJS or TEN: stop the drug. Stop the offending medication now. Admit to ICU or a burn unit. Replace fluids, correct electrolytes, and treat the skin like a burn.
  3. 3
    Eczema flare: targeted steroid. Apply a topical steroid to the red, itchy patches only. Low potency (hydrocortisone 1%) on the face, folds, and infants. Medium potency (triamcinolone 0.1%) on the body. BID for 1 to 2 weeks, then taper.
  4. 4
    Eczema daily: moisturize and protect. Apply a thick, fragrance-free emollient within 3 minutes of the bath. Reapply 2 to 3 times a day. Short baths and barrier care hold the skin together.
  5. 5
    Hives: H1 antihistamine. Cetirizine or loratadine by day. Diphenhydramine or hydroxyzine at night. Add an H2 antihistamine or oral steroids only when the hives are refractory or widespread.
  6. 6
    Contact dermatitis: remove the trigger. Find the exposure and stop it. Wash urushiol off fast. Add a topical steroid, or an oral steroid if it is widespread.
  7. 7
    Diaper dermatitis: barrier and air. Zinc oxide barrier cream with every change. Frequent changes and air time. Add an antifungal only if it is not better in 2 to 3 days.

Medications at a glance

Eczema daily care

Emollients and moisturizers. Fragrance-free and thick, petrolatum-based. Apply within 3 minutes of the bath and reapply 2 to 3 times a day.

CeraVe, Vanicream, Eucerin, plain petroleum jelly.

Eczema and contact

Topical corticosteroids. Low potency (hydrocortisone 1%) for the face, folds, and infants. Medium potency (triamcinolone 0.1%) for the body and limbs.

Apply BID during flares for 1 to 2 weeks; taper as the skin clears. Long-term use risks skin atrophy and striae.

Steroid-sparing

Topical calcineurin inhibitors. Tacrolimus (Protopic) or pimecrolimus (Elidel). Good for the face and folds where long-term steroids risk atrophy.

Mild burning for the first few days is common.

Urticaria and itch

H1 antihistamines. Non-sedating cetirizine (Zyrtec) or loratadine (Claritin) by day. Sedating diphenhydramine (Benadryl) or hydroxyzine (Atarax) for nighttime itch.

First-line for hives.

Severe inflammation

Oral corticosteroids. Prednisone or prednisolone for severe contact dermatitis, a widespread eczema flare, or severe urticaria.

A short course of 5 to 7 days; taper if longer. Watch for mood changes, blood glucose elevation, and GI upset.

Anaphylaxis, first-line

Epinephrine (IM). Into the vastus lateralis. 0.15 mg if <30 kg (EpiPen Jr); 0.3 mg if ≥30 kg (EpiPen).

Repeat every 5 to 15 minutes if needed. Always first; antihistamines second.

Diaper rash

Barrier creams (zinc oxide). Desitin, Triple Paste, A and D ointment.

Apply generously with each change to protect the skin from urine and stool.

Refractory eczema

Dupilumab (biologic). An IL-4 and IL-13 inhibitor, injected every 2 weeks, for moderate-to-severe atopic dermatitis refractory to topicals.

Approved down to age 6 months.

HSV-triggered EM, eczema herpeticum

Acyclovir. Oral for recurrent EM from HSV; IV for eczema herpeticum.

Keep the child hydrated to prevent nephrotoxicity.

Bottom Line: Moisturize plus a steroid for eczema. An antihistamine for hives. Epinephrine first for anaphylaxis. Stop the drug for SJS and TEN.
09

Family education and prevention

The daily habits and the emergency response.

Most of this care happens at home. Teach the routine, then teach the red flags. The family runs both.

Eczema daily care

  1. Bath daily for 5 to 10 minutes in lukewarm water with a mild, fragrance-free cleanser.
  2. Pat dry, do not rub, and moisturize within 3 minutes.
  3. Moisturize thickly, 2 to 3 times a day, with plain petrolatum or a fragrance-free cream.
  4. Use a topical steroid on active red or itchy patches only, BID during a flare; stop when clear.
  5. Keep fingernails short; use cotton gloves or mittens at night to stop scratching.
  6. Identify and avoid triggers: wool, synthetics, fragrances, heat, stress, and known allergens.
  7. Call the provider for honey-colored crusts, fever, painful clustered vesicles, or a spreading red area.

Allergy avoidance and auto-injector use

Know the allergen. Read food labels, learn the ingredients, and avoid it at school and parties.

Carry two EpiPens at all times. One may fail, and a biphasic reaction may need a second dose.

Practice with a trainer. Teach every caregiver, including daycare and school staff.

Medical alert bracelet. It names the allergy in an emergency.

Action plan. Keep a written plan at school and review it every year.

If in doubt, give the EpiPen. Over-treating anaphylaxis is not dangerous; under-treating is.

When to call or go to the ED

Throat tightness, wheezing, stridor. Use the EpiPen and call 911.

Facial or lip swelling, difficulty breathing after a medication or food.

Widespread rash with mucosal erosions (mouth sores, eye redness); rule out SJS.

Skin sloughs with pressure or large blisters forming.

Painful clustered vesicles on eczematous skin; possible eczema herpeticum.

Honey-colored crusts, fever, warmth, spreading redness; possible Staph infection.

Normalize the skin journey

Children with chronic skin conditions often feel self-conscious and worn down. Acknowledge the toll; not just the itch, but the stares, the comments, and the missed sleep. Reassure families that many children outgrow atopic dermatitis by adolescence or adulthood, and that daily care prevents most flares. Let older children own their skin routine; self-efficacy is part of the treatment.

Bottom Line: Moisturize daily and steroid the flares. Carry two EpiPens. Know the red flags: anaphylaxis, SJS, eczema herpeticum. Empower the child.
