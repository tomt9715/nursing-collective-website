Hypertension

01

What actually gets you

Hypertension has almost no symptoms, so there is nothing to picture and nothing to recognise. The whole topic is numbers, thresholds and drug side effects. That is why it feels boring and then costs you points.

Points get lost here in four predictable ways.

You staged it off the wrong number

A pressure of 145/82 is Stage 2, not Stage 1. The systolic is in Stage 2, and the higher category always wins.

Half the classification questions are built on exactly this, with the two numbers deliberately in different stages.

You saw 190/120 and reached for the fastest drug

A number over 180/120 does not tell you what to do. The next question does: is there organ damage right now.

Damage means emergency, ICU and IV drugs. No damage means urgency, oral drugs, and days rather than minutes.

You thought fast was safe

Dropping a chronically high pressure quickly causes strokes. The brain has spent years adapting its blood flow to that pressure, and it cannot readapt in ten minutes.

No more than 25 percent off the mean arterial pressure in the first hour. There is one exception, and it is aortic dissection.

You learned the drugs as a list of names

Nobody asks what lisinopril is. They ask what you do when the patient develops a dry cough, or is pregnant, or has a potassium of 5.7.

Learn each class by what it costs the patient, not by what it does.

Bottom Line: Study this as four discriminations: which number stages it, primary or secondary, urgency or emergency, and which drug for which comorbidity.
02

The four things

For each point on the hypertension spectrum, you should be able to say four things with the book closed: the one-sentence definition, the clue you would see in a patient, the priority action, and the first-line treatment with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Category Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line treatment
Elevated Systolic 120 to 129 with a diastolic under 80. A number that is drifting, in a patient who feels fine. Teach, and recheck. This is the window before disease. Lifestyle only. No drug yet.
Stage 1 130 to 139 systolic, or 80 to 89 diastolic. Two readings in this range on two separate occasions. Assess cardiovascular risk; it decides whether a drug starts now. Lifestyle alone if risk is low. Add a drug if risk is high.
Stage 2 140 systolic or higher, or 90 diastolic or higher. Either number reaching the threshold, not both. Start medication and lifestyle together, from day one. Thiazide, ACE inhibitor, ARB or calcium channel blocker.
Hypertensive urgency Over 180/120 with no acute organ damage. A very high number and a patient who is otherwise well. Find out what changed. Non-adherence is the usual answer. Oral drugs, lowered over 24 to 48 hours. Follow-up in 24 to 72.
Hypertensive emergency Over 180/120 with acute organ damage happening now. Confusion, chest pain, vision change, seizure, low urine output. ICU. Continuous monitoring, IV drugs, controlled reduction. IV nicardipine, labetalol or nitroprusside. Drop the MAP by 25 percent at most in hour one.
Copy this down

Five rows on one index card, by hand. 120, 130, 140, 180. Four numbers mark every boundary in this topic. Then one line underneath: organ damage is what separates urgency from emergency. That card is most of the exam.

03

What the number is made of

Blood pressure is the force of blood against the arterial wall. Two things set it: how much blood the heart pushes out, and how hard the vessels resist it.

Blood pressure equals cardiac output times systemic vascular resistance. That equation is not trivia. Every antihypertensive drug attacks one side of it, and once you know which side, you never have to memorise a mechanism again.

TermWhat it isWhy it matters
Systolic pressurePressure while the ventricle contractsReflects cardiac output and how stiff the arteries are
Diastolic pressurePressure while the ventricle relaxesReflects peripheral vascular resistance
Pulse pressureSystolic minus diastolicNormal 30 to 40 mmHg. A wide one means stiff arteries
Mean arterial pressureDiastolic plus one third of the pulse pressureNormal 70 to 105 mmHg. This is the number you titrate against in a crisis

What pushes it up

  • Cardiac output: heart rate and stroke volume.
  • Blood volume: sodium and fluid retention add volume.
  • Peripheral resistance: vasoconstriction narrows the pipe.
  • Blood viscosity: thicker blood is harder to move.
  • Arterial elasticity: stiff arteries drive the systolic up.
The equation tells you the drug

Beta-blockers lower cardiac output; slower rate, weaker squeeze. Diuretics lower volume, which lowers output too. ACE inhibitors, ARBs, calcium channel blockers and vasodilators lower resistance.

So when one drug is not controlling a pressure, the next question is which side of the equation is untreated. That is how combination therapy gets chosen.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
BP = CO x SVR
Two levers, and every drug pulls one of them. Beta-blockers and diuretics lower cardiac output. ACE inhibitors, ARBs, calcium channel blockers and vasodilators lower resistance. When a pressure will not come down, ask which lever has not been pulled yet.
Bottom Line: Blood pressure is output times resistance. Every drug lowers one or the other, and that is the whole pharmacology of this topic.

The rule that decides the stage
  • When the two numbers land in different categories, the higher category wins.
  • 145/82 is Stage 2. The systolic decided it, and the diastolic is irrelevant.
  • Diagnosis needs two or more readings, on two or more separate occasions.
  • Average at least two readings taken a minute or more apart.

The last two rules are why one high reading in a clinic is never the answer to a diagnosis question.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Classify by the Higher Number
When systolic and diastolic sit in different stages, the worse one names the stage. 152/84 is Stage 2 because 152 is Stage 2, even though 84 is only Stage 1. The reasoning is safety: treat to the greater risk.

Taking the pressure properly

Every classification question assumes the reading was accurate. In practice it often is not, and that assumption is what the measurement questions test.

  1. 1
    Prepare the patient. Seated and resting for 5 minutes. Bladder empty. No caffeine, exercise or smoking for 30 minutes beforehand. No talking during the reading.
  2. 2
    Position them. Back supported, feet flat on the floor and not crossed. Arm supported at heart level on a flat surface, with the sleeve out of the way.
  3. 3
    Choose the cuff. The bladder should wrap 80 percent of the arm. Too small reads falsely high. Too large reads falsely low.
  4. 4
    Take more than one. At least two readings, 1 to 2 minutes apart, and record the average. If they differ by over 10 mmHg, take more.
How a reading goes wrong
  • Cuff too small. The most common error there is, and it reads high.
  • Arm below heart level. Reads high.
  • Arm above heart level. Reads low.
  • White coat hypertension. High in the clinic only. Confirm with home monitoring.
  • Masked hypertension. Normal in the clinic, high at home. The dangerous one, because nobody treats it.

Notice the pattern. Anything that squeezes harder or sits lower reads high.

Write this down

Blank paper. Write the five categories and their exact numbers. Then write three readings of your own invention where the systolic and diastolic disagree, and stage each one.

Bottom Line: 120, 130, 140, 180 are the boundaries. The higher number names the stage. Diagnosis needs two readings on two occasions.
05

Primary or secondary

Discrimination axis: is there a cause you could remove.

Almost everyone has primary hypertension, which has no single cause and never goes away. A small group has secondary hypertension, where something identifiable is driving it, and treating that thing can cure the pressure.

The exam cares about this because the presentation gives it away.

Primary, also called essential
  • 90 to 95 percent of all hypertension
  • No identifiable cause
  • Builds gradually over years
  • Many contributing factors, none sufficient alone
  • Genetics play a part
  • Managed for life, not cured

Risk factors: age, family history, higher prevalence in Black populations, obesity, inactivity, high sodium and low potassium intake, heavy alcohol, tobacco, chronic stress.

Secondary
  • 5 to 10 percent of cases
  • An identifiable underlying cause
  • Often sudden in onset
  • Often resistant to treatment
  • Potentially curable if the cause is fixed
  • Suspect it before age 30 or after 55

Causes: renal artery stenosis and chronic kidney disease; pheochromocytoma, Cushing's, hyperaldosteronism and thyroid disorders; coarctation of the aorta; NSAIDs, steroids, oral contraceptives and decongestants; obstructive sleep apnea.

The axis that separates them

Primary is slow, expected and stubborn. Secondary is fast, unexpected, or refuses to respond.

Four red flags say secondary:

  • New hypertension in someone under 30, or over 55
  • Sudden severe hypertension in someone whose pressure was fine
  • Resistant to three or more drugs at proper doses
  • Unexplained low potassium, with no diuretic to blame it on

That last one points at hyperaldosteronism. Episodic headache, sweating and palpitations with pressure spikes points at pheochromocytoma.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Secondary HTN Red Flags
Young, sudden, resistant, or hypokalemic without a diuretic. Any one of those four should make you look for a cause instead of adding a fifth drug. Finding it can cure the hypertension outright, which is not true of the other 90 percent.
Bottom Line: Primary is 90 to 95 percent, slow and lifelong. Suspect secondary when it is young, sudden, resistant, or comes with unexplained hypokalemia.
06

What the pressure breaks

Hypertension does not hurt. It damages vessels, quietly, everywhere, for years. Every complication in this section is the same injury in a different organ.

Knowing which organ shows which finding is what lets you spot an emergency later in the guide.

OrganWhat goes wrongWhat you would find
BrainIschemic and hemorrhagic stroke, TIA, vascular dementia, hypertensive encephalopathyNeuro deficits, altered consciousness, cognitive change, headache
EyesHypertensive retinopathy, vision loss, papilledemaOn fundoscopy: AV nicking, copper or silver wiring, hemorrhages, exudates
HeartLeft ventricular hypertrophy, heart failure, coronary disease, MI, arrhythmiasS4 gallop, displaced apical impulse, chest pain, dyspnea, ECG changes
KidneysNephrosclerosis, chronic kidney disease, proteinuria, renal failureRising creatinine and BUN, falling GFR, protein in the urine, edema
VesselsAtherosclerosis, aortic dissection, peripheral arterial disease, aneurysmWeak pulses, bruits, claudication, cool extremities
BREAK

Five organs, one word.

  • Brain: stroke and TIA
  • Retina: hypertensive retinopathy
  • Extremities and vessels: peripheral arterial disease, aneurysm
  • Aorta and heart: hypertrophy, heart failure, MI
  • Kidneys: nephrosclerosis, chronic kidney disease
Why it is called the silent killer

There are no symptoms until something is already damaged. A patient feels exactly the same at 190/110 as at 120/70.

That is not a piece of trivia; it is the reason adherence collapses. A patient who feels fine, and feels worse on the medication, has every reason to stop taking it. Teaching that gap is the nursing intervention.

Bottom Line: Brain, retina, extremities, heart, kidneys. No symptoms until one of them is damaged, which is why screening and adherence carry the whole topic.
07

Assessment, in order

You are doing two things at once here. Establishing a baseline, and hunting for organ damage that would change the urgency of everything else.

  1. 1
    Pressure in both arms. The first time, do both. A difference over 10 to 15 mmHg suggests vascular disease. Use the higher arm from then on.
  2. 2
    Orthostatics. Lying, sitting, standing, with 1 to 3 minutes between positions. A drop of 20 systolic or 10 diastolic on standing is orthostatic hypotension, and it is often a drug effect.
  3. 3
    Cardiovascular. Heart sounds, listening for an S4 that suggests hypertrophy. Carotid bruits, peripheral pulses, edema, JVD. Ask about chest pain and dyspnea.
  4. 4
    Neurological. Level of consciousness, orientation, speech, motor and sensory function. Headache, visual change and dizziness matter here; they can mean encephalopathy or stroke.
  5. 5
    Renal. Intake and output, daily weights, edema. Review BUN, creatinine, GFR and the urinalysis for protein. Falling urine output is an organ-damage finding.
  6. 6
    Medications and lifestyle. Ask about NSAIDs, decongestants and steroids, because all three raise pressure. Then diet, alcohol, exercise, smoking, and what is actually stopping them taking the tablets.
Step 6 is the one that gets skipped

A patient whose pressure suddenly stopped responding is far more likely to have started ibuprofen than to have developed a new disease.

Ask about over-the-counter drugs specifically. Patients do not think of them as medications, so they do not mention them.

The tests, and what each is for

TestWhy it is orderedWhat you are looking for
Basic metabolic panelKidney function and electrolytesRising BUN and creatinine mean kidney damage. Potassium guides the drug choice
UrinalysisScreening the kidneysProtein or microalbumin means nephropathy has started
Lipid panelCardiovascular riskHigh LDL adds atherosclerosis to the picture

Bottom Line: Both arms first, then use the higher one. Orthostatic drop is 20 systolic or 10 diastolic. Always ask about over-the-counter drugs.
08

What lowers it without a drug

Lifestyle change is not the soft option here. Combined, these interventions lower systolic pressure by more than 20 mmHg, which is more than most single drugs manage.

They are recommended for every patient, at every stage. For elevated pressure and low-risk Stage 1, they are the whole treatment for the first 3 to 6 months.

ChangeWhat you actually ask forSystolic drop
DASH dietFruit, vegetables, whole grains, low-fat dairy; less saturated fatAbout 11 mmHg
Sodium restrictionUnder 2,300 mg a day; under 1,500 mg is better5 to 6 mmHg
Potassium intake3,500 to 5,000 mg a day from food, unless contraindicated4 to 5 mmHg
Weight lossReach and hold a BMI of 18.5 to 24.9About 1 mmHg per kilogram lost
Physical activity90 to 150 minutes of aerobic exercise a week, plus resistance training three times5 to 8 mmHg
Alcohol moderationTwo drinks a day for men, one for womenAbout 4 mmHg
Stopping smokingComplete cessationCuts cardiovascular risk sharply, though not the pressure directly
What DASH actually means day to day
  • 4 to 5 servings of fruit daily
  • 4 to 5 servings of vegetables daily
  • 6 to 8 servings of whole grains daily
  • 2 to 3 servings of low-fat dairy daily
  • Nuts, seeds and legumes included
  • Red meat, sweets and saturated fat limited
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Lifestyle First
These changes are additive, so the numbers stack. DASH alone is worth about 11 mmHg. For newly diagnosed Stage 1 without high cardiovascular risk, lifestyle alone for 3 to 6 months is the expected first answer, not a drug.

Teaching sodium reduction

Most dietary sodium is not from the salt shaker. It is already in the food when the patient buys it, which is why label reading matters more than table salt.

Four things to teach

Read the labels
Sodium per serving, and how many servings
Skip processed food
Canned goods, frozen meals, deli meats
Cook at home
You control what goes in
Season differently
Herbs and spices instead of salt
Bottom Line: Combined lifestyle change is worth over 20 mmHg systolic. For low-risk Stage 1, it is the first treatment, for 3 to 6 months.
09

The drugs, and what each one costs

You will not be asked what a drug does. You will be asked what you do when it causes something.

So learn each class by its cost: the side effect, the lab, the population who cannot have it.

First line

ClassExamplesWhat it lowersWhat it costs you
Thiazide diuretics Hydrochlorothiazide, chlorthalidone Volume, by excreting sodium and water Potassium falls. Watch sodium and glucose too. Give in the morning. Can raise glucose and uric acid
ACE inhibitors Lisinopril, enalapril, ramipril; the -prils Resistance, by blocking angiotensin II and aldosterone Dry cough in 10 to 20 percent. Potassium rises. Angioedema. Never in pregnancy
ARBs Losartan, valsartan, irbesartan; the -sartans Resistance, by blocking the angiotensin II receptor The answer when the ACE cough will not settle. Same potassium watch. Never in pregnancy
Calcium channel blockers Amlodipine and nifedipine; diltiazem and verapamil Resistance, by relaxing vascular smooth muscle Peripheral edema and constipation. Diltiazem and verapamil also slow the heart, so avoid pairing them with a beta-blocker

Second line and add-ons

ClassExamplesWhat you need to know
Beta-blockersMetoprolol, atenolol, carvedilol; the -ololsNot first line for hypertension alone. Preferred alongside heart failure, post-MI, or tachyarrhythmias. Never stop abruptly
Aldosterone antagonistsSpironolactone, eplerenoneUseful in resistant hypertension. Potassium rises. Spironolactone can cause gynecomastia
Alpha-1 blockersPrazosin, doxazosin, terazosinFirst-dose hypotension, so give the first dose at bedtime. Also treats benign prostatic hyperplasia
Direct vasodilatorsHydralazine, minoxidilFor resistant hypertension. Reflex tachycardia. Hydralazine can cause a lupus-like syndrome
Central alpha-2 agonistsClonidineWhen other agents fail. Rebound hypertension if stopped abruptly. A patch helps adherence
The ACE and ARB rules
  • Contraindicated in pregnancy. Both classes. They are teratogenic
  • Potassium rises. Hold above 5.5 mEq/L
  • Renal function can worsen, especially with bilateral renal artery stenosis
  • Angioedema is more common with ACE inhibitors. If it happens, stop the drug immediately
  • Never combine an ACE inhibitor with an ARB. More harm, no more benefit
Cough or angioedema

Both come from an ACE inhibitor, and both come from bradykinin. They are not the same problem.

A dry cough is a nuisance; you switch the patient to an ARB and everyone is happy. Angioedema is airway swelling; you stop the drug now and do not switch classes casually. Cough is annoying, angioedema is an emergency.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The ACE Inhibitor Cough
Dry, non-productive, and caused by bradykinin building up. It can take weeks to appear and weeks to fade after stopping. The answer is switching to an ARB, which gives the same blood pressure and kidney protection without the cough. Adding a cough suppressant is the wrong answer.

Which drug for which patient

When a question gives you a comorbidity alongside the hypertension, the comorbidity is choosing the drug.

ComorbidityPreferred classWhy

After an MIACE inhibitor and beta-blockerLimits remodelling and lowers mortality
Diabetes with proteinuriaACE inhibitor or ARBProtects the kidney
Chronic kidney diseaseACE inhibitor or ARBSlows the progression
Stroke preventionACE inhibitor and a thiazideShown to cut recurrence

Bottom Line: Thiazides, ACE inhibitors, ARBs and calcium channel blockers are first line, and the comorbidity picks between them. Never combine an ACE inhibitor with an ARB, and never give either in pregnancy.
10

Urgency or emergency

Discrimination axis: is an organ being damaged right now.

Over 180/120 is a hypertensive crisis. The number does not tell you what to do next; the organs do.

This is the highest-yield discrimination in the guide, and the two answers are completely different.

Emergency: organ damage now
  • Over 180/120 with acute target organ damage
  • Immediately life-threatening
  • Needs ICU admission

What damage looks like: encephalopathy with confusion, seizures or visual change; acute MI or pulmonary edema; stroke or intracranial hemorrhage; acute renal failure; eclampsia; aortic dissection.

Treatment: IV drugs, titrated. Nicardipine, labetalol, nitroprusside. Drop the MAP by no more than 25 percent in the first hour, then gradually across 24 to 48 hours.

Urgency: high number, intact organs
  • Over 180/120 with no acute organ damage
  • Serious, but not immediately life-threatening
  • Usually managed outside hospital

Usual causes: stopped taking the medication, ran out, was never on enough, high sodium intake, anxiety or pain.

Treatment: oral drugs, lowered over 24 to 48 hours. Restart or adjust the regular medication. Follow-up within 24 to 72 hours.

The axis that separates them

Not the number. Both are over 180/120. The question is whether an organ is failing as you watch.

So the assessment comes before the treatment. Neuro check, chest pain, vision, urine output. Those four answers decide ICU or clinic.

Why you do not drop it fast

Organs autoregulate. After months at a high pressure, the brain, heart and kidneys have reset their range and now need that pressure to perfuse.

Bring it down too quickly and they are suddenly underperfused. That causes ischemic stroke, MI or renal failure, from treatment rather than from disease.

  • Emergency: reduce the MAP by no more than 25 percent in the first hour
  • Urgency: lower it over 24 to 48 hours
  • The exception: aortic dissection needs the systolic under 120 within 20 minutes, because the tear extends with every beat
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Slow and Steady
Organs adapted to the high pressure and now depend on it. Cut the MAP by more than 25 percent in the first hour and you can cause the stroke you were preventing. The single exception is aortic dissection, where systolic under 120 within 20 minutes is the goal.
