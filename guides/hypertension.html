Understanding Blood Pressure

Blood pressure (BP) is the force of blood pushing against arterial walls. It is expressed as systolic over diastolic pressure (e.g., 120/80 mmHg). Understanding the physiology behind blood pressure is essential for managing hypertension effectively.

Components of Blood Pressure

Component Definition Clinical Significance
Systolic Pressure (SBP) Pressure during ventricular contraction Reflects cardiac output and arterial stiffness
Diastolic Pressure (DBP) Pressure during ventricular relaxation Reflects peripheral vascular resistance
Pulse Pressure Difference between SBP and DBP Normal: 30-40 mmHg; widened indicates stiff arteries
Mean Arterial Pressure (MAP) Average pressure during cardiac cycle MAP = DBP + 1/3(Pulse Pressure); Normal: 70-105 mmHg

Factors Affecting Blood Pressure

  • Cardiac Output: Heart rate and stroke volume
  • Blood Volume: Sodium and fluid retention increase BP
  • Peripheral Resistance: Vasoconstriction increases BP
  • Blood Viscosity: Thicker blood increases resistance
  • Arterial Elasticity: Stiff arteries increase systolic BP
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
BP = CO x SVR
This equation is the key to understanding antihypertensive medications! Blood Pressure equals Cardiac Output times Systemic Vascular Resistance. Beta-blockers lower BP by decreasing CO (slower heart rate, weaker contractions). Vasodilators, ACE inhibitors, and calcium channel blockers lower BP by decreasing SVR (relaxing blood vessels). Diuretics work by decreasing blood volume, which reduces CO. When a patient's BP isn't controlled, think about which factor needs to be targeted next.

BP Classification & Staging

The American College of Cardiology (ACC) and American Heart Association (AHA) updated hypertension guidelines in 2017, lowering the threshold for hypertension diagnosis. This classification is essential for NCLEX and clinical practice.

2017 ACC/AHA Blood Pressure Categories

Normal
<120/80
Maintain healthy lifestyle habits
Elevated
120-129/<80
Lifestyle modifications recommended
Stage 1 HTN
130-139/80-89
Lifestyle changes; medication if high CV risk
Stage 2 HTN
≥140/90
Lifestyle changes PLUS medication therapy
Hypertensive Crisis
>180/120
Immediate medical attention required - assess for target organ damage
NCLEX Tip: Classification Rules
  • If systolic and diastolic fall into different categories, classify by the HIGHER category
  • Example: 145/82 = Stage 2 (even though diastolic is Stage 1)
  • Diagnosis requires two or more readings on two or more occasions
  • Use the average of 2+ readings taken at least 1 minute apart
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Classify by Higher Number
When in doubt, go with the worse category! If a patient's systolic and diastolic pressures fall into different stages, always classify them based on the higher (more severe) category. For example, 152/84 mmHg would be Stage 2 hypertension because the systolic is 152 (Stage 2), even though the diastolic of 84 is only Stage 1. This ensures patients receive appropriately aggressive treatment based on their greatest risk factor.

Proper Blood Pressure Measurement Technique

Steps for Accurate BP Measurement

1
Prepare the Patient

Rest for 5 minutes in a seated position. Empty bladder. No caffeine, exercise, or smoking for 30 minutes prior. Avoid talking during measurement.

2
Proper Positioning

Seated with back supported, feet flat on floor (not crossed). Arm supported at heart level on a flat surface. Remove clothing covering the arm.

3
Select Correct Cuff Size

Bladder should encircle 80% of arm circumference. Cuff too small = falsely HIGH reading. Cuff too large = falsely LOW reading.

4
Take Multiple Readings

Take at least 2 readings, 1-2 minutes apart. Record the average. If readings differ by more than 10 mmHg, take additional readings.

Common Measurement Errors
  • Wrong cuff size: Most common error! Using a small cuff on a large arm gives falsely elevated readings
  • Arm below heart level: Gives falsely HIGH reading
  • Arm above heart level: Gives falsely LOW reading
  • White coat hypertension: Elevated BP only in clinical settings (confirm with home monitoring)
  • Masked hypertension: Normal BP in office but elevated at home

Primary vs Secondary Hypertension

Understanding the difference between primary and secondary hypertension is crucial because it determines the treatment approach. Most patients have primary hypertension, but identifying secondary causes can lead to potentially curable conditions.

Primary (Essential) HTN

Characteristics

  • 90-95% of all hypertension cases
  • No identifiable cause
  • Develops gradually over years
  • Multifactorial etiology
  • Genetic predisposition plays a role
  • Requires lifelong management

Risk Factors

  • Age (risk increases with age)
  • Family history
  • Race (higher in Black populations)
  • Obesity, sedentary lifestyle
  • High sodium, low potassium diet
  • Excessive alcohol, tobacco use
  • Chronic stress
Secondary HTN

Characteristics

  • 5-10% of all hypertension cases
  • Identifiable underlying cause
  • Often has sudden onset
  • May be resistant to treatment
  • Potentially curable if cause is treated
  • Suspect if onset before age 30 or after 55

Common Causes

  • Renal: Renal artery stenosis, CKD
  • Endocrine: Pheochromocytoma, Cushing's, Hyperaldosteronism, Thyroid disorders
  • Vascular: Coarctation of aorta
  • Medications: NSAIDs, steroids, OCPs, decongestants
  • Sleep apnea: Obstructive sleep apnea
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Secondary HTN Red Flags
Think secondary hypertension when the presentation seems "off"! Key red flags include: hypertension in a young, healthy patient under 30; sudden onset of severe HTN in a previously normotensive patient; HTN resistant to 3+ medications; and unexplained hypokalemia (low potassium without diuretic use suggests hyperaldosteronism). A patient describing episodic severe headaches, sweating, and palpitations with BP spikes might have a pheochromocytoma - a tumor that secretes catecholamines. Finding and treating the underlying cause can sometimes cure the hypertension!

Target Organ Damage

Uncontrolled hypertension damages blood vessels throughout the body, leading to serious complications in multiple organ systems. Understanding these complications helps communicate the importance of BP control to patients.

End-Organ Effects of Chronic Hypertension

Target Organ Complications Assessment Findings
Brain Stroke (ischemic/hemorrhagic), TIA, Vascular dementia, Hypertensive encephalopathy Neuro deficits, altered LOC, cognitive changes, headache
Eyes Hypertensive retinopathy, Vision loss, Papilledema Fundoscopic exam: AV nicking, copper/silver wiring, hemorrhages, exudates
Heart Left ventricular hypertrophy (LVH), Heart failure, CAD, MI, Arrhythmias S4 gallop, displaced PMI, chest pain, dyspnea, ECG changes
Kidneys Nephrosclerosis, Chronic kidney disease, Proteinuria, Renal failure Elevated creatinine/BUN, decreased GFR, proteinuria, edema
Blood Vessels Atherosclerosis, Aortic dissection, Peripheral arterial disease, Aneurysm Diminished pulses, bruits, claudication, cool extremities
Memory Aid: Target Organs

HTN damages "BREAK" organs:

  • Brain (stroke, TIA)
  • Retina (hypertensive retinopathy)
  • Extremities/vessels (PAD, aneurysm)
  • Aorta/heart (LVH, HF, MI)
  • Kidneys (nephrosclerosis, CKD)
The "Silent Killer"

Hypertension is often called the "silent killer" because it usually has NO SYMPTOMS until target organ damage occurs. This is why regular BP screening and patient education about the importance of treatment adherence are critical nursing responsibilities.

Nursing Assessment

A thorough nursing assessment helps identify hypertension, evaluate for target organ damage, and establish baseline data for monitoring treatment effectiveness.

Comprehensive Hypertension Assessment

Key Assessment Components

1
Blood Pressure in Both Arms

Check BP in both arms initially. A difference of more than 10-15 mmHg between arms may indicate vascular disease. Use the arm with the higher reading for future measurements.

2
Orthostatic Vitals

Measure BP and HR lying, sitting, and standing. Wait 1-3 minutes between position changes. Orthostatic hypotension: drop of 20+ mmHg systolic or 10+ mmHg diastolic upon standing.

3
Cardiovascular Assessment

Auscultate heart sounds (S4 suggests LVH). Check for carotid bruits, peripheral pulses, and edema. Assess for JVD. Note any chest pain or dyspnea.

4
Neurological Assessment

Assess LOC, orientation, speech, motor/sensory function. Check for headache, visual changes, or dizziness. These may indicate hypertensive encephalopathy or stroke.

5
Renal Assessment

Monitor I&O, daily weights, and edema. Review BUN, creatinine, GFR, and urinalysis for proteinuria. Decreased urine output may indicate renal impairment.

6
Medication and Lifestyle History

Review current medications (especially NSAIDs, decongestants, steroids). Assess diet (sodium, alcohol), exercise, smoking, and stress. Evaluate adherence barriers.

Diagnostic Studies for Hypertension

Test Purpose Key Findings
Basic Metabolic Panel (BMP) Assess renal function, electrolytes Elevated BUN/Cr indicates kidney damage; K+ levels for med monitoring
Urinalysis Screen for kidney damage Proteinuria, microalbuminuria indicate nephropathy
Lipid Panel Assess cardiovascular risk Elevated LDL increases atherosclerosis risk
Fasting Glucose/HbA1c Screen for diabetes DM is a major cardiovascular risk factor
ECG Assess cardiac effects LVH patterns, arrhythmias, prior MI
Echocardiogram Evaluate heart structure/function LVH, wall motion abnormalities, EF

Lifestyle Modifications

Lifestyle modifications are the foundation of hypertension management. They are recommended for ALL patients with elevated BP or hypertension, either alone (elevated/Stage 1) or in combination with medications (Stage 1-2). These changes can significantly reduce BP and cardiovascular risk.

Non-Pharmacological Interventions

Modification Recommendation Expected BP Reduction
DASH Diet Rich in fruits, vegetables, whole grains, low-fat dairy; reduced saturated fat ~11 mmHg SBP
Sodium Restriction Less than 2,300 mg/day; ideally less than 1,500 mg/day 5-6 mmHg SBP
Potassium Intake 3,500-5,000 mg/day from food sources (unless contraindicated) 4-5 mmHg SBP
Weight Loss Achieve/maintain BMI 18.5-24.9 kg/m² ~1 mmHg per 1 kg lost
Physical Activity 90-150 min/week of aerobic exercise; 3 sessions/week resistance training 5-8 mmHg SBP
Alcohol Moderation Men: ≤2 drinks/day; Women: ≤1 drink/day ~4 mmHg SBP
Smoking Cessation Complete cessation of tobacco use Reduces overall CV risk (not direct BP effect)
The DASH Diet

Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension

  • 4-5 servings of fruits daily
  • 4-5 servings of vegetables daily
  • 6-8 servings of whole grains daily
  • 2-3 servings of low-fat dairy daily
  • Limit red meat, sweets, and saturated fat
  • Include nuts, seeds, and legumes
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Lifestyle First, Always
Lifestyle modifications are powerful medicine! Combined lifestyle changes can lower systolic BP by 20+ mmHg - that's as much as some medications! For patients with elevated BP or Stage 1 HTN without high CV risk, 3-6 months of lifestyle modifications alone may be tried before starting medications. For NCLEX, if a question asks about initial management for newly diagnosed Stage 1 HTN without complications, lifestyle modifications are the answer. The DASH diet alone can reduce SBP by about 11 mmHg. Teach patients that these changes are additive - the more changes they make, the greater the BP reduction.

Patient Education Tips

Teaching Points for Sodium Reduction

Read Labels
Check sodium content per serving
Avoid Processed Foods
Canned, frozen meals, deli meats
Cook at Home
Control ingredients and portions
Use Herbs/Spices

Pharmacological Management

When lifestyle modifications alone are insufficient, antihypertensive medications are prescribed. The choice of medication depends on the patient's BP level, comorbidities, and potential side effects. Understanding these drug classes is essential for safe medication administration.

First-Line Antihypertensive Medications

Drug Class Examples Mechanism Key Nursing Considerations
Thiazide Diuretics Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ), Chlorthalidone Decrease blood volume by increasing Na+ and water excretion Monitor K+, Na+, glucose. Give in AM. May cause hyperglycemia, hyperuricemia.
ACE Inhibitors Lisinopril, Enalapril, Ramipril (-pril) Block conversion of angiotensin I to II; decrease SVR and aldosterone Dry cough (10-20%), hyperkalemia, angioedema risk. Avoid in pregnancy!
ARBs Losartan, Valsartan, Irbesartan (-sartan) Block angiotensin II receptors; similar effects to ACE-I Alternative if ACE-I cough. Same K+ monitoring. Avoid in pregnancy!
Calcium Channel Blockers Amlodipine, Nifedipine (DHP); Diltiazem, Verapamil (non-DHP) Relax vascular smooth muscle (vasodilation) Peripheral edema, constipation. Non-DHPs also slow HR (avoid with beta-blockers).

Second-Line & Add-On Medications

Drug Class Examples Key Points
Beta-Blockers Metoprolol, Atenolol, Carvedilol (-olol) Not first-line for HTN alone; preferred if concurrent HF, post-MI, tachyarrhythmias. Don't stop abruptly!
Aldosterone Antagonists Spironolactone, Eplerenone Good for resistant HTN. Risk of hyperkalemia. Spironolactone may cause gynecomastia.
Alpha-1 Blockers Prazosin, Doxazosin, Terazosin First-dose hypotension risk - give at bedtime. Also used for BPH.
Direct Vasodilators Hydralazine, Minoxidil Used for resistant HTN. May cause reflex tachycardia. Hydralazine can cause lupus-like syndrome.
Central Alpha-2 Agonists Clonidine Used if other agents fail. Rebound HTN if stopped abruptly! Available as patch for adherence.
Critical: ACE-I/ARB Precautions
  • CONTRAINDICATED in pregnancy - teratogenic (can cause fetal abnormalities)
  • Monitor potassium - can cause hyperkalemia (hold if K+ > 5.5)
  • Monitor renal function - can worsen renal function, especially if bilateral renal artery stenosis
  • Angioedema risk - more common with ACE-I; if occurs, discontinue immediately
  • Do NOT combine ACE-I + ARB (increased adverse effects without benefit)
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
ACE-I Cough Solution
That persistent dry cough? Switch to an ARB! ACE inhibitors (like lisinopril) cause a dry, nonproductive cough in 10-20% of patients due to bradykinin accumulation. The cough can take weeks to months to develop and may persist for weeks after stopping the medication. If a patient develops this cough, the solution is to switch to an ARB (like losartan). ARBs provide similar BP-lowering and renal-protective benefits without causing cough. Don't just add a cough suppressant - the right answer is to change the medication class.

Compelling Indications for Specific Medications

Comorbidity Preferred Medication Class Rationale
Heart Failure (HFrEF) ACE-I/ARB, Beta-blocker, Aldosterone antagonist, Diuretic Improve survival and reduce symptoms
Post-MI ACE-I, Beta-blocker Reduce remodeling and mortality
Diabetes with Proteinuria ACE-I or ARB Renoprotective effects
Chronic Kidney Disease ACE-I or ARB Slow progression of kidney disease
Stroke Prevention ACE-I, Thiazide diuretic Proven to reduce recurrent stroke
Atrial Fibrillation (rate control) Non-DHP CCB or Beta-blocker Slow ventricular response
Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia Alpha-1 blocker Treats HTN and BPH symptoms

Hypertensive Crisis

Hypertensive crisis is a severe elevation in blood pressure (typically >180/120 mmHg) that requires immediate assessment and intervention. The distinction between urgency and emergency is critical for determining the appropriate treatment setting and speed of BP reduction.

Hypertensive Emergency

Definition

  • Severe HTN (>180/120) WITH acute target organ damage
  • Life-threatening condition
  • Requires ICU admission

Signs of End-Organ Damage

  • Encephalopathy (confusion, seizures, visual changes)
  • Acute MI, Pulmonary edema
  • Acute stroke, Intracranial hemorrhage
  • Acute renal failure
  • Eclampsia
  • Aortic dissection

Treatment

IV antihypertensives (nitroprusside, nicardipine, labetalol). Reduce MAP by ~25% in first hour, then gradually over 24-48 hours.

Hypertensive Urgency

Definition

  • Severe HTN (>180/120) WITHOUT acute target organ damage
  • Serious but not immediately life-threatening
  • Can be managed in outpatient setting

Common Causes

  • Medication non-adherence
  • Abrupt discontinuation of meds
  • Inadequate treatment
  • High sodium intake
  • Anxiety, pain

Treatment

Oral antihypertensives. Lower BP gradually over 24-48 hours. Resume/adjust chronic medications. Close outpatient follow-up in 24-72 hours.

Critical: Don't Drop BP Too Fast!

Rapid BP reduction can cause ischemic stroke, MI, or renal failure due to impaired autoregulation. The brain, heart, and kidneys have adapted to higher pressures.

  • Emergency: Reduce MAP by no more than 25% in the first hour
  • Urgency: Lower BP over 24-48 hours
  • Exception: Aortic dissection requires rapid BP reduction to SBP <120 within 20 minutes
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Slow and Steady Saves Lives
Dropping blood pressure too quickly can be just as dangerous as the high BP itself! In hypertensive crisis, the body's organs have adjusted their blood flow regulation to higher pressures. If BP drops too rapidly, organs may not get adequate blood flow, causing ischemic stroke, heart attack, or kidney failure. For emergencies, reduce MAP by only 25% in the first hour, then gradually normalize over 24-48 hours. The only exception is aortic dissection, where rapid reduction (SBP <120 in 20 minutes) is needed to prevent aortic rupture.

IV Medications for Hypertensive Emergency

Medication Mechanism Key Points
Nitroprusside Arterial and venous vasodilator Rapid onset (seconds). Light-sensitive (cover IV bag). Risk of cyanide toxicity with prolonged use.
Nicardipine IV calcium channel blocker Preferred in stroke. Easily titratable. Avoid in heart failure.
Labetalol Alpha and beta blocker Good for aortic dissection. Avoid in asthma, heart block, cocaine use.
Esmolol Short-acting beta blocker Good for aortic dissection. Very short half-life - easy to titrate.
Hydralazine Direct vasodilator Commonly used in pregnancy/eclampsia. May cause reflex tachycardia.
Fenoldopam Dopamine agonist Increases renal perfusion. Good if renal impairment. Avoid with glaucoma.

Nursing Priorities in Hypertensive Crisis

Assess for End-Organ Damage
Neuro, cardiac, renal, visual symptoms
Continuous BP Monitoring
Arterial line in ICU if emergency
IV Access
Large-bore IV for medication administration
Identify Cause
Non-adherence? Secondary HTN? Precipitant?

Key Takeaways

Classification: Normal <120/80; Elevated 120-129/<80; Stage 1: 130-139/80-89; Stage 2: ≥140/90

Primary HTN (90-95%): No identifiable cause; requires lifelong management

DASH Diet + Lifestyle: First-line treatment; can reduce SBP 10-20+ mmHg combined

First-line meds: Thiazides, ACE-I, ARBs, CCBs - choice depends on comorbidities

Pregnancy: ACE-I and ARBs are contraindicated - use methyldopa, labetalol, nifedipine

Crisis: Emergency = organ damage (ICU, IV meds); Urgency = no damage (oral meds, gradual reduction)
