Immune Deficiency, HIV & Anaphylaxis

Primary immune deficiency warning signs, pediatric HIV and ART, and life-saving anaphylaxis care. Vaccines in the immunocompromised, IM epinephrine, and home care for families.

Learning Objectives

Recognize warning signs of primary immune deficiency in children.
Describe pediatric HIV transmission, presentation, and antiretroviral therapy.
Identify anaphylaxis signs and deliver IM epinephrine as the priority intervention.
Apply vaccine rules for immunocompromised children (no live vaccines).
Teach families HIV home care, adherence, and severe infection signs.
Coach caregivers on epinephrine auto-injector use and biphasic reaction watch.

High-Yield Summary

Ten facts that every peds nurse needs to know.

  • Primary immune deficiency (PID) warning signs: persistent thrush or skin candidiasis, two or more serious infections (sepsis, meningitis, osteomyelitis), failure to thrive, \u2265 2 episodes of pneumonia in 1 year, family history of PID, poor response to standard antibiotics, recurrent deep infections.
  • Common PIDs: SCID (severe combined immune deficiency — \u201cbubble boy\u201d), Bruton\u2019s X-linked agammaglobulinemia (B-cell defect), DiGeorge syndrome (T-cell defect + cardiac/calcium issues), Wiskott-Aldrich (platelets + eczema + infection).
  • NO LIVE VACCINES in immunocompromised children or while on significant immunosuppression. Live vaccines include MMR, varicella, rotavirus, intranasal influenza, BCG, yellow fever. Inactivated vaccines (injectable flu, Tdap, pneumococcal) are safe and encouraged.
  • Pediatric HIV — most commonly perinatal (vertical) transmission. Prevention: maternal ART, intrapartum zidovudine, C-section if high viral load, infant ART prophylaxis, avoid breastfeeding in the US.
  • HIV presentation: recurrent/opportunistic infections, failure to thrive, persistent thrush, lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly, developmental delays. Infant test: HIV DNA/RNA PCR (maternal antibodies confound serology until 18–24 months).
  • HIV treatment: combination antiretroviral therapy (cART/HAART) — typically 3+ drugs from different classes (NRTIs, NNRTIs, protease inhibitors, integrase inhibitors). Daily adherence is critical — missed doses drive resistance. Monitor CD4 count and viral load.
  • Anaphylaxis = rapid, life-threatening IgE-mediated systemic allergic reaction. Two or more systems: skin (hives, flushing, angioedema), respiratory (stridor, wheezing, SOB), GI (vomiting, diarrhea, cramps), cardiovascular (hypotension, tachycardia), CNS (altered mental status).
  • Anaphylaxis treatment: AIRWAY FIRST, then IM EPINEPHRINE 0.01 mg/kg (max 0.3 mg child; 0.5 mg adolescent) in the MID-ANTEROLATERAL THIGH (vastus lateralis). Repeat every 5–15 min as needed. Adjuncts: antihistamines, steroids, albuterol, IV fluids — but epi FIRST.
  • Biphasic reaction can occur up to 72 hours after initial resolution. Observe for 4–8 hours minimum. Discharge with 2 epinephrine auto-injectors, action plan, and allergist referral.
  • HIV home care teaching: medication adherence, nutrition (high-calorie, high-protein), pain management, recognize signs of severe infection (fever, cough, thrush, new rash), school/daycare safety, mental health, transition to adult care.

Fundamentals of Immune Function & Failure

When the immune system doesn\u2019t work, overreacts, or attacks itself

The immune system has two arms: innate (neutrophils, macrophages, complement — fast, nonspecific) and adaptive (B cells making antibodies, T cells recognizing specific antigens). Problems fall into three buckets nurses need to differentiate:

  • Immune deficiency — the system is broken (primary/inherited or secondary/acquired like HIV, chemo, steroids). Result: recurrent and unusual infections.
  • Allergy / hypersensitivity — the system overreacts to harmless stimuli. Anaphylaxis is the life-threatening end of this spectrum.
  • Autoimmunity — the system attacks the body\u2019s own tissues (JIA, SLE — covered in the related guide).

Three Categories at a Glance

Problem Example Treatment Concept
Immune deficiencySCID, HIV, steroid/chemo suppressionReplace (IVIG), protect (reverse isolation), treat infection aggressively
HypersensitivityAnaphylaxis, food/drug allergy, asthmaAvoid trigger + emergency rescue (epi, antihistamines, steroids)
AutoimmunityJIA, SLE, autoimmune thyroiditisSuppress the overactive system (NSAIDs, DMARDs, biologics, steroids)
🎯 Bottom Line: Deficiency = too little immune system. Hypersensitivity = too much. Autoimmunity = pointed the wrong way.

Primary Immune Deficiency

Warning signs every pediatric nurse should know

Primary immune deficiencies (PIDs) are inherited disorders of immune function. Early recognition can be life-saving — many of these children present with severe infections in the first year of life. SCID (severe combined immune deficiency) is often diagnosed on newborn screening.

Warning Signs of PID

Persistent thrush (oral candidiasis) or stubborn skin candidiasis in an infant past the usual age

Two or more serious infections in 1 year (sepsis, meningitis, osteomyelitis, cellulitis)

\u2265 2 episodes of pneumonia in one year

\u2265 8 ear infections or \u2265 2 sinus infections in a year

Failure to thrive, chronic diarrhea, malabsorption

Family history of PID or unexplained childhood deaths

Poor response to standard antibiotics or need for IV antibiotics to clear infection

Deep-seated or unusual infections (deep skin abscesses, Pneumocystis pneumonia, atypical organisms)

Common Pediatric PIDs

SCID

T + B Cell
Problem

Both T and B cell dysfunction — the classic \u201cbubble boy\u201d disease.

Findings

Severe, unusual, opportunistic infections in infancy; FTT, thrush, chronic diarrhea.

Treatment

Bone marrow transplant is curative. Strict protective isolation until transplant.

Screen

Now part of universal newborn screening.

Bruton\u2019s / XLA

B Cell
Problem

X-linked agammaglobulinemia — no B cells, no antibodies. Boys only.

Findings

Recurrent bacterial infections (encapsulated organisms) after 6 months as maternal IgG wears off.

Treatment

Monthly IVIG infusions to replace antibodies.

DiGeorge (22q11.2 Deletion)

T Cell
Problem

Thymic hypoplasia → low T cells. Also cardiac defects, hypocalcemia from parathyroid hypoplasia.

CATCH-22 Mnemonic

Cardiac, Abnormal facies, Thymic hypoplasia, Cleft palate, Hypocalcemia.

Treatment

Calcium, cardiac repair, possibly thymus transplant.

Wiskott-Aldrich

X-linked
Problem

Combined defect; boys only.

Classic Triad

Thrombocytopenia (small platelets), eczema, infections.

Treatment

IVIG, antibiotics; HSCT curative.

SCID Is a Newborn Emergency

A baby with confirmed SCID has essentially NO working immune system. Strict protective isolation, no live vaccines, only irradiated, CMV-negative, leukoreduced blood products, and urgent referral for bone marrow transplant. Most centers aim for HSCT within the first 3.5 months of life. Delay is life-threatening.

🎯 Bottom Line: PID red flags \u2013 persistent thrush, \u22652 severe infections, \u22652 PNA, FTT, family history. SCID, XLA (B cell), DiGeorge (T cell + heart/Ca), WAS.

Pediatric HIV

Transmission, diagnosis, and lifelong therapy

How Children Get HIV

Perinatal (vertical) transmission — most common route: in utero, intrapartum, or via breastfeeding.

Bloodborne — contaminated needles, rare transfusions in countries without screening.

Adolescents — sexual transmission, shared needles.

Prevention — maternal ART during pregnancy reduces transmission from ~25% to <1%.

Preventing Perinatal Transmission

  1. Prenatal HIV testing for all pregnant patients; if positive, start or continue ART throughout pregnancy.
  2. Intrapartum IV zidovudine if viral load is detectable near delivery.
  3. Scheduled C-section at 38 weeks if viral load > 1,000 copies/mL.
  4. Infant post-exposure prophylaxis — oral zidovudine \u00b1 additional ART for 4–6 weeks.
  5. NO breastfeeding in the US — formula feeding only (breast milk transmits virus).

Clinical Presentation

Failure to thrive, poor growth, weight loss.

Lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly.

Developmental delays, encephalopathy.

Persistent thrush, chronic diarrhea, parotid enlargement.

Opportunistic infections: Pneumocystis jirovecii pneumonia (PCP), CMV, mycobacteria, candida esophagitis.

Lower CD4 counts than adults at same age; rapid progression possible in infants.

Diagnosis

Age Test of Choice Why
Infants < 18 months (perinatally exposed) HIV DNA/RNA PCR at birth, 2–3 weeks, 1\u20132 months, 4\u20136 months Maternal antibodies cross the placenta, so antibody tests are unreliable until \u226518–24 months.
Children \u2265 18 months, adolescents, adults HIV antibody / antigen combo test Standard serologic testing.
Monitor disease activity CD4 count and viral load at regular intervals CD4 = immune status; viral load = disease activity and response to ART.

Treatment (Antiretroviral Therapy / ART)

  • Start ART in ALL children with HIV, regardless of CD4 count or symptoms (modern recommendation).
  • Combination therapy (cART / HAART) — typically 3 or more drugs from different classes.
  • Drug classes: NRTIs (zidovudine, lamivudine), NNRTIs (efavirenz, nevirapine), protease inhibitors (lopinavir/ritonavir), integrase inhibitors (dolutegravir, raltegravir), entry inhibitors.
  • Goal: undetectable viral load; CD4 count restored and maintained.
  • Daily adherence is everything. Missed doses \u2192 resistance \u2192 treatment failure.
  • Prophylaxis: TMP-SMX for PCP when CD4 falls; consider azithromycin for MAC.
HIV Is a Chronic, Manageable Condition

With daily ART, most children and adolescents with HIV achieve undetectable viral loads and a near-normal life expectancy. Untreatable-to-manageable is one of the most dramatic success stories in pediatric medicine. Your job as the nurse: support adherence relentlessly, coach the family through stigma, and treat this like a chronic condition (diabetes, asthma) — serious but lived with.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Infant HIV Testing Uses PCR, Not Antibodies
Babies born to HIV-positive mothers have maternal IgG crossing the placenta, so an antibody test will be positive without the baby actually being infected. Until ~18 months, the only reliable tests are HIV DNA or RNA PCR, which detect viral nucleic acid directly. Two negative PCRs after 1 month of age typically rule out infection; definitive antibody testing at 18–24 months confirms.
🎯 Bottom Line: Perinatal transmission is #1. Maternal ART prevents it. Test infants with PCR. Treat everyone with cART. Adherence is survival.

Anaphylaxis

Minutes matter: airway, then IM epinephrine

Anaphylaxis is a rapid, life-threatening IgE-mediated systemic allergic reaction. The most common pediatric triggers are food (peanut, tree nuts, milk, egg, shellfish), insect stings, medications (beta-lactams), and latex. Severity ranges from cutaneous reactions to cardiovascular collapse and airway compromise.

Definition (Any One of These)

  • Acute onset of a reaction involving two or more body systems (skin, respiratory, GI, CV, CNS) after exposure to a likely allergen.
  • Rapidly progressive hypotension after exposure to a known allergen.
  • Acute involvement of skin / mucosa + respiratory compromise OR hypotension OR end-organ dysfunction.

System-by-System Findings

System Signs
SkinFlushing, generalized hives (urticaria), pruritus, angioedema (lips/tongue/eyelids)
RespiratoryStridor, hoarseness, throat tightness, cough, wheezing, dyspnea, hypoxia
GINausea, vomiting, crampy abdominal pain, diarrhea
CardiovascularTachycardia, hypotension, dizziness, syncope, poor perfusion, shock
CNSAnxiety, confusion, altered mental status, \u201csense of doom,\u201d loss of consciousness

Anaphylaxis Management (In Order)

  1. ASSESS the AIRWAY — look for stridor, angioedema, inability to speak. Secure airway if compromised; prepare for intubation or cricothyrotomy if severe.
  2. INTRAMUSCULAR EPINEPHRINE — the FIRST medication. 0.01 mg/kg of 1:1,000 concentration IM in the MID-ANTEROLATERAL THIGH (vastus lateralis). Max dose: 0.3 mg child, 0.5 mg adolescent. Repeat every 5\u201315 minutes as needed.
  3. Remove the trigger if possible (stop IV antibiotic, remove stinger).
  4. Supine position with legs elevated (Trendelenburg) for hypotension; sit up if respiratory distress.
  5. High-flow oxygen and continuous pulse oximetry.
  6. IV access x 2; large-bore. Rapid normal saline boluses 20 mL/kg for hypotension.
  7. Adjunct medications (NEVER substitute for epi):
    • Antihistamines — H1 (diphenhydramine), H2 (famotidine) — for skin/itching.
    • Bronchodilators (albuterol nebulizer) for wheezing.
    • Corticosteroids (methylprednisolone IV or prednisone PO) — may reduce biphasic reaction.
  8. Continuous monitoring; transfer to the appropriate level of care (ED, PICU).
  9. Observe for biphasic reaction — minimum 4\u20138 hours of observation after symptoms resolve; some centers extend longer for severe cases.
  10. Discharge plan: 2 epinephrine auto-injectors, written anaphylaxis action plan, allergy referral, avoidance education, MedicAlert bracelet.
Epi First — Don\u2019t Wait for Full Criteria

If you\u2019re thinking "maybe anaphylaxis," give the epinephrine. There is no safe delay, and delayed epi is the strongest predictor of death. Antihistamines are NOT a substitute for epinephrine — they don\u2019t treat airway swelling or hypotension. IM in the vastus lateralis (mid-anterolateral thigh) for fastest absorption. NOT subQ, NOT deltoid (slower, less reliable).

Epinephrine Auto-Injectors (EpiPen, Auvi-Q, generics)

EpiPen Jr (0.15 mg) for children 15\u201330 kg (~33\u201366 lb). EpiPen (0.3 mg) for \u226530 kg.

Technique: pull off safety cap, press firmly against the outer mid-thigh through clothing if needed, hold 3\u201310 seconds per device instructions.

Always call 911 AFTER giving — epinephrine is a bridge to emergency care, not a substitute.

If symptoms persist or worsen, give a second dose after 5\u201315 minutes; that\u2019s why families get two devices.

School action plan — auto-injectors stored accessibly (NOT locked away), teachers trained, allergens removed from cafeteria when feasible.

Check expiration regularly; discolored or cloudy epi should be replaced.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Biphasic Catch
Up to 20% of anaphylaxis cases have a biphasic reaction — symptoms recur hours (usually within 8\u201310, but up to 72) after initial resolution. That\u2019s why we observe at least 4\u20138 hours and give families a clear plan. Teach: \u201cIf any symptoms come back, use the second epinephrine and call 911 again.\u201d And always discharge with TWO auto-injectors \u2014 not one.
🎯 Bottom Line: Airway \u2192 IM epi 0.01 mg/kg vastus lateralis \u2192 O2, IV fluids, position \u2192 antihistamines, steroids, albuterol as ADJUNCTS \u2192 observe 4\u20138 hr for biphasic \u2192 2 epi pens home.

Vaccines in Immunocompromised Children

No live vaccines, and why it matters

Live vs Inactivated

LIVE — AVOID if immunocompromised or household contact in some cases INACTIVATED / Non-live — Safe
MMR (measles, mumps, rubella) Injectable influenza
Varicella (chickenpox) Tdap / DTaP
Rotavirus (oral, infants) Pneumococcal conjugate (PCV)
Intranasal (live-attenuated) influenza Hib, HepA, HepB
BCG (TB, not routine in US) Meningococcal conjugate
Yellow fever HPV

Key Vaccine Rules

NO LIVE VACCINES to immunocompromised children (HIV with low CD4, chemo, biologics, high-dose steroids, SCID) — risk of vaccine-strain infection.

Inactivated vaccines are SAFE and STRONGLY ENCOURAGED, though the immune response may be blunted. Boost as recommended.

Household contacts of severely immunocompromised kids should get INJECTABLE flu (not nasal); generally safe to receive other live vaccines (MMR, varicella, rotavirus) but monitor for shedding.

HIV-exposed and HIV-positive children — follow CDC guidance; MMR and varicella may be given if CD4 is adequate; rotavirus per HIV status.

Catch-up immunization after immune recovery (post-chemo, post-transplant, off biologics) — work with the team.

Update school vaccine records; some states allow medical exemption for immunocompromised.

NCLEX Point — Sibling Flu Shot

The younger sibling of a child on chemo or biologics wants a flu shot at the pediatrician. The sibling should get the injectable (inactivated) influenza vaccine, NOT the intranasal live-attenuated spray. Intranasal flu can shed and potentially infect the immunocompromised sibling.

🎯 Bottom Line: No live vaccines (MMR, varicella, rotavirus, nasal flu, BCG) in immunocompromised. Injectable flu, Tdap, pneumo, HPV, etc. are fine. Household: injectable flu.

Nursing Priorities

Assessment, isolation, medication safety, and emergency readiness

Assessment Priorities

Fever response — immunocompromised children may not mount a normal fever; any fever is evaluated urgently.

Weight and growth — failure to thrive is a hallmark of both PID and HIV. Track at every visit.

Respiratory assessment — tachypnea, hypoxia, cough can signal PCP or bacterial pneumonia.

Oral/mucous membrane exam — thrush, ulcers, lesions.

Skin — eczema (Wiskott-Aldrich), rashes, wounds, central line site.

Developmental assessment — delays in motor and cognitive milestones.

Protective Measures

  • Private room, reverse isolation for severe PID and profound immunosuppression.
  • Strict handwashing, HEPA filtration, limited visitors (no sick contacts).
  • Avoid live plants, fresh flowers, raw foods (neutropenic-diet approach).
  • Irradiated, CMV-negative, leukoreduced blood products.
  • Central line care per strict protocol; monitor for CLABSI.
  • No live vaccines; catch up inactivated vaccines per schedule.

Medication Safety

ART adherence is nonnegotiable in HIV — missed doses cause resistance. Use pill organizers, reminders, partnership with family.

Prophylactic antibiotics (TMP-SMX for PCP, penicillin for some PIDs) — teach purpose and stress adherence.

IVIG infusions for antibody deficiencies — monitor for reactions (fever, rash, anaphylaxis); pre-medicate per protocol.

Epi pen preparedness — for kids with known severe allergies, ensure pens are current, accessible, and family/school know how to use.

Lab monitoring — CD4 count, viral load (HIV); immunoglobulin levels (PID); routine labs per medication.

Drug interaction awareness — ART has many drug-drug interactions; coordinate with pharmacy.

Anaphylaxis Readiness

Epinephrine at the bedside for any patient receiving a new biologic, IV antibiotic, or contrast

Allergy verification before every medication; clarify the reaction type

Continuous monitoring during first doses of biologics and IVIG

Rapid-response activation for any suspected anaphylaxis — don\u2019t wait for full presentation

🎯 Bottom Line: Assess fever/growth/development; protect with isolation + irradiated blood + no live vaccines; ART + IVIG + prophylaxis adherence; epi ready for new infusions.

Family Education & Home Care

HIV home care, epi plans, and the long road

HIV Home Care (Your Requested Teaching)

Nutrition — high-calorie, high-protein diet; small frequent meals; oral supplements if needed; monitor growth.

Medication compliance — daily ART exactly as prescribed; pill organizers, phone alarms, pairing with routines (tooth-brushing).

Pain management — acetaminophen for fever/mild pain, address chronic pain with provider; avoid aspirin in younger children.

Signs of severe infection — fever, cough, new rash, thrush, persistent diarrhea, lethargy, trouble breathing. Call the clinic right away.

School/daycare — standard precautions; confidentiality per state law; IEP or 504 plan if cognitive delays.

Mental health, stigma, and adolescent transition — ongoing psychosocial support; disclosure counseling when developmentally appropriate.

Epi Pen / Allergy Family Plan

  1. Carry TWO epinephrine auto-injectors at all times; check expiration dates.
  2. Have a written Anaphylaxis Action Plan in the pocket of the carrying case, at school, and at activities.
  3. Teach caregivers, teachers, coaches, and older siblings how to use the auto-injector.
  4. MedicAlert bracelet identifying the allergy.
  5. Read all food labels; ask about ingredients when eating out or at social events.
  6. Avoid cross-contamination in the kitchen; wash hands and surfaces after allergen exposure.
  7. Review action plan with every new school year or activity.

Primary Immune Deficiency — Home Care

  • Strict handwashing by every caregiver; avoid sick contacts.
  • No live vaccines; catch up inactivated vaccines.
  • Prophylactic antibiotics if prescribed (TMP-SMX, penicillin) — daily, don\u2019t miss doses.
  • Monthly IVIG infusions for antibody deficiencies — can be done at home with infusion nurse support.
  • Early evaluation of any fever or illness.
  • Genetic counseling for family planning.
  • Connect with support organizations (Immune Deficiency Foundation, NIH genetic disorder networks).

When to Call or Go to the ED

Fever in any immunocompromised child — always call

Signs of anaphylaxis — give epi, call 911

Respiratory distress, cough, hypoxia

Severe diarrhea, dehydration, vomiting

New rash, especially with fever

Central line redness/drainage/fever — CLABSI risk

Chronic, Serious — but Not Defining

Children with immune deficiency, HIV, and serious allergies grow up, attend school, play sports, form friendships, and build adult lives. The diagnosis is part of their story, not the whole of it. Your consistent, informed, compassionate nursing care helps families see past the crisis of diagnosis into a sustainable future.

🎯 Bottom Line: HIV home care = nutrition + adherence + pain + infection signs + mental health. Anaphylaxis = 2 epi pens + action plan + MedicAlert. PID = antibiotics + IVIG + no live vaccines + fever response.
