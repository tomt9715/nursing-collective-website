Immune Deficiency, HIV & Anaphylaxis

One reaction where the antihistamine is the wrong answer, one vaccine rule that flips in an immunocompromised child, and the warning signs that turn "he is always sick" into a diagnosis.

  • Say whether a reaction is anaphylaxis, and reach for intramuscular epinephrine first
  • Name the vaccines you never give an immunocompromised child, and why
  • Separate primary from secondary immune deficiency from the history alone

01

What actually gets you

This guide covers three things that look unrelated. They share one exam theme: the obvious move is often the wrong one. Read these three traps before anything else.

You froze on anaphylaxis and reached for the antihistamine

A child has hives, lip swelling, wheezing, and a falling blood pressure. Diphenhydramine feels like the allergy answer. It is not. Antihistamines do nothing for airway swelling or hypotension. Airway or blood pressure is the trigger to act, and the action is intramuscular epinephrine in the thigh, first.

You gave a live vaccine to a child who cannot fight it

MMR, varicella, rotavirus, and intranasal flu are live. In a healthy child they teach immunity. In an immunocompromised child or one with a low CD4 count, a live vaccine can cause the disease. The rule flips, and the exam loves that flip.

You called a broken immune system a run of bad luck

"He is always sick" hides primary immune deficiency. The pattern that gives it away is recurrent, severe, and unusual infections, plus failure to thrive. Two pneumonias in a year is not bad luck. It is a warning sign.

Bottom Line: Treat anaphylaxis with epinephrine, not an antihistamine. Hold live vaccines when the immune system cannot fight one. Read recurrent severe infection as a diagnosis, not misfortune.
02

The four things

For each condition below, you should be able to say four things with the book closed. The one-sentence definition. The clue you would actually see in a child. The priority nursing action. The first-line treatment with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Condition Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line treatment
Primary immune deficiency An inherited defect leaves the immune system unable to fight infection. Recurrent severe infections, persistent thrush, failure to thrive, family history. Protect the child; treat any fever urgently. IVIG for antibody defects; transplant is curative for SCID. No live vaccines.
Pediatric HIV The virus is passed mother to child and destroys immune cells over time. Failure to thrive, thrush, opportunistic infection, low CD4 count. Support daily medication adherence relentlessly. Combination antiretroviral therapy, every day. Formula, not breast milk, in the US.

Write these three rows on one index card, by hand. Deficiency is too little immune system. Anaphylaxis is too much. HIV is an immune system being taken apart. That card frames most of the exam.

03

Primary or secondary

Discrimination axis: was the immune system born broken, or was it broken later.

The immune system fails in three ways. It can be missing (deficiency), it can overreact (allergy), or it can attack the body (autoimmunity). This guide lives in the first two. Deficiency means too little immune system, and the result is recurrent, unusual infection.

Deficiency comes in two flavors. The exam wants you to tell them apart from the history.

Primary: inherited, present from birth
  • A genetic defect in immune cells
  • Severe infections in the first year
  • SCID is caught on newborn screening
  • Family history of PID or early deaths

Named examples: SCID, Bruton XLA, DiGeorge, Wiskott-Aldrich.

Secondary: acquired, broke later
  • The immune system was working, then something suppressed it
  • HIV, chemotherapy, high-dose steroids, biologics
  • Onset tracks the cause, not birth
  • Often reversible when the cause is removed

Nursing overlap: both need protection, aggressive infection treatment, and no live vaccines.

The axis that separates them

Timing and cause. Primary is inherited and shows up in infancy with no external reason. Secondary has a reason you can name: a virus, a drug, a treatment. The nursing care rhymes; the workup and the counseling do not.

The warning signs of primary immune deficiency

Do not memorize a random list. Memorize the pattern: recurrent, severe, unusual, and failing to grow.

Red flagWhat counts
Persistent infectionThrush or skin candidiasis past the usual age
Two serious infectionsSepsis, meningitis, or osteomyelitis in one year
Recurrent pneumonia2 or more episodes in a single year
Recurrent minor infection8 or more ear infections, or 2 or more sinus infections, in a year
Failure to thrivePoor growth with chronic diarrhea or malabsorption
Family historyKnown PID or unexplained childhood deaths
Poor antibiotic responseNeeds IV antibiotics to clear ordinary infections
Unusual organismsDeep abscesses or Pneumocystis pneumonia

Four named deficiencies worth knowing

DisorderDefectClueTreatment
SCID Both T and B cells; the "bubble boy" disease Severe opportunistic infection in infancy, thrush, diarrhea Bone marrow transplant is curative; strict isolation until then
Bruton XLA No B cells, no antibodies; boys only Recurrent bacterial infection after 6 months, as maternal IgG fades Monthly IVIG to replace the antibodies he cannot make
DiGeorge Thymic hypoplasia, low T cells (22q11.2 deletion) CATCH-22: cardiac, abnormal facies, thymic, cleft palate, hypocalcemia Calcium, cardiac repair, possible thymus transplant
Wiskott-Aldrich Combined defect; boys only Triad of thrombocytopenia, eczema, and infection IVIG and antibiotics; transplant is curative
SCID is a newborn emergency

A baby with SCID has essentially no working immune system. Use strict protective isolation. Give no live vaccines. Transfuse only irradiated, CMV-negative, leukoreduced blood products. Refer urgently for transplant; centers aim for it within the first 3.5 months of life. Delay is life-threatening.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
"Always sick" has a pattern
One ear infection is childhood. Recurrent, severe, and unusual infection with failure to thrive is a red flag. When a stem stacks pneumonias, thrush, and poor growth, it is pointing at primary immune deficiency, not a run of colds.
Bottom Line: Primary is inherited and infant-onset; secondary has a cause you can name. The PID pattern is recurrent, severe, unusual infection plus failure to thrive. SCID and XLA are B-cell heavy; DiGeorge is T-cell plus heart and calcium.
04

Pediatric HIV

Discrimination axis: an infant's antibody test is the mother's, not the baby's.

Most children get HIV by perinatal transmission: in utero, during delivery, or through breast milk. Maternal antiretroviral therapy in pregnancy drops that transmission from about a quarter of exposures to under 1 in 100. Prevention is the whole game here.

Preventing perinatal transmission

  1. 1
    Test every pregnant patient. If positive, start or continue ART throughout pregnancy.
  2. 2
    Intrapartum IV zidovudine if the viral load is still detectable near delivery.
  3. 3
    Scheduled C-section at 38 weeks if the viral load is > 1,000 copies/mL.
  4. 4
    Infant prophylaxis. Oral zidovudine, sometimes with added drugs, for 4 to 6 weeks.
  5. 5
    No breastfeeding in the US. Formula only, because breast milk transmits the virus.

Testing the infant

An antibody test in a baby measures the mother's antibodies, which cross the placenta. It stays positive without the baby being infected. So you order a test that finds the virus itself.

WhoTestWhy
Infants under 18 months, perinatally exposed HIV DNA or RNA PCR, repeated over the first months Maternal antibodies confound serology until 18 to 24 months
Children 18 months and older, adolescents HIV antibody / antigen combo test Standard serology now reflects the child's own status
Anyone on treatment CD4 count and viral load at intervals CD4 shows immune status; viral load shows disease activity

Treatment and monitoring

  • Start ART in every child with HIV, whatever the CD4 count or symptoms.
  • Combination therapy uses 3 or more drugs from different classes: NRTIs, NNRTIs, protease inhibitors, integrase inhibitors.
  • Daily adherence is everything. Missed doses breed resistance, and resistance means treatment failure.
  • Goal: an undetectable viral load with the CD4 count restored.
  • Prophylaxis: TMP-SMX for Pneumocystis pneumonia when the CD4 count falls.
Treat HIV like a chronic condition

With daily ART, most children reach an undetectable viral load and a near-normal life expectancy. Your job is steady: support adherence, coach the family through stigma, and manage it like diabetes or asthma. Serious, but lived with.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Infant HIV testing uses PCR, not antibodies
A baby born to an HIV-positive mother carries the mother's IgG across the placenta, so an antibody test reads positive on its own. Until about 18 months, only HIV DNA or RNA PCR is reliable, because it detects viral nucleic acid directly. Two negative PCRs after 1 month typically rule out infection; antibody testing at 18 to 24 months confirms.
Bottom Line: Perinatal transmission is number one, and maternal ART prevents it. Test infants with PCR. Treat everyone with combination ART. Adherence is survival, and formula replaces breast milk in the US.
05

Anaphylaxis or a milder reaction

Discrimination axis: airway or blood pressure. That is the line.

Anaphylaxis is a rapid, life-threatening IgE-mediated reaction. The common pediatric triggers are food, insect stings, medications, and latex. The exam question is rarely "is this an allergy." It is "is this the kind that kills," and the answer decides whether you reach for epinephrine.

Milder allergic reaction
  • Skin only: hives, flushing, itching
  • Breathing is comfortable
  • Blood pressure is normal
  • No vomiting, no dizziness

Action: an antihistamine is reasonable here. Watch closely, because it can escalate.

Anaphylaxis
  • Two or more systems involved
  • Respiratory: stridor, wheeze, throat tightness
  • Cardiovascular: hypotension, tachycardia, syncope
  • Or skin plus a rapid drop in blood pressure

Action: intramuscular epinephrine now. Do not wait for full criteria.

The axis that separates them

Airway or blood pressure. Skin-only reactions are uncomfortable, not deadly. The moment breathing is threatened or the pressure drops, it is anaphylaxis, and epinephrine is the treatment. An antihistamine treats itch. It does not open an airway or hold up a blood pressure.

Signs, system by system

SystemSigns
SkinFlushing, hives, itching, angioedema of lips, tongue, and eyelids
RespiratoryStridor, hoarseness, throat tightness, cough, wheeze, dyspnea, hypoxia
GINausea, vomiting, crampy abdominal pain, diarrhea
CardiovascularTachycardia, hypotension, dizziness, syncope, shock
CNSAnxiety, confusion, a sense of doom, loss of consciousness
Epinephrine first, and do not wait

If you are thinking "maybe anaphylaxis," give the epinephrine. There is no safe delay, and late epinephrine is the strongest predictor of death. Give it IM in the vastus lateralis, the mid-anterolateral thigh, for the fastest absorption. Not subcutaneous. Not deltoid. Antihistamines are not a substitute.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The antihistamine is a decoy
In a "what do you do first" stem, oral diphenhydramine will sit there looking like the allergy answer. It treats itching and nothing else. When the stem has wheeze, throat tightness, or a falling blood pressure, the first action is always IM epinephrine in the thigh.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The biphasic catch
Symptoms can return hours after they resolve, usually within 8 to 10 hours, sometimes up to 72. That second wave is why you observe for at least 4 to 8 hours and send the family home with two auto-injectors, not one. Teach the plan: if symptoms come back, use the second pen and call 911 again.
Bottom Line: Skin only is mild; airway or blood pressure is anaphylaxis. IM epinephrine in the vastus lateralis, first. Antihistamines, steroids, and albuterol are adjuncts. Observe 4 to 8 hours for the biphasic return, then two pens home.
06

Live or inactivated

Discrimination axis: does the vaccine contain a living pathogen.

A live vaccine is a weakened pathogen. A healthy immune system defeats it and learns. A weak one may not, and the vaccine becomes the infection. That single fact drives every vaccine question in an immunocompromised child.

Live: avoid if immunocompromised
  • MMR (measles, mumps, rubella)
  • Varicella (chickenpox)
  • Rotavirus (oral, infants)
  • Intranasal influenza
  • BCG and yellow fever
Inactivated: safe and encouraged
  • Injectable influenza
  • Tdap and DTaP
  • Pneumococcal conjugate
  • Hib, Hep A, Hep B
  • Meningococcal and HPV
The axis that separates them

Living or not. Hold every live vaccine in a child with HIV and a low CD4, on chemotherapy, on biologics, on high-dose steroids, or with SCID. Give the inactivated vaccines freely; the response may be blunted, but the vaccine is safe. Boost as the schedule recommends.

The household rule

The people around the child matter too. A live vaccine can shed. Match the family's flu vaccine to the child's fragility.

  • Household contacts get injectable flu, not the intranasal spray, because the live nasal vaccine can shed virus.
  • Other live vaccines are generally fine for contacts (MMR, varicella, rotavirus), with monitoring for shedding.
  • HIV-exposed children follow CDC guidance; MMR and varicella may be given when the CD4 count is adequate.
  • Catch up inactivated vaccines after immune recovery, working with the team.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The sibling flu shot
A classic stem: the younger sibling of a child on chemotherapy wants a flu vaccine. The sibling gets the injectable, inactivated shot, not the intranasal spray. The nasal vaccine is live and can shed to the immunocompromised child at home.
Bottom Line: No live vaccines (MMR, varicella, rotavirus, nasal flu, BCG) in the immunocompromised. Inactivated vaccines are safe and encouraged. Household contacts take injectable flu, never the nasal spray.
07

What you do first

Two emergencies live in this topic: the anaphylaxis you have to reverse, and the immunocompromised child you have to protect. Both are ordering problems. Know the sequence, not just the pieces.

The ordering rules
  • Airway beats everything. Assess it before you reach for a drug.
  • Epinephrine beats the adjuncts. Antihistamines and steroids come after, never instead.
  • In an immunocompromised child, any fever is urgent, even a low one.
  • Prevention beats rescue. Protect before the infection starts.

Anaphylaxis, in order

  1. 1
    Assess the airway. Look for stridor, angioedema, and trouble speaking. Prepare to secure it if it is failing.
  2. 2
    Intramuscular epinephrine. The first drug. 0.01 mg/kg of 1:1,000 in the vastus lateralis. Repeat every 5 to 15 minutes as needed.
  3. 3
    Remove the trigger. Stop the IV antibiotic; remove the stinger.
  4. 4
    Position and oxygen. Supine with legs raised for hypotension; sit up for respiratory distress. High-flow oxygen with pulse oximetry.
  5. 5
    IV fluids. Two large-bore lines. Normal saline boluses at 20 mL/kg for hypotension.
  6. 6
    Adjuncts, never substitutes. H1 and H2 antihistamines for skin, albuterol for wheeze, corticosteroids to blunt the biphasic wave.
  7. 7
    Monitor and observe. Continuous monitoring; watch at least 4 to 8 hours for a biphasic reaction before discharge.

Protecting the immunocompromised child

  • Private room, reverse isolation for severe PID and profound immunosuppression.
  • Strict handwashing, HEPA filtration, and no sick visitors.
  • No live plants, fresh flowers, or raw foods, the neutropenic-diet approach.
  • Irradiated, CMV-negative, leukoreduced blood products only.
  • Strict central line care; watch for a line infection.
  • No live vaccines; keep inactivated vaccines current.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
A fever is an emergency here
An immunocompromised child may not mount a normal fever, so any temperature is treated as serious. Do not wait and watch. Evaluate it urgently, draw cultures, and start antibiotics per protocol. The reassurance you would give a healthy child does not apply.
Bottom Line: Anaphylaxis order is airway, IM epinephrine, remove trigger, position and oxygen, fluids, then adjuncts. For the immunocompromised child, protect hard and treat every fever as urgent.
08

Send them home knowing

These conditions are managed at home, by families, for years. The discharge teaching is the intervention. Three plans go home: HIV daily care, the anaphylaxis plan, and PID protection.

HIV home care

  • Medication adherence is the whole plan. Daily ART, pill organizers, phone alarms, paired with tooth-brushing.
  • Nutrition: high-calorie, high-protein, small frequent meals, growth tracked.
  • Watch for severe infection: fever, cough, new rash, thrush, persistent diarrhea, trouble breathing. Call the clinic.
  • Pain and comfort: acetaminophen for fever; avoid aspirin in young children.
  • School and mental health: standard precautions, confidentiality, stigma support, and a plan for adolescent transition.

The anaphylaxis family plan

  1. 1
    Carry two auto-injectors at all times, and check the expiration dates.
  2. 2
    Keep a written action plan in the case, at school, and at every activity.
  3. 3
    Teach the technique to caregivers, teachers, coaches, and older siblings. Press firmly on the outer thigh, through clothing if needed.
  4. 4
    Give the drug, then call 911. Epinephrine is a bridge to care, not a substitute for it.
  5. 5
    Match the device to weight. EpiPen Jr, 0.15 mg, for 15 to 30 kg; EpiPen, 0.3 mg, at 30 kg and up.
  6. 6
    Add a MedicAlert bracelet, read every food label, and avoid cross-contamination at home.

Primary immune deficiency at home

  • Strict handwashing by every caregiver, and no sick contacts.
  • Prophylactic antibiotics if prescribed (TMP-SMX, penicillin), taken daily.
  • Monthly IVIG for antibody deficiencies, often given at home with nurse support.
  • Early evaluation of any fever or illness.
  • Genetic counseling for family planning, and support organizations for the long road.
Serious, but not the whole story

Children with immune deficiency, HIV, and severe allergies grow up. They attend school, play sports, and build adult lives. The diagnosis is part of their story, not all of it. Steady, informed nursing care is what helps a family see past the crisis of diagnosis.

Bottom Line: HIV home care is adherence, nutrition, and infection watch. Anaphylaxis is two pens, an action plan, and a MedicAlert bracelet. PID is antibiotics, IVIG, no live vaccines, and an urgent fever response.
