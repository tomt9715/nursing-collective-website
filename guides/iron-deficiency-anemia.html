Iron-Deficiency Anemia

Three look-alike anemias, one toddler on too much milk, and how to give a dose of iron the child will actually absorb.

Reading Time
10-15 minutes
Difficulty Level
Intermediate
Last Updated

The Nursing Collective

By the end, without notes

  • Name the toddler feeding pattern that quietly drains iron, and say why milk does it
  • Separate iron deficiency from thalassemia trait using ferritin, RDW, and the red cell count
  • Say which lab drops first, and what a normal hemoglobin with a low ferritin means
  • Teach a parent to give liquid iron so it absorbs and does not stain the teeth
  • State how long iron continues after the hemoglobin looks normal, and why
  • Act on a swallowed bottle of iron pills in the right order, with the right antidote
01

What actually gets you

Iron-deficiency anemia is not a hard topic. It is an easy topic that hides three traps, and missing any one turns a defensible answer wrong.

Read these before anything else.

You treated "microcytic anemia" as one thing

Small pale cells are not automatically iron deficiency. Thalassemia trait looks the same on the count. Anemia of chronic disease can look close too. The ferritin and the red cell count tell them apart, not the cell size.

You missed the toddler on too much milk

This is the classic cause, and it is quiet. A toddler drinking heavy cow's milk fills up on it, and milk has almost no iron. It also crowds out iron-rich food, and it can cause slow gut blood loss. Over 24 ounces a day is the red flag.

You gave the iron wrong

Iron goes in between meals with vitamin C, not with milk. Liquid iron stains teeth, so it goes through a straw, and dark stools are expected, not a bleed. And you do not stop when the hemoglobin normalizes.

Bottom Line: Study this as three jobs: tell iron deficiency from its look-alikes, catch the milk-fed toddler, and give iron so it actually works.
02

The four things

For each anemia below, say four things with the book closed. The one-sentence definition. The clue you would see in a child. The priority action. The first-line treatment with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet.

Type Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line treatment
Iron-deficiency anemia Too little iron, so red cells come out small and pale. Toddler on heavy milk, pale, tired, eating ice or dirt. Confirm the ferritin, then start oral iron. Oral ferrous sulfate with vitamin C. Dark stools are expected.
Thalassemia trait An inherited hemoglobin problem, not an iron problem. Small cells but a near-normal red cell count; family ancestry. Do not load with iron; check ferritin first. Usually no iron. Confirm with hemoglobin electrophoresis.
Anemia of chronic disease Iron exists but is locked away during inflammation. A chronically ill child; ferritin normal or high. Treat the underlying illness. Iron alone does not fix it; address the disease.
Copy this down

Write these three rows on one index card, by hand. Iron deficiency: low ferritin, high TIBC, responds to iron. Thalassemia trait: normal ferritin, normal red cell count, no iron. Chronic disease: iron is there but hidden. That card sorts most of the confusion.

03

How a child runs out of iron

Iron is the core of hemoglobin. Without enough of it, red cells cannot carry enough oxygen. Children are at high risk because they grow fast, and growth outpaces their iron stores. This is the most common anemia of childhood.

A baby is born with iron stores built in the womb. Those stores run low around 6 months, and after that the diet has to replace them. Miss that handoff and deficiency follows.

Who is at highest risk

Infants 6 to 24 months. Fetal stores are gone; the diet must take over.

Heavy cow's milk intake. Over 24 ounces a day crowds out iron and can cause slow gut bleeding.

Prematurity or low birth weight. Less iron was stored, and growth demand is higher.

Adolescent girls. Menstrual loss, rapid growth, and often thin diets stack up.

Restrictive diets. Unplanned vegan or vegetarian eating; food insecurity.

Chronic blood loss. Gut sources, menses, or parasitic infection.

The physiology in one paragraph

Iron from food is absorbed in the duodenum. Heme iron from meat absorbs best. The body then builds it into hemoglobin in new red cells. Spare iron is stored as ferritin in the liver, spleen, and marrow. When intake drops or losses rise, ferritin falls first. Then serum iron drops. Then the iron-binding capacity rises. Hemoglobin falls last. That sequence is the whole lab story.

The milk problem

Cow's milk is the single most common preventable cause. It has almost no iron, and it fills the stomach so the child eats less iron-rich food. In infants it can cause slow gut blood loss. Teach families the rule: no cow's milk before age 1, and no more than 24 ounces a day after.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Cap the Milk
A toddler who drinks the day away on milk is the setup you will see again and again. No cow's milk before the first birthday. After that, hold it to 24 ounces a day. When a stem says "40 ounces of milk and barely eats," the answer is iron deficiency.
Bottom Line: Fetal stores run out near 6 months. Diet has to take over. The toddler on too much milk is the classic miss.
04

How it shows up

Mild deficiency can be silent, caught only on a routine screen. Moderate to severe deficiency shows classic findings. Learn to recognize them at the bedside.

Pallor. Best seen in the conjunctiva, palms, nail beds, and oral mucosa.

Fatigue and irritability. Slower play, more sleep, poor appetite.

Tachycardia with a flow murmur. The heart works harder to move oxygen.

Shortness of breath. On exertion; poor feeding in infants.

Pica. Eating non-food items: dirt, ice, paper, paint chips. Classic for iron deficiency.

Spoon nails. Concave, brittle nails in long-standing deficiency.

Glossitis. Smooth red tongue; cracks at the corners of the mouth.

Developmental concerns. Cognitive delay and poor school performance; some deficits can persist after correction.

Pica opens a second door: lead

Children with iron deficiency often develop pica. If they eat paint chips or dust from old housing, lead poisoning follows. Screen a blood lead level in any child with pica, especially in homes built before 1978. Iron deficiency and lead toxicity also make each other worse.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Assess Pallor Fairly
Skin color is not reliable in children with darker skin tones. Pull down the lower eyelid and look at the conjunctiva. Check the palmar creases, the oral mucosa, and the nail beds. Pale conjunctiva and palms are the most reliable signs across every skin color.
Bottom Line: Pallor at the conjunctiva and palms, fatigue, a flow murmur, pica, spoon nails. Pica means check a lead level too.
05

Which microcytic anemia is it

Discrimination axis: the ferritin, the RDW, and the red cell count.

Small pale cells send you looking, not to a diagnosis. Iron deficiency, thalassemia trait, and chronic disease can all look microcytic. The size of the cell will not separate them, but three other numbers will.

Screening

  • Universal screen at 12 months, at the well-child visit.
  • Screen earlier with risk factors: preterm, heavy milk, picky eater.
  • Screen adolescent girls once they start menstruating.

  • Treat the underlying disease
The axis that separates them

Ferritin is the pivot. Low means iron deficiency. Normal or high sends you elsewhere. Then use two more clues. Iron deficiency has a low red cell count and a high RDW. Thalassemia trait has a near-normal red cell count and a normal RDW. Chronic disease has a high or normal ferritin with a low TIBC.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Ferritin Drops First
Storage iron empties before circulating iron. So ferritin falls before hemoglobin does. A low ferritin with a normal hemoglobin catches deficiency early, before the anemia shows. When a stem asks which lab changes first, the answer is ferritin.
Bottom Line: Microcytic plus low ferritin plus high TIBC equals iron deficiency. Ferritin drops first. A normal ferritin points to thalassemia trait or chronic disease.
06

First-line: oral iron

The plan is simple: oral iron, plus vitamin C, plus diet. Give it time and track the response, and most children turn around within weeks.

First-line oral iron
Ferrous sulfate

Dose: 3 to 6 mg/kg/day of elemental iron, usually split into two or three doses.

Give between meals. An emptier stomach absorbs better. If it upsets the gut, give with a little food.

Give with vitamin C. Orange juice or a chewable C boosts absorption.

Alternative oral forms
Ferrous gluconate or fumarate

Sometimes easier on the stomach than ferrous sulfate.

Same administration rules apply.

When oral fails
Intravenous iron

For severe deficiency, intolerance, or malabsorption.

Watch for infusion reactions; older forms can cause anaphylaxis.

Severe or symptomatic
Packed red cell transfusion

Only for severe anemia with instability, such as heart failure or hypoxia.

Oral iron and diet remain the long-term plan.

What helps and what blocks absorption

Give WITH iron (helps) Keep AWAY from iron (blocks)
Vitamin C: orange juice, strawberries, peppers, a chewable C Milk and dairy; calcium binds iron
Meat, fish, poultry as heme iron Tea and coffee; tannins block it
A little acid, such as lemon in water Antacids, proton-pump inhibitors, calcium supplements

The response you are tracking

  1. 1
    Days 2 to 3. The child often feels better. Irritability and fatigue ease first.
  2. 2
    Days 5 to 7. Reticulocytes rise. The marrow is responding.
  3. 3
    Weeks 2 to 4. Hemoglobin climbs. A rise over 1 g/dL is a good response.
  4. 4
    Months 1 to 2. Hemoglobin returns to normal.
  5. 5
    Then keep going. Continue iron 2 to 3 more months to refill the stores.
Do not stop when the hemoglobin looks good

This is a common miss. The hemoglobin normalizes and everyone relaxes. But the stores are still empty. Keep the iron going 2 to 3 months after a normal hemoglobin to rebuild ferritin. Stop early and it comes right back.

Bottom Line: Oral ferrous sulfate with vitamin C, between meals. Recheck the hemoglobin at 2 to 4 weeks. Keep giving iron 2 to 3 months after it normalizes.
07

What you do first

Two things drive the ordering here. Safety beats teaching, and what you can do now beats what needs an order. Give the dose right, and lock the bottle up.

  1. 1
    Use a straw or syringe to the back of the cheek. Liquid iron stains teeth. Rinse the mouth or brush after.
  2. 2
    Give between meals with vitamin C. An emptier stomach and a splash of orange juice both raise absorption.
  3. 3
    Keep it away from milk and antacids. Dairy, calcium, and antacids block iron. Separate them by at least 2 hours.
  4. 4
    Warn about the stool. It turns dark green to black. That is expected, not a bleed.
  5. 5
    Store it locked, high, out of sight. Iron overdose is a leading cause of pediatric poisoning death.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Straw Saves the Smile
Draw the dose into an oral syringe and aim for the back of the cheek. Follow with water or juice, then rinse or brush. For older kids, a straw bypasses the front teeth. And the dark stool is just unabsorbed iron; it is not melena.

When a child swallows the pills

Iron looks like candy

Children mistake adult iron pills for candy. Even a few adult-dose tablets can be life-threatening. This is a leading cause of poisoning death in young children. Store iron like any dangerous drug. Call poison control the moment ingestion is suspected.

Stages of iron toxicity

Phase Timing Findings
1. Gut0 to 6 hoursVomiting, bloody diarrhea, abdominal pain, blood in vomit
2. Latent6 to 24 hoursLooks better; deceptive, organ damage continues
3. Shock12 to 48 hoursHypotension, tachycardia, metabolic acidosis
4. Liver2 to 4 daysLiver failure, clotting problems, jaundice
5. Scarring2 to 6 weeksGut scarring leading to obstruction

Managing the overdose

  • Call poison control right away; bring the container.
  • Keep the child NPO. Get IV access. Give fluids for shock.
  • Abdominal x-ray shows the tablets; iron is radio-opaque.
  • Whole-bowel irrigation flushes the remaining pills.
  • Deferoxamine is the antidote. It binds iron and clears it in urine.
  • Activated charcoal does not bind iron, so it does not help.
The urine tells you it is working

Deferoxamine chelates free iron. The bound iron leaves in the urine and turns it reddish-orange. That color is the expected sign, not a complication.

Bottom Line: Straw, vitamin C, between meals, no milk. Dark stool is normal. Store it locked. Overdose means poison control, then deferoxamine; no charcoal, no ipecac.
08

What families need to hear

Most of this condition is prevented at the kitchen table. Good feeding advice does more than any pill, so keep the teaching plain and short.

Iron-rich foods to recommend

Heme iron, best absorbed. Lean beef, chicken, turkey, fish, liver.

Non-heme iron. Beans, lentils, tofu, spinach, fortified cereals.

Iron-fortified infant cereal. The first solid food, around 4 to 6 months.

Pair with vitamin C. Oranges, strawberries, peppers, tomatoes at the same meal.

Everyday extras. Eggs, dried apricots, raisins, blackstrap molasses.

Fortified grains. Breads and pastas; check the label.

Milk guidance

No cow's milk before age 1. Almost no iron, and it can cause gut bleeding.

Hold milk to 24 ounces a day after age 1. More fills the tummy and blocks iron.

Use iron-fortified formula in the first year if not breastfeeding.

Start iron at 4 months in the exclusively breastfed infant.

Home medication teaching

  • Give the iron exactly as prescribed.
  • Use a straw or oral syringe toward the back of the cheek.
  • Give it with orange juice or another vitamin C source.
  • Keep milk, dairy, and antacids at least 2 hours apart from it.
  • Expect dark green or black stools; this is normal.
  • Store it with a child-proof cap, high and out of sight.
  • Continue it 2 to 3 months after the hemoglobin normalizes.

When to call

Suspected ingestion. Call poison control at once: 1-800-222-1222.

Severe fatigue, chest pain, breathlessness. Go to the emergency department.

Pica. Eating paint, dust, or dirt; call to discuss a lead screen.

No gain after 2 to 4 weeks. Call the provider; check adherence and losses.

This one is preventable and treatable

Iron deficiency is one of the most fixable problems in pediatric nursing. A little teaching goes a long way, and most children respond within weeks. Your words at a well-child visit can prevent lasting harm.

Bottom Line: Iron-rich food plus vitamin C. No cow's milk before age 1, then hold it to 24 ounces a day. Give by straw, with juice, between meals. Store it safe.
