Iron-Deficiency Anemia

The most common childhood anemia. Risk factors, classic signs (pallor, fatigue, pica), microcytic hypochromic labs, iron supplementation with vitamin C, and the family teaching pearls every peds nurse needs.

High-Yield Summary

Eight facts that unlock every iron-deficiency anemia question.

  • Iron-deficiency anemia is the MOST COMMON anemia of childhood. Peak incidence: infants 6–24 months and adolescent girls with menstruation or rapid growth.
  • Top risk factors in infants/toddlers: introduction of cow\u2019s milk before 12 months, consuming >24 oz/day of cow\u2019s milk after 12 months (fills the stomach & reduces iron absorption), prolonged exclusive breastfeeding without iron supplementation, and prematurity.
  • Classic signs: pallor, fatigue, irritability, tachycardia, systolic flow murmur, delayed development, pica (eating non-food items like dirt, ice, paper), spoon nails (koilonychia), glossitis.
  • Lab pattern: MICROCYTIC (low MCV), HYPOCHROMIC (low MCH) anemia. Low Hgb, low Hct, LOW ferritin (first to drop), low serum iron, HIGH TIBC, low reticulocytes initially. Peripheral smear shows small, pale RBCs.
  • First-line treatment: oral ferrous sulfate 3–6 mg/kg/day elemental iron, given between meals, with vitamin C (orange juice) to boost absorption. Avoid with milk/dairy/antacids (inhibit absorption).
  • Administration pearls: give via straw or syringe toward the back of the cheek to prevent tooth staining; stool turns dark green/black (normal, expected); rinse mouth after; brush teeth.
  • Iron toxicity is a LEADING CAUSE of poisoning death in children. Store in child-proof containers out of reach. Overdose causes GI hemorrhage, shock, and liver failure. Antidote: deferoxamine.
  • Dietary prevention: iron-fortified infant cereal starting 4–6 months; lean meats, poultry, fish; leafy greens, beans, fortified grains; NO cow\u2019s milk before age 1; limit to 24 oz/day after.

Fundamentals

Why kids run out of iron

Iron is the critical component of hemoglobin. Without enough iron, red blood cells can\u2019t carry adequate oxygen. Children are at particular risk because they are growing rapidly and can quickly outpace their iron stores. Iron-deficiency anemia (IDA) is the most common cause of anemia in children worldwide.

Who Is at Highest Risk?

Infants 6–24 months — stores built up in utero are depleted around 6 months; diet must replace them.

Heavy cow\u2019s milk intake — >24 oz/day displaces iron-rich foods and can cause microscopic GI bleeding.

Prematurity / low birth weight — less iron stored in utero; higher growth demand.

Adolescent girls — menstrual losses + rapid growth + sometimes poor dietary intake.

Restrictive diets (vegan/vegetarian without planning), food insecurity.

Chronic blood loss — GI (Meckel\u2019s diverticulum, polyps), menses, parasitic infection.

Physiology in One Paragraph

Iron in food (especially heme iron from meat) is absorbed in the duodenum. Absorbed iron is incorporated into hemoglobin in developing RBCs. Body iron stores are held as ferritin in the liver, spleen, and marrow. When intake drops or losses rise, ferritin falls FIRST, then serum iron drops, total iron-binding capacity (TIBC) rises, and eventually hemoglobin declines. Understanding this sequence explains the lab pattern.

The Milk Problem

Cow\u2019s milk is the single most common modifiable risk factor for pediatric iron-deficiency anemia. It has almost no iron, it reduces appetite for iron-rich foods, and in infants it can cause microscopic GI bleeding. Teach families: no cow\u2019s milk before age 1, and after 1, limit to 16–24 oz/day.

🎯 Bottom Line: IDA = most common childhood anemia. Peak at 6–24 mo and adolescent girls. Milk overuse is the big preventable cause.
2

Clinical Manifestations

How IDA actually looks at the bedside

Mild IDA can be clinically silent — caught only on routine screening. Moderate to severe IDA produces classic findings that every peds nurse should recognize.

Pallor — best seen in the conjunctiva, palms, nail beds, and oral mucosa. Skin color can be deceiving in children with dark skin tones.

Fatigue, weakness — slower play, decreased appetite, sleeping more; irritability in younger kids.

Tachycardia, systolic flow murmur — heart compensating for low oxygen-carrying capacity.

Shortness of breath on exertion, poor feeding in infants.

Pica — craving and eating non-food items (dirt, ice, paper, paint chips). Classic & unique to iron deficiency.

Spoon-shaped nails (koilonychia) — concave, brittle nails in prolonged deficiency.

Glossitis / angular cheilitis — smooth red tongue; cracks at the corners of the mouth.

Developmental concerns — cognitive delay, irritability, poor school performance; persistent deficits can remain even after correction.

Pica Can Cause Lead Poisoning

Children with iron deficiency often develop pica, and if they eat paint chips, dirt, or dust from older homes, they are at risk for lead poisoning. Screen for lead exposure (blood lead level) in any child with pica, especially if they live in housing built before 1978. IDA and lead toxicity also potentiate each other.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
How to Assess Pallor Fairly
Skin pallor is not always reliable in kids with darker skin tones. Check the conjunctiva (pull down lower eyelid), the palmar creases, the oral mucosa, and the nail beds. Pale conjunctiva and palms are the most reliable findings across all skin colors. In light-skinned children, the face and lips may also appear pale.
🎯 Bottom Line: Pallor (conjunctiva/palms), fatigue, tachycardia + flow murmur, PICA (think IDA or lead), spoon nails. Screen developmentally-delayed or pale infants.

Diagnostic Workup

CBC pattern plus iron studies

Screening Recommendations

  • Universal screening at age 12 months (routine well-child visit).
  • Selective screening if risk factors (preterm, low SES, heavy milk intake, picky eater).
  • Adolescent girls screened at well-visits once menstruating.

NCLEX Point — Ferritin Drops FIRST

In early iron deficiency, ferritin is the first lab to drop (storage iron is used up before circulating iron). Low ferritin with a normal hemoglobin identifies early deficiency before overt anemia develops. Later, serum iron falls, TIBC rises, and finally hemoglobin drops.

Differentiating from Other Microcytic Anemias

Condition Key Difference
Iron deficiency anemia Low ferritin, high TIBC, responds to iron
Thalassemia Normal or high ferritin; target cells; ancestry; abnormal Hgb electrophoresis
Anemia of chronic disease Usually normocytic; high or normal ferritin; low TIBC
Lead poisoning Basophilic stippling on smear; elevated blood lead level; may coexist with IDA
🎯 Bottom Line: Microcytic, hypochromic anemia + LOW ferritin + high TIBC = IDA. Ferritin drops first. Rule out thalassemia and lead if atypical.

Treatment

Oral iron + vitamin C + diet — in that order

Oral Iron Supplementation

Ferrous Sulfate
First-Line Oral Iron

Dose: 3–6 mg/kg/day elemental iron, usually divided BID–TID.

Give between meals (empty stomach improves absorption); if GI upset, give with a small amount of food.

Take with vitamin C (orange juice, chewable vitamin C) to maximize absorption.

Ferrous Gluconate / Fumarate
Alternative Oral Forms

Sometimes tolerated better if ferrous sulfate causes GI side effects.

Similar administration principles.

Intravenous Iron (iron sucrose, ferric carboxymaltose)
When Oral Fails

For severe deficiency, intolerance, malabsorption, or inflammatory bowel disease.

Monitor for infusion reactions; older preparations can cause anaphylaxis.

PRBC Transfusion
Severe / Symptomatic

Reserved for severe anemia with hemodynamic compromise (heart failure, hypoxia).

Oral iron + diet = the long-term plan.

Foods That Help or Hinder Absorption

Enhancers (take WITH iron) Inhibitors (AVOID with iron)
Vitamin C — orange juice, strawberries, citrus, peppers, chewable C supplement Milk & dairy (calcium binds iron)
Meat, fish, poultry (heme iron) Tea & coffee (tannins)
A little acid helps (lemon in water) Antacids, PPIs, calcium supplements
Whole grains & bran (phytates — less effect if moderate)

Expected Response

  1. Within 48–72 hours: child often feels better; irritability, fatigue improve.
  2. Day 3–7: reticulocyte count rises (marrow responding).
  3. Day 14–21: hemoglobin begins to rise measurably (>1 g/dL typical response).
  4. By 1–2 months: hemoglobin normalizes.
  5. Continue iron for 2–3 more months AFTER hemoglobin normalizes to replenish ferritin stores.
Keep Giving Iron After the Numbers Look Good

A common mistake: families (and clinicians) stop iron as soon as hemoglobin normalizes. Stores must also be replenished. Continue iron for an additional 2–3 months after normal Hgb to rebuild ferritin. Stopping early leads to quick recurrence.

🎯 Bottom Line: Oral ferrous sulfate + vitamin C between meals. Check Hgb in 2–4 weeks. Continue 2–3 months AFTER Hgb normalizes. IV iron if oral fails.

Nursing & Safety Pearls

Administration pearls and poisoning prevention

Iron Administration Pearls

  1. Give liquid iron through a straw or with a syringe toward the back of the cheek to prevent iron from staining the teeth. Rinse the mouth or brush teeth afterward.
  2. Give between meals on a relatively empty stomach for best absorption. If GI upset, give with a small snack (not dairy).
  3. Give with vitamin C (orange juice, chewable vitamin C, or citrus) — dramatically increases absorption.
  4. Avoid giving with milk, dairy, antacids, or calcium supplements — separate by at least 2 hours.
  5. Warn about stool changes: stool becomes dark green to black — this is normal and expected (NOT a sign of GI bleeding).
  6. Expect some GI side effects: nausea, constipation, diarrhea, abdominal cramping. Usually resolves within a few days; adjust dose or form if severe.
  7. Schedule follow-up CBC in 2–4 weeks to confirm response. Good response = Hgb rise of >1 g/dL.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Straw Saves the Smile
Liquid iron stains teeth. To prevent discoloration in little ones: draw the dose into an oral syringe, aim for the back of the cheek, and follow immediately with water or juice. Rinse the mouth or brush teeth afterward. For older kids, use a straw that bypasses the front teeth. And remind parents: the dark green/black stool is just unabsorbed iron — not melena, not a bleed.

Iron Poisoning — A Pediatric Emergency

Iron Is a Leading Cause of Pediatric Poisoning Death

Children often mistake adult iron pills for candy. Even a small number of adult-dose tablets can cause life-threatening iron toxicity. Store in child-proof containers out of reach and sight. Teach families to treat iron as seriously as any other medication. Call poison control immediately if ingestion is suspected.

Stages of Iron Toxicity

Phase Timing Findings
1 — GI0–6 hoursVomiting, bloody diarrhea, abdominal pain, hematemesis
2 — Latent6–24 hoursApparent improvement — deceptive; organ damage continuing
3 — Shock / metabolic acidosis12–48 hoursHypotension, tachycardia, shock, metabolic acidosis
4 — Hepatic2–4 daysHepatic failure, coagulopathy, jaundice
5 — Obstruction2–6 weeksPyloric/intestinal scarring → obstruction

Management of Overdose

  • Poison control immediately; bring medication container.
  • NPO, IV access, aggressive fluid resuscitation for shock.
  • Abdominal X-ray to see radio-opaque iron tablets.
  • Whole-bowel irrigation with polyethylene glycol solution to flush out pills.
  • Deferoxamine is the ANTIDOTE — chelates iron and is excreted in urine (turns urine reddish-orange — expected, tells you it\u2019s working).
  • Activated charcoal does NOT bind iron — not useful.
NCLEX Point — Antidote and Urine Color

The antidote for iron poisoning is deferoxamine, which chelates free iron. It\u2019s excreted in the urine, turning it reddish-brown / \u201crose-colored\u201d. This is the expected therapeutic sign — not a complication.

🎯 Bottom Line: Straw/syringe, vitamin C, between meals, no milk. Stool turns dark/black (normal). Store safely — iron overdose kills. Antidote = deferoxamine.

Family Education & Prevention

Iron-rich diet + safe feeding for infants

Iron-Rich Foods to Recommend

Heme iron (best absorbed): lean beef, chicken, turkey, fish, liver.

Non-heme iron: beans, lentils, tofu, spinach, fortified cereals, pumpkin seeds.

Iron-fortified infant cereal as first solid food around 4–6 months.

Pair with vitamin C (oranges, strawberries, bell peppers, tomatoes) at the same meal.

Eggs, whole grains, blackstrap molasses, dried apricots, raisins.

Fortified breads and pastas; check labels.

Milk Guidance

NO cow\u2019s milk before age 1 — minimal iron, can cause GI bleeding.

Limit cow\u2019s milk to 16–24 oz/day after age 1 (more fills the tummy & blocks iron).

Iron-fortified infant formula if not breastfeeding during the first year.

Breastfed infants: iron supplementation starting at 4 months if exclusively breastfed.

Home Medication Teaching

  • Give iron exactly as prescribed — daily or divided doses.
  • Use a straw or oral syringe toward the back of the cheek.
  • Give with orange juice or other vitamin C source.
  • Avoid milk, dairy, antacids at the same time — separate by ≥2 hours.
  • Expect dark green/black stools — this is NORMAL.
  • Store safely — child-proof cap, high shelf, out of sight.
  • Continue for 2–3 months after hemoglobin normalizes.
  • Follow-up labs as directed.

Safety Net

Call poison control immediately if ingestion is suspected: 1-800-222-1222.

Severe fatigue, chest pain, shortness of breath → ED.

Pica behavior — eating paint, dust, dirt → call provider to discuss lead screening.

No improvement after 2–4 weeks of iron → consult provider; rule out adherence issues, malabsorption, ongoing loss.

A Preventable, Treatable Condition

IDA is one of the most reliably preventable and treatable problems in pediatric nursing. A little nutrition education goes a long way. Most children respond to oral iron within weeks. Your teaching during a well-child visit or an inpatient admission can prevent cognitive and growth consequences later.

🎯 Bottom Line: Iron-rich foods + vitamin C. No cow milk <1 yr, limit <24 oz/day after. Give via straw, with OJ, between meals. Dark stools are normal. Store safely.
