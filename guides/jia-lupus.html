JIA & Pediatric Lupus

Juvenile idiopathic arthritis types (oligoarticular, polyarticular, systemic) and pediatric systemic lupus erythematosus. Clinical presentation, medication risks, and family education.

7 Sections 14 Quiz Questions ~12 Min Read

Learning Objectives

Differentiate the three main types of juvenile idiopathic arthritis.
Recognize the clinical signs of pediatric SLE including rash and nephritis.
Identify uveitis screening needs and prolonged fever with systemic JIA.
Understand medication classes used and their major risks.
Apply nursing priorities including joint care, sun protection, and infection prevention.
Educate families about flare recognition, medication adherence, and sick contact avoidance.

High-Yield Summary

Ten facts to remember about JIA and pediatric lupus.

  • Juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA) = chronic joint inflammation beginning before age 16 with symptoms lasting ≥6 weeks. Three main types: oligoarticular, polyarticular, and systemic.
  • Oligoarticular (pauciarticular) JIA = MOST COMMON. \u22644 joints, often a single large joint (knee), usually young girls. Main risk: asymptomatic uveitis → blindness. Needs regular eye exams.
  • Polyarticular JIA: \u22655 joints, symmetric, small joints of hands/feet. More rapidly progressive; can resemble adult rheumatoid arthritis. RF-positive subset is most aggressive.
  • Systemic JIA (Still disease): daily spiking fevers, salmon-colored evanescent rash, hepatosplenomegaly, lymphadenopathy, pericarditis/pleuritis, then joint symptoms. Fever persists even with antibiotic treatment and often spikes in late afternoon/evening.
  • Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) = multi-organ autoimmune disease with antinuclear antibodies (ANA) positive. Classic findings: malar ("butterfly") rash over cheeks sparing the nasolabial folds, photosensitivity, oral ulcers, joint pain, fatigue.
  • Lupus organ involvement — any system: lupus nephritis (renal biopsy, leading cause of mortality), serositis (pericarditis/pleuritis), CNS (seizures, psychosis), hematologic (cytopenias), antiphospholipid syndrome (thrombosis).
  • Labs: JIA — elevated ESR/CRP, positive ANA in many (increases uveitis risk), RF in some polyarticular. SLE — ANA positive (screening), anti-dsDNA and anti-Smith (specific), low complement (C3, C4) during flares.
  • Treatment — NSAIDs + disease-modifying agents (methotrexate most common in JIA), biologics (TNF inhibitors, IL-6 inhibitors), and corticosteroids for flares. Lupus uses similar agents + hydroxychloroquine.
  • Medication risks: corticosteroids increase INFECTION risk, cause hyperglycemia, HTN, weight gain, AVN, growth suppression. Methotrexate = hepatotoxic, teratogenic (give folic acid). NSAIDs = GI / renal. IVIG = anaphylaxis risk.
  • Family teaching: fevers may persist even with antibiotics — that\u2019s inflammatory, not infectious; meticulous sun protection for lupus; medication adherence; avoid live vaccines on immunosuppression; report new symptoms promptly.

Autoimmune Rheumatic Disease Fundamentals

When the immune system turns on the joints and organs

Pediatric rheumatic diseases are chronic autoimmune conditions in which the immune system produces inflammation against the child\u2019s own tissues. The two most important examples tested in pediatric nursing are juvenile idiopathic arthritis (JIA) — primarily joint-based — and systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) — multi-organ.

What They Share

  • Autoimmune etiology — the cause is unknown; likely genetic predisposition + environmental trigger (viral illness, UV light, hormones).
  • Chronic relapsing course — periods of flare and remission; lifelong condition.
  • Inflammation — not infection. Fevers are cytokine-driven, NOT bacterial. Antibiotics won\u2019t bring the fever down.
  • Immunosuppressive treatment — NSAIDs, DMARDs, biologics, corticosteroids.
  • Growth, school, and social impact — chronic disease in childhood touches every domain.

JIA vs SLE at a Glance

Feature JIA Pediatric SLE
Primary target Joints (and eyes in oligoarticular) Any organ — skin, kidney, heart, brain
Peak age Young children (1–6) for oligoarticular; any age Adolescence (10–14+)
Sex ratio Girls > boys (most types) Girls 4:1
Key labs ESR/CRP up, ANA may be +, RF in polyarticular ANA + (screen), dsDNA + (specific), low complement
Big complication Uveitis (oligoarticular), MAS (systemic) Lupus nephritis
Why the Fever Keeps Coming

Parents are often frightened by persistent daily fevers. Teach them: in rheumatic disease, fever is caused by inflammatory cytokines, not infection. It doesn\u2019t respond to antibiotics. It DOES respond to anti-inflammatories like NSAIDs, steroids, and IL-1/IL-6 inhibitors. Expect fever to persist until the underlying inflammation is controlled — sometimes weeks.

🎯 Bottom Line: JIA = joints; SLE = multi-organ. Both are autoimmune, chronic, and fever from inflammation doesn\u2019t respond to antibiotics.

Juvenile Idiopathic Arthritis (JIA)

Three distinct patterns, different worries

JIA is diagnosed in a child under 16 with arthritis of at least 6 weeks\u2019 duration. The ILAR classification has several subtypes; for pediatric nursing the three classic patterns are oligoarticular, polyarticular, and systemic.

Oligoarticular (Pauciarticular)

\u2264 4 joints
Who

Most common type (~50%). Young girls, typically age 1–6.

Joints

\u22644 joints in first 6 months. Often the knee; sometimes ankle or elbow. Asymmetric.

Key Risk

Asymptomatic chronic UVEITIS in up to 20% \u2014 can cause blindness if not detected. Regular ophthalmology slit-lamp exams every 3\u20136 months (ANA+ girls are highest risk).

Prognosis

Generally favorable; many outgrow it.

Polyarticular

\u2265 5 joints
Who

~30%. Girls more than boys. Two peaks: 2\u20135 yr and 10\u201314 yr.

Joints

\u22655 joints in first 6 months; symmetric, small joints of hands and feet; also neck and TMJ.

Key Feature

Rapidly progressing and more destructive. RF-positive subset resembles adult RA and has the worst prognosis.

Prognosis

Variable; biologics have transformed outcomes.

Systemic JIA (Still Disease)

Multi-system
Who

~10%. Boys = girls; any age. Often younger children.

Classic Triad
  • Daily (\u201cquotidian\u201d) high spiking fevers — often afternoon/evening. Fever returns to or below baseline between spikes.
  • Salmon-colored, evanescent macular rash that comes and goes with fever.
  • Arthritis \u2014 may develop weeks to months after systemic symptoms.
Other Features

Hepatosplenomegaly, lymphadenopathy, pericarditis, pleuritis, elevated WBC and platelets.

Complication

Macrophage activation syndrome (MAS) \u2014 life-threatening cytokine storm.

General JIA Clinical Findings

Morning stiffness — gel phenomenon; stiffness improves with activity over the day.

Limping or refusing to walk — especially on waking; classic in oligoarticular.

Warm, swollen, tender joints; loss of ROM; may have effusion.

Growth disturbance — leg-length discrepancy from inflamed knee; micrognathia from TMJ involvement.

Uveitis can be \u201cquiet\u201d — child may not complain; slit-lamp exam is the only reliable detection.

Growth delay, fatigue, anemia of chronic disease are common.

Quiet Uveitis → Silent Blindness

Oligoarticular JIA can cause asymptomatic chronic uveitis. There are no pain, redness, or obvious symptoms. Without regular slit-lamp exams every 3–6 months, it can progress to cataracts, glaucoma, and blindness. ANA-positive girls under 6 with oligoarticular JIA are highest risk. Build the ophthalmology appointment into the plan of care.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Afternoon Fever + Salmon Rash + Swollen Joints
Systemic JIA\u2019s classic board presentation: a young child with daily afternoon/evening spiking fevers, a salmon-colored blanching macular rash that waxes and wanes with the fevers, and eventually joint symptoms. The child often appears well between fever spikes. Extensive infection workups come back negative. That\u2019s the pattern \u2014 name the disease, and rule out macrophage activation syndrome.
🎯 Bottom Line: Oligo (\u22644, knee, uveitis) \u2192 Poly (\u22655, symmetric, hands/feet) \u2192 Systemic (fevers, salmon rash, hepatosplenomegaly, pericarditis, MAS risk).

Pediatric Systemic Lupus Erythematosus (SLE)

The "great imitator" in adolescents

Pediatric SLE is a chronic multi-organ autoimmune disease. It is more common in adolescent girls and in children of African American, Hispanic, Asian, and Native American ancestry. Pediatric SLE is often more severe than adult-onset SLE.

Classic Clinical Manifestations

Skin Findings

Visible
  • Malar ("butterfly") rash over cheeks and bridge of nose, SPARING the nasolabial folds.
  • Discoid rash — scaly plaques, can scar.
  • Photosensitivity — rash/flare with sun exposure.
  • Oral and nasal ulcers (often painless).
  • Alopecia.

Musculoskeletal

Common
  • Arthralgia / arthritis — often symmetric, small joints; non-erosive.
  • Myalgia, myositis.
  • Avascular necrosis (from steroid therapy or disease).

Renal (Lupus Nephritis)

Serious
  • Affects up to 50–80% of pediatric lupus.
  • Proteinuria, hematuria, hypertension, edema; may progress to nephrotic/nephritic syndrome or ESRD.
  • Renal biopsy classifies severity (Class I–VI).
  • Leading cause of lupus mortality.

Cardiopulmonary / CNS / Heme

Multi-System
  • Serositis — pericarditis, pleuritis.
  • CNS — seizures, psychosis, cognitive changes.
  • Hematologic — anemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia (ITP-like).
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome — thrombosis, recurrent pregnancy loss.
  • Raynaud\u2019s phenomenon.

SOAP BRAIN MD — Classic ACR Criteria Mnemonic

Serositis (pleuritis, pericarditis)

Oral ulcers (usually painless)

Arthritis (non-erosive)

Photosensitivity

Blood disorders (hemolytic anemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia)

Renal (proteinuria, casts)

ANA positive

Immunologic (dsDNA, Smith, antiphospholipid)

Neurologic (seizures, psychosis)

Malar rash

Discoid rash

Lupus Nephritis Drives Mortality

Renal involvement in pediatric SLE is very common and is the leading cause of morbidity and mortality. Monitor urinalysis (proteinuria, hematuria, RBC casts), BP, BUN/Cr, complement levels (C3/C4) at every visit. A renal biopsy stages disease and guides therapy. Aggressive treatment preserves kidney function.

🎯 Bottom Line: SLE = adolescent girl + multi-organ + malar rash (spares nasolabial folds) + ANA+. Watch kidneys and complement. SOAP BRAIN MD = 11 criteria.

Diagnostic Workup

Labs that separate the two (and rule out infection)

JIA Workup

  • History + physical — arthritis >6 weeks in a child <16 is the core diagnosis; infection, trauma, and malignancy must be excluded.
  • ESR, CRP — often elevated; systemic JIA may have very high levels.
  • CBC — anemia of chronic disease; systemic JIA often has leukocytosis and thrombocytosis.
  • ANA — positive in up to 70% of oligoarticular; raises uveitis risk.
  • Rheumatoid factor (RF), anti-CCP — differentiates RF-positive polyarticular (aggressive).
  • HLA-B27 — associated with enthesitis-related arthritis (a JIA subtype).
  • Ferritin — extremely high in systemic JIA and MAS.
  • Imaging — joint X-rays, MRI; ultrasound for synovitis.
  • Ophthalmology slit-lamp exam — essential in oligoarticular (every 3–6 mo).

SLE Workup

  • ANA (screening test) — positive in >95% of SLE, but not specific.
  • Anti-dsDNA — specific for SLE; correlates with disease activity and nephritis.
  • Anti-Smith (Sm) — specific but less common.
  • Complement levels (C3, C4) — LOW during active disease (consumed by immune complexes).
  • Antiphospholipid antibodies (lupus anticoagulant, anti-cardiolipin, anti-beta-2-glycoprotein) — thrombosis risk.
  • CBC — anemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia.
  • Urinalysis — proteinuria, hematuria, RBC casts indicate nephritis.
  • BUN/Cr, electrolytes — renal function.
  • Renal biopsy — classifies lupus nephritis when renal involvement present.
NCLEX Point — Complement Drops in Lupus Flare

During an SLE flare, immune complexes consume complement — so C3 and C4 DECREASE. That\u2019s a key lab to monitor disease activity. Anti-dsDNA rises during flares too. These two trend opposite each other and tell you what\u2019s happening.

🎯 Bottom Line: JIA \u2192 arthritis >6 wk + ESR/CRP + ANA + RF, eye exams. SLE \u2192 ANA + dsDNA + low C3/C4 + UA for nephritis.

Treatment & Medication Risks

NSAIDs, methotrexate, biologics, steroids, IVIG

Treatment aims to control inflammation, preserve function, and prevent complications. A step-up approach is common: start with NSAIDs, escalate to disease-modifying agents and biologics, with steroids for flares.

NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen, indomethacin)
Anti-Inflammatory

First-line for pain and inflammation in JIA. Take with food; monitor renal and GI.

Risks: GI ulcer/bleeding, renal dysfunction, bronchospasm (especially aspirin), fluid retention.

Methotrexate (MTX)
DMARD — Gold Standard

Oral or subQ weekly. First-line DMARD for JIA and lupus arthritis.

Risks: hepatotoxicity (monitor LFTs), bone marrow suppression, mouth sores, teratogenic (contraception essential, give folic acid to reduce side effects).

Hydroxychloroquine (Plaquenil)
Antimalarial — Lupus

Cornerstone lupus therapy; reduces flares and mortality. Also used in JIA.

Risks: retinal toxicity (yearly ophthalmology exam), GI upset, rash.

Corticosteroids (prednisone, methylprednisolone)
Potent Anti-Inflammatory

For severe flares, nephritis, CNS involvement, MAS. Short bursts preferred when possible.

Risks: increased infection risk, hyperglycemia, HTN, weight gain, osteoporosis, AVN, growth suppression, mood changes, cushingoid appearance, cataracts. Taper slowly to prevent adrenal crisis.

Biologics — TNF inhibitors (etanercept, adalimumab)
Targeted DMARD

SubQ injections; effective in polyarticular JIA.

Risks: infection (especially TB, reactivate latent infections), injection site reactions, rare malignancy. Screen for TB before starting.

IL-1 / IL-6 Inhibitors (anakinra, tocilizumab)
Cytokine-Specific Biologics

For systemic JIA and MAS; tocilizumab also used in refractory disease.

Risks: infection, neutropenia, elevated lipids (tocilizumab), injection reactions.

Immunosuppressants (mycophenolate, cyclophosphamide, azathioprine)
For Lupus Nephritis / Severe Disease

Used for severe SLE and lupus nephritis.

Risks: bone marrow suppression, infection, infertility (cyclophosphamide), secondary malignancy, teratogenicity.

IVIG
Immunomodulatory

Used in refractory disease, some MAS cases; lupus with cytopenias.

Risks: anaphylaxis, aseptic meningitis, renal dysfunction, volume overload, rare thromboembolism.

Antivirals (acyclovir, valacyclovir)
Infection Prophylaxis / Treatment

May be prescribed for herpes zoster prophylaxis or active infection in immunosuppressed children.

Risks: renal dose adjustment, GI upset, neuropsychiatric effects with high doses.

Steroids + Immunosuppression = Infection Risk

Every agent on the list above suppresses the immune system to some degree. Corticosteroids are the classic example. Educate families that fever, cough, rash, or mouth sores need prompt evaluation, especially because these kids also can\u2019t mount a normal fever response. No live vaccines while on immunosuppression. Vaccinate household contacts with inactivated flu.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Methotrexate + Folic Acid = Fewer Side Effects
Methotrexate is a folate antagonist. Giving folic acid (or folinic acid/leucovorin) daily or weekly dramatically reduces MTX side effects like mouth sores, nausea, and hair thinning — WITHOUT reducing MTX\u2019s therapeutic effect on inflammation. Confirm folic acid is prescribed alongside MTX and that the family understands to take it (often on non-MTX days). Never give folic acid and MTX simultaneously \u2014 separate them.
🎯 Bottom Line: NSAIDs → MTX (+ folic acid) → biologics (TNFi, IL-1/IL-6) → steroids for flares. Lupus adds hydroxychloroquine. All suppress immunity → infection risk.

Nursing Priorities

Joint care, sun protection, and infection prevention

Joint & Activity Care (JIA)

Morning stiffness — warm bath or shower at wake-up; gentle morning ROM.

Heat (warm compresses, heating pad on low) eases stiffness; cold eases acute swelling after activity.

Encourage low-impact activity (swimming, biking, yoga); PT/OT for ROM and strengthening.

Balance rest and activity; splints for joint protection (especially at night).

Slit-lamp eye exams every 3–6 months for oligoarticular JIA (uveitis screening).

504 plan at school — extra time between classes, option for elevator/ground floor, PE modifications.

Skin & Sun Care (SLE)

Broad-spectrum sunscreen SPF 50+ daily, even indoors with windows; reapply q2h outdoors.

Wide-brim hats, UV-protective clothing, sunglasses.

Avoid peak sun hours (10 am–4 pm); seek shade.

UV from fluorescent bulbs can trigger flare; use UV-filter films if needed.

Monitor for mouth/nasal ulcers; rinses and soft diet.

Daily hydroxychloroquine improves skin disease and prevents flares.

Infection Prevention (Both Conditions)

Meticulous handwashing, avoid sick contacts, avoid crowded spaces during flares or high-dose steroids.

Stay current on INACTIVATED vaccines (injectable flu, pneumococcal, meningococcal, Tdap); NO LIVE vaccines on immunosuppression.

TB screen BEFORE starting biologic therapy (TNF inhibitors can reactivate TB).

Fever response — the immunosuppressed child may not mount a normal fever; any fever gets prompt evaluation.

Dental care for mouth ulcers; avoid flossing if thrombocytopenic.

Lab monitoring: CBC, LFTs, renal function, UA, complement, dsDNA per schedule.

Monitoring for Complications

Pericarditis/pleuritis: chest pain, dyspnea, friction rub, pleural effusion

CNS lupus: seizures, psychosis, confusion, severe headache

Nephritis: proteinuria, hematuria, new hypertension, edema, weight gain

Macrophage activation syndrome (MAS): persistent fever, very high ferritin, cytopenias, hepatitis, DIC — ICU emergency

Infection while immunosuppressed: any fever, skin rash, cough, mouth sores → call

Uveitis red eye or vision changes: ophthalmology same day

🎯 Bottom Line: Joint care + morning warmth + splints + eye exams (JIA). Sunscreen + hydroxychloroquine + UA monitoring (SLE). Infection prevention for both. Watch for MAS.

Family Education

Flares, adherence, and sick-day plans

What Families Need to Know

Fever from inflammation keeps coming — antibiotics won\u2019t stop it. Trust the rheum team; call for new symptoms.

Daily medications are essential — even when the child feels well, medications prevent flare and joint damage.

Sun protection for SLE — sunscreen, hats, clothing, avoid peak hours; UV from windows and fluorescent bulbs matters.

Avoid sick contacts — handwashing, no crowds during flares; consider masks in high-risk periods.

Eye exams on schedule — every 3–6 months for oligoarticular JIA; yearly retinal exam on hydroxychloroquine.

School accommodations (504 plan) — extra time between classes, locker on ground floor, PE modifications, access to water and bathroom.

When to Call the Rheum Team or ED

Fever > 101\u00b0F while on biologics/immunosuppressants — always call

New or worsening rash, mouth ulcers

Sudden swelling (face/eyes/legs), weight gain, dark or foamy urine — nephritis

Chest pain, trouble breathing — serositis or PE

Eye pain, redness, vision change — uveitis or keratitis

Seizure, psychosis, severe headache, confusion — CNS lupus / emergency

Psychosocial & Adolescent Considerations

  • Body image — steroid-induced cushingoid appearance, malar rash, alopecia; validate distress; connect with mental health resources.
  • Adherence challenges — teens may stop taking medications. Open, judgment-free conversation; pill organizers; reminder apps; teen support groups.
  • Fertility and pregnancy — counsel adolescents about teratogenicity (MTX, mycophenolate, cyclophosphamide); contraception planning.
  • Mental health — depression and anxiety are common; screen at every visit.
  • Transition to adult care — begin planning by mid-adolescence; coordinate hand-off to adult rheumatologist.
Chronic Doesn\u2019t Mean Limited

With modern therapy, most children with JIA live essentially normal lives; many achieve long-term remission. Pediatric lupus, while more serious, can be controlled with proper medication and lifestyle. Encourage school, sports within the plan, friendships, and future-oriented thinking. Your long-term presence during visits matters to how they cope and adhere.

🎯 Bottom Line: Inflammatory fevers are expected. Adherence + sun protection + eye exams + school plan. Call for fever/rash/edema/chest pain/vision change. Transition planning matters.
