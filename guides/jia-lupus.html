JIA & Pediatric Lupus

Two autoimmune diseases students blur together. The joint pain that is inflammatory not mechanical, the eye that goes blind with no symptoms, and the kidney that decides a lupus outcome.

By the end, without notes

  • Say whether a swollen joint is inflammatory or mechanical, and name the finding that told you
  • Explain why oligoarticular JIA needs a slit-lamp eye exam even when the eye looks fine
  • Read a lupus case as a multi-organ disease, and name the organ that decides the outcome

01

What actually gets you

These two diseases are not hard to define. They are hard to tell apart, and hard to take seriously enough.

Points get lost here in three ways. Read these first.

You read the joint pain as mechanical

Inflammatory joint pain is worst in the morning. It eases as the child moves through the day. Mechanical pain does the opposite; it worsens with use. Call the morning stiffness that loosens up mechanical, and you miss JIA.

You forgot the silent eye

Oligoarticular JIA can inflame the eye with no symptoms. No pain, no redness, no complaint. By the time vision changes, the damage is done. That is why the slit-lamp exam is scheduled, not offered.

You treated lupus as a rash

The butterfly rash is the famous part. It is not the dangerous part. Lupus attacks the kidneys, and lupus nephritis is what kills. Read a lupus case as a whole-body disease.

Bottom Line: Stop studying these as two rashes. Study them as decisions: inflammatory or mechanical pain, screen the eye or lose it, lupus rash or lupus kidney.
02

The four things

For each disease below, you should be able to say four things with the book closed. The definition. The clue in a scenario. The priority action. The first-line drug and its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet.

Type Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line drug
Oligoarticular JIA Four or fewer joints inflamed, often one large joint like the knee. A young girl limps in the morning with one swollen knee. Book the slit-lamp eye exam. Protect the joint. NSAID first. Methotrexate if it spreads.
Polyarticular JIA Five or more joints, symmetric, the small joints of hands and feet. Symmetric swollen fingers that resemble adult rheumatoid arthritis. Start a disease-modifying drug early. This type destroys joints. Methotrexate with folic acid. Biologics if it progresses.
Systemic JIA Daily spiking fevers and a salmon rash, then arthritis weeks later. Afternoon fever, a rash that comes and goes, well between spikes. Rule out macrophage activation syndrome. Treat the inflammation. Steroids and IL-1 or IL-6 inhibitors, not antibiotics.
Pediatric SLE A multi-organ autoimmune disease with a positive ANA. Adolescent girl, butterfly rash, joint pain, fatigue, protein in the urine. Check the kidneys: urinalysis, blood pressure, complement. Hydroxychloroquine for everyone. Immunosuppressants for organ disease.

Write the four rows on one index card, by hand. JIA is joints. Lupus is every organ. That card is most of the exam.

03

Where these two come from

Both diseases are autoimmune. The immune system attacks the child's own tissue.

Both run a chronic course of flare and remission. Both are lifelong.

One idea carries most of the confusion here. The fever is inflammation, not infection. Antibiotics will not touch it.

What they share

  • Autoimmune cause. The trigger is unknown; likely a genetic tendency plus a trigger like a virus, UV light, or hormones.
  • Flares and remissions. The disease comes and goes for life.
  • Inflammation, not infection. Fevers are cytokine-driven, so antibiotics do nothing.
  • Immunosuppressive treatment. NSAIDs, DMARDs, biologics, corticosteroids.
  • A whole-childhood impact. Chronic disease touches growth, school, and friendships.

JIA versus lupus at a glance

Feature JIA Pediatric SLE
Primary target Joints, and the eyes in the oligoarticular type Any organ: skin, kidney, heart, brain
Peak age Young children for oligoarticular; any age otherwise Adolescence, roughly 10 to 14 and up
Sex ratio Girls more than boys in most types Girls about 4 to 1
Key labs ESR and CRP up, ANA may be positive, RF in some polyarticular ANA positive to screen, dsDNA positive to confirm, low complement
Big complication Uveitis in oligoarticular, MAS in systemic Lupus nephritis
The axis that separates them

JIA lives in the joints. Lupus lives everywhere. When a case gives you kidneys, blood counts, and a facial rash together, it is lupus, not arthritis. When it gives you one hot joint and a limp, it is JIA.

Why the fever keeps coming

Parents are frightened by daily fevers. Teach them the cause. The fever is driven by inflammatory cytokines, not infection. It does not answer to antibiotics. It answers to anti-inflammatories, and it can persist for weeks until the disease is controlled.

Bottom Line: JIA is joints. Lupus is multi-organ. Both are autoimmune and chronic, and an inflammatory fever does not respond to antibiotics.
04

Juvenile idiopathic arthritis

JIA means arthritis in a child under age 16, lasting at least 6 weeks. The pattern in the first 6 months sorts it into three types.

Oligoarticular: four or fewer joints

The most common type, about half of cases. Usually young girls, ages 1 to 6.

Four or fewer joints in the first 6 months. Often the knee. Asymmetric.

The risk: silent uveitis, in up to 1 in 5. ANA-positive girls are highest risk. Prognosis is otherwise good, and many outgrow it.

Polyarticular: five or more joints

About 30 percent. Girls more than boys. Peaks at 2 to 5 and 10 to 14 years.

Five or more joints, symmetric, the small joints of hands and feet. Also the neck and the jaw.

The worry: it progresses fast and destroys joints. The RF-positive subset behaves like adult rheumatoid arthritis and has the worst outlook.

Systemic (Still disease): whole body

About 10 percent. Boys and girls equally. Often younger children.

Daily high spiking fevers, often afternoon or evening. A salmon-colored rash comes and goes with the fever. Arthritis follows weeks to months later.

Also hepatosplenomegaly, lymphadenopathy, pericarditis, and pleuritis. WBC and platelets run high.

The emergency: macrophage activation syndrome, a life-threatening cytokine storm.

The joint pain that fools you

Inflammatory or mechanical

Inflammatory pain is stiff in the morning and loosens with movement. This is the gel phenomenon. Mechanical pain is the reverse; it is fine at rest and worse with use. A child who limps on waking and warms up over the morning has inflammatory pain.

What you see

  • Morning stiffness that eases as the day goes on.
  • Limping or refusing to walk, worst on waking; classic in the oligoarticular type.
  • Warm, swollen, tender joints, with lost range of motion and sometimes an effusion.
  • Growth disturbance, such as leg-length difference from an inflamed knee or a small jaw from TMJ disease.
  • Quiet uveitis, which the child will not report; only the slit-lamp finds it.
  • Fatigue and anemia of chronic disease.
Quiet uveitis, silent blindness

Oligoarticular JIA causes uveitis with no pain and no redness. Left alone, it moves to cataracts, glaucoma, and blindness. ANA-positive girls under 6 are the highest risk. Build the slit-lamp exam every 3 to 6 months into the plan of care.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Loosens Up or Locks Up
Inflammatory joint pain loosens up with movement; mechanical pain locks up with use. If the stiffness is worst on waking and better by lunchtime, think JIA. That one question sorts most joint-pain stems.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Afternoon Fever Plus Salmon Rash
The systemic JIA picture is a young child with daily afternoon fevers, a salmon rash that waxes and wanes with them, and joints later. The child often looks well between spikes and the infection workup is negative. Name the disease, then rule out macrophage activation syndrome.
Bottom Line: Oligo is four or fewer joints, the knee, and the eye. Poly is five or more, symmetric, hands and feet. Systemic is fevers, salmon rash, organomegaly, and MAS risk.
05

Pediatric lupus

Pediatric lupus is a chronic disease of the whole body. It hits adolescent girls most.

It runs more severe in children than in adults. It is more common in Black, Hispanic, Asian, and Native American children.

Skin
  • Malar butterfly rash over the cheeks and nose, sparing the nasolabial folds.
  • Discoid rash, scaly plaques that can scar.
  • Photosensitivity; the rash flares with sun.
  • Oral and nasal ulcers, often painless.
  • Alopecia.
Musculoskeletal
  • Arthralgia and arthritis, often symmetric small joints, and non-erosive.
  • Myalgia and myositis.
  • Avascular necrosis, from the disease or from steroids.
Renal (lupus nephritis)
  • Affects up to half to four-fifths of pediatric lupus.
  • Proteinuria, hematuria, hypertension, edema; can reach nephrotic or nephritic syndrome or kidney failure.
  • A renal biopsy classifies the severity.
  • The leading cause of lupus death.
Heart, lung, brain, blood
  • Serositis: pericarditis and pleuritis.
  • CNS: seizures, psychosis, cognitive change.
  • Blood: anemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia.
  • Antiphospholipid syndrome: clots and pregnancy loss.
  • Raynaud phenomenon.
Copy this down

The classic criteria spell SOAP BRAIN MD. Serositis. Oral ulcers. Arthritis. Photosensitivity. Blood disorders. Renal. ANA positive. Immunologic, meaning dsDNA and Smith. Neurologic. Malar rash. Discoid rash.

Lupus nephritis drives the outcome

Kidney disease in pediatric lupus is common, and it is the leading cause of death. Watch the urinalysis for protein, blood, and RBC casts. Watch the blood pressure, the BUN and creatinine, and the complement. A renal biopsy stages the disease and guides therapy.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Rash That Spares the Folds
The lupus butterfly rash crosses the cheeks and the bridge of the nose, and it spares the nasolabial folds. That sparing is the detail that separates it from other facial rashes. If a stem stresses the smile lines staying clear, it is describing lupus.
Bottom Line: Lupus is an adolescent girl, multi-organ disease, a malar rash that spares the folds, and a positive ANA. Watch the kidneys and the complement.
06

The labs that separate them

The labs are where JIA and lupus split apart. They also rule out infection.

JIA workup

  • History and physical: arthritis over 6 weeks in a child under 16 is the core. Exclude infection, trauma, and cancer.
  • ESR and CRP: often up; systemic JIA can run very high.
  • CBC: anemia of chronic disease; systemic JIA often adds high WBC and platelets.
  • ANA: positive in up to 70 percent of oligoarticular; it raises uveitis risk.
  • RF and anti-CCP: mark the aggressive RF-positive polyarticular subset.
  • HLA-B27: tied to enthesitis-related arthritis.
  • Ferritin: extremely high in systemic JIA and in MAS.
  • Imaging and slit-lamp: joint X-ray, MRI, ultrasound; eye exams in oligoarticular.

Lupus workup

  • ANA: the screening test, positive in over 95 percent, but not specific.
  • Anti-dsDNA: specific for lupus; it tracks disease activity and nephritis.
  • Anti-Smith: specific but less common.
  • Complement C3 and C4: low in active disease, consumed by immune complexes.
  • Antiphospholipid antibodies: lupus anticoagulant, anti-cardiolipin, anti-beta-2-glycoprotein; clot risk.
  • CBC: anemia, leukopenia, thrombocytopenia.
  • Urinalysis: protein, blood, and RBC casts point to nephritis.
  • BUN, creatinine, and renal biopsy: stage the kidney disease.
ANA screens, dsDNA confirms

ANA is sensitive but not specific; it turns positive in many conditions. Anti-dsDNA and anti-Smith are the specific ones. In a flare, complement drops as anti-dsDNA rises. Those two trend opposite each other, and together they read out disease activity.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Complement Down, dsDNA Up
A lupus flare consumes complement, so C3 and C4 fall while anti-dsDNA climbs. Falling complement plus rising dsDNA is the flare signature. When a stem hands you both, the disease is active and treatment is about to escalate.
Bottom Line: JIA is arthritis over 6 weeks, ESR and CRP, ANA and RF, plus eye exams. Lupus is ANA to screen, dsDNA to confirm, low complement, and a urinalysis for nephritis.
07

The drugs and what they cost

Treatment controls inflammation, protects function, and prevents damage. The approach steps up.

Start with NSAIDs. Add a disease-modifying drug. Escalate to biologics. Use steroids for flares.

NSAIDs
Ibuprofen, naproxen, indomethacin

First-line for JIA pain and inflammation. Give with food.

Risks: GI ulcer and bleeding, renal dysfunction, fluid retention.

Methotrexate
DMARD, the gold standard

Oral or subQ, once a week. First-line DMARD for JIA and lupus arthritis.

Risks: hepatotoxic, so monitor LFTs. Bone marrow suppression and mouth sores. Teratogenic. Give folic acid to cut side effects.

Hydroxychloroquine
Antimalarial, the lupus backbone

Cuts flares and mortality in lupus, and is used in JIA too.

Risks: retinal toxicity, so a yearly eye exam. GI upset and rash.

Corticosteroids
Prednisone, methylprednisolone

For severe flares, nephritis, CNS disease, and MAS. Prefer short bursts.

Risks: infection, hyperglycemia, hypertension, weight gain, osteoporosis, avascular necrosis, growth suppression, cushingoid look, cataracts. Taper slowly to avoid adrenal crisis.

TNF inhibitors
Etanercept, adalimumab

SubQ injections. Effective in polyarticular JIA.

Risks: infection, including reactivated TB. Screen for TB before starting. Injection reactions, rare malignancy.

IL-1 and IL-6 inhibitors
Anakinra, tocilizumab

For systemic JIA and MAS, and refractory disease.

Risks: infection, neutropenia, high lipids with tocilizumab, injection reactions.

Immunosuppressants
Mycophenolate, cyclophosphamide, azathioprine

For severe lupus and lupus nephritis.

Risks: bone marrow suppression, infection, infertility with cyclophosphamide, secondary cancer, teratogenicity.

IVIG
Immunomodulatory

For refractory disease, some MAS, and lupus with low blood counts.

Risks: anaphylaxis, aseptic meningitis, renal dysfunction, volume overload.

Antivirals
Acyclovir, valacyclovir

For herpes zoster prophylaxis or active infection in immunosuppressed children.

Risks: renal dose adjustment, GI upset, neuropsychiatric effects at high doses.

Immunosuppression means infection risk

Every drug on this list suppresses the immune system. Steroids are the classic example. Teach families that fever, cough, rash, or mouth sores need prompt evaluation. Give no live vaccines during immunosuppression, and vaccinate household contacts with inactivated flu.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Methotrexate Plus Folic Acid
Methotrexate is a folate antagonist, so folic acid cuts the mouth sores, nausea, and hair thinning without weakening the drug. Confirm folic acid is prescribed with it. Take them on separate days, never together, so the folate does not blunt the weekly dose.
Bottom Line: NSAIDs, then methotrexate with folic acid, then biologics, with steroids for flares. Lupus adds hydroxychloroquine. All of them suppress immunity, so infection is the shared risk.
08

What you do first

The exam asks what you do first, not what you know. Here is the order.

  1. 1
    Protect the joints and warm them up. A warm bath or shower on waking, then gentle range of motion. Heat eases stiffness; cold eases the acute swelling after activity. Encourage low-impact movement like swimming or biking, and use night splints for joint protection.
  2. 2
    Screen the silent eye. Slit-lamp exams every 3 to 6 months in oligoarticular JIA, even when the eye looks normal. A red eye or a vision change goes to ophthalmology the same day.
  3. 3
    Guard the skin from the sun. Broad-spectrum sunscreen SPF 50 or higher daily, reapplied every 2 hours outdoors. Add wide-brim hats and UV-protective clothing. Avoid peak hours from 10 to 4, and remember that fluorescent bulbs and windows still pass UV.
  4. 4
    Teach every medication. NSAIDs with food. Methotrexate weekly with folic acid on a different day, plus LFTs and CBC. Steroids never stopped abruptly and taken with food. TB screen before a biologic. A yearly retinal exam on hydroxychloroquine.
  5. 5
    Prevent infection. Meticulous handwashing and avoiding sick contacts. Keep inactivated vaccines current, and give no live vaccines during immunosuppression. Any fever gets evaluated, because these children may not mount a normal one. Avoid flossing if the platelets are low.
  6. 6
    Watch for the complications below. Monitor labs on schedule: CBC, LFTs, renal function, urinalysis, complement, and dsDNA.

Pericarditis or pleuritis: chest pain, dyspnea, a friction rub, an effusion.

CNS lupus: seizures, psychosis, confusion, a severe headache.

Nephritis: proteinuria, hematuria, new hypertension, edema, weight gain.

Macrophage activation syndrome: persistent fever, sky-high ferritin, low counts, hepatitis, DIC. An ICU emergency.

Infection while immunosuppressed: any fever, rash, cough, or mouth sores.

Uveitis: a red eye or a vision change. Ophthalmology the same day.

Bottom Line: Joint care and eye screening for JIA. Sunscreen and complement monitoring for lupus. Infection prevention for both. Watch for MAS.
09

What families take home

Families carry this disease at home between visits. A few points decide how well they cope.

  • Inflammatory fever keeps coming, and antibiotics will not stop it. Trust the rheum team and call for new symptoms.
  • Daily medications are essential, even when the child feels well; they prevent flares and joint damage.
  • Sun protection for lupus: sunscreen, hats, clothing, and avoiding peak hours. Windows and fluorescent bulbs count.
  • Avoid sick contacts: handwashing and no crowds during flares or high-dose steroids.
  • Eye exams on schedule: every 3 to 6 months for oligoarticular JIA, and yearly on hydroxychloroquine.
  • School accommodations, a 504 plan with extra time between classes, a ground-floor locker, and PE changes.

When to call the team or the ED

Fever over 101 F on a biologic or immunosuppressant. Always call.

New or worsening rash or mouth ulcers.

Sudden swelling of the face, eyes, or legs, weight gain, or dark foamy urine. Think nephritis.

Chest pain or trouble breathing. Think serositis or a clot.

Eye pain, redness, or a vision change.

Seizure, psychosis, severe headache, or confusion. Treat as CNS lupus.

The adolescent piece

  • Body image: the cushingoid look, the malar rash, and hair loss hurt. Validate it and offer mental health support.
  • Adherence: teens stop taking pills. Talk openly, use organizers and reminder apps, and connect them with peers.
  • Fertility: counsel on teratogenic drugs like methotrexate, mycophenolate, and cyclophosphamide, and plan contraception.
  • Mental health: depression and anxiety are common; screen at every visit.
  • Transition to adult care: start planning by mid-adolescence.
Chronic does not mean limited

Most children with JIA live essentially normal lives, and many reach long remission. Lupus is more serious, but it can be controlled with medication and care. Encourage school, sport within the plan, and friendships. Your presence at visits shapes how they cope and adhere.

Bottom Line: Inflammatory fevers are expected. Adherence, sun protection, eye exams, and a school plan carry the home care. Call for fever, rash, edema, chest pain, or a vision change.
