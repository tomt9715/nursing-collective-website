Kawasaki Disease

Acute febrile vasculitis in young children — the leading cause of acquired pediatric heart disease. CRASH and BURN criteria, IVIG, and coronary aneurysm surveillance.

8 Sections 14 Quiz Questions ~12 Min Read

Learning Objectives

Apply the CRASH and BURN criteria to recognize Kawasaki disease at the bedside.
Differentiate the acute, subacute, and convalescent phases of the illness.
Identify coronary artery aneurysms as the most serious complication and understand echo follow-up.
Administer IVIG safely within the first 10 days of illness.
Explain why high-dose aspirin is used in Kawasaki despite the usual pediatric Reye-syndrome caution.
Educate families on aspirin precautions, delayed vaccines, and long-term cardiac follow-up.

High-Yield Summary

Eight facts that will carry you through any Kawasaki question.

  • Kawasaki Disease = acute medium-vessel vasculitis. Cause unknown (likely post-infectious autoimmune). Peak age 6 months–5 years; more common in boys and children of Asian descent.
  • #1 cause of acquired pediatric heart disease in developed countries. The emergency is NOT the rash — it's the coronary artery aneurysms.
  • CRASH and BURN: Conjunctivitis (bilateral, non-purulent) · Rash · Adenopathy (cervical) · Strawberry tongue / lip changes · Hands/feet swollen & peeling · BURN = fever ≥5 days. Diagnosis requires fever + 4 of 5 criteria.
  • Irritability is disproportionate. These kids are inconsolable — out of proportion to how sick they look. Classic clinical pearl.
  • Treatment = IVIG + high-dose aspirin. IVIG 2 g/kg single infusion within first 10 days drops aneurysm risk from 25% to <5%. Aspirin 80–100 mg/kg/day while febrile, then low-dose 3–5 mg/kg/day for 6–8 weeks.
  • Kawasaki is the ONE exception to "no aspirin in children" — Reye benefit/risk reversed. But STOP aspirin if child exposed to flu or varicella.
  • Platelets SKYROCKET in subacute phase (can exceed 1 million). Combined with vasculitis = thrombotic risk. This is why aspirin antiplatelet therapy is continued.
  • Hold live vaccines (MMR, varicella) for 11 months after IVIG. IVIG antibodies neutralize the vaccine. Echo follow-up at 2 weeks and 6–8 weeks minimum.

Pathophysiology & Epidemiology

Why this disease is a cardiac concern

Kawasaki disease (KD) is an acute, self-limited vasculitis of medium-sized arteries. The exact cause is unknown, but it appears to be a post-infectious, immune-mediated response in genetically susceptible children. Inflammation damages blood vessel walls — most dangerously, the coronary arteries.

Who gets it

  • Peak age: 6 months to 5 years (80% of cases). Rare after age 8.
  • Sex: Boys > girls (about 1.5:1)
  • Ethnicity: Highest incidence in children of Japanese / East Asian descent; lower in White and Black populations. Occurs worldwide.
  • Seasonality: Peaks in late winter and early spring.
  • Recurrence: Uncommon but possible (1–3% of cases).
Why We Call It an Acquired Heart Disease

Kawasaki has surpassed rheumatic fever as the leading cause of acquired heart disease in children in the US and Japan. The inflammation damages the coronary artery wall, which can weaken (aneurysm), narrow (stenosis), or clot (thrombosis). Long-term risk: myocardial infarction in adolescence or early adulthood.

🎯 Bottom Line: Kawasaki = post-infectious medium-vessel vasculitis. Target organ = coronary arteries. Toddlers and preschoolers are the main victims.
2

CRASH and BURN: Diagnostic Criteria

Fever ≥5 days PLUS 4 of 5 principal features

There is no specific lab test for Kawasaki disease — it's a clinical diagnosis. The classic criteria are unrelenting fever for at least 5 days plus at least 4 of the 5 principal features (conjunctivitis, rash, adenopathy, oral changes, extremity changes). The "CRASH and BURN" mnemonic packs all six into one phrase.

Conjunctivitis

Bilateral, non-purulent, bulbar (whites of the eyes). No discharge — distinguishes it from bacterial conjunctivitis.

Rash

Polymorphous — maculopapular, diffuse erythematous, or urticarial. Trunk and extremities. Not vesicular.

Adenopathy

Cervical lymphadenopathy — usually unilateral, at least one node >1.5 cm. Least common criterion.

Strawberry Tongue & Oral Changes

Strawberry tongue, red cracked lips, diffuse oral/pharyngeal erythema. No ulcers or exudate.

Hands & Feet Changes

Acute: erythema & edema of palms/soles. Subacute: desquamation (peeling) starting at fingertips and toes.

BURN: Fever ≥5 Days

High fever (often 39–40 °C / 102–104 °F), unresponsive to antipyretics and antibiotics. The entry ticket to the diagnosis.

NCLEX Memory Hook

If the stem describes a child with prolonged high fever + bilateral non-purulent conjunctivitis + strawberry tongue + rash + swollen red hands — the answer is Kawasaki disease, not scarlet fever, not viral exanthem, not measles.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Extreme Irritability Is the Unwritten Criterion
Kawasaki kids are inconsolable. Parents often describe it as "nothing we do calms him down" — crying that seems out of proportion to how sick the child appears. It's not in the formal criteria, but it's such a consistent finding that many pediatricians use it as a diagnostic clue. If a febrile toddler is unusually miserable with any 2-3 of the principal features, keep Kawasaki on the list.
Incomplete (Atypical) Kawasaki

Infants under 6 months may have incomplete Kawasaki — fever + only 2 or 3 criteria. They are also at HIGHER risk for coronary aneurysms. When a baby has prolonged unexplained fever, suspect incomplete Kawasaki, order an echo, and treat if labs support it. Don't wait for all 5 criteria.

🎯 Bottom Line: Fever ≥5 days + 4 of 5 principal features = Kawasaki. Infants can have incomplete Kawasaki — a lower threshold still requires treatment.
3

Three Phases of Kawasaki

Recognize where the child is — it changes priorities

Kawasaki disease follows a predictable three-phase course. The subacute phase carries the highest cardiovascular risk — including sudden cardiac death — so assessment priorities shift as the child moves through the phases.

Acute Phase

Week 1–2
  • High fever ≥5 days
  • All principal criteria appear
  • Inconsolable irritability
  • Red palms/soles, swollen hands
  • Strawberry tongue, cracked lips
  • Cervical adenopathy
  • Myocarditis may occur

Subacute Phase

Week 2–4
  • Fever subsides
  • Peeling of fingertips & toes
  • Arthritis / joint pain
  • Platelets skyrocket (>1 million)
  • Coronary aneurysms develop
  • Highest risk of sudden death
  • Aspirin still essential

Convalescent

Week 4–8
  • All clinical signs resolve
  • ESR & CRP normalize
  • Child appears well
  • Beau lines on nails
  • Echo follow-up essential
  • Aneurysms may persist
  • Ends when labs normal
Subacute Phase Is the Danger Zone

Parents often relax when the fever breaks — but the subacute phase is when coronary aneurysms form and platelet counts can exceed 1 million. The combination of damaged arteries and hypercoagulability is why continuing aspirin is non-negotiable, even when the child looks better.

🎯 Bottom Line: Acute = fever + criteria. Subacute = peeling, platelets, aneurysms (most dangerous). Convalescent = labs normalize, echoes continue.
4

Labs & Diagnostics

Supportive findings — none are diagnostic alone

There is no single confirmatory test. Labs support the diagnosis and help track disease activity. The most important test is the echocardiogram — done at diagnosis and repeated at follow-up to catch coronary artery aneurysms early.

Test Typical Finding in Kawasaki Why It Matters
ESR & CRP Markedly elevated Inflammation marker; tracks disease activity
WBC Elevated with left shift Neutrophilic predominance; active inflammation
Platelets Normal/low early → very high in subacute (>450K, often >1 million) Thrombocytosis + vasculitis = thrombotic risk
Hemoglobin Mild normocytic anemia Common in prolonged inflammation
Sodium Hyponatremia Associated with higher aneurysm risk
Urinalysis Sterile pyuria (WBCs, no bacteria) Common; do not mistake for UTI
LFTs Mildly elevated AST/ALT, low albumin Liver inflammation; low albumin = severity marker
Echocardiogram Baseline + repeated at 2 weeks & 6–8 weeks Only way to detect coronary aneurysms
EKG May show arrhythmias, ST changes, long PR Screens for myocarditis / ischemia
🎯 Bottom Line: Elevated ESR/CRP/WBC, low Na+, rising platelets, sterile pyuria. The echocardiogram is the test that matters most — not optional.
5

Complications: Why This Disease Is Dangerous

Coronary artery aneurysms are the main concern

Coronary artery aneurysms: The defining complication. Untreated children have a ~25% aneurysm rate; IVIG drops this to <5%. Small aneurysms often regress; giant aneurysms (>8 mm) rarely do.

Myocardial infarction: From thrombosis or stenosis at an aneurysm. Can occur in adolescence or adulthood if aneurysms persist.

Myocarditis: Common during the acute phase. May cause tachycardia disproportionate to fever, gallop rhythm, or heart failure signs.

Pericarditis / pericardial effusion: Seen on echo in acute phase; usually self-limiting.

Arrhythmias: From myocarditis or aneurysm-related ischemia. Monitor cardiac rhythm in acute phase.

Mortality: <0.5% with treatment, but higher in untreated cases or those with giant aneurysms.

Red Flag: Cardiac Decompensation

Tachycardia that is more than expected for the fever, a new gallop, or signs of heart failure in a Kawasaki child suggests myocarditis or coronary compromise. Notify the provider immediately — these findings can precede cardiac arrest.

🎯 Bottom Line: Aneurysms are the reason we treat aggressively and do serial echoes. Treated Kawasaki has <5% aneurysm rate; untreated is five times higher.
6

Treatment: IVIG + High-Dose Aspirin

Given together, within the first 10 days

The goal is to reduce inflammation and prevent coronary artery aneurysms. Treatment is most effective when started within the first 10 days of fever; delayed treatment still provides some benefit but increases the aneurysm risk.

IVIG (Intravenous Immunoglobulin)
Immune Modulator · First-Line

Dose: 2 g/kg as a single infusion over 10–12 hours.

Why: Reduces aneurysm risk from ~25% to <5%.

Best timing: Within first 10 days of fever.

Watch for: Fever, chills, hypotension, headache, aseptic meningitis during infusion. Slow/stop infusion if reaction.

Hold live vaccines (MMR, varicella) for 11 months after IVIG — passive antibodies neutralize the vaccine.

Aspirin (High-Dose)
NSAID · Anti-Inflammatory

Dose: 80–100 mg/kg/day divided every 6 hours while febrile.

Purpose: Anti-inflammatory during acute phase.

After fever resolves: Drop to low-dose 3–5 mg/kg/day (antiplatelet) for 6–8 weeks, or until platelets and ESR normalize.

If aneurysms are present, aspirin continues indefinitely, sometimes with anticoagulation.

IVIG-Resistant Kawasaki
Refractory Therapy

About 10–20% of kids don't respond (fever recurs within 36 hours of IVIG).

Second-line: Second IVIG dose, IV corticosteroids, or infliximab (TNF-alpha inhibitor).

Resistant cases have higher aneurysm risk — need closer cardiology follow-up.

The Aspirin Exception — with a Caveat

Kawasaki is the one pediatric diagnosis where high-dose aspirin is the standard of care despite Reye syndrome concerns — the cardiovascular benefit outweighs the risk. BUT: If the child is exposed to influenza or varicella while on aspirin, stop the aspirin immediately and call the cardiologist. Clopidogrel can sometimes substitute. Ensure the child receives the annual flu vaccine.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Two Aspirin Doses, Two Jobs
High-dose aspirin (80–100 mg/kg/day) is anti-inflammatory — used during the acute phase while the child is febrile. Once fever breaks, we transition to low-dose aspirin (3–5 mg/kg/day) which is antiplatelet — continued through the subacute phase to prevent clots on damaged coronary walls. Same drug, different dose, different mechanism.
🎯 Bottom Line: IVIG 2 g/kg + high-dose aspirin, within 10 days. Step aspirin down after fever breaks. Delay live vaccines 11 months. Stop aspirin if flu/varicella exposure.

Priority Nursing Care

Comfort, safety, and vigilance

During the IVIG Infusion

  • Obtain baseline vitals; monitor q15 minutes during the first hour, then q30 minutes.
  • Start infusion slowly; titrate up per protocol.
  • Watch for reactions: fever, chills, headache, hypotension, urticaria, anaphylaxis. Slow or stop the infusion and notify the provider.
  • Have diphenhydramine and epinephrine available at the bedside.
  • Document pre-medication (acetaminophen, diphenhydramine) per order.

Comfort & Supportive Care

  • Fever management: acetaminophen; cool, loose clothing; tepid sponge baths if high fever. Avoid ibuprofen once on high-dose aspirin.
  • Mouth care every 2–4 hours: soft swabs, lip balm or petroleum jelly, avoid irritating mouthwashes.
  • Diet: cool, soft foods and liquids. Avoid spicy, acidic, or rough-textured foods that irritate mucositis.
  • Skin: gentle handling of desquamating hands/feet. Moisturize. Protect newly exposed skin.
  • Quiet, calm environment: these children are intensely irritable. Limit visitors, dim lights, cluster care, encourage parent presence.
  • Eye comfort: cool compresses, lubricating drops if prescribed. Avoid bright lights.

Cardiac Monitoring

  • Continuous cardiac telemetry during the acute phase.
  • Assess for tachycardia out of proportion to fever, gallop rhythm, new murmur, poor peripheral perfusion — report immediately.
  • Daily weights; strict I&O.
  • Prepare the child and family for echocardiograms at diagnosis, 2 weeks, and 6–8 weeks minimum.

Labs & Safety

  • Monitor CBC (platelets rising into subacute phase), ESR, CRP, LFTs, BMP as ordered.
  • Bleeding precautions while on aspirin: soft toothbrush, no contact sports, monitor for epistaxis, bruising, GI bleeding.
  • Monitor for signs of congestive failure: diaphoresis, poor feeding, dyspnea, hepatomegaly.
🎯 Bottom Line: Watch the IVIG infusion, soothe the irritable child, protect the mouth and skin, and monitor the heart. Cardiac signs beyond the fever are always a red flag.
8

Family Education & Discharge Planning

A long recovery with strict follow-up

Families often feel whiplashed by how sick the child was and how quickly the fever resolves after IVIG. Teaching has to shift the focus to the subacute and convalescent phases, where the heart is still at risk even though the child looks well.

Aspirin Teaching

  • Give low-dose aspirin every day exactly as ordered — missing doses increases clot risk.
  • Stop aspirin immediately and call the cardiologist if the child is exposed to influenza or varicella (chickenpox).
  • Ensure the child gets the annual flu shot while on aspirin.
  • Watch for bleeding: nose bleeds, bloody stools, bruising, gum bleeding.
  • No ibuprofen or other NSAIDs without provider approval (they can blunt aspirin's antiplatelet effect).

Vaccines After IVIG

  • Delay live vaccines (MMR, varicella) for 11 months after IVIG. Passive antibodies from IVIG neutralize the live vaccine.
  • Inactivated vaccines (DTaP, Hib, flu, Hep B, pneumococcal) can be given on schedule.
  • Inform the pediatrician's office so they can adjust the vaccine calendar.

Cardiac Follow-Up

  • Echocardiogram schedule: at diagnosis, 2 weeks, 6–8 weeks minimum. If aneurysms are found, more frequent.
  • If aneurysms persist, the child will need lifelong cardiology follow-up, possibly ongoing antiplatelet or anticoagulation therapy.
  • Activity restrictions in adolescence/adulthood if coronary damage remains.

What to Report

  • Return of fever after treatment
  • Chest pain, fainting, extreme fatigue (possible coronary event)
  • Flu/varicella exposure
  • Any unusual bleeding or bruising
Emotional Support for the Family

Kawasaki is frightening — the child looked well one day and was hospitalized the next. Validate parents' anxiety, explain what each test is looking for, and emphasize that prompt treatment dramatically reduces the risk of long-term problems. Include the child in their care with age-appropriate explanations (choosing which arm for the IV, listening to their own heartbeat, picking a comfort item for echoes).

🎯 Bottom Line: Give aspirin. Avoid flu/varicella. Delay live vaccines 11 months. Show up for echoes. Report fever return or any chest symptoms.
