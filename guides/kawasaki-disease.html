Kawasaki Disease

The rash is not the emergency. The coronary arteries are. Recognize it by criteria, treat it inside ten days, and watch the subacute phase like a hawk.

  • Explain why the coronary arteries, not the rash, are the emergency
  • Say why IVIG has to go in within ten days, and monitor the infusion safely

01

What actually gets you

Kawasaki is not hard to describe. It is easy to miss, easy to under-treat, and easy to second-guess. Points get lost in three predictable places.

Read these before anything else.

You called it a viral rash

A febrile toddler with a rash usually is viral. Kawasaki is the one you cannot let walk out. It is a clinical diagnosis, not a lab. The rule is fever for 5 or more days plus 4 of 5 principal features. The tell is the fever that will not break on antipyretics or antibiotics.

You treated the rash as the emergency

The rash resolves. The coronary arteries are the reason this disease matters. Inflammation can leave a coronary artery aneurysm behind. That is why every child gets an echo, and why IVIG has to go in within 10 days. Treat the arteries, not the skin.

The aspirin threw you

You learned to keep aspirin away from children because of Reye syndrome. Kawasaki is the exception. Here you give it, high-dose first, then low-dose. Same drug, two doses, two jobs. Hold it only if the child meets flu or chickenpox.

Bottom Line: Stop studying Kawasaki as a rash. Study it as four moves: recognize it by criteria, protect the coronaries, treat inside ten days, and use aspirin the one time you do.
02

The four things

Kawasaki lives at four decision points. Recognition, danger, treatment, monitoring.

For each one, say the one-sentence version, the clue you would see in a scenario, and what you do. Say it with the book closed. Familiarity is not recall.

Decision point In one sentence Clue in the scenario What you do
Recognition Fever 5 or more days plus 4 of 5 principal features is Kawasaki, and it is clinical. Long fever, red eyes, strawberry tongue, rash, swollen red hands. Name it as Kawasaki, not a viral rash. Get an echo.

03

Kawasaki or a viral rash

Discrimination axis: the criteria and the fever that will not quit.

This is the pair the diagnosis turns on. Most febrile toddlers with a rash have a virus. Kawasaki has no confirming test. You call it on the pattern.

The rule: fever for 5 or more days, plus at least 4 of the 5 principal features. The mnemonic CRASH and BURN holds all of it.

Looks like Kawasaki
  • Fever 5 or more days, high, and unmoved by antipyretics or antibiotics
  • Bilateral non-exudative conjunctivitis, no discharge
  • Strawberry tongue, red cracked lips, no ulcers
  • Polymorphous rash on trunk and extremities, not vesicular
  • Hands and feet red and swollen, then peeling
  • Cervical node, usually one-sided and larger than 1.5 cm
  • A child who is inconsolable, out of proportion to how sick they look
Points to a benign virus instead
  • Fever that breaks with antipyretics or fades in a few days
  • Cough and runny nose leading the picture
  • Eye discharge, or Koplik spots, suggesting measles
  • Sore throat with exudate, suggesting strep or scarlet fever
  • Milder systemic upset, and no extremity swelling
  • The child consoles and settles between fevers
The axis that separates them

Count the days and count the features. Five days of unrelenting fever plus 4 of 5 is Kawasaki. A rash with a fever that behaves is usually viral. When the fever will not break and the eyes, mouth, hands and rash line up, stop reaching for a virus.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
CRASH and BURN
Conjunctivitis, Rash, Adenopathy, Strawberry tongue, Hands and feet. That is CRASH, the 4 of 5 you look for. BURN is the fever, 5 or more days. The adenopathy is the one most often missing, and it is usually one-sided.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The inconsolable toddler
Extreme irritability is not on the formal list, but it is one of the most consistent clues. These children are miserable beyond what the exam findings explain. A febrile toddler who will not be soothed, with even 2 or 3 features, keeps Kawasaki on the table.
Incomplete Kawasaki in infants

Babies under 6 months can show incomplete Kawasaki. That means fever plus only 2 or 3 features. They also carry a higher aneurysm risk. When an infant has long unexplained fever, suspect it, order the echo, and treat if the labs support it. Do not wait for all 5.

Bottom Line: Fever 5 or more days plus 4 of 5 is Kawasaki. A well-behaved fever with a rash is usually viral. Infants can present incomplete and still need treatment.
04

The three phases

Discrimination axis: where the child is in the course changes your priority.

Kawasaki runs a predictable three-phase course. The middle phase is the dangerous one.

Parents relax when the fever breaks. That is exactly when the coronary arteries are most at risk.

Acute, week 1 to 2
  • High fever, 5 or more days
  • All the principal features appear
  • Inconsolable irritability
  • Red, swollen palms and soles
  • Strawberry tongue, cracked lips
  • Myocarditis can begin here
Subacute, week 2 to 4: the danger zone
  • Fever subsides, child looks better
  • Fingertips and toes peel (desquamation)
  • Platelets skyrocket, often past 450,000
  • Coronary artery aneurysms form here
  • Highest risk of sudden cardiac death
  • Aspirin stays essential
Convalescent, week 4 to 8
  • Clinical signs resolve
  • ESR and CRP return to normal
  • Beau lines may cross the nails
  • Child appears well
  • Aneurysms may persist
  • Echo surveillance continues
Why the subacute phase wins priority

Two things collide here. The coronary walls are damaged, and the platelets are climbing past 450,000, sometimes past a million. Damaged arteries plus sticky blood is how clots form on a coronary. That is why aspirin does not stop when the fever does.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Peeling plus platelets
Acute is fever and red. Subacute is peeling and platelets, and it owns the cardiac risk. Convalescent is resolution. If a scenario mentions peeling fingertips or a soaring platelet count, the child is in the phase that can kill, and the answer usually protects the heart.
Bottom Line: Acute is fever plus criteria. Subacute brings peeling, high platelets, and aneurysms, and it is the most dangerous. Convalescent normalizes, but the echoes keep going.
05

Why this disease is dangerous

The rash is noise. The coronary arteries are the signal.

Kawasaki is the leading cause of acquired heart disease in children in the United States and Japan. Everything you do is aimed at one complication.

Coronary artery aneurysm. The defining complication. Untreated, about a quarter of children develop one. IVIG in time drops that under 5 percent. Small ones often regress; giant ones over 8 mm rarely do.

Myocardial infarction. From a clot or a narrowing at an aneurysm. It can strike in adolescence or adulthood if the aneurysm persists.

Myocarditis. Common in the acute phase. Watch for a heart rate beyond the fever, a gallop, or signs of heart failure.

Pericarditis and effusion. Seen on the acute echo. Usually settles on its own.

Arrhythmias. From myocarditis or ischemia at an aneurysm. Keep the child on cardiac monitoring in the acute phase.

Aneurysm size drives the plan. Small means low-dose aspirin and follow-up echoes. Giant means aspirin plus anticoagulation, and a surgical opinion.

Red flag: cardiac decompensation

A heart rate higher than the fever explains, a new gallop, or heart-failure signs point to myocarditis or coronary trouble. Notify the provider at once. These findings can come before an arrest.

Bottom Line: Aneurysms are the reason you treat hard and echo often. Treated Kawasaki keeps the aneurysm rate under 5 percent. Cardiac signs beyond the fever always get reported.
06

Labs and diagnostics

No single test confirms Kawasaki. The labs support the picture and track the disease.

The echocardiogram is the one that matters. It is the only way to see a coronary aneurysm, and it is not optional.

07

IVIG and high-dose aspirin

Two drugs, given together, aimed at the coronaries. The window is the first 10 days of fever.

Treat inside that window and the aneurysm rate falls under 5 percent. Treat late and it still helps, but the arteries have had more time to remodel.

Immune modulator, first-line
IVIG

Dose: 2 g/kg as a single infusion over 10 to 12 hours.

Why: Cuts the aneurysm rate from about a quarter to under 5 percent.

Timing: Within the first 10 days of fever.

Watch for: Fever, chills, hypotension, headache during the infusion. Slow or stop it if a reaction starts.

Vaccines: Hold live vaccines like MMR and varicella for 11 months. The IVIG antibodies neutralize them.

Anti-inflammatory, then antiplatelet
Aspirin

High-dose: 80 to 100 mg/kg/day, divided, while the child is febrile.

Job now: Anti-inflammatory in the acute phase.

After the fever breaks: Drop to low-dose, 3 to 5 mg/kg/day, for 6 to 8 weeks.

Job then: Antiplatelet, to keep clots off damaged coronary walls.

If aneurysms are present: Aspirin continues indefinitely, sometimes with anticoagulation.

When IVIG does not work
IVIG-resistant Kawasaki

How you know: Fever returns within 36 hours of the infusion.

How often: About 10 to 20 percent of children.

Next step: A second IVIG dose, IV steroids, or infliximab.

Then: These children need closer cardiology follow-up.

The aspirin exception, with a caveat

Kawasaki is the one pediatric diagnosis where high-dose aspirin is standard. The heart benefit outweighs the Reye risk. But if the child is exposed to influenza or chickenpox while on aspirin, stop it and call the cardiologist. Clopidogrel can bridge. Give the annual flu shot.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Two doses, two jobs
High-dose aspirin is anti-inflammatory, given while febrile. Low-dose aspirin is antiplatelet, given after the fever breaks and through the subacute phase. Same drug, different dose, different mechanism. And stop it entirely for flu or chickenpox exposure.
Bottom Line: IVIG 2 g/kg plus high-dose aspirin, inside 10 days. Step the aspirin down after the fever breaks. Delay live vaccines 11 months. Stop aspirin for flu or chickenpox.
08

What you do first

The care splits into four jobs. Run the infusion safely, soothe an inconsolable child, protect the mouth and skin, and watch the heart.

  1. 1
    Run the IVIG safely. Take baseline vitals. Monitor every 15 minutes in the first hour, then every 30. Start slow and titrate up. If fever, chills, headache, hypotension or hives appear, slow or stop the infusion and notify the provider. Keep diphenhydramine and epinephrine at the bedside.
  2. 2
    Give the aspirin correctly. High-dose while febrile, then low-dose once the fever breaks. Avoid ibuprofen while on aspirin. Run bleeding precautions: soft toothbrush, no contact sports, and watch for nosebleeds and bruising.
  3. 3
    Comfort the irritable child. These toddlers are inconsolable. Keep the room quiet and dim. Cluster your care so rest is uninterrupted. Limit visitors and keep the parent close.
  4. 4
    Protect the mouth and skin. Do mouth care every 2 to 4 hours with soft swabs and lip balm. Offer cool, soft foods; skip spicy and acidic. Handle peeling hands and feet gently and moisturize.
  5. 5
    Watch the heart. Keep the child on cardiac monitoring in the acute phase. A heart rate beyond the fever, a new gallop, or poor perfusion gets reported at once. Prepare the child for echoes at diagnosis, 2 weeks, and 6 to 8 weeks.
  6. 6
    Delay live vaccines. After IVIG, hold MMR and varicella for about 11 months. The passive antibodies would neutralize them. Inactivated vaccines stay on schedule.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
When the drug is the problem, stop the drug
If a child reacts during IVIG, the first move is to slow or stop the infusion, then notify the provider. Diphenhydramine, acetaminophen and fluids may follow, but stopping the trigger comes first. Same instinct as a transfusion reaction.
Bottom Line: Watch the infusion, dose the aspirin right, calm the child, guard the mouth and skin, and monitor the heart. Any cardiac sign beyond the fever is a red flag.
09

What families take home

Families feel whiplashed. The child was very sick, and the fever broke fast after IVIG.

Shift their focus to the weeks after. The heart is still at risk while the child looks well.

Aspirin at home

  • Give the low-dose aspirin every day, exactly as ordered. A missed dose raises the clot risk.
  • Stop it and call the cardiologist if the child meets influenza or chickenpox.
  • Get the annual flu shot while the child is on aspirin.
  • Watch for bleeding: nosebleeds, bloody stools, bruising, bleeding gums.
  • No ibuprofen or other NSAIDs without provider approval.

Vaccines after IVIG

  • Delay live vaccines, MMR and varicella, for about 11 months.
  • Inactivated vaccines stay on schedule.
  • Tell the pediatric office so they adjust the calendar.

Cardiac follow-up

  • Echoes at diagnosis, 2 weeks, and 6 to 8 weeks. More often if aneurysms appear.
  • Persistent aneurysms mean lifelong cardiology follow-up.
  • Activity limits later if coronary damage remains.

What to report

  • Fever that comes back after treatment.
  • Chest pain, fainting, or extreme fatigue.
  • Any flu or chickenpox exposure.
  • Unusual bleeding or bruising.
Support the family too

This is frightening. The child looked well one day and was hospitalized the next. Name the fear, explain what each test looks for, and be clear that prompt treatment sharply lowers the long-term risk. Let the child share in their care, choosing the IV arm or a comfort item for the echo.

Bottom Line: Give the aspirin. Avoid flu and chickenpox. Delay live vaccines 11 months. Show up for the echoes. Report a returning fever or any chest symptom.
