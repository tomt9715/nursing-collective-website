Male GU Disorders

Phimosis, cryptorchidism, and testicular torsion — three distinct conditions where one is a six-hour surgical emergency. Telling them apart fast is the nursing skill.

6 Sections 13 Quiz Questions ~10 Min Read

Learning Objectives

Distinguish physiologic from pathologic phimosis and recognize paraphimosis as an emergency.
Understand the timing of cryptorchidism treatment to reduce cancer and infertility risk.
Identify testicular torsion immediately and know why the six-hour window matters.
Differentiate torsion from epididymitis and torsion of appendix testis on physical exam.
Provide age-appropriate education on hygiene, self-exam, and emotional support.
Support adolescents through embarrassment and maintain dignity during GU exams.

High-Yield Summary

Eight facts that separate these three conditions on any test.

  • Physiologic phimosis (inability to retract foreskin) is normal in boys under 3–5 years. Never forcibly retract. Usually resolves on its own.
  • Paraphimosis = the retracted foreskin cannot return to its original position, causing venous congestion and swelling. Urologic emergency — manual reduction needed.
  • Cryptorchidism (undescended testis) occurs in ~3% of term males. Most descend spontaneously by 3–6 months. After 6 months — surgery (orchiopexy) to prevent infertility and cancer.
  • Untreated cryptorchidism increases testicular cancer risk 4–8× — but orchiopexy by 18 months reduces this significantly. The undescended testis is at scrotal temperature when corrected = preserves spermatogenesis.
  • Testicular torsion is a 6-hour emergency. The spermatic cord twists, cutting off blood supply. Salvage rate drops sharply after 6 hours; near 0% after 24 hours.
  • Torsion hallmarks: sudden severe unilateral scrotal/groin pain, absent cremasteric reflex, high-riding testis, horizontal lie, nausea/vomiting. Peak ages: neonatal and 12–18 years.
  • Don't delay surgery for imaging if torsion is clinically obvious. Doppler ultrasound confirms but should not hold up the OR.
  • Emotional care matters. Adolescents with GU concerns are embarrassed and scared. Private exams, clear language, and validating their discomfort build trust and protect their dignity.

Phimosis

When the foreskin won't retract — and when that's OK

Phimosis & Paraphimosis

Foreskin Disorders

Definition

Phimosis = inability to retract the foreskin over the glans penis. Needs to be divided into physiologic (normal age-related) and pathologic (scarred, true obstruction).

Physiologic Phimosis

  • Normal in almost all newborn males — the foreskin is naturally adherent to the glans.
  • Gradual separation happens during early childhood.
  • By age 3: ~10% remain non-retractable.
  • By age 16: <1% remain non-retractable.
  • Reassurance is the treatment — gentle hygiene, no forced retraction.

Pathologic Phimosis

  • Caused by scarring from recurrent balanitis, BXO (balanitis xerotica obliterans / lichen sclerosus), or forced retraction injury.
  • Foreskin appears pale, rigid, scarred.
  • Signs: ballooning of the foreskin during urination, weak urinary stream, pain, recurrent UTIs or balanitis.
  • Requires treatment — topical steroids first, surgery if refractory.

Treatment

  • Topical corticosteroid (betamethasone) 0.05% cream applied to the foreskin 2× daily for 4–8 weeks — 80–90% success in pathologic cases.
  • Gentle retraction exercises paired with steroid cream.
  • Circumcision or preputioplasty for refractory or scarred cases.
Paraphimosis Is a Urologic Emergency

Paraphimosis = the retracted foreskin cannot return to its original position over the glans. The tight ring of foreskin acts as a tourniquet, cutting off venous and lymphatic return — the glans becomes painfully swollen, purple, and without intervention, ischemic.
Treatment: manual reduction ASAP (firm, gentle compression of the glans to reduce edema, then pulling the foreskin forward). Refractory: dorsal slit or emergency circumcision.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Never Forcibly Retract a Child's Foreskin
Forced retraction is a classic cause of iatrogenic phimosis. It tears the physiologic adhesions, causes scarring, and risks paraphimosis. Teach parents: "Let the foreskin do its own thing. Gentle cleansing of the outside is enough." Retraction should happen gradually and painlessly as the child grows, often by age 5.
🎯 Bottom Line: Physiologic phimosis = normal; don't retract. Pathologic phimosis = scarring; treat with steroid cream. Paraphimosis = emergency; reduce immediately.

Cryptorchidism (Undescended Testes)

Why the 6-to-18-month window matters

Cryptorchidism

Congenital

Pathophysiology

During normal fetal development, testes migrate from the abdomen through the inguinal canal into the scrotum by the third trimester. When this process fails, the testis remains intra-abdominal, in the inguinal canal, or at the superficial inguinal pouch. The scrotal temperature (about 2–3°C cooler than the body core) is required for normal spermatogenesis — hence the infertility concern.

Epidemiology

  • ~3% of full-term boys at birth
  • ~30% of premature boys
  • Most (70–80%) descend spontaneously by 3–6 months
  • After 6 months: unlikely to descend without surgery
  • Risk factors: prematurity, low birth weight, family history, maternal smoking
  • Often associated with inguinal hernia (patent processus vaginalis)

Clinical Findings

  • Empty, asymmetric, or flat scrotum on exam
  • Unilateral (most) or bilateral
  • Testis may be palpable in the inguinal canal or non-palpable (intra-abdominal)
  • Retractile testis (can be manipulated into the scrotum and stays there) = normal, NOT cryptorchidism

Complications If Untreated

  • Infertility — elevated temperature damages spermatogenic cells
  • Testicular cancer — 4–8× increased risk; early orchiopexy reduces but doesn't eliminate risk
  • Testicular torsion — higher risk in undescended testes
  • Inguinal hernia frequently coexists
  • Psychosocial concerns about body image in adolescence

Treatment

  • Observation until 6 months — many descend on their own.
  • If not descended by 6 months: orchiopexy (surgical descent and fixation in the scrotum), ideally between 6 and 18 months to preserve fertility.
  • Non-palpable testes: laparoscopic exploration → orchiopexy if intra-abdominal, or orchiectomy if testis is atrophied or non-viable.
  • Post-op: monitor site for bleeding/infection, limit activity, teach self-exam at adolescence.
Retractile vs Cryptorchid

A retractile testis can be gently brought into the scrotum and stays there — this is normal and doesn't need treatment. The cremasteric reflex in a cold or anxious child can pull the testis up temporarily. A cryptorchid testis either cannot be brought into the scrotum at all, or slips back up immediately when released. The distinction matters on exam.

🎯 Bottom Line: Empty scrotum at 6 months = refer for orchiopexy. Surgery by 6–18 months to preserve fertility and reduce cancer risk. Teach self-exam at adolescence.

Testicular Torsion

The six-hour emergency

Testicular Torsion

Surgical Emergency

Pathophysiology

The testis rotates on the spermatic cord, twisting the vascular supply and cutting off blood flow. Without immediate surgical detorsion, ischemia leads to necrosis. The usual anatomic predisposition is the "bell clapper deformity" — the testis is not properly fixed to the scrotum and hangs freely, allowing rotation.

Who Gets It

  • Bimodal age distribution: neonates (in-utero or perinatal) and adolescents 12–18 years.
  • Most common cause of acute scrotum in post-pubertal boys.
  • Risk factors: bell-clapper deformity, undescended testis, trauma, rapid growth, prior intermittent torsion episodes.

Classic Signs & Symptoms

Sudden, severe, unilateral scrotal, groin, or lower abdominal pain — often waking the patient from sleep.

Nausea and vomiting — pain is severe enough to cause autonomic response.

Absent cremasteric reflex on the affected side (stroke the inner thigh → normally the testis rises on the same side).

High-riding testis or horizontal lie — the twisted cord pulls it up.

Scrotal swelling, redness, firmness as ischemia progresses.

Negative Prehn's sign — elevating the scrotum does NOT relieve pain (unlike epididymitis, which it does).

Time Matters

  • Within 6 hours: salvage rate 90–100%
  • 6–12 hours: salvage rate ~50%
  • 12–24 hours: salvage rate ~20%
  • >24 hours: salvage rate near 0%

Treatment

  • Immediate surgical detorsion and bilateral orchiopexy (fix BOTH testes to prevent future torsion on the other side).
  • Manual detorsion ("open book" technique, rotating outward from the median raphe) can be attempted while preparing for the OR — buys time but not a substitute for surgery.
  • Orchiectomy if the testis is non-viable.
Don't Delay Surgery for Imaging

Doppler ultrasound can confirm decreased blood flow, but in a clinically classic case (sudden severe pain, absent cremasteric reflex, abnormal lie), the OR should not wait for imaging. Time is testicle. If the patient is a teen with typical symptoms, the surgeon should be called immediately and the patient prepared for OR while imaging is arranged.

Differential: Acute Scrotum in Peds

Diagnosis Typical Features Cremasteric / Prehn
Testicular torsion Sudden severe pain, N/V, high-riding horizontal testis Absent / Negative (no relief)
Epididymitis Gradual pain, fever, dysuria, often sexually active adolescents Present / Positive (pain relieved on elevation)
Torsion of appendix testis Localized upper-pole pain, "blue dot sign", gradual onset Present / Variable
Trauma / hematoma History of injury; ecchymosis, swelling Variable
Incarcerated hernia Groin bulge, scrotal mass, GI symptoms Palpable mass; surgical emergency
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Two Findings That Nail Torsion
On NCLEX and in clinic, the two findings that tip you strongly to torsion (over epididymitis or appendix testis torsion) are: (1) sudden severe pain, often waking from sleep, and (2) absent cremasteric reflex on the affected side. If both are present in a teenage boy, torsion is the diagnosis until proven otherwise — and you should be prepping for the OR, not waiting for tests.
🎯 Bottom Line: Torsion = sudden severe unilateral pain + absent cremasteric reflex + high-riding testis + 6-hour window. Don't delay surgery for imaging.

GU Assessment in Males

Age-appropriate examination and the tests that confirm

Physical Exam

  • Privacy & dignity first — close doors, use drapes, limit observers, explain each step in age-appropriate language.
  • Inspect external genitalia in good light: symmetry, scrotum, penis, foreskin, meatus.
  • Palpate both testes with the child warm and relaxed — a cold exam triggers the cremasteric reflex and may retract a normal testis.
  • Assess size, shape, position, tenderness.
  • Cremasteric reflex: stroke upper inner thigh; normal response is the ipsilateral testis rising. Absent in torsion, neurologic disease, or absent in some normal infants.
  • Prehn's sign: elevate the scrotum; pain relieved = epididymitis (positive), pain NOT relieved = torsion (negative).

Labs & Imaging

Test Use Notes
Urinalysis Rule out UTI, pyuria (epididymitis) Pyuria supports epididymitis over torsion
Doppler ultrasound Blood flow to testis; vegetations, hydrocele, tumor First-line imaging for acute scrotum
Scrotal scintigraphy (nuclear) Perfusion to testis Less common now; Doppler usually suffices
Laparoscopy Find non-palpable undescended testis Diagnostic and therapeutic in cryptorchidism
Adolescents Hate GU Exams — Prepare Them

Teen boys are often mortified to be examined. Talk through what you're about to do, keep the exam brief, offer a chaperone (often required), allow a parent to step in or out per the teen's choice, and acknowledge the awkwardness. "I know this isn't fun; we'll be quick and professional" builds trust and reduces the chance they'll hide symptoms next time. Their dignity matters.

🎯 Bottom Line: Warm, private exam. Cremasteric reflex and Prehn's sign. Doppler ultrasound confirms torsion. Always treat the adolescent with dignity.

Treatment & Post-Op Care

Stepwise care for each condition

Phimosis / Paraphimosis

  1. Physiologic phimosis: reassurance + gentle hygiene; DO NOT force retraction.
  2. Pathologic phimosis: topical betamethasone 0.05% BID for 4–8 weeks with gradual gentle retraction.
  3. Circumcision or preputioplasty if refractory or scarred (BXO).
  4. Paraphimosis emergency: manual reduction (firm compression of the glans to reduce edema, then pull the foreskin forward) — under analgesia or sedation. Dorsal slit or emergency circumcision if manual reduction fails.

Cryptorchidism

  1. Observe until 6 months — many descend on their own.
  2. If still undescended at 6 months: refer to pediatric urologist.
  3. Orchiopexy (surgical descent and fixation) between 6 and 18 months.
  4. Non-palpable testis: laparoscopic exploration to locate and bring down; orchiectomy if non-viable.
  5. Post-op: monitor incision, pain management, activity restriction (no straddle toys for 2–4 weeks), follow-up to confirm scrotal position.
  6. Teach testicular self-exam starting in adolescence regardless of prior history.

Testicular Torsion

  1. Emergency recognition — sudden pain, absent cremasteric reflex, high-riding testis in a teen boy.
  2. Call the surgeon immediately. NPO. IV access. Pain control.
  3. Doppler ultrasound to confirm if time allows — but do not delay OR for imaging in classic cases.
  4. Manual detorsion ("open book" — rotate the affected testis outward from the median raphe, 180–360 degrees, up to 720) can be attempted by an experienced provider while prepping for surgery.
  5. Emergency surgery: detorsion + bilateral orchiopexy (fix both testes to prevent contralateral torsion). Orchiectomy if testis is necrotic.
  6. Post-op: pain management, scrotal elevation, ice, activity restriction, emotional support. Monitor for healing and fertility counseling later if indicated.
Why Both Testes Are Fixed

The anatomic bell-clapper deformity that predisposes one testis to torsion is usually present bilaterally. Surgical fixation of the unaffected testis at the same time as detorsion of the torsed side prevents future contralateral torsion. It's standard of care.

🎯 Bottom Line: Phimosis = steroid cream. Paraphimosis = emergency reduction. Cryptorchidism = orchiopexy by 18 months. Torsion = OR immediately + bilateral fixation.

Nursing Care & Family Education

Hygiene, self-exam, and emotional support across the age spectrum

Parent Education: Hygiene

  • Uncircumcised infants and young boys: gently wash the outside of the penis with soap and water. Do NOT forcibly retract the foreskin. It will separate naturally over time.
  • Once the foreskin retracts easily (typically by age 5–10), teach gentle retraction during bathing, rinse with water, and return the foreskin to its original position.
  • Circumcised infants: routine bathing; watch for redness, bleeding, or discharge around the meatus.
  • Always teach parents to return a retracted foreskin — leaving it retracted risks paraphimosis.

Cryptorchidism Post-Op Teaching

  • Incision care: keep clean and dry; watch for infection (redness, drainage, fever).
  • Activity restrictions: no straddle toys, tricycles, bicycles, or rough play for 2–4 weeks.
  • Pain management: scheduled acetaminophen ± short-course opioid if needed.
  • Follow-up with urology to confirm testicular position and growth.
  • Adolescent self-exam starting around age 14 — monthly in the shower, palpate each testis for lumps, tenderness, or changes. Even after successful orchiopexy, cancer risk is slightly elevated; self-exam matters.

Testicular Torsion Post-Op Support

  • Pain management, scrotal elevation with athletic supporter, ice for swelling (first 24–48 hours).
  • Activity restriction: no sports, bicycles, or heavy lifting for 2–4 weeks.
  • Monitor incisions for healing; signs of infection.
  • Emotional and body-image support — especially if orchiectomy was needed. Connect with urology counselor or adolescent health specialist.
  • Fertility counseling if indicated (single remaining testis usually produces normal fertility).

Adolescent Self-Exam Teaching

Best time: monthly, in the shower when the scrotum is warm and relaxed.

Technique: roll each testis between thumb and fingers. Each should be smooth, oval, and firm but not hard.

Report: any lump, hardness, heaviness, dull ache, or size change.

Normal findings: epididymis (soft cord-like structure at the back) and slight asymmetry are normal.

When to Go to the ED

Sudden severe scrotal, groin, or lower abdominal pain — possible torsion.

Swollen, painful, purple glans with retracted foreskin — paraphimosis.

Scrotal swelling + fever — could be epididymitis, torsion, or abscess.

New testicular lump in adolescents — rule out cancer.

Normalize the Conversation

Boys and their parents are often uncomfortable discussing GU topics. Starting with a matter-of-fact approach — "Let's talk about how to take care of this part of your body" — reduces shame and increases the likelihood that teens will report a problem early. Empowering them to recognize what's normal for their own body is one of the most valuable lifelong health skills we can teach.

🎯 Bottom Line: Teach hygiene without retraction, post-op care after orchiopexy, and monthly self-exam from adolescence. Treat teens with dignity and openness.
