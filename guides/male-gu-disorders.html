01

What actually gets you

This topic is small. It is also where a testicle gets lost. The exam hides one emergency among three look-alikes.

Points go here in three predictable ways. Read these first.

You treated torsion as something to work up

A teen has sudden one-sided scrotal pain. He is nauseated. The testis rides high and the cremasteric reflex is gone. That is torsion. The testis is salvageable for only about 6 hours. Imaging never delays the operating room.

You confused torsion with epididymitis

Both hurt. The split is in four findings. Torsion is sudden, the reflex is absent, the Prehn sign is negative, and there is no fever. Epididymitis is gradual, the reflex is present, elevation relieves the pain, and there is fever.

You let the wrong baby get circumcised

A boy with hypospadias, epispadias, or tight phimosis is not circumcised. The surgeon needs that foreskin for the repair. And an undescended testis gets fixed by orchiopexy, not watched forever.

Bottom Line: Stop studying four diseases. Study four decisions: is this the clock, is it torsion or epididymitis, does this baby keep his foreskin, and does this testis need surgery.

The four things

For each disorder below, say four things with the book closed. The one-sentence definition. The clue you would see in a scenario. The priority nursing action. The treatment and its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet.

03

Testicular torsion

Discrimination axis: sudden and reflex-absent, or gradual and febrile.

This is the one that cannot wait. The cord twists. Blood flow stops. The testis dies without surgery.

The usual setup is a bell clapper deformity. The testis is not fixed to the scrotum, so it hangs free and rotates. It peaks in two groups. Newborns are one. Boys 12 to 18 are the other.

Points to torsion
  • Sudden severe pain, often waking from sleep
  • Absent cremasteric reflex on that side
  • Negative Prehn sign: elevation does not relieve it
  • No fever
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • High-riding testis or horizontal lie
Points to epididymitis
  • Gradual onset over hours to days
  • Present cremasteric reflex
  • Positive Prehn sign: elevation relieves the pain
  • Fever and dysuria
  • Often a sexually active adolescent
  • Pyuria on urinalysis supports it
Do not delay surgery for imaging

Doppler ultrasound can confirm lost blood flow. It should not hold up the operating room. In a classic case, call the surgeon and prep the patient while imaging is arranged. Time is testicle.

The salvage clock

Within 6 hours the testis is usually saved. By 12 hours the odds are about half. After 24 hours it is rarely viable. That is why the diagnosis is clinical and the response is immediate.

The acute scrotum, side by side

Diagnosis Typical features Cremasteric / Prehn
Testicular torsion Sudden severe pain, nausea and vomiting, high-riding horizontal testis Absent / Negative (no relief)
Epididymitis Gradual pain, fever, dysuria, often sexually active adolescents Present / Positive (relief on elevation)
Torsion of appendix testis Localized upper-pole pain, "blue dot sign", gradual onset Present / Variable
Trauma / hematoma History of injury, ecchymosis, swelling Variable
Incarcerated hernia Groin bulge, scrotal mass, GI symptoms Palpable mass; surgical emergency

One more pattern belongs here. Pain that comes and goes over weeks, with a normal exam in between, is intermittent torsion. It is a warning, not a reassurance. Refer that teen to urology before the next episode.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Two findings nail torsion
Two findings tip you hard toward torsion over the look-alikes. Sudden pain that woke him from sleep. An absent cremasteric reflex on that side. If both are there in a teenage boy, it is torsion until proven otherwise, and you prep for the operating room instead of waiting for tests.
Bottom Line: Torsion is sudden pain, an absent reflex, a negative Prehn sign, and a high-riding testis. It is a 6-hour clock. Do not delay surgery for imaging.
04

Cryptorchidism

Discrimination axis: truly undescended, or just retractile.

An undescended testis never reached the scrotum. It sits in the abdomen, the inguinal canal, or the superficial pouch. The scrotum runs a few degrees cooler than the core. That cooler temperature is what sperm production needs.

Timing drives the plan. About 3 in 100 term boys are affected, and more preterm boys. Most descend on their own by 3 to 6 months. After 6 months, spontaneous descent is unlikely.

Retractile testis (normal)
  • Can be brought into the scrotum by hand
  • Stays down once it is there
  • A cold or anxious child pulls it up briefly
  • No treatment; this is normal anatomy
Cryptorchid testis (refer)
  • Cannot be brought down, or slips right back up
  • Empty, flat, or asymmetric scrotum on exam
  • May be palpable in the canal or non-palpable
  • Needs referral if still up at 6 months

Why it matters if left alone

  • Infertility. The warmer position damages sperm-forming cells over time.
  • Testicular cancer. Risk runs 4 to 8 times higher; early orchiopexy lowers but does not erase it.
  • Torsion. An undescended testis twists more easily.
  • Inguinal hernia. A patent processus vaginalis often rides along.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Retractile is not cryptorchid
Warm the child and relax him before you decide. A retractile testis comes down and stays down. A cryptorchid one will not come down at all, or springs back up the moment you let go. That single move on exam is the whole distinction, and the exam loves it.
Bottom Line: Empty scrotum still empty at 6 months means refer. Orchiopexy by 6 to 18 months protects fertility and lets you watch for cancer. Retractile is normal; leave it.
05

Phimosis and paraphimosis

Discrimination axis: physiologic and hands-off, or trapped and reduced now.

Phimosis means the foreskin will not retract. In a young boy that is usually normal. It separates on its own over years.

Paraphimosis is the opposite problem. The foreskin was pulled back and got stuck behind the glans. It is an emergency.

Physiologic phimosis (normal)
  • The foreskin is naturally stuck to the glans at birth
  • Separation happens gradually through childhood
  • By age 3, about 1 in 10 still will not retract
  • By age 16, under 1 in 100 still will not
  • Treatment is reassurance and gentle hygiene
Pathologic phimosis (treat)
  • Comes from scarring, not from normal adhesions
  • Causes: recurrent balanitis, BXO, forced retraction
  • Foreskin looks pale, rigid, and scarred
  • Ballooning on urination, weak stream, pain
  • Treat with steroid cream first, surgery if it fails
Paraphimosis is a urologic emergency

The trapped foreskin acts like a tourniquet. Venous and lymphatic return stops. The glans swells, turns purple, and without help goes ischemic. Reduce it fast: firm gentle compression of the glans to squeeze out edema, then draw the foreskin forward. A dorsal slit or emergency circumcision follows if reduction fails.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Never force a child's foreskin
Forced retraction is a classic cause of the very scarring it is meant to prevent. It tears the natural adhesions and can trap the foreskin as paraphimosis. Teach parents plainly: leave it alone, wash the outside, and let it separate on its own. Whoever pulls it back always returns it forward.
Bottom Line: Physiologic phimosis is normal; do not retract. Pathologic phimosis is scarred; treat with steroid cream. Paraphimosis is trapped; reduce it now.
06

Treatment and the operating room

Each disorder has a clean plan. Torsion is ordered by the clock. The others are ordered by age and by tissue.

Testicular torsion, in order

  1. 1
    Recognize it. Sudden pain, absent cremasteric reflex, high-riding testis in a teen boy. Name it as torsion out loud.
  2. 2
    Call the surgeon. Keep the patient NPO. Get IV access. Give pain control. This is the step that saves the testis.
  3. 3
    Image only if it does not slow you down. Doppler confirms lost flow. In a classic case, the operating room does not wait for it.
  4. 4
    Manual detorsion buys time. An experienced provider rotates the testis outward from the median raphe. It is a bridge to surgery, not a substitute.
  5. 5
    Surgery fixes both sides. Detorsion plus bilateral orchiopexy. Orchiectomy if the testis is already dead.
Why both testes get fixed

The bell clapper deformity that let one side twist is usually on both sides. Fixing the healthy testis at the same time prevents a future twist there. It is standard of care, not extra caution.

Cryptorchidism

  • Observe until 6 months. Many testes descend on their own by then.
  • Refer to a pediatric urologist if it is still up at 6 months.
  • Orchiopexy brings it down and fixes it in place, ideally by 6 to 18 months.
  • Non-palpable testis: laparoscopy locates it; orchiectomy if it is atrophied or non-viable.

Phimosis and paraphimosis

  • Physiologic phimosis: reassurance and gentle hygiene; do not retract.
  • Pathologic phimosis: betamethasone 0.05% cream twice daily for 4 to 8 weeks, with gentle retraction.
  • Refractory or scarred: circumcision or preputioplasty.
  • Paraphimosis: manual reduction under analgesia; dorsal slit or emergency circumcision if it fails.

Hypospadias and epispadias

The urethral opening is misplaced. Hypospadias opens on the underside. Epispadias opens on the top. Surgeons repair it around 6 to 12 months, often using the foreskin as tissue.

Do not circumcise these babies

Hold the routine circumcision for any boy with hypospadias, epispadias, or significant phimosis planned for surgery. The repair uses that foreskin. Once it is gone, the surgeon has lost tissue that is hard to replace. Flag it clearly in the chart and to the parents.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
A misplaced meatus is a stop sign
On the newborn exam, look at where urine actually exits. If the meatus is not at the tip, think hypospadias or epispadias, and put a hold on circumcision. The foreskin is the graft material. This is one exam question that is really a safety catch.
Bottom Line: Torsion goes to the operating room now, both sides fixed. Cryptorchidism gets orchiopexy by 18 months. Phimosis gets steroid cream. Hypospadias keeps its foreskin for repair.
07

Nursing care and family teaching

The exam and the bedside share the same priorities here. Protect the airway of the emergency, protect the repair after surgery, and protect the dignity of a scared adolescent.

The GU exam, done right

  • Privacy first. Close doors, use drapes, limit observers, explain each step in plain words.
  • Warm and relaxed. A cold exam fires the cremasteric reflex and can pull a normal testis up.
  • Cremasteric reflex: stroke the upper inner thigh; the testis should rise on that side.
  • Prehn sign: elevate the scrotum; relief points to epididymitis, no relief points to torsion.
  • Doppler ultrasound is the first imaging for an acute scrotum, but it never delays torsion surgery.

Post-op priorities

  1. 1
    Pain and swelling. Scheduled acetaminophen, scrotal elevation with a supporter, ice for the first day or two.
  2. 2
    The incision. Keep it clean and dry. Watch for redness, drainage, or fever.
  3. 3
    Activity limits. No straddle toys, bikes, sports, or rough play for 2 to 4 weeks. That tension can undo the fixation.
  4. 4
    Follow-up. Confirm the testis stays in the scrotum, and start self-exam teaching at adolescence.

After a hypospadias repair

  • A stent or catheter drains the new urethra for several days. Keep it taped and unkinked.
  • Double diaper the infant so the stent stays protected and urine drains freely.
  • Watch output and comfort. Bladder spasms are common; treat them as ordered.
  • Keep the site clean. Report bleeding, poor drainage, or fever.

Hygiene teaching for parents

  • For uncircumcised boys, wash the outside with soap and water. Do not force the foreskin back.
  • Once it retracts easily, usually by age 5 to 10, clean under it and return it forward.
  • Always return a retracted foreskin. Leaving it back risks paraphimosis.

Self-exam from adolescence

Timing. Monthly, in the shower, when the scrotum is warm and loose.

Technique. Roll each testis between thumb and fingers. It should feel smooth and firm.

Report. Any lump, hardness, heaviness, dull ache, or size change.

Normal. The epididymis is a soft cord at the back, and slight asymmetry is fine.

When to send them to the ED

Sudden severe scrotal, groin, or lower belly pain. Treat as torsion.

Swollen, painful, purple glans with the foreskin stuck back. Paraphimosis.

Scrotal swelling with fever. Could be epididymitis, torsion, or abscess.

A new testicular lump in an adolescent. Rule out cancer.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Monthly, in the shower
The self-exam answer is monthly, in the shower, when the scrotum is warm and relaxed. Yearly is too rare to catch an early change. Teach it to every adolescent boy, even after a successful orchiopexy, because the cancer risk stays slightly raised.
Guard their dignity, keep them talking

Teen boys are often mortified by a GU exam. Say what you will do, keep it brief, offer a chaperone. Name the awkwardness rather than ignore it. A boy who feels respected reports the next problem early instead of hiding it.

Bottom Line: Warm private exams, protected repairs, and monthly self-exam from adolescence. Never force a foreskin, and never delay for a scared teen who is embarrassed to speak.
