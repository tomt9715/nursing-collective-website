Coronary Anatomy & Physiology

Understanding coronary artery anatomy is essential for recognizing which areas of the heart are affected during a myocardial infarction and predicting potential complications.

Coronary Artery Blood Supply

The heart muscle (myocardium) receives its blood supply from the coronary arteries, which branch off the aorta just above the aortic valve. There are two main coronary arteries:

Clinical Pearl: Coronary Perfusion

Unlike other organs, the heart receives most of its blood supply during diastole (when the heart is relaxed), not during systole. This is why tachycardia is problematic in cardiac patients - less diastolic filling time means less coronary perfusion!

Coronary Artery Area Supplied If Occluded, Results In
Left Anterior Descending (LAD) Anterior wall of LV, septum, bundle branches Anterior MI - most common, worst prognosis
Left Circumflex (LCx) Lateral and posterior LV walls Lateral or posterior MI
Right Coronary Artery (RCA) Right ventricle, inferior LV, SA and AV nodes Inferior MI - watch for bradycardia/heart block
Left Main Coronary Branches into LAD and LCx "Widow maker" - massive MI, often fatal
Memory Aid: LAD = "Left Anterior Descending"

The LAD is sometimes called the "widow maker" because it supplies the largest portion of the left ventricle. An occlusion here causes an Anterior MI - the most serious type with highest mortality.

Oxygen Supply and Demand

The myocardium requires a constant supply of oxygen. When oxygen demand exceeds supply, ischemia occurs. Understanding this balance is key to MI management.

  • Oxygen Supply depends on: Coronary blood flow, arterial oxygen content, hemoglobin level
  • Oxygen Demand increases with: Heart rate, contractility, wall tension (preload/afterload)
NCLEX Tip: Supply vs Demand

Treatment goals focus on increasing supply (oxygen, nitrates to dilate coronaries) and decreasing demand (beta-blockers, rest, morphine). Think: "More supply, less demand!"

Bottom Line: The heart perfuses during diastole, not systole. Tachycardia shortens diastole and reduces coronary perfusion — that's why it's dangerous in MI patients.

Pathophysiology of Myocardial Infarction

A myocardial infarction occurs when blood flow to a portion of the heart muscle is severely reduced or completely blocked, leading to myocardial cell death (necrosis).

The Atherosclerotic Process

Most MIs result from atherosclerosis - the buildup of fatty plaques in the coronary arteries over time:

Progression to MI

1
Endothelial Injury

Risk factors (HTN, smoking, diabetes, hyperlipidemia) damage the inner arterial lining, allowing lipid accumulation.

2
Plaque Formation

LDL cholesterol deposits in the arterial wall, forming a fibrous plaque that gradually narrows the vessel lumen.

3
Plaque Rupture

An unstable plaque can rupture, exposing its thrombogenic core to the bloodstream. This triggers the clotting cascade.

4
Thrombus Formation

Platelets aggregate and a blood clot (thrombus) forms at the rupture site, potentially completely occluding the artery.

5
Myocardial Ischemia → Injury → Infarction

Blocked blood flow causes ischemia within minutes, injury within 20-30 minutes, and irreversible necrosis begins after ~20 minutes of total occlusion.

Critical Understanding: Time = Muscle

"Time is muscle" - Every minute of ischemia results in more myocardial cell death. The goal of treatment is rapid reperfusion to salvage as much myocardium as possible.

  • Door-to-balloon time goal: < 90 minutes for PCI
  • Door-to-needle time goal: < 30 minutes for thrombolytics
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Time is Muscle
Every minute counts during a heart attack! When a coronary artery is blocked, heart muscle begins dying within 20-30 minutes. The longer the blockage, the more permanent damage occurs. That's why we have strict time goals: door-to-balloon time less than 90 minutes for PCI (percutaneous coronary intervention) or door-to-needle time less than 30 minutes for thrombolytic therapy. Your rapid assessment and immediate activation of the cath lab can save a patient's heart function.

Zones of Injury

During an MI, the affected area has three distinct zones:

Zone Description EKG Change Reversibility
Ischemia (outer) Reduced blood flow, cells still viable T wave inversion Reversible with reperfusion
Injury (middle) Severely compromised, cells dying ST segment elevation Potentially reversible if treated quickly
Infarction (center) Necrotic tissue, cells dead Pathological Q waves Irreversible - permanent damage
Bottom Line: Ischemia → injury → infarction occurs in minutes. Irreversible necrosis begins after ~20 minutes of occlusion. "Time is muscle" — rapid reperfusion saves myocardium.

STEMI vs NSTEMI

Acute Coronary Syndrome (ACS) includes a spectrum of conditions: unstable angina, NSTEMI, and STEMI. Distinguishing between these is critical because treatment urgency differs significantly.

Key Distinction

STEMI = Complete coronary artery occlusion → ST elevation on EKG → EMERGENCY reperfusion needed

NSTEMI = Partial/intermittent occlusion → No ST elevation but positive troponin → Urgent (not emergent) intervention

STEMI

ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction

  • Complete coronary artery occlusion
  • ST elevation in contiguous leads
  • Elevated troponin (may take hours)
  • Transmural infarction (full-thickness)
  • Q waves develop over hours-days

Treatment Priority

EMERGENT reperfusion: PCI (preferred) or thrombolytics within 12 hours of symptom onset

NSTEMI

Non-ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction

  • Partial/subtotal coronary occlusion
  • ST depression or T wave inversion
  • Elevated troponin (confirms MI vs UA)
  • Subendocardial infarction (partial thickness)
  • No pathological Q waves

Treatment Priority

URGENT intervention: Anti-ischemic therapy, anticoagulation, early invasive strategy within 24-72 hours

Clinical Presentation of Acute MI

Classic MI symptoms include:

  • Chest pain: Crushing, pressure, "elephant on chest," may radiate to left arm, jaw, neck, back
  • Diaphoresis: Cold, clammy skin is a hallmark sign
  • Dyspnea: Shortness of breath from decreased cardiac output
  • Nausea/vomiting: Vagal response, especially in inferior MI
  • Anxiety: Sense of impending doom
  • Weakness/fatigue: From decreased perfusion
Atypical Presentations

Women, elderly, and diabetic patients often present atypically:

  • Fatigue, weakness, or malaise instead of chest pain
  • Nausea, indigestion, or epigastric discomfort
  • Shortness of breath without chest pain
  • Diabetics may have "silent MIs" due to neuropathy

Always maintain high suspicion in these populations!

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Atypical Presentations
Not everyone presents with classic chest pain! Women, elderly patients, and diabetics often have atypical MI symptoms. They may complain of fatigue, nausea, indigestion, or just "not feeling right" instead of the classic crushing chest pain. Diabetics are especially tricky because neuropathy can cause "silent MIs" with minimal or no pain. Maintain high clinical suspicion in these populations and get an EKG if anything seems off - early detection saves lives.
Memory Aid: MI Symptoms

PULSE

  • Pressure/Pain in chest
  • Upper body discomfort (arm, jaw, neck)
  • Lightheadedness
  • Shortness of breath
  • Exhaustion/diaphoresis
Bottom Line: STEMI = complete occlusion, ST elevation, emergent reperfusion. NSTEMI = partial occlusion, positive troponin, urgent (not emergent) intervention. Both are acute coronary syndromes.

Cardiac Biomarkers

Cardiac biomarkers are proteins released from damaged myocardial cells. They are essential for diagnosing MI, especially when EKG findings are equivocal.

Troponin: The Gold Standard

Cardiac troponins (cTnI and cTnT) are the most sensitive and specific markers for myocardial injury.

Biomarker Initial Rise Peak Return to Normal Clinical Use
Troponin I/T 2-4 hours 12-24 hours 7-14 days Gold standard; most sensitive and specific
High-Sensitivity Troponin 1-3 hours 12-24 hours 7-14 days Earlier detection; rule out MI faster
CK-MB 4-6 hours 18-24 hours 2-3 days Detect reinfarction (faster return to baseline)
Myoglobin 1-3 hours 6-9 hours 24 hours Early marker but not cardiac-specific
Clinical Pearl: Serial Troponins

A single troponin level is not enough to rule out MI. Serial troponins (at presentation, 3-6 hours, and sometimes 12 hours) are needed to detect the rise and fall pattern characteristic of acute MI.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Serial Troponins
One negative troponin does NOT rule out a heart attack! Troponin takes 2-4 hours to rise after myocardial injury begins. A patient who arrives at the ED with chest pain that started 30 minutes ago may have a completely normal initial troponin. That's why we draw serial troponins - typically at 0 hours (arrival), 3-6 hours, and sometimes 12 hours - to catch the characteristic rise and fall pattern. Never send a patient home based on a single negative troponin.

Interpreting Troponin Results

  • Elevated troponin alone does not = MI. Other causes include: heart failure, myocarditis, PE, renal failure, sepsis, cardiac surgery
  • Diagnosis of MI requires: Rise and/or fall of troponin PLUS clinical evidence of ischemia (symptoms, EKG changes, imaging findings)
  • The higher the troponin, the larger the infarct and the worse the prognosis

Biomarker Quick Reference

Troponin I Normal
< 0.04 ng/mL (varies by lab)
High-Sens Troponin
< 14-19 ng/L (varies)
CK-MB Normal
< 3-6% of total CK
BNP (HF marker)
< 100 pg/mL
NCLEX Tip: Biomarker Timing

If a patient presents with chest pain and the initial troponin is negative:

  • This does NOT rule out MI - troponin takes 2-4 hours to rise
  • Repeat troponin in 3-6 hours
  • Treat based on clinical presentation and EKG while waiting for results
Bottom Line: Troponin is the gold standard — but a single normal level doesn't rule out MI. Serial troponins at 0, 3–6, and 12 hours are needed to detect the rise-and-fall pattern.

EKG Changes & MI Location

The 12-lead EKG is crucial for diagnosing STEMI, determining MI location, and guiding treatment. Different leads correspond to different areas of the heart.

EKG Changes in MI

EKG Finding Represents Timing
ST Elevation Acute injury (active infarction) Minutes to hours after occlusion
ST Depression Ischemia or reciprocal changes Can be early sign; seen in NSTEMI
T Wave Inversion Ischemia (evolving or recent MI) Hours to days after event
Pathological Q Waves Necrosis (dead tissue) Hours to days; may be permanent
Hyperacute T Waves Very early ischemia First minutes - easily missed!

Localizing MI by EKG Leads

MI Location ST Elevation in Leads Coronary Artery Potential Complications
Anterior V1, V2, V3, V4 LAD Cardiogenic shock, HF, VT/VF
Lateral I, aVL, V5, V6 LCx or LAD Papillary muscle dysfunction
Inferior II, III, aVF RCA (usually) Bradycardia, AV blocks, RV infarct
Posterior V7-V9 (use posterior leads); reciprocal ST depression V1-V3 RCA or LCx Often occurs with inferior MI
Right Ventricular V1, V4R (right-sided leads) Proximal RCA Hypotension, JVD, clear lungs
Anterior MI

Most Dangerous - "Widow Maker"

  • ST elevation in V1-V4
  • LAD occlusion
  • Largest area of LV affected
  • High risk: cardiogenic shock
  • High risk: ventricular arrhythmias
  • Worst overall prognosis
Inferior MI

Watch for RV Involvement

  • ST elevation in II, III, aVF
  • RCA occlusion (most common)
  • Monitor for bradycardia
  • AV blocks (RCA supplies AV node)
  • Check right-sided leads (V4R)
  • Generally better prognosis
Critical: Right Ventricular Infarct

If inferior MI is present, always check right-sided leads (V4R) to rule out RV infarct!

RV Infarct triad: Hypotension + JVD + Clear lungs

Treatment: AVOID nitrates and diuretics! These patients are preload-dependent. Give IV fluids to maintain RV filling.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
RV Infarct Triad
Know the classic triad: Hypotension + Jugular Vein Distension (JVD) + Clear lungs! Right ventricular infarcts often accompany inferior MIs (look at leads II, III, aVF). The key difference from left-sided failure: lungs are clear because the left side is still working fine. These patients are preload-dependent - their weak right ventricle needs all the blood volume it can get. NEVER give nitrates or diuretics to RV infarct patients; instead, give IV fluids to maintain cardiac output.
Bottom Line: ST elevation = acute injury. Leads tell you location: V1–V4 = anterior (LAD), II/III/aVF = inferior (RCA). Always check right-sided leads (V4R) in inferior MI to rule out RV involvement.

Thrombolytic Therapy (tPA)

Thrombolytics (fibrinolytics) dissolve blood clots by activating plasminogen. They are used in STEMI when primary PCI is not available within 120 minutes.

Thrombolytic Agents

Agent Generic Name Administration
tPA (Alteplase) Tissue plasminogen activator IV bolus + infusion over 90 min
Reteplase (Retavase) Recombinant tPA Two IV boluses 30 min apart
Tenecteplase (TNKase) Modified tPA Single IV bolus (weight-based)

Indications for Thrombolytics

Thrombolytic Criteria (ALL must be met)
  • STEMI on EKG (ST elevation ≥1mm in 2+ contiguous leads)
  • Symptom onset within 12 hours (best within 3 hours)
  • PCI not available within 120 minutes
  • No absolute contraindications

Contraindications to Thrombolytics

Absolute Contraindications Relative Contraindications
Prior intracranial hemorrhage (any time) Recent TIA (within 3 months)
Known cerebral vascular malformation or tumor Oral anticoagulant therapy
Ischemic stroke within 3 months Pregnancy or 1 week postpartum
Suspected aortic dissection Non-compressible vascular punctures
Active bleeding or bleeding diathesis Traumatic CPR (>10 min) or major surgery <3 weeks
Significant closed head or facial trauma within 3 months Severe uncontrolled hypertension (SBP >180 or DBP >110)
Memory Aid: Thrombolytic Contraindications

HEADS - Think "bleeding in the head is the worst complication"

  • Hemorrhagic stroke (history or recent)
  • Extreme BP (uncontrolled hypertension)
  • Aortic dissection suspected
  • Disorder of bleeding (coagulopathy)
  • Surgery/trauma (recent major)

Nursing Responsibilities for Thrombolytics

Before, During, and After tPA

1
Pre-Administration

Verify STEMI diagnosis, check contraindications, obtain baseline neuro assessment, establish two large-bore IVs, draw labs (CBC, PT/INR, type and screen).

2
During Administration

Monitor vital signs every 15 minutes, watch for signs of bleeding (gums, IV sites, urine, stool), assess neuro status frequently, avoid invasive procedures.

3
Signs of Successful Reperfusion

Resolution of chest pain, return of ST segments to baseline, reperfusion arrhythmias (especially PVCs, accelerated idioventricular rhythm), early peak of cardiac markers.

4
Post-Administration

Continue monitoring for bleeding 24-48 hours, avoid IM injections, use soft toothbrush, apply pressure to venipuncture sites, report any neuro changes immediately.

Most Feared Complication

Intracranial hemorrhage (ICH) occurs in ~1% of patients receiving thrombolytics. It is often fatal.

Stop thrombolytic immediately and notify MD if patient develops:

  • Sudden severe headache
  • Altered level of consciousness
  • New focal neurological deficits
  • Nausea/vomiting with neuro changes
Bottom Line: tPA must be given within 3–4.5 hours of symptom onset. The most feared complication is intracranial hemorrhage (~1%). Stop tPA immediately if patient develops sudden headache or neuro changes.

Priority Nursing Interventions

Nursing care for acute MI focuses on reducing myocardial oxygen demand, relieving pain, preventing complications, and facilitating rapid reperfusion.

Memory Aid: Initial MI Treatment

MONA-B (Morphine, Oxygen, Nitroglycerin, Aspirin, Beta-blockers)

  • Morphine - pain relief, reduces preload and anxiety
  • Oxygen - only if SpO2 < 90% or in respiratory distress
  • Nitroglycerin - dilates coronary arteries, reduces preload
  • Aspirin - antiplatelet, 162-325 mg chewed immediately
  • Beta-blockers - reduce HR and myocardial oxygen demand
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
MONA-B Therapy
MONA-B is your go-to mnemonic for initial MI treatment! Morphine relieves pain and reduces preload through venodilation. Oxygen is given only if SpO2 is below 90% (routine oxygen is no longer recommended). Nitroglycerin dilates coronary arteries and reduces preload - but check blood pressure first and hold if systolic is below 90 mmHg. Aspirin (162-325 mg chewed, not swallowed whole) for rapid antiplatelet effect. Beta-blockers reduce heart rate and oxygen demand. Remember: not all components are given to every patient - assess contraindications first!

Immediate Nursing Actions

First 10 Minutes

1
Obtain 12-Lead EKG

Goal: within 10 minutes of arrival. This is the most important diagnostic test for identifying STEMI and guiding treatment.

2
Establish IV Access

Two large-bore IVs (18 gauge or larger). Draw cardiac biomarkers, CBC, BMP, coagulation studies, type and screen.

3
Administer Aspirin

162-325 mg chewed (not swallowed whole) for rapid absorption. This should happen unless patient has true aspirin allergy.

4
Continuous Cardiac Monitoring

Place on telemetry immediately. Watch for arrhythmias - VT/VF is the leading cause of death in the first hour.

5
Supplemental Oxygen

Only if SpO2 < 90%, dyspnea present, or signs of heart failure. Routine oxygen is no longer recommended for uncomplicated MI.

Medication Administration

Medication Purpose Key Nursing Considerations
Nitroglycerin Coronary vasodilation, reduce preload Check BP before each dose. Hold if SBP <90. Contraindicated if recent PDE5 inhibitor use or RV infarct.
Morphine Pain relief, reduce preload, decrease anxiety Monitor respiratory status. May cause hypotension. Have naloxone available.
Beta-blockers Reduce HR, BP, and myocardial O2 demand Monitor for bradycardia, hypotension, heart block. Contraindicated in cardiogenic shock.
Heparin Anticoagulation, prevent clot extension Monitor PTT (goal 1.5-2x normal), watch for bleeding.
P2Y12 Inhibitors Antiplatelet (clopidogrel, ticagrelor) Given with aspirin for dual antiplatelet therapy. Increases bleeding risk.
Statins Plaque stabilization, lipid lowering High-intensity statin started within 24 hours of MI.
Nitroglycerin Cautions

Do NOT give nitroglycerin if:

  • SBP < 90 mmHg
  • Severe bradycardia (<50 bpm) or tachycardia (>100 bpm)
  • Right ventricular infarction (preload-dependent)
  • Patient has taken PDE5 inhibitor (sildenafil, tadalafil) within 24-48 hours

Ongoing Nursing Care

  • Pain assessment: Use 0-10 scale, reassess after interventions
  • Vital signs: Every 15 minutes until stable, then every 1-2 hours
  • Telemetry monitoring: Continuous; report arrhythmias immediately
  • Activity restriction: Bed rest initially, then gradual mobilization
  • Intake and output: Monitor for signs of heart failure
  • Psychosocial support: Address anxiety, provide education
  • Prevent Valsalva: Stool softeners to prevent straining
Bottom Line: Aspirin first (162–325 mg chewed), then MONA-B. Never give nitroglycerin if SBP < 90, RV infarct, or PDE5 inhibitor use within 24–48 hours.

Complications of Myocardial Infarction

Complications can occur during or after MI. Early recognition and intervention are essential for patient survival.

Electrical Complications (Arrhythmias)

Arrhythmia When It Occurs Nursing Response
Ventricular Fibrillation First 4 hours - leading cause of death Immediate defibrillation, ACLS protocol
Ventricular Tachycardia First 24-48 hours Sustained: cardioversion. Non-sustained: monitor, antiarrhythmics
Sinus Bradycardia Inferior MI (vagal response) Atropine if symptomatic, temporary pacing
AV Blocks Inferior MI (RCA supplies AV node) Monitor, atropine, transcutaneous/transvenous pacing
Atrial Fibrillation Any time post-MI Rate control, anticoagulation, address underlying cause
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Watch for VF
Ventricular fibrillation is the leading cause of death in the first hour after MI! The damaged, ischemic heart muscle is electrically unstable and can suddenly go into VF (ventricular fibrillation) - a lethal arrhythmia that causes cardiac arrest. This is why every MI patient needs continuous cardiac monitoring and a defibrillator must be immediately available. The first few hours are the most dangerous. If VF occurs, immediate defibrillation following ACLS protocol is the only way to save the patient.

Mechanical Complications

Complication Timing Signs/Symptoms Treatment
Cardiogenic Shock Hours to days Hypotension, tachycardia, cold/clammy, oliguria, AMS Inotropes, IABP, mechanical support
Papillary Muscle Rupture 2-7 days Sudden severe mitral regurgitation, new murmur, pulmonary edema Emergency surgery
Ventricular Septal Rupture 3-5 days New harsh systolic murmur, rapid deterioration Emergency surgery
Free Wall Rupture 5-14 days Sudden cardiac tamponade, PEA arrest Usually fatal; emergency surgery if caught
Ventricular Aneurysm Weeks to months Heart failure, arrhythmias, emboli Anticoagulation, surgical repair
Cardiogenic Shock Recognition

Cardiogenic shock is the most common cause of in-hospital death from MI. Recognize early:

  • SBP < 90 mmHg for 30+ minutes (or requiring vasopressors)
  • Signs of end-organ hypoperfusion: cool extremities, oliguria, AMS
  • Cardiac index < 2.2 L/min/m²
  • PCWP > 15 mmHg (if measured)

Other Complications

  • Pericarditis: Chest pain worse with inspiration, pericardial friction rub. Treat with aspirin, colchicine. Avoid anticoagulants if present.
  • Dressler's Syndrome: Autoimmune pericarditis occurring weeks after MI. Fever, pleuritic chest pain, pericardial effusion.
  • Left Ventricular Thrombus: Forms on akinetic wall segments. Requires anticoagulation to prevent embolic stroke.
  • Heart Failure: May develop acutely or chronically depending on extent of damage.
Clinical Pearl: Timing of Complications

Mechanical complications (rupture of papillary muscle, septum, or free wall) typically occur 3-7 days post-MI when the necrotic tissue is weakest. Be especially vigilant during this period for sudden hemodynamic deterioration or new murmurs.

Bottom Line: VF is the leading cause of death in the first hour — immediate defibrillation saves lives. Watch for mechanical complications at 3–7 days when necrotic tissue is weakest.
