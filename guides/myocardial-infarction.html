Myocardial Infarction

Three questions decide every scenario: how blocked the artery is, which wall died, and how long ago it happened. Answer those and the rest is ordering.

01

What actually gets you

Nobody fails this topic because they cannot define a heart attack. They fail it on ordering, on timing, and on the one patient where the usual drug is the wrong drug.

Points get lost here in four predictable ways. Read them before anything else.

You learned MONA and gave it in that order

MONA spells a name. It does not rank anything. Aspirin is the drug that changes mortality and it sits fourth in the mnemonic. Oxygen sits second and most MI patients should not get it at all.

When a question asks what you do first, the mnemonic is actively working against you.

You trusted one troponin

Troponin takes 2 to 4 hours to rise. A patient whose pain started 30 minutes ago can be having a large MI with a perfectly normal first draw.

A single negative troponin is a timestamp, not a verdict.

You gave nitroglycerin to the wrong MI

Right ventricular infarct patients live on preload. Nitroglycerin drops preload. Give it and the pressure falls off a cliff.

This is one of the few places in nursing school where the standard drug is the harmful one, which is exactly why it gets tested.

You learned chest pain as the symptom

Women, older adults and people with diabetes often arrive without it. They arrive tired, nauseated, short of breath, or just not right.

If you are waiting for crushing chest pain, you will miss the patient the question is built around.

Bottom Line: Study MI as three questions. How blocked is the artery. Which wall died. How long ago. Every scenario is answering one of those.
02

The four things

Acute coronary syndrome is a spectrum, not a diagnosis. For each point on it you should be able to say four things with the book closed: the one-sentence definition, the clue you would see in a patient, the priority action, and the first-line drug with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Type Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line drug
Unstable angina Ischemia without cell death; the muscle is starved but nothing has died yet. Chest pain at rest or worsening, troponin stays negative. Serial troponins and serial EKGs; do not discharge on one set. Aspirin, chewed. Anti-ischemic therapy after it.
NSTEMI Partial occlusion that killed the inner layer of muscle. ST depression or T wave inversion, troponin positive. Urgent, not emergent. Anticoagulate and plan catheterisation. Aspirin plus a P2Y12 inhibitor. Bleeding risk goes up with both.
STEMI Complete occlusion killing muscle through the full wall thickness. ST elevation in two or more neighbouring leads. Activate the cath lab. Reperfusion is the whole plan. Aspirin, chewed, then reperfusion by PCI or thrombolytics.
RV infarct An inferior MI that took the right ventricle with it. Hypotension, JVD, and lungs that are clear. Give IV fluids. Withhold nitrates and diuretics. Fluids first. Nitroglycerin here is the wrong answer.
Copy this down

Four rows on one index card, by hand. Angina hurts and nothing dies. NSTEMI kills the inside layer. STEMI kills the whole wall. RV infarct needs volume, not nitro. That card is most of the exam.

03

How the muscle dies

An MI is a supply problem with a deadline. Blood flow to part of the myocardium stops, and the cells behind the blockage start dying. Everything you do is about that deadline.

Supply and demand

The myocardium needs constant oxygen. When demand passes supply, ischemia starts.

  • Supply depends on: coronary blood flow, arterial oxygen content, hemoglobin level.
  • Demand rises with: heart rate, contractility, and wall tension from preload and afterload.

Every treatment either raises supply or lowers demand. Oxygen and nitrates raise supply. Beta-blockers, rest and morphine lower demand. That is the whole pharmacology of this topic in one line.

Why tachycardia is the enemy here

The heart perfuses itself during diastole, not systole. Speed the heart up and diastole shortens first.

So a fast rate raises oxygen demand and cuts coronary supply at the same time. That is why rate control matters in a patient with chest pain.

From plaque to infarct

Most MIs start with atherosclerosis and end with a clot. Five steps.

  1. 1
    Endothelial injury. Hypertension, smoking, diabetes and high lipids damage the arterial lining, and lipid starts collecting there.
  2. 2
    Plaque formation. LDL deposits in the wall and builds a fibrous plaque. The lumen narrows slowly, over years.
  3. 3
    Plaque rupture. An unstable plaque tears open and exposes its core to the blood. Clotting starts immediately.
  4. 4
    Thrombus formation. Platelets pile onto the tear. The clot may narrow the artery or close it completely.
  5. 5
    Ischemia, then injury, then infarction. Ischemia starts within minutes. Irreversible necrosis begins after roughly 20 minutes of total occlusion.

Notice what step 3 means. The plaque that kills is not always the biggest one. A modest plaque that ruptures beats a large stable one, which is why a patient with no history can arrest in the waiting room.

Three zones, three EKG signs

The damaged area is not uniform. It has a dead centre and a salvageable edge, and each zone writes its own mark on the EKG.

ZoneWhat is happeningEKG changeCan you get it back
Ischemia, the outer ringReduced flow, cells still aliveT wave inversionYes, with reperfusion
Injury, the middleSeverely starved, cells dying nowST elevationSometimes, if you are fast
Infarction, the centreNecrotic, cells deadPathological Q wavesNo. That muscle is gone

This is the reason speed is the treatment. You cannot save the centre. You are fighting for the middle ring.

Time is muscle

Every minute of occlusion costs myocardium. Two numbers hold the whole idea.

  • Door to balloon under 90 minutes for PCI.
  • Door to needle under 30 minutes for thrombolytics.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Time is Muscle
Necrosis begins around 20 minutes into a total occlusion. The clock starts at symptom onset, not at arrival, so ask when the pain started and write down the answer. Door to balloon under 90 minutes, door to needle under 30.
Bottom Line: Every treatment raises supply or lowers demand. Necrosis starts at about 20 minutes, so the only real intervention is speed.
04

Complete or partial

Discrimination axis: how much of the artery is blocked.

Unstable angina, NSTEMI and STEMI are the same disease at three depths. The scenario tells you the depth with two pieces of data: the ST segments and the troponin.

STEMI: the artery is shut
  • Complete coronary occlusion
  • ST elevation in two or more neighbouring leads
  • Troponin rises, though it may lag by hours
  • Transmural infarct, the full wall thickness
  • Q waves develop over hours to days

Priority: emergent reperfusion. PCI is preferred; thrombolytics if PCI is out of reach, within 12 hours of symptom onset.

NSTEMI: the artery is narrowed
  • Partial or intermittent occlusion
  • ST depression or T wave inversion
  • Troponin positive, which is what separates it from angina
  • Subendocardial infarct, the inner layer only
  • No pathological Q waves

Priority: urgent, not emergent. Anti-ischemic therapy, anticoagulation, catheterisation within 24 to 72 hours.

The axis that separates them

ST elevation means the artery is closed and the clock is running in minutes. No elevation means it is narrowed and the clock runs in hours.

Then troponin splits the bottom two. Negative troponin with ischemic pain is unstable angina. Positive troponin without ST elevation is NSTEMI. Same symptoms, different lab.

What the patient looks like

The classic picture is worth knowing because most scenarios still use it.

  • Chest pain: crushing or heavy, often described as a weight, radiating to the left arm, jaw, neck or back.
  • Diaphoresis: cold and clammy. This one is a hallmark and it belongs in your answer.
  • Dyspnea: from falling cardiac output.
  • Nausea and vomiting: a vagal response, most common in inferior MI.
  • Anxiety: often described as a sense of impending doom.
  • Weakness and fatigue: from poor perfusion.
The presentation that gets missed

Women, older adults and people with diabetes often present atypically.

  • Fatigue, weakness or malaise instead of pain
  • Nausea, indigestion or epigastric discomfort
  • Shortness of breath with no chest pain at all
  • Diabetic neuropathy can produce a silent MI

The exam question rarely says crushing chest pain for these patients. It says a 74 year old woman with diabetes feels tired and nauseated. Get the EKG.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Atypical Presentations
Three groups present without classic pain: women, older adults, and people with diabetes. Neuropathy is why diabetic MIs can be silent. If any of the three feels unwell and you cannot explain it, get a 12 lead.
Write this down

Blank paper. Three rows: unstable angina, NSTEMI, STEMI. Two columns: ST segments and troponin. Fill all six cells from memory. That grid answers a surprising number of questions on its own.

Bottom Line: ST elevation means shut and emergent. No elevation means narrowed and urgent. Troponin then splits angina from NSTEMI.
05

Troponin and the clock

Discrimination axis: is a negative troponin a negative patient.

Cardiac biomarkers are proteins that leak out of dying myocardial cells. They matter most when the EKG is unclear, which is often.

The trap in this section is not which marker. It is when.

Biomarker Starts rising Peaks Back to normal What you use it for
Troponin I or T 2 to 4 hours 12 to 24 hours 7 to 14 days The gold standard. Most sensitive and most specific
High-sensitivity troponin 1 to 3 hours 12 to 24 hours 7 to 14 days Detects earlier, so it rules out faster
CK-MB 4 to 6 hours 18 to 24 hours 2 to 3 days Catches reinfarction, because it clears quickly
Myoglobin 1 to 3 hours 6 to 9 hours 24 hours Rises early but is not cardiac specific
The axis that separates them

Troponin answers whether muscle died. CK-MB answers whether it died again. Troponin stays up for two weeks, so it cannot report a second event. CK-MB is back to baseline in two or three days, so a fresh rise means fresh damage.

That is the only reason CK-MB is still drawn.

The timing trap

A single troponin cannot rule out an MI. The level takes 2 to 4 hours to rise, so a patient one hour into their pain can have a normal result and a closing artery.

Serial troponins are drawn at arrival, at 3 to 6 hours, and sometimes at 12 hours. You are looking for a rise and fall pattern, not one number.

Treat what is in front of you while you wait. The EKG and the presentation do not need lab confirmation to start aspirin.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Serial Troponins
One negative troponin rules out nothing. It takes 2 to 4 hours to rise, so an early draw only tells you the pain is recent. Draw at 0, at 3 to 6 hours, sometimes at 12, and read the trend.

What a high troponin does not mean

  • Elevated troponin is not automatically an MI. Heart failure, myocarditis, pulmonary embolism, renal failure, sepsis and cardiac surgery all raise it.
  • Diagnosis needs two things: a rise or fall in troponin, plus clinical evidence of ischemia from symptoms, EKG or imaging.
  • Height tracks size. The higher the peak, the larger the infarct and the worse the outlook.

Reference values

Troponin I normal
Under 0.04 ng/mL, varies by lab
High-sensitivity troponin
Under 14 to 19 ng/L, varies
CK-MB normal
Under 3 to 6 percent of total CK
BNP, heart failure marker
Under 100 pg/mL
Bottom Line: Troponin is the standard, but one value is a timestamp. Serial draws show the rise and fall, and that pattern is the diagnosis.
06

Which wall died

Discrimination axis: which artery closed, and what that predicts.

This section is one chain, and it runs in one direction. Leads tell you the wall. The wall tells you the artery. The artery tells you what goes wrong next.

Learn it as a chain and you stop memorising four separate tables.

The chain

Leads with ST elevationWallArteryWhat you then watch for
V1, V2, V3, V4AnteriorLADCardiogenic shock, heart failure, VT and VF
I, aVL, V5, V6LateralCircumflex, sometimes LADPapillary muscle dysfunction
II, III, aVFInferiorRCA, usuallyBradycardia, AV blocks, RV infarct
V7 to V9, with reciprocal depression in V1 to V3PosteriorRCA or circumflexUsually travels with an inferior MI
V4R and right-sided leadsRight ventricleProximal RCAHypotension, JVD, clear lungs

Why the artery predicts the problem

Each coronary artery supplies specific structures, and those structures are what fail.

ArteryWhat it feedsSo an occlusion causes
Left anterior descendingAnterior left ventricle, septum, bundle branchesAnterior MI. The largest territory and the worst outlook
Left circumflexLateral and posterior left ventricleLateral or posterior MI
Right coronaryRight ventricle, inferior left ventricle, SA and AV nodesInferior MI, and the conduction problems that follow
Left mainSplits into the LAD and the circumflexThe widow maker. Massive infarct, often fatal

The RCA row explains the whole inferior MI picture. It feeds the SA and AV nodes, so an inferior MI brings bradycardia and heart block. It feeds the right ventricle, so an inferior MI can take that with it.

What each EKG change means

FindingWhat it representsWhen it shows up
ST elevationAcute injury, infarction happening nowMinutes to hours after occlusion
ST depressionIschemia, or reciprocal change from elevation elsewhereEarly. Seen in NSTEMI
T wave inversionIschemia, evolving or recentHours to days after the event
Pathological Q wavesNecrosis. Muscle that is goneHours to days, often permanent
Hyperacute T wavesVery early ischemiaThe first minutes, and easily missed

Anterior or inferior

Discrimination axis: which one kills quickly and which one blocks the conduction.

Anterior: the biggest territory
  • ST elevation in V1 to V4
  • LAD occlusion
  • The largest area of left ventricle
  • High risk of cardiogenic shock
  • High risk of ventricular arrhythmias
  • Worst prognosis of the wall locations
Inferior: the conduction problem
  • ST elevation in II, III and aVF
  • RCA occlusion in most cases
  • Watch for bradycardia
  • AV blocks, because the RCA feeds the AV node
  • Check right-sided leads, V4R
  • Generally a better prognosis
Right ventricular infarct

If the MI is inferior, check right-sided leads. Up to a third of inferior MIs involve the right ventricle, and it changes your management completely.

The triad: hypotension, JVD, and clear lungs. Clear lungs is the finding that separates it from left-sided failure, because the left ventricle is still working.

Treatment: IV fluids. Withhold nitrates and diuretics. These patients are preload dependent, and both drugs take preload away.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
RV Infarct Triad
Hypotension, JVD, clear lungs. The clear lungs is the giveaway; left-sided failure would have crackles. A preload dependent ventricle needs volume, so give fluids and withhold nitrates and diuretics.
Write this down

Blank paper. Three rows: anterior, inferior, right ventricular. For each, write the leads, the artery, and the one complication you are watching for. Three by three, from memory, in under two minutes.

Bottom Line: Leads give you the wall, the wall gives you the artery, the artery gives you the complication. V1 to V4 is anterior and the LAD. II, III and aVF is inferior and the RCA, so check V4R.
07

tPA, and who cannot have it

Thrombolytics dissolve the clot by activating plasminogen. They are the backup plan for STEMI, used when primary PCI cannot happen within 120 minutes.

Most questions here are not about how the drug works. They are about who you refuse to give it to, and what you do when the patient starts bleeding.

AgentWhat it isHow it is given
Alteplase, tPATissue plasminogen activatorIV bolus, then infusion over 90 minutes
Reteplase, RetavaseRecombinant tPATwo IV boluses, 30 minutes apart
Tenecteplase, TNKaseModified tPAOne IV bolus, weight based
All four must be true before you give it
  • STEMI on the EKG, with ST elevation of at least 1 mm in two or more neighbouring leads
  • Symptoms started within 12 hours, and within 3 hours is far better
  • PCI is not available within 120 minutes
  • No absolute contraindication

Who cannot have it

Absolute contraindicationsRelative contraindications
Any prior intracranial hemorrhageTIA within 3 months
Known cerebral vascular malformation or tumourCurrently on an oral anticoagulant
Ischemic stroke within 3 monthsPregnancy, or within 1 week postpartum
Suspected aortic dissectionNon-compressible vascular punctures
Active bleeding or a bleeding disorderTraumatic CPR over 10 minutes, or major surgery within 3 weeks
Significant closed head or facial trauma within 3 monthsSevere uncontrolled hypertension, systolic over 180 or diastolic over 110
HEADS

The absolute contraindications share one theme: somewhere in this patient is a place that will bleed and cannot be compressed.

  • Hemorrhagic stroke, ever
  • Extreme blood pressure
  • Aortic dissection suspected
  • Disorder of bleeding
  • Surgery or trauma, recent

Your job, before and after

  1. 1
    Before. Confirm the STEMI, screen the contraindications, document a baseline neuro assessment, place two large-bore IVs, and draw CBC, PT and INR, and a type and screen.
  2. 2
    During. Vital signs every 15 minutes. Look for bleeding at the gums, the IV sites, the urine and the stool. Reassess neuro status often. No invasive procedures.
  3. 3
    Signs it worked. Chest pain resolves, ST segments come back toward baseline, cardiac markers peak early, and reperfusion arrhythmias appear. PVCs and accelerated idioventricular rhythm are expected here, not alarming.
  4. 4
    After. Watch for bleeding for 24 to 48 hours. No intramuscular injections. Soft toothbrush. Hold pressure on venipuncture sites. Report any neuro change immediately.
The complication you are watching for

Intracranial hemorrhage happens in about 1 percent of patients given thrombolytics, and it is often fatal.

Stop the infusion and call the provider for any of these:

  • Sudden severe headache
  • Change in level of consciousness
  • New focal neurological deficit
  • Nausea or vomiting alongside a neuro change

Stop first, then call. You do not need an order to stop an infusion that is causing harm.

Bottom Line: Thrombolytics are for STEMI when PCI is over 120 minutes away, within 12 hours of onset. The screening question is always where this patient might bleed.
08

What you do first

Priority questions give you four defensible actions and ask which comes first. All four are things a nurse would do. That is the trick. You are being tested on ordering, not on correctness.

The ordering rules
  • Diagnose before you treat. The 12 lead comes within 10 minutes of arrival, because it decides everything after it.
  • The drug that changes mortality goes first. That is aspirin, chewed, and it goes in unless there is a true allergy.
  • Check the pressure before any drug that lowers it. Nitroglycerin and morphine both drop it.
  • Monitoring is an intervention. Telemetry in the first hour is what catches the VF that kills them.
  • Oxygen is a treatment for hypoxia, not for anxiety. Under 90 percent saturation, give it. Above that, it does not help.

The first ten minutes

  1. 1
    12 lead EKG. Within 10 minutes of arrival. It is the test that sorts STEMI from everything else, and the whole pathway branches on it.
  2. 2
    Aspirin, 162 to 325 mg, chewed. Chewed, not swallowed whole, because chewing gets it absorbed faster. Give it unless the patient has a true aspirin allergy.
  3. 3
    Continuous cardiac monitoring. On telemetry immediately, with a defibrillator nearby. VF is what kills in the first hour.
  4. 4
    IV access. Two large-bore lines, 18 gauge or bigger. Draw cardiac markers, CBC, BMP, coagulation studies, and a type and screen.
  5. 5
    Oxygen, only if it is needed. Saturation under 90 percent, dyspnea, or signs of heart failure. Routine oxygen for an uncomplicated MI is no longer recommended.
MONA-B, and what it gets wrong

MONA-B lists morphine, oxygen, nitroglycerin, aspirin and beta-blockers. It is useful for remembering the set. It is misleading about the sequence.

  • Aspirin is the one that changes mortality, and it is fourth in the mnemonic.
  • Oxygen is second in the mnemonic and is withheld unless the saturation is under 90 percent.
  • Nitroglycerin needs a blood pressure check first, and is contraindicated outright in RV infarct.
  • Morphine relieves pain and drops preload, but it can drop the pressure too.
  • Beta-blockers lower rate and demand, and are held in cardiogenic shock.

Remember the letters. Do not follow them in order.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
MONA-B Is a Set, Not a Sequence
Aspirin is the drug that changes mortality and it sits fourth in the mnemonic. Oxygen sits second and most patients should not get it. Use MONA-B to recall the five, then order them yourself.

The drugs and what you check first

MedicationWhat it doesWhat you check or watch

NitroglycerinDilates coronaries, drops preloadBlood pressure before every dose. Hold if systolic is under 90

Four times you withhold nitroglycerin
  • Systolic blood pressure under 90 mmHg
  • Heart rate under 50 or over 100
  • Right ventricular infarct, because the patient is preload dependent
  • A PDE5 inhibitor such as sildenafil or tadalafil in the last 24 to 48 hours

The last one is easy to miss because you have to ask, and the patient may not volunteer it.

Ongoing care

  • Pain: 0 to 10 scale, reassessed after every intervention. New pain means new ischemia.
  • Vital signs: every 15 minutes until stable, then every 1 to 2 hours.
  • Telemetry: continuous. Report arrhythmias as they happen.
  • Activity: bed rest at first, then graded mobilisation.
  • Intake and output: watch for heart failure developing.
  • Anxiety: address it. Anxiety raises rate, and rate raises demand.
  • Stool softeners: straining against a closed glottis drops cardiac output, and this heart has no reserve.
Bottom Line: EKG in 10 minutes, aspirin chewed, telemetry on. Then check a pressure before anything that lowers it.
09

What kills them, and when

Discrimination axis: how long since the infarct.

Complications after an MI are not a random list. They arrive on a schedule, and the schedule is the answer to most complication questions.

First hour, electrical. First days, pump failure. Days three to seven, the tissue tears. Weeks later, the immune system.

The electrical complications

ArrhythmiaWhenWhat you do
Ventricular fibrillationFirst 4 hours. The leading cause of deathDefibrillate immediately, then ACLS
Ventricular tachycardiaFirst 24 to 48 hoursSustained gets cardioversion. Non-sustained gets monitoring and antiarrhythmics
Sinus bradycardiaInferior MI, a vagal responseAtropine if symptomatic, temporary pacing if it persists
AV blocksInferior MI, because the RCA feeds the AV nodeMonitor, atropine, transcutaneous or transvenous pacing
Atrial fibrillationAny time after the MIRate control, anticoagulation, treat the cause
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Watch for VF
Ischemic muscle is electrically unstable, and VF is what kills in the first hour. That is the reason for continuous telemetry and a defibrillator at the bedside. The only treatment is immediate defibrillation.

The mechanical complications

ComplicationTimingHow it shows upTreatment
Cardiogenic shockHours to daysHypotension, tachycardia, cold and clammy, low urine output, confusionInotropes, intra-aortic balloon pump, mechanical support
Papillary muscle rupture2 to 7 daysSudden mitral regurgitation, new murmur, pulmonary edemaEmergency surgery
Ventricular septal rupture3 to 5 daysNew harsh systolic murmur, rapid deteriorationEmergency surgery
Free wall rupture5 to 14 daysSudden tamponade, PEA arrestUsually fatal. Surgery if it is caught
Ventricular aneurysmWeeks to monthsHeart failure, arrhythmias, emboliAnticoagulation, surgical repair
Why day three to seven is the dangerous window

Necrotic muscle is softest while the body is clearing it and before scar has formed. That is roughly days 3 to 7.

So a new murmur or a sudden crash on day four is a rupture until proven otherwise. Early on you are watching rhythm; midweek you are watching structure.

Cardiogenic shock

This is the most common cause of in-hospital death after MI. Recognise it early.

  • Systolic under 90 mmHg for 30 minutes or more, or needing vasopressors to stay above it
  • Signs the organs are not being perfused: cool extremities, low urine output, altered mental status
  • Cardiac index under 2.2 L/min/m2
  • Pulmonary capillary wedge pressure over 15 mmHg, if it is being measured

The rest of the list

  • Pericarditis: chest pain that worsens with a breath in, and a pericardial friction rub. Treated with aspirin and colchicine. Avoid anticoagulants while it is present.
  • Dressler's syndrome: autoimmune pericarditis weeks after the MI. Fever, pleuritic pain, pericardial effusion.
  • Left ventricular thrombus: forms on a wall segment that stopped moving. Needs anticoagulation to prevent an embolic stroke.
  • Heart failure: may appear acutely or develop over time, depending on how much muscle was lost.
Pericarditis or reinfarction

Both give post-MI chest pain. The axis is what changes the pain.

Pericarditis is pleuritic; it gets worse when the patient breathes in, and often better sitting forward. A friction rub seals it. Reinfarction pain is the original crushing pain returning, and it does not care about breathing.

Bottom Line: First hour, VF. First days, cardiogenic shock. Days 3 to 7, rupture. Weeks later, Dressler's. Time since the infarct tells you what to look for.
