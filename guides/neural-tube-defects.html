Neural Tube Defects

Three severities from one embryologic failure, the pre-op sac that is an emergency to protect, and the latex link that starts at birth.

By the end, without notes

  • Rank occulta, meningocele and myelomeningocele by what sits in the sac
  • Say how you protect an intact sac before surgery, in one breath

01

What actually gets you

This topic is not hard to understand. It is easy to lose points on, and always in the same three places. Read these before anything else.

You treated the intact sac as a wound to dress and move on from

The pre-op sac is an emergency to protect. Keep it covered with a sterile saline-moistened dressing. Position the baby prone. Never diaper over it. Never let it dry out or rupture. Watch for infection and for clear fluid leaking, which is CSF. A dry or torn sac means more permanent nerve damage.

You waited for a reaction before thinking about latex

By then it is too late. These children face repeated surgeries and catheterizations. Every exposure sensitizes them. So latex precautions start at birth, not after the first hives. Assume the child is latex-allergic from day one.

You learned the defect and stopped before the complication

Myelomeningocele rarely travels alone. Chiari II malformation blocks CSF flow. Hydrocephalus follows in most of these babies. That is what the shunt is for. Miss the increased-ICP signs and you miss the emergency behind the diagnosis.

Bottom Line: Study this as three jobs, not one disease. Protect the sac, guard against latex, and catch the hydrocephalus behind it.

03

The spina bifida spectrum

A neural tube defect starts with one failure. The neural tube becomes the brain and spinal cord. In neural tube defects it does not close during weeks 3-4 of gestation.

By day 28 the tube should be closed from the cranial to the caudal end. Where it fails decides the defect. A cranial failure gives anencephaly or encephalocele. A caudal failure gives spina bifida.

Severity depends on where the tube fails, and on how much. That single idea sorts the three forms below.

Occulta: hidden, mildest
  • A hidden vertebral defect with no sac
  • Often silent, found by chance
  • Skin may show a tuft of hair, dimple, lipoma or port-wine stain
  • Usually no neurologic deficit
  • Sometimes linked to a tethered cord later
  • Most need no treatment, just observation
Meningocele: membranes only
  • A sac of meninges and CSF only
  • No neural tissue inside the sac
  • A visible sac on the spine at birth
  • Minimal or no neurologic deficit
  • Surgical closure gives an excellent prognosis
  • Hydrocephalus is rare
Myelomeningocele: cord in the sac
  • A sac of meninges plus spinal cord and nerve roots
  • Flaccid paralysis below the lesion
  • Neurogenic bowel and bladder
  • 80-90% develop hydrocephalus from Chiari II
  • Often club foot, hip dislocation, scoliosis
  • Needs lifelong interdisciplinary care
Cranial defects
  • Anencephaly: absent brain and skull
  • Anencephaly is incompatible with life; care is palliative
  • Encephalocele: brain tissue protrudes through a skull defect
  • Outcomes vary by site and how much brain is involved
  • Some encephaloceles can be repaired surgically

Who is at risk

Incidence is about 1 in 1,500 live births in the US. Folic acid fortification of grains has cut that sharply. Rates run higher in Hispanic populations, and family history raises the risk.

Folate deficiency is the main modifiable risk. Other risks are maternal diabetes, obesity, and first-trimester hyperthermia. Certain anticonvulsants add risk too, including valproic acid and carbamazepine.

Prevention and prenatal detection

  • Folic acid 400 mcg per day for all women of childbearing age, ideally before conception and through the first trimester. This lowers risk by up to 70%.
  • High-risk women, meaning a prior affected pregnancy or an anticonvulsant, take 4 mg per day from one month before conception.
  • Maternal serum alpha-fetoprotein at 15-20 weeks is elevated in open defects.
  • Targeted fetal ultrasound identifies most defects by 18-22 weeks.
  • Amniocentesis confirms the diagnosis when screening is abnormal.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Folic acid runs on a clock
The tube closes by day 28. Many women do not know they are pregnant yet. So folate has to be on board before conception, not after a positive test. That is why the advice is every woman of childbearing age, not only those trying. Starting late is often too late.
Bottom Line: Occulta hides, meningocele holds membranes, myelomeningocele holds cord. Folic acid before conception prevents most of it.
04

Myelomeningocele up close

This is the severe end of the spectrum. The spinal cord and nerve roots sit exposed in the sac. That exposure lets them be damaged in utero and at birth.

The deficits below the lesion are permanent. How much function remains tracks with how high the lesion sits.

Level of lesion sets the function

Lesion level Typical motor and functional impact
Thoracic Full leg paralysis; wheelchair for mobility.
High lumbar (L1-L3) Hip flexion preserved; may walk with braces and crutches; wheelchair for distance.
Low lumbar (L4-L5) Knee flexion and extension preserved; most ambulate with an ankle-foot orthosis.
Sacral (S1 and below) Ambulation usually possible; bowel and bladder problems predominate.

What you see at birth

  • A visible sac low on the back, usually lumbar or lumbosacral, with thin or transparent covering over neural tissue.
  • Flaccid paralysis of the legs, varying with the lesion level.
  • Loss of sensation below the lesion.
  • Neurogenic bladder and bowel: constant dribbling of urine, constipation, bowel incontinence.
  • Hip dislocation, club foot and scoliosis, often already present.
  • Early hydrocephalus signs: bulging fontanelle, rapid head growth, sunset eyes, lethargy, poor feeding.

What travels with it

  • Arnold-Chiari II malformation. The cerebellar tonsils and medulla herniate through the foramen magnum. It is almost universal here, and it blocks CSF flow, which drives hydrocephalus.
  • Hydrocephalus in 80-90% of cases, usually needing a ventriculoperitoneal shunt.
  • Tethered cord can appear later as the spine grows. It brings progressive weakness, bladder changes and back pain, and needs surgical release.
  • Latex allergy from cumulative exposure, requiring lifelong avoidance.
Watch for hydrocephalus from day one

Most of these infants develop hydrocephalus. So serial head circumference is a daily task. Increased-ICP signs in an infant include a bulging fontanelle and rapid head growth. Add sunset eyes, widened sutures, a high-pitched cry, vomiting, poor feeding and lethargy. Cushing's triad of hypertension, bradycardia and irregular breathing is a late and ominous sign.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The head tells you first
In an infant, the skull can still expand. So rising head circumference and a bulging fontanelle are early ICP signs, not late ones. Measure the head daily. A number climbing across the growth chart is your cue that the CSF is backing up.
Bottom Line: Exposed cord means flaccid paralysis, neurogenic bowel and bladder, Chiari II and hydrocephalus. The level of the lesion predicts function.
05

The complications that follow

Myelomeningocele is not a one-surgery fix. It is a lifelong condition across many teams. Knowing the common complications lets you see them coming.

Hydrocephalus and the VP shunt

  • Most patients, 80-90%, need a VP shunt at birth or within the first weeks.
  • The shunt drains excess CSF from the ventricles to the peritoneum.
  • Shunt malfunction signs at any age: headache, vomiting, irritability, decreased level of consciousness, seizures. In infants, a bulging fontanelle. In older children, personality changes.
  • Fever plus a shunt means possible shunt infection, and urgent evaluation.
  • Revisions are common as the child grows or the shunt fails.
The shunt triad that means an emergency

A child with a VP shunt arrives with headache, vomiting and new lethargy. Treat it as shunt malfunction until proven otherwise. The shunt may be blocked, disconnected or infected. Untreated, rising ICP can herniate. This is a call to the provider, not a wait-and-see.

Neurogenic bladder

  • Most patients cannot empty the bladder completely on their own.
  • Clean intermittent catheterization every 3-4 hours is the standard. It starts in the newborn period, taught to parents first, then the child.
  • Goals: preserve renal function, keep continence, prevent UTIs, protect the upper tracts.
  • Medications: anticholinergics such as oxybutynin relax the bladder; alpha-blockers such as doxazosin ease outlet resistance.
  • Urology follows with regular renal ultrasound, urodynamics and urinalysis.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Empty on a schedule, not on overflow
A neurogenic bladder that fills and overflows damages the upper tracts. Clean intermittent catheterization empties it on a clock instead, every 3-4 hours. An indwelling Foley invites infection, so CIC is preferred. Parents learn it early, and the child takes over with age.

Neurogenic bowel

  • Chronic constipation and fecal incontinence are common.
  • A bowel program builds continence: scheduled toileting, high-fiber diet, fluids, stool softeners, suppositories and enemas as needed.
  • Older children may use transanal irrigation or an antegrade continence enema procedure.

Orthopedic and mobility issues

Club foot and hip dislocation are often present at birth.

Scoliosis develops in most patients; orthopedics follows across growth.

Braces, orthotics and wheelchairs support mobility as needed.

Physical and occupational therapy start in infancy.

Pressure ulcers form on insensate skin; teach daily skin checks.

Fractures can occur without obvious trauma from disuse osteopenia.

Development and cognition

  • Intelligence varies widely, and many children have a normal IQ.
  • Specific learning disabilities are common, often in math and executive function.
  • Early intervention and school-based services help.
  • Psychosocial support matters as the child moves toward independence and adult care.
Bottom Line: Lifelong care means shunt watching, catheterization, a bowel program, orthopedic follow-up and developmental support. Many teams, long game.
06

The latex link

These children carry the highest latex allergy rate in pediatrics. Up to 40-65% are allergic by adolescence. Repeated mucosal exposures sensitize the immune system.

The exposures add up fast: surgeries, shunt revisions, catheterizations, procedures. An allergic child who meets latex can have anaphylaxis. It can happen in the operating room, the clinic or at home.

Why the risk climbs so high

  • Multiple surgeries across childhood, each exposing mucosa to latex.
  • Clean intermittent catheterization up to six times a day, for years.
  • Shunt revisions throughout childhood.
  • A young age at first exposure raises sensitization.

Latex-free from day one

Latex-free gloves, tape, catheters, tubing and equipment in every setting.

Flag the chart and ID bracelet with latex precautions from the first admission.

Teach the family to avoid balloons, rubber toys, rubber balls, bandages and some pacifiers.

Cross-reactive foods include banana, avocado, kiwi, chestnut, tomato, potato and papaya. Introduce them with care.

A medical alert bracelet reading latex allergy, starting in childhood.

An epinephrine auto-injector for sensitized children; teach its use.

Spotting a reaction

  • Mild: local contact dermatitis, itching, a rash at the exposure site.
  • Moderate: generalized hives, angioedema, wheezing, abdominal pain.
  • Severe: anaphylaxis with hypotension, stridor, bronchospasm and shock. Treat with IM epinephrine, airway support, IV fluids, antihistamines and steroids.
The deadly presentation is in the operating room

A myelomeningocele child goes in for routine surgery. Under anesthesia comes sudden hypotension, bronchospasm and flushing. That can be intraoperative latex anaphylaxis. This is why every operating and procedure room treats these patients as latex-allergic until proven otherwise. Prevention saves the life.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Allergic until proven otherwise
You do not wait for a positive test here. Assume the latex allergy and build a latex-free world around the child. Gloves, catheters, tape, toys and foods all count. The three fruits to remember are banana, avocado and kiwi.
Bottom Line: Every myelomeningocele patient is latex-allergic until proven otherwise. Latex-free from day one, for life, with the family in on it.
07

Nursing care, in order

Repair happens within 24-72 hours of birth. Early closure lowers the risk of infection and further nerve damage. Between delivery and the operating room, your job is the sac.

Before surgery, protect the sac

  1. 1
    Position prone or side-lying. Never supine. Pressure on the sac can rupture it or damage the cord.
  2. 2
    Cover the sac with a sterile saline-moistened dressing. Keep it non-adherent. Change it as ordered. Never let the sac dry.
  3. 3
    Keep the environment strictly latex-free. From the first moment of care. Latex-free gloves, tape, monitors and equipment.
  4. 4
    Maintain thermoregulation. Use a radiant warmer or isolette. Exposed neural tissue cannot hold temperature.
  5. 5
    Measure head circumference daily. Watch for hydrocephalus: bulging fontanelle, lethargy, vomiting, high-pitched cry.
  6. 6
    Do baseline neuro checks. Assess movement and reflexes below the lesion, so change is visible later.
  7. 7
    Watch for a CSF leak. Any clear drainage from the sac is a medical emergency.
  8. 8
    Prepare for the operating room. NPO, IV access, type and crossmatch, and family consent and teaching.
Never position supine

Before surgery and early after, supine can rupture the sac or disrupt the closure. Prone is preferred. Side-lying is acceptable with careful support. This is a classic pediatric priority.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Sac care in three words
The sac needs three things. Moist, from a sterile saline dressing, never dry. Prone, to take pressure off it. Sterile, because infection here means meningitis. Get those right in the first 24 hours and you have done your job before the surgeon arrives.

After closure

  • Continue prone or side-lying to protect the site. Back-lying is usually restricted for 1-2 weeks.
  • Incision care: watch for infection, CSF leakage and drainage.
  • Head circumference daily. Hydrocephalus often shows or worsens once the sac stops decompressing CSF.
  • Watch for shunt candidacy. Many need a VP shunt within the first 2 weeks.
  • Pain control: scheduled acetaminophen, with opioids for breakthrough pain.
  • Bladder: check for a full bladder; use the Credé maneuver or catheterization per order.
  • Bowel: gentle diaper changes without pressure on the incision; stool softeners as needed.
  • Skin: turn every 2 hours and protect the repaired site.
  • Bonding: help parents hold the infant and breastfeed in a side-lying position.

The family and the long haul

Your role often starts in the delivery room. You translate the diagnosis, connect families to resources, and stay present through hard moments.

  • Many defects are found prenatally by elevated MSAFP and ultrasound at 15-20 weeks. Discuss the specific defect, prognosis, surgical plan and care needs.
  • Connect the family to a multidisciplinary spina bifida center when possible.
  • Consider fetal surgery, an in-utero closure, when eligible. It improves motor outcomes and lowers the shunt rate.
  • Teach the early skills: catheterization, the bowel program, shunt warning signs, daily skin checks, and latex avoidance.
  • Support independence at school age: self-catheterization, self-advocacy, and a 504 plan or IEP.
  • Start transition planning to adult care in the early teen years.
  • For the next pregnancy, the mother needs 4 mg of folic acid, a high-risk OB and genetic counseling.
The long game is doable

Many children with myelomeningocele grow up to finish school and live independently. The road is long, not hopeless. Families who feel supported early do better. Point them to the Spina Bifida Association, local groups and regional clinics.

Bottom Line: Before surgery it is prone, moist sterile dressing, latex-free and daily head measures. After, keep protecting the site and partner with the family.
