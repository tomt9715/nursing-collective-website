Oxygen Physiology Review

Understanding oxygen transport and delivery is fundamental to safe and effective oxygen therapy. Oxygen must travel from the atmosphere to the mitochondria in cells, where it is used for cellular respiration.

The Oxygen Cascade

Oxygen moves through a series of decreasing partial pressures from atmosphere to tissues:

Location PO2 (mmHg) Description
Atmosphere (sea level) 159 21% of atmospheric pressure (760 mmHg)
Trachea (humidified) 149 Water vapor displaces some oxygen
Alveoli 100-104 CO2 present, mixed with residual air
Arterial blood (PaO2) 80-100 Normal range for healthy adults
Tissues 40 Where O2 is consumed by mitochondria
Mitochondria 1-10 Final destination for cellular respiration

Oxygen Transport Mechanisms

How Oxygen Travels in Blood

1
Bound to Hemoglobin (98.5%)

Each hemoglobin molecule carries 4 oxygen molecules. Measured by SpO2 (oxygen saturation). 1 gram of Hgb carries 1.34 mL of O2 when fully saturated.

2
Dissolved in Plasma (1.5%)

Small amount dissolved directly in plasma. Measured by PaO2 (partial pressure). Only dissolved O2 can diffuse into tissues, but hemoglobin acts as a reservoir.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
SpO2 vs PaO2: Know the Difference
SpO2 (pulse oximetry) tells you what percentage of hemoglobin is carrying oxygen - it's a saturation measurement (normal: 94-100%). PaO2 (arterial blood gas) tells you the partial pressure of oxygen dissolved in blood (normal: 80-100 mmHg). A patient can have "normal" SpO2 with dangerously low hemoglobin (anemia) and still be hypoxic at the tissue level! Always consider the whole picture: SpO2 + hemoglobin level + cardiac output = oxygen delivery.

Oxyhemoglobin Dissociation Curve

The relationship between PaO2 and SpO2 is not linear - it follows an S-shaped curve. This has important clinical implications:

PaO2 (mmHg) Approximate SpO2 Clinical Significance
100 98% Normal - fully saturated
80 95% Still on the "flat" part of curve
60 90% Critical point - steep drop begins
40 75% Severe hypoxemia
27 50% P50 - half saturation point
Critical Value: The 90/60 Rule

SpO2 of 90% correlates with PaO2 of approximately 60 mmHg

This is the critical threshold! Below this point, small drops in PaO2 cause dramatic drops in SpO2 because you're on the "steep" part of the curve. Above 90%, you're on the "flat" plateau where saturation stays relatively stable even with PaO2 changes.

Factors Affecting the Curve

Shift Direction Causes Effect on O2 Release
Right Shift â†‘ Temperature, â†‘ CO2, â†“ pH (acidosis), â†‘ 2,3-DPG Hemoglobin releases O2 more easily (good for tissues)
Left Shift â†“ Temperature, â†“ CO2, â†‘ pH (alkalosis), â†“ 2,3-DPG, CO poisoning Hemoglobin holds onto O2 more tightly (bad for tissues)
Memory Aid: Right Shift Causes

CADET, face Right!

  • CO2 increased
  • Acid (pH decreased)
  • DPG (2,3-DPG) increased

Indications for Oxygen Therapy

Oxygen therapy is indicated when tissue oxygenation is inadequate or at risk. The goal is to maintain adequate tissue oxygenation while minimizing complications.

Primary Indications

Indication Criteria Clinical Examples
Hypoxemia PaO2 < 60 mmHg or SpO2 < 90% ARDS, pneumonia, PE, CHF exacerbation
Suspected Hypoxemia Clinical signs pending ABG results Trauma, acute respiratory distress, cardiac arrest
Severe Trauma Regardless of SpO2 Major bleeding, shock, multiple injuries
Acute MI If SpO2 < 94% or signs of hypoxemia STEMI, NSTEMI with respiratory compromise
Post-operative During anesthesia recovery PACU, post-general anesthesia
Carbon Monoxide Poisoning 100% O2 regardless of SpO2 Fire victims, suicide attempts, heater malfunctions
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
CO Poisoning: Don't Trust the Pulse Ox!
In carbon monoxide poisoning, pulse oximetry can show normal or high SpO2 readings even though the patient is severely hypoxic! Why? CO binds to hemoglobin 200x more strongly than O2, and the pulse ox cannot distinguish between oxyhemoglobin (HbO2) and carboxyhemoglobin (HbCO). A fire victim with SpO2 of 99% may actually have dangerously low oxygen-carrying capacity. Always give 100% O2 via NRB for suspected CO poisoning and get a co-oximetry blood test (not regular pulse ox).

Target Oxygen Saturation Ranges

Patient Population Target SpO2 Rationale
Most Adults 94-98% Adequate oxygenation without excess
COPD (CO2 retainers) 88-92% Avoid suppressing hypoxic drive
Acute MI (no hypoxia) 94-98% Hyperoxia may be harmful
Stroke (acute) 94-98% Avoid hyperoxia; may worsen outcomes
Premature Neonates 91-95% Prevent retinopathy of prematurity
Resuscitation 100% initially Titrate down after ROSC
Hyperoxia Can Be Harmful

More oxygen is not always better! Current evidence shows that targeting SpO2 of 100% can cause harm in several conditions:

  • Post-cardiac arrest: Hyperoxia associated with worse neurological outcomes
  • Acute MI: May increase infarct size
  • Acute stroke: May worsen ischemic injury
  • COPD: Suppresses respiratory drive, causes CO2 narcosis

Titrate to the minimum FiO2 needed to achieve target SpO2.

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: O2 is indicated when SpO2 < 94% (or < 88% for COPD). It's a treatment, not a cure â€” always identify and treat the underlying cause of hypoxemia.

Oxygen Delivery Devices

Oxygen delivery devices are categorized by their ability to provide consistent FiO2 and their flow characteristics. Selecting the right device depends on the patient's oxygen needs, comfort, and clinical situation.

Low-Flow vs High-Flow Systems

Low-Flow Systems

Characteristics

  • Flow rate < patient's inspiratory demand
  • Patient inhales room air to supplement
  • FiO2 varies with breathing pattern
  • Comfortable, well-tolerated

Examples

  • Nasal cannula
  • Simple face mask
  • Partial rebreather mask
  • Non-rebreather mask
High-Flow Systems

Characteristics

  • Flow rate â‰¥ patient's inspiratory demand
  • Provides entire inspiratory volume
  • FiO2 is precise and consistent
  • Less comfortable but more reliable

Examples

  • Venturi mask
  • High-flow nasal cannula (HFNC)
  • Aerosol mask
  • T-piece/trach collar
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Low-Flow vs High-Flow: The Key Difference
The key question: Does the device meet ALL of the patient's inspiratory demand? A normal adult inhales about 30-40 L/min at peak. A nasal cannula at 2 L/min obviously can't supply all that, so the patient dilutes it with room air - making FiO2 unpredictable. A Venturi mask uses air entrainment to deliver 40+ L/min at a set FiO2, meeting inspiratory demand with a consistent oxygen concentration. When you need precise FiO2 control (like in COPD patients), use a high-flow device like a Venturi mask!

Venturi Mask: The COPD Patient's Best Friend
For COPD patients who are CO2 retainers, the Venturi mask is ideal because it delivers a precise, consistent FiO2 regardless of the patient's breathing pattern. You can set it to exactly 24% or 28% and know that's what they're getting - not "somewhere between 24-44%" like with a nasal cannula. This precision helps you target SpO2 of 88-92% without accidentally over-oxygenating and suppressing their hypoxic drive. Remember: these patients breathe because of LOW oxygen, not high CO2!

FiO2 Calculations

Understanding how to calculate and estimate FiO2 is essential for titrating oxygen therapy appropriately. Different devices have different FiO2 characteristics.

The 4% Rule for Nasal Cannula

Quick FiO2 Estimation

FiO2 = 20% + (4% Ã— L/min)

Example: 3 L/min â†’ 20% + (4% Ã— 3) = 20% + 12% = 32% FiO2

Note: This is an approximation. Actual FiO2 varies with patient's respiratory rate, tidal volume, and breathing pattern.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The 4% Rule: Quick Mental Math
For nasal cannula, remember: Start at 21% (room air) and add 4% for each liter per minute. So 1L = 25%, 2L = 29%, 3L = 33%, etc. Some sources round to 24%, 28%, 32%, 36%, 40%, 44% for simplicity. This rule ONLY works for nasal cannula and is just an estimate - actual FiO2 depends on the patient's breathing pattern. A patient breathing fast and deep will dilute the oxygen more with room air, getting a lower actual FiO2.

Oxygen-to-Air Entrainment Ratios

Venturi masks work by entraining room air through the Bernoulli principle. The entrainment ratio determines the final FiO2:

24% 1:25 4 L/min ~104 L/min
28% 1:10 4 L/min ~44 L/min
35% 1:5 8 L/min ~48 L/min
40% 1:3 8 L/min ~32 L/min
50% 1:1.7 12 L/min ~32 L/min

Special Populations

Certain patient populations require special consideration when administering oxygen therapy due to unique physiological vulnerabilities.

COPD Patients with Chronic Hypercapnia

Hypoxic Drive in COPD

COPD patients with chronic CO2 retention have lost their normal hypercapnic drive (response to high CO2). Their respiratory drive depends on hypoxia (low oxygen levels) instead.

Danger: High-flow oxygen removes the hypoxic stimulus, causing hypoventilation â†’ CO2 retention â†’ respiratory acidosis â†’ CO2 narcosis â†’ respiratory failure.

Parameter COPD Guideline Rationale
Target SpO2 88-92% Maintains hypoxic drive while avoiding severe hypoxia
Initial Device Venturi mask (24-28%) Precise FiO2 control
Alternative Nasal cannula 1-2 L/min Low flow, comfortable
Monitoring ABG within 30-60 min Check for CO2 retention
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
COPD: Don't Chase 100%!
When caring for a COPD patient, resist the urge to keep increasing oxygen to achieve "normal" saturations. Target 88-92% SpO2 - this is adequate! A COPD patient who normally runs SpO2 of 89% does NOT need you to get them to 98%. Doing so may suppress their breathing and cause them to retain even more CO2. Watch for signs of CO2 narcosis: confusion, decreased LOC, flushed skin, asterixis (hand flapping tremor). If these appear, notify the provider immediately.

Premature Neonates

Concern Target Clinical Implication
Retinopathy of Prematurity (ROP) SpO2 91-95% Hyperoxia damages developing retinal vessels
Bronchopulmonary Dysplasia Avoid prolonged high FiO2 Oxygen toxicity damages immature lungs
Free Radical Injury Lowest effective FiO2 Premature infants have limited antioxidant defenses

Post-Cardiac Arrest

Oxygen Management After ROSC

1
During Resuscitation

Use 100% oxygen - maximizing oxygen delivery is priority during CPR.

2
After ROSC (Return of Spontaneous Circulation)

Titrate FiO2 to achieve SpO2 94-98%. Avoid hyperoxia - associated with worse neurological outcomes.

3
Monitoring

Use ABG to guide therapy. Target PaO2 of 80-100 mmHg. Avoid PaO2 > 300 mmHg.

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: COPD = low flow (1â€“2 L/min), target 88â€“92% SpO2. Pediatric = use age-appropriate devices, humidify always. Post-op = incentive spirometry + O2 to prevent atelectasis.

Oxygen Toxicity

While oxygen is essential for life, prolonged exposure to high concentrations can cause significant damage. Understanding oxygen toxicity is crucial for safe oxygen administration.

Mechanisms of Oxygen Toxicity

How High FiO2 Causes Damage

1
Free Radical Formation

High PO2 increases production of reactive oxygen species (ROS) - superoxide, hydrogen peroxide, hydroxyl radicals - that damage cell membranes and DNA.

2
Absorption Atelectasis

High FiO2 washes out nitrogen, which normally helps keep alveoli open. Without nitrogen, alveoli collapse more easily when oxygen is absorbed.

3
Surfactant Dysfunction

Oxygen radicals damage type II pneumocytes that produce surfactant, leading to alveolar instability and collapse.

4
Inflammatory Response

Oxidative stress triggers inflammatory cascade, causing capillary leak, pulmonary edema, and fibrosis over time.

Types of Oxygen Toxicity

Type FiO2 Threshold Time Course Manifestations
Pulmonary Toxicity > 50% FiO2 24-48+ hours Tracheobronchitis, decreased lung compliance, ARDS-like picture
CNS Toxicity (Paul Bert Effect) > 100% at > 2 ATA Minutes to hours Seizures, tunnel vision, tinnitus (hyperbaric settings)
Retinopathy of Prematurity Variable (neonates) Days to weeks Abnormal retinal vessel growth, potential blindness
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Early Signs of Pulmonary Oxygen Toxicity
Watch for these early warning signs in patients on high FiO2 for extended periods: substernal chest pain or burning (tracheobronchitis), dry cough, dyspnea that worsens despite oxygen, and decreased lung compliance (harder to ventilate if on vent). The general rule: try to keep FiO2 â‰¤ 50% if possible, and always use the lowest FiO2 needed to achieve target SpO2. If a patient needs > 50% FiO2 for > 24-48 hours, consider other interventions (PEEP, prone positioning, ECMO).

Prevention Strategies

Minimizing Oxygen Toxicity Risk
  • Use the lowest effective FiO2 - Target SpO2, not FiO2
  • Wean oxygen as tolerated - Don't leave patients on high FiO2 unnecessarily
  • Target FiO2 â‰¤ 50% if possible for prolonged therapy
  • Optimize PEEP - Allows lower FiO2 while maintaining oxygenation
  • Treat underlying cause - Address the reason for hypoxemia
  • Monitor closely - ABGs, lung compliance, clinical status
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: FiO2 > 50% for > 24â€“48 hours damages alveoli (absorption atelectasis, then ARDS). Early signs: substernal chest pain, dry cough, dyspnea. Prevention: use lowest effective FiO2, wean as soon as possible.

Ongoing Monitoring

What to Monitor During Oxygen Therapy

1
Oxygen Saturation (Continuous)

Maintain target range. Report values outside parameters. Remember: SpO2 is a trend, not absolute truth - correlate with clinical picture.

2
Respiratory Assessment (Q1-4h)

Rate, depth, effort, lung sounds. Watch for increasing work of breathing, use of accessory muscles, or decreasing level of consciousness.

3
Equipment Function

Check flow rate settings, tubing connections, humidifier levels, mask seal. Ensure equipment matches ordered prescription.

4
Skin Integrity

Check ears, nose, cheeks for pressure injury from masks/tubing. Reposition prongs, use protective barriers as needed.

5
ABG Results (When Ordered)

Evaluate PaO2, PaCO2, pH. Especially important in COPD patients to detect CO2 retention.

Safety Considerations

Oxygen Fire Safety

Oxygen supports combustion! Critical safety measures:

  • No smoking within 10 feet of oxygen source
  • Keep away from open flames, sparks, heat sources
  • No petroleum-based products (Vaseline) near oxygen
  • Use water-based lubricants for nasal cannula
  • Ensure electrical equipment is properly grounded
  • Post "Oxygen in Use" signs

Patient Education

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
When to Question the Pulse Ox
Pulse oximetry can be inaccurate in several situations: poor perfusion (shock, hypothermia, vasoconstriction), nail polish (especially dark colors - remove or use earlobe), motion artifact, ambient light interference, anemia (SpO2 may look "fine" but oxygen delivery is low), CO poisoning (falsely normal), methemoglobinemia (reads ~85% regardless of true saturation). Always correlate with clinical assessment - if the patient looks hypoxic but SpO2 says 98%, believe the patient!

  • Breathing - Is the patient breathing adequately? Assess respiratory effort
  • Circulation - Is perfusion adequate? Check pulse, BP, cap refill

Then check the equipment and titrate oxygen as needed!
