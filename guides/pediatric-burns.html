Pediatric Burns

Burn depth, BSA estimation, Parkland fluid resuscitation, emergency ABCDE, wound care, and the long road of rehabilitation. Everything a pediatric nurse needs on the first day in a burn case.

High-Yield Summary

Ten facts that will carry you through any pediatric burn scenario.

  • Scalds are the #1 cause of pediatric burns in children under 5 (bath water, pulled-down pots). Thermal, chemical, electrical, radiation, and inhalation are the major categories.
  • Kids' skin is thinner & their BSA-to-weight ratio is bigger — same exposure makes a deeper burn, larger fluid loss, and faster hypothermia than in adults.
  • Burn depth ladder: superficial (1st, epidermis only) → superficial partial (blisters, painful) → deep partial (pale, less pain) → full-thickness (3rd, painless, leathery, needs grafting) → 4th (muscle/bone).
  • Full-thickness burns are painless in the burned area because nerve endings are destroyed. Pain from surrounding partial-thickness burn.
  • Pediatric BSA: Rule of Nines is modified — infants' heads are ~18% and legs ~14% (versus adult's 9% and 18%). Lund-Browder chart is most accurate in children.
  • Palm method: the child's own palm (including fingers) = ~1% BSA — useful for scattered or irregular burns.
  • Parkland Formula: 4 mL × kg × %TBSA of Lactated Ringer's over 24 hours. Half in the first 8 hours from the time of burn (not ED arrival), half over next 16 hours. Add maintenance fluids in children.
  • Urine output is the best fluid status indicator in burns: 1–2 mL/kg/hr in young children, 0.5–1 mL/kg/hr in older children. Titrate fluids to UOP.
  • Inhalation injury red flags: facial burns, singed nasal hair, carbonaceous sputum, hoarseness, stridor. Early intubation — the airway will swell.
  • Infection is the #1 cause of death beyond the first 48 hours. Silver sulfadiazine is standard topical; early excision and grafting of deep burns reduces infection risk.

Pediatric Burn Fundamentals

Why kids' burns are a different problem than adults'

Pediatric burns are among the leading causes of pediatric injury and death in the US. They account for thousands of hospital admissions and ED visits each year, with children under 5 at highest risk — usually from preventable household exposures.

Causes of Pediatric Burns

Type Typical Scenario Considerations
Scalds Hot bath water, pulled-down pots/pans, hot beverages spilled Most common in <5 years. Suspect abuse if injury pattern doesn't match history (e.g., glove/sock distribution, symmetric bilateral, clear demarcation lines).
Flames / house fires Structural fires, matches, campfires Often large BSA plus inhalation injury. Account for carbon monoxide exposure.
Contact Hot irons, stoves, curling irons, hot food Localized; often hands and face.
Chemical Household cleaners, batteries, acids, alkalis Continues burning until chemical is fully removed. Copious irrigation is key.
Electrical Outlets, cords (oral burns when chewing), lightning Deep tissue damage beyond visible skin injury. Cardiac arrhythmias possible. Oral commissure cord burns are classic.
Radiation Severe sunburn, radiation therapy Usually superficial; occasional partial-thickness.
Inhalation Smoke inhalation in closed fires ARDS risk; low threshold for intubation. Airway can swell rapidly.

Why Kids Burn Differently

Thinner skin — same temperature and duration produce a deeper burn than in adults.

Higher BSA-to-weight ratio — larger surface means more fluid loss and faster hypothermia.

Different body proportions — infants have a proportionally larger head (18%) and smaller legs (14%) than adults.

Less subcutaneous fat in infants → less thermal insulation, greater heat loss.

Growth plate vulnerability — burns over joints can cause scarring and contractures affecting growth.

Developing immune system — higher infection risk, slower healing, need for close monitoring.

Screen Every Pediatric Burn for Abuse

Patterns that raise concern include glove/stocking distribution (forced immersion), symmetric bilateral injuries, sharp line of demarcation, cigarette burns (round punched-out), burns sparing the buttock folds (child held in flexion during immersion), or a history inconsistent with injury pattern or developmental stage. Document objectively and involve child protective services — mandatory reporting.

🎯 Bottom Line: Scalds lead pediatric burns. Kids burn deeper and lose fluid/heat faster. Check every burn for abuse patterns.

Burn Depth Classification

Skin layers and how to read what you're seeing

Accurate burn depth classification drives treatment: superficial burns heal without intervention; deep partial and full-thickness burns often require grafting. Depth is determined by appearance, pain, capillary refill, and sensation.

Skin Layers (Quick Anatomy)

  • Epidermis — outermost layer; barrier function.
  • Dermis — contains sweat glands, hair follicles, nerves, blood vessels; superficial (papillary) and deep (reticular) layers.
  • Subcutaneous tissue (hypodermis) — fat, deeper vessels.
  • Deeper structures — fascia, muscle, bone.

The Degrees at a Glance

Superficial (1st Degree)

Epidermis
Appearance

Red, dry, no blisters, blanches with pressure.

Pain

Painful.

Classic Example

Sunburn.

Healing

3–7 days, NO scarring.

Treatment

Moisturizer, cool compresses, acetaminophen.

Superficial Partial (2nd)

Upper Dermis
Appearance

Red, moist, blistered, blanches with pressure.

Pain

Very painful, sensitive to air/touch.

Cap Refill

Brisk.

Healing

1–3 weeks, minimal scarring.

Treatment

Wound cleansing, topical antibiotic, nonadherent dressing.

Deep Partial (2nd)

Deep Dermis
Appearance

Pale, waxy, may blister, dry or slightly moist.

Pain

Less painful (nerve damage), but pressure still felt.

Cap Refill

Sluggish or absent.

Healing

3–6 weeks, significant scarring. Often needs grafting.

Treatment

Surgical evaluation, possible excision + grafting.

Full-Thickness (3rd)

Through Dermis
Appearance

Dry, leathery, white / brown / black, may see thrombosed vessels.

Pain

PAINLESS in the burned area (nerves destroyed).

Cap Refill

Absent.

Healing

Will NOT heal without grafting. Permanent scarring.

Treatment

Excision + skin grafting. Monitor for circumferential burns (escharotomy).

4th Degree

Muscle / Bone
Appearance

Charred, exposed tissue, muscle, tendon, bone.

Pain

Painless at the burn; severe systemic illness.

Treatment

Extensive debridement, reconstruction, often amputation.

Outcomes

High mortality; prolonged recovery; permanent disability.

Painless Burn = Full-Thickness

In partial-thickness burns, pain is out of proportion to visible injury. In full-thickness burns, the burned area itself is painless because nerve endings are destroyed — but surrounding partial-thickness skin still hurts. A child with a "painless" burn isn't doing well; they have a deep burn that likely needs grafting.

🎯 Bottom Line: 1st = red/dry/painful. 2nd sup = blistered/very painful. 2nd deep = pale/less pain. 3rd = leathery/painless. Painless = deep.

BSA Estimation Methods

Why "percent burned" is different in kids

Accurate BSA estimation drives fluid resuscitation and determines severity. In adults, we use the Rule of Nines. In children, body proportions differ significantly — infants have proportionally larger heads and smaller legs. Always use an age-appropriate method.

Pediatric Rule of Nines

Body Region Adult Infant / Young Child
Head & neck9%18%
Each arm9%9%
Chest (anterior torso)18%18%
Back (posterior torso)18%18%
Each leg18%14%
Perineum1%1%

As children grow, the head BSA decreases and the legs increase, gradually matching adult proportions by about age 10.

Lund-Browder Chart

The Lund-Browder chart is the most accurate BSA estimation tool for children. It breaks body regions into smaller areas with age-adjusted percentages. Used in burn centers; essential for severe burns. Don't try to memorize the table — learn that it exists and how to read one.

Palm Method (Quick Scattered-Burn Estimation)

Palm Method
1 palm (incl. fingers) = 1% BSA
Use the child's OWN palm (including fingers) to estimate scattered or irregular burn areas.
Exclude Superficial Burns from BSA for Fluid Calculation

Only partial-thickness and full-thickness burns count toward %TBSA for the Parkland formula. Superficial (1st degree) burns do NOT count because they don't cause the same fluid loss. A child with 90% sunburn and 10% second-degree burn = 10%TBSA for fluid calculation purposes.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Kids Have Big Heads and Small Legs
Think of a newborn: head takes up almost a fifth of the body. The classic "Rule of Nines" is WRONG for infants because it assigns only 9% to the head — reality is 18% for a newborn head and just 14% each leg. As children grow, these shift toward adult proportions. Under-estimating head burn BSA in an infant can dangerously under-resuscitate.
🎯 Bottom Line: Use pediatric Rule of Nines (head 18%, legs 14%). Lund-Browder chart is most accurate. Palm = 1%. Count only 2nd and 3rd degree.

Severity & Burn Center Transfer

Knowing when to transfer to a burn center

Burn Severity Classification

Severity Criteria Management
Minor <10% BSA partial-thickness, no other complications Outpatient with close follow-up
Moderate 10–20% BSA partial-thickness; small full-thickness (<2%) Hospitalization; consider burn center
Major / Severe >20% BSA; >10% full-thickness; critical locations; inhalation; electrical/chemical Burn center; ICU; multidisciplinary team

Burn Center Transfer Criteria (American Burn Association)

Partial-thickness >10% BSA in children under 10 or >20% in older children.

Any full-thickness burn — regardless of size.

Burns of face, hands, feet, perineum, genitalia, or major joints.

Electrical or chemical burns (any size).

Inhalation injury (singed nose hair, carbonaceous sputum, stridor).

Circumferential burn of trunk or limb — risk of compartment syndrome / escharotomy.

The Lower Threshold in Kids

In pediatric patients, the transfer threshold is lower than adults. Even 5–10% partial-thickness burns in young children often warrant a burn center transfer because of the higher fluid losses, infection risk, and specialized wound/rehab needs. When in doubt, call the nearest burn center for consultation.

🎯 Bottom Line: >10% BSA, any full-thickness, critical locations, inhalation, electrical/chemical = burn center. Kids transfer earlier.

Emergency Management (First Hours)

Stop the burning, secure the ABCs, prepare for resuscitation

Initial Actions

  1. Stop the burning process. Remove from heat source. For thermal burns, cool the burn with running tap water for 15–20 min (NOT ice — causes vasoconstriction and worse tissue damage). For chemical burns, irrigate copiously with water for at least 20 minutes (except dry chemicals — brush off first). For electrical burns, ensure source is disconnected before touching the victim.
  2. Remove clothing and jewelry from the burned area BEFORE swelling develops; cut around adherent clothing rather than pulling.
  3. Airway first (ABCDE). Assess for inhalation injury signs; intubate early if any doubt — airway edema can develop rapidly and close the airway.
  4. Breathing: 100% oxygen (especially if carbon monoxide concern), SpO2 monitoring, chest auscultation for wheezing/crackles.
  5. Circulation: establish 2 large-bore IVs (intraosseous if needed), begin fluid resuscitation. Check pulses distal to any circumferential burns.
  6. Disability: assess neuro status, GCS. Consider hypoxia, CO poisoning.
  7. Exposure: fully expose to assess extent, then cover with warm clean dry sheets to prevent hypothermia (major risk in kids).
  8. Tetanus prophylaxis, pain management, NG tube if intubated or ileus, Foley for accurate UOP, labs (CBC, BMP, lactate, ABG if inhalation).

Recognizing Inhalation Injury

Facial burns or burns around mouth/nose

Singed nose hair or eyebrows

Carbonaceous sputum (black/sooty)

Hoarseness, stridor, drooling

Closed space fire exposure

Altered mental status (CO / hypoxia)

Intubate Before the Airway Closes

Airway swelling from inhalation injury can progress rapidly. A child who sounds mildly hoarse now may be unable to be intubated in 30 minutes. When in doubt, intubate early — ideally in a controlled setting rather than as an emergency later. Call anesthesia and/or ENT for difficult airway backup.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Warm Water, Not Ice — and Cover Up
A common mistake is applying ice or very cold water to a burn — this actually worsens injury by causing vasoconstriction and additional tissue loss. Use cool running tap water (not cold) for about 15–20 min. And once assessment is done, cover the child with warm dry sheets and blankets. Kids lose heat fast through burn wounds; hypothermia can kill them faster than the burn itself.
🎯 Bottom Line: Stop burning (cool water, NOT ice). ABCDE. Intubate early for inhalation. 2 IVs. Keep warm. Foley for UOP.

Parkland Formula & Fluid Resuscitation

The math that saves the patient

Burns cause massive fluid shifts due to vascular permeability. Without aggressive resuscitation in the first 24 hours, the child develops hypovolemic shock. The Parkland formula provides a starting estimate, titrated to urine output.

Parkland Formula
4 mL × kg × %TBSA
Total Lactated Ringer's over 24 hours. Half in the first 8 hours from the time of burn. Half over the next 16 hours.

Pediatric Modifications

  • Add maintenance fluids in children — they have higher baseline fluid requirements relative to weight. Use the Holliday-Segar formula:
    • 100 mL/kg for first 10 kg
    • 50 mL/kg for next 10 kg (11–20 kg)
    • 20 mL/kg for each kg above 20
  • For children <30 kg: maintenance may include D5 LR or add dextrose to prevent hypoglycemia.
  • Use Lactated Ringer's (LR) preferentially over Normal Saline for large-volume resuscitation — NS can cause hyperchloremic acidosis.

Example Calculation

A 20 kg child with a 30% TBSA partial-thickness burn:

Parkland: 4 × 20 × 30 = 2,400 mL LR over 24 hours

First 8 hours (from time of burn): 1,200 mL = 150 mL/hr

Next 16 hours: 1,200 mL = 75 mL/hr

Plus maintenance: 100 × 10 + 50 × 10 = 1,500 mL/day = 62.5 mL/hr added

Titrating to Urine Output

The Parkland formula is a starting point, not a fixed prescription. Urine output is the gold-standard endpoint for fluid resuscitation.

Age Group Target Urine Output
Infants / young children (<30 kg)1–2 mL/kg/hr
Older children (≥30 kg) / adults0.5–1 mL/kg/hr
Electrical burns (any age)1–2 mL/kg/hr (myoglobin flush)
  • If UOP is below target, increase fluid rate.
  • If UOP is above target (over-resuscitation), decrease rate — excess fluid worsens edema and pulmonary complications.
  • Continuously monitor: vitals, mental status, lactate, hemoglobin, electrolytes.
"First 8 Hours" = From the Time of Burn

The clock starts when the burn occurred, NOT when the child arrived at the ED. If a 20-kg child with a 30% burn arrives 3 hours post-burn, they already "owe" the fluid for those 3 hours (approximately 450 mL). Catch them up — they need 1,200 mL minus what's already been given, administered over the remaining 5 hours of the first window.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Urine Output > Formulas
The Parkland formula is a starting estimate. What actually matters: the child is making 1–2 mL/kg/hr of urine. Under that, you're under-resuscitating — increase fluid. Far over, you're over-resuscitating — decrease fluid, protect the lungs from pulmonary edema. Check UOP hourly in the first 24 hours. Titrate, titrate, titrate.
🎯 Bottom Line: Parkland = 4 × kg × %TBSA. LR. Half in first 8 hr (from burn time). Add maintenance. Titrate to UOP 1–2 mL/kg/hr.

Ongoing Care: Wound, Pain, Nutrition, Infection

The priorities after the first 24 hours

Wound Care

  • Cleanse gently with sterile saline or mild antiseptic; debride loose tissue and ruptured blisters.
  • Topical antimicrobials — see med cards below.
  • Non-adherent dressings with outer wrap; change 1–2 times daily.
  • Hydrotherapy daily in burn units for cleaning and range of motion.
  • For circumferential burns (especially torso, limbs): monitor for compartment syndrome; escharotomy may be needed to relieve pressure.
  • Surgical excision and grafting for deep partial and full-thickness burns — early excision reduces infection and improves outcome.

Wound-Care Medications

Silver Sulfadiazine (Silvadene)
Topical Antimicrobial

Broad-spectrum coverage; apply 1–2 mm thick 1–2×/day.

Avoid in: infants <2 months, G6PD deficiency, sulfa allergy, face (discoloration concern).

Can cause transient leukopenia.

Bacitracin / Mupirocin
Topical Antibiotic

For face and smaller burns; less cosmetic concern than silver sulfadiazine.

Use in infants where silver sulfadiazine contraindicated.

Silver-Impregnated Dressings
Modern Dressings

Acticoat, Aquacel Ag — slow-release silver for antimicrobial coverage.

Less frequent dressing changes; less pain.

Mafenide (Sulfamylon)
Deep / Eschar Penetration

Penetrates eschar — for deep burns, cartilage (ears).

Painful on application; can cause metabolic acidosis (carbonic anhydrase inhibition).

Pain Management

  • IV opioids (morphine, fentanyl, hydromorphone) in acute phase; scheduled + PRN.
  • Procedural sedation (ketamine) for dressing changes.
  • Transition to oral opioids, then non-opioid analgesics (acetaminophen, ibuprofen if safe) as pain improves.
  • Non-pharmacologic: distraction, music, child life services, virtual reality (innovative and effective).
  • Treat anxiety separately — benzodiazepines (lorazepam) for procedural anxiety.

Nutrition

  • Burns cause a hypermetabolic state — caloric needs may be 1.5–2× baseline for weeks.
  • Early enteral feeding (within 24–48 hours if possible) preserves gut function, reduces bacterial translocation, and supports immune function.
  • High-protein diet for wound healing; supplementation common.
  • Monitor nitrogen balance, prealbumin, daily weights.
  • TPN if enteral not tolerated.

Infection Surveillance

Infection Is the Leading Cause of Mortality After 48 Hours

Burn wounds lose their skin barrier and are hypermetabolic — a perfect storm for infection. Watch for: fever >38.5°C, tachycardia disproportionate to vitals, wound changes (green/purple discoloration, increased drainage, foul odor), hypothermia in severe sepsis, changes in mental status, rising lactate. Surveillance cultures are routine in burn centers. Common organisms: Staph (including MRSA), Pseudomonas, Klebsiella.

🎯 Bottom Line: Clean, apply topical antimicrobial, dress. Early excision/grafting for deep. IV opioids + distraction. Feed early, high protein. Watch for infection.

Rehabilitation & Long-Term Care

Burns heal — but recovery is years, not weeks

A child with a major burn enters a recovery that spans surgeries, rehabilitation, scar management, and psychological support over years. The goal is not just survival but restored function, appearance, and emotional health.

Physical & Occupational Therapy

  • Begins early — often before the child is stable enough for active PT; passive ROM.
  • Prevent contractures (a joint stuck in the wrong position from scarring) — particularly neck, axilla, elbow, hand, hip, knee.
  • Splinting the affected areas in functional position.
  • Graduated ADL training — eating, dressing, self-care.

Scar Management

  • Pressure garments worn 23 hours/day for 12–24 months to prevent hypertrophic scarring.
  • Massage, silicone sheets, moisturizers.
  • Protect scars from sun (hyperpigmentation risk) — SPF 30+, hats, long sleeves for a year or more.
  • Multiple reconstructive surgeries may be needed over childhood, especially as the child grows.

Psychological Care

PTSD, anxiety, depression are common; start support early.

Body image concerns intensify at school age and adolescence.

Peer interactions — teasing, staring, exclusion can affect development.

Burn camps (for kids) provide community and normalize the experience.

Child life specialists, social workers, psychologists are essential team members.

Family support — parents also experience trauma, guilt, financial stress.

The Child Is More Than the Burn

Burn children can and do live full lives — go to school, play sports, build friendships, grow into resilient adults. The medical journey is long but outcomes are often excellent with good team-based care. Your role as the nurse: medical excellence on the floor PLUS emotional presence and advocacy. Often you spend more time with the family than any other clinician, and your reassurance carries tremendous weight.

🎯 Bottom Line: Rehab = years. Prevent contractures with PT/OT. Pressure garments for scars. Sun protection. Emotional support and burn camps. Family needs care too.

Family Education & Prevention

Prevention + home care + when to return

Home Fire & Burn Prevention

Water heater <120°F (49°C) — prevents scalding.

Test bath water with a thermometer or back of the wrist before child gets in.

Turn pot handles inward on the stove; use back burners when possible.

Working smoke detectors on every floor; test monthly, change batteries yearly.

Family escape plan — two ways out of every room; designated meeting place.

Outlet covers; secure cords; keep lighters/matches locked away.

Sunscreen SPF 30+, hats, and sun-protective clothing.

Chemical safety — lock up cleaners; avoid transferring to unlabeled bottles.

Home Wound Care (Minor Burns)

  1. Keep the wound clean — daily gentle wash with mild soap and water.
  2. Apply the prescribed topical antibiotic or cream.
  3. Cover with non-adherent dressing; change per instructions.
  4. Keep the child hydrated; small frequent meals with protein.
  5. Give scheduled pain medication; don't wait for severe pain.
  6. Avoid sun exposure on healing skin for at least 1 year.

When to Return to the ED

Fever >38.5°C or worsening appearance of the wound.

Increased pain, redness, warmth, foul odor, or drainage.

Decreased urine output or signs of dehydration.

Trouble breathing, lethargy, confusion.

Cold, pale, or blue extremity beyond a circumferential burn.

Separation or loss of graft; new bleeding.

Psychological Support

  • Validate the family's emotions — fear, guilt, grief.
  • Connect with hospital social work, child life, counseling resources.
  • Refer to support organizations (Phoenix Society for Burn Survivors, local burn camps).
  • Include siblings in age-appropriate conversations and planning.
  • School re-entry planning — many hospitals offer re-entry programs.
🎯 Bottom Line: Prevent with water temp, smoke detectors, outlet covers, sunscreen. Teach home wound care and red flags. Connect with burn camps and support.
