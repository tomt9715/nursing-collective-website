Pediatric Burns

Four decisions carry every burn scenario: how deep, how much, airway or wound, and the fluid math. Children are not small adults, and that is where the points are won.

  • Say why a child's head and legs change how you size a burn
  • Read burn depth from appearance and pain, and know why painless is worse

  • Run the Parkland math, split it, and start the clock at the burn

01

What actually gets you

Pediatric burns are not adult burns scaled down. Children differ in shape, skin, and reserve. Miss that and you misjudge the whole burn.

Points get lost here in three predictable ways. Read these before anything else.

You sized the burn like an adult

A child's head is proportionally larger. The legs are smaller. The adult Rule of Nines gives the head 9 percent. In a young child it is closer to 18 percent. Use the adult chart and you under-count the burn. Lund-Browder is the pediatric tool. Under-counting means under-resuscitating.

You treated the wound before the airway

The wound looks dramatic. The airway kills faster. Singed nares, soot, a hoarse cry, facial burns: these come first. Airway swelling moves fast in a child. One who sounds mildly hoarse now can be impossible to intubate soon. Secure the airway, then dress the burn.

You botched the fluid math or the clock

Parkland is 4 mL times kilograms times percent TBSA. That volume is Lactated Ringers over 24 hours. Half goes in the first 8 hours. The clock starts at the burn, not at the ED. Miss the start time and you fall behind. Then you titrate to urine output, not to the formula.

Bottom Line: Stop studying burns as one topic. Study four decisions: how deep, how much, airway or wound, and the fluid math.
02

The four things

For any pediatric burn, four decisions carry you. How deep is it. How much is burned. What comes first. How much fluid, and when.

Say each one with the book closed. The table below is the spine.

Decision The goal The clue Priority action Teaching point
Depth Read how deep the burn goes. Painless and leathery means full-thickness. Blistered and painful means partial. Superficial heals alone. Deep partial and full-thickness go for grafting. Painless is not good news. The nerves are gone.
Extent Estimate percent burned. A young child's head is 18 percent, each leg 14 percent. Use pediatric Rule of Nines or Lund-Browder. Count only partial and full-thickness. The adult chart under-counts a child's head burn.
Emergency Decide what comes first. Singed nares, soot, facial burns, hoarse cry, stridor. Airway before the wound. Intubate early if in doubt. The airway swells fast. Waiting loses the window.
Fluids Replace the loss on time. Percent TBSA and the child's weight. Parkland with Lactated Ringers. Half in the first 8 hours. Titrate to urine. The 8-hour clock starts at the burn, not the ED.
Copy this down

Write these four rows on one card, by hand. Depth. Extent. Airway. Fluids. That card is most of any burn scenario.

03

Why kids burn differently

Burns are a leading cause of injury and death in children. Children under 5 are hit hardest. Most exposures are preventable and happen at home.

The causes, and what each one hides

Type Typical scenario What to watch for
Scalds Hot bath water, pulled-down pots, spilled hot drinks Most common under 5. Suspect abuse if the pattern does not match the history (glove or sock distribution, symmetric, sharp lines).
Flames / house fires Structural fires, matches, campfires Often large TBSA plus inhalation injury. Account for carbon monoxide.
Contact Hot irons, stoves, curling irons, hot food Localized. Often hands and face.
Chemical Household cleaners, batteries, acids, alkalis Keeps burning until fully removed. Copious irrigation is the key.
Electrical Outlets, chewed cords, lightning Deep tissue damage beyond the visible wound. Cardiac arrhythmias possible. Oral commissure cord burns are classic.
Radiation Severe sunburn, radiation therapy Usually superficial. Occasionally partial-thickness.
Inhalation Smoke in a closed-space fire ARDS risk. Low threshold for intubation. The airway can swell rapidly.

Why the same exposure does more damage

Thinner skin. The same heat and time make a deeper burn than in an adult.

Higher surface-to-weight ratio. More area means more fluid loss and faster hypothermia.

Different proportions. Infants have a larger head, near 18 percent, and smaller legs at 14 percent.

Less fat. Infants have little insulation. Heat leaves fast.

Growth plates. Burns over joints scar and contract, and that can affect growth.

Young immune system. Higher infection risk. Slower healing. Close monitoring.

Screen every pediatric burn for abuse

Some patterns raise concern. A glove or stocking distribution suggests forced immersion. So does a symmetric injury with a sharp line of demarcation. Round punched-out marks suggest cigarette burns. Spared buttock folds mean the child was held in flexion. A history that does not fit the injury or the child's development is a flag. Document objectively. Involve child protective services. Reporting is mandatory.

Caustic ingestion at home

A toddler swallows drain cleaner. Do not induce vomiting. The chemical burns again on the way up. Do not give milk to neutralize it. That can trigger an exothermic reaction. Call poison control and 911. Rinse the mouth if there is no aspiration risk. Go to the ED. For skin contact, irrigate with water for 20 minutes or more.

Bottom Line: Scalds lead. Kids burn deeper and lose fluid and heat faster. Check every burn for abuse patterns.
04

How deep is it

Depth drives treatment. Superficial burns heal on their own. Deep partial and full-thickness burns often need grafting. You read depth from appearance, pain, capillary refill, and sensation.

The skin, quickly

  • Epidermis is the outer barrier layer.
  • Dermis holds sweat glands, hair follicles, nerves, and vessels.
  • Subcutaneous tissue is fat and deeper vessels.
  • Deeper still lie fascia, muscle, and bone.

The degrees at a glance

Superficial (1st degree)

Epidermis only. Red, dry, no blisters, blanches with pressure.

Painful. Think sunburn.

Heals in 3 to 7 days with no scarring. Moisturizer, cool compresses, acetaminophen.

Superficial partial (2nd)

Upper dermis. Red, moist, blistered, blanches. Capillary refill is brisk.

Very painful and sensitive to air and touch.

Heals in 1 to 3 weeks with minimal scarring. Cleanse, topical antibiotic, nonadherent dressing.

Deep partial (2nd)

Deep dermis. Pale, waxy, dry or slightly moist. Capillary refill is sluggish or absent.

Less painful because nerves are damaged. Pressure is still felt.

Heals in 3 to 6 weeks with significant scarring. Often needs excision and grafting.

Full-thickness (3rd)

Through the dermis. Dry, leathery, white, brown, or black. Thrombosed vessels may show. Capillary refill is absent.

Painless in the burned area. The nerves are destroyed.

Will not heal without grafting. Watch circumferential burns for escharotomy.

4th degree

Into muscle and bone. Charred, with exposed tissue, tendon, or bone.

Painless at the burn. The child is systemically ill.

Extensive debridement and reconstruction. Often amputation. High mortality and long recovery.

A painless burn is a deep burn

In partial-thickness burns, the pain is out of proportion to what you see. In full-thickness burns, the burned area itself does not hurt. The nerve endings are gone. The pain a child reports comes from shallower burns nearby. A painless burn is not a child doing well. It is a deep burn that likely needs grafting.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Painless Equals Deep
A burn that does not hurt has lost its nerve endings. That points to full-thickness, and it will need grafting. The pain you do hear about comes from shallower burns beside it. Do not let a calm-looking wound reassure you.
Bottom Line: First is red, dry, painful. Superficial partial is blistered and very painful. Deep partial is pale with less pain. Full-thickness is leathery and painless. Painless means deep.
05

How much is burned

The percent burned drives fluids and severity. Adults use the Rule of Nines. Children have different proportions, with larger heads and smaller legs. Always use an age-appropriate method.

Pediatric Rule of Nines

Body region Adult Infant / young child
Head and neck9%18%
Each arm9%9%
Chest (anterior torso)18%18%
Back (posterior torso)18%18%
Each leg18%14%
Perineum1%1%

As a child grows, the head share falls and the legs rise. By about age 10, proportions match the adult chart.

Lund-Browder and the palm

The Lund-Browder chart is the most accurate tool for children. It splits the body into smaller regions with age-adjusted percentages. Burn centers rely on it for severe burns. Do not memorize the table. Learn that it exists and how to read one.

The palm method

Use the child's own palm, fingers included, as roughly 1 percent of body surface. It is handy for scattered or irregular burns. It is an estimate, not the primary tool for a large burn.

Count only the deep burns for fluids

Only partial-thickness and full-thickness burns count toward percent TBSA for Parkland. Superficial burns do not. They do not cause the same fluid loss. A child with 90 percent sunburn and 10 percent second-degree burn is 10 percent TBSA for the fluid math.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Big Heads, Small Legs
Picture a newborn. The head takes up almost a fifth of the body. The adult Rule of Nines is wrong for infants because it gives the head only 9 percent. Reality is closer to 18 percent for the head and 14 percent for each leg. Under-estimate an infant head burn and you under-resuscitate.
Bottom Line: Use the pediatric Rule of Nines, head 18 percent and legs 14 percent. Lund-Browder is most accurate. Palm is 1 percent. Count only second and third degree.
06

How severe, and where it goes

Severity decides the setting. A minor burn goes home. A major burn goes to a burn center. The threshold for a child is lower than for an adult.

Severity classification

Severity Criteria Management
Minor Under 10% BSA partial-thickness, no other complications Outpatient with close follow-up
Moderate 10 to 20% BSA partial-thickness; small full-thickness under 2% Hospitalize. Consider a burn center.
Major / severe Over 20% BSA; over 10% full-thickness; critical sites; inhalation; electrical or chemical Burn center, ICU, multidisciplinary team

Burn center transfer criteria

Partial-thickness over 10% BSA in children under 10, or over 20% in older children.

Any full-thickness burn, whatever its size.

Burns of the face, hands, feet, perineum, genitalia, or major joints.

Electrical or chemical burns of any size.

Inhalation injury. Singed nose hair, carbonaceous sputum, stridor.

Circumferential burns of a limb or the trunk. Watch for escharotomy.

The threshold is lower in kids

Children transfer earlier than adults. Even a 5 to 10 percent partial-thickness burn in a young child often warrants a burn center. Fluid losses are higher. Infection risk is higher. Wound and rehab needs are specialized. When in doubt, call the nearest burn center.

Bottom Line: Over 10 percent BSA, any full-thickness, critical sites, inhalation, or electrical and chemical means burn center. Kids transfer earlier.
07

The first hours

The first minutes are about stopping the burn and securing the ABCs. Order matters more than completeness. Airway is the true first priority.

  1. 1
    Stop the burning. Remove from the heat. For thermal burns, cool with running tap water for 15 to 20 minutes. Not ice; ice causes vasoconstriction and worse damage. For chemical burns, irrigate with water for at least 20 minutes, but brush off dry chemicals first. For electrical burns, confirm the source is disconnected before touching the child.
  2. 2
    Remove clothing and jewelry from the area before swelling starts. Cut around anything stuck rather than pulling it.
  3. 3
    Airway. Assess for inhalation injury. Intubate early if there is any doubt. Airway edema can develop fast and close it.
  4. 4
    Breathing. Give 100 percent oxygen, especially with a carbon monoxide concern. Monitor saturation. Auscultate for wheeze and crackles.
  5. 5
    Circulation. Place two large-bore IVs, or intraosseous if needed. Begin fluids. Check pulses distal to any circumferential burn.
  6. 6
    Disability. Assess neuro status and GCS. Consider hypoxia and carbon monoxide poisoning.
  7. 7
    Exposure. Fully expose to assess extent, then cover with warm clean dry sheets. Hypothermia is a major risk in children.
  8. 8
    Then the rest. Tetanus prophylaxis, pain control, an NG tube if intubated or with ileus, a Foley for accurate urine output, and labs.

Inhalation injury red flags

Facial burns or burns around the mouth and nose.

Singed nose hair or eyebrows.

Carbonaceous sputum, black or sooty.

Hoarseness, stridor, drooling.

A closed-space fire exposure.

Altered mental status from CO or hypoxia.

Intubate before the airway closes

Swelling from inhalation injury moves fast. A child who is mildly hoarse now may be unintubatable in 30 minutes. When in doubt, intubate early, in a controlled setting rather than as a crisis. Call anesthesia or ENT for difficult-airway backup.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Cool Water, Not Ice, Then Cover Up
A common mistake is ice or very cold water on a burn. That worsens the injury through vasoconstriction and added tissue loss. Use cool running tap water for about 15 to 20 minutes. Once you have assessed, cover the child with warm dry sheets. Kids lose heat fast through a burn wound, and hypothermia can kill faster than the burn.
Bottom Line: Stop the burn with cool water, not ice. Run ABCDE. Intubate early for inhalation. Two IVs. Keep the child warm. Foley for urine output.
08

The fluid math

Burns cause massive fluid shifts as vessels leak. Without aggressive fluids in the first 24 hours, the child slides into hypovolemic shock. Parkland gives a starting estimate. Urine output steers it from there.

The Parkland formula

4 mL times kilograms times percent TBSA. That total is Lactated Ringers over 24 hours. Half goes in the first 8 hours, timed from the burn. Half goes over the next 16 hours.

Pediatric modifications

  • Add maintenance fluids. Children need more baseline fluid per kilogram. Use Holliday-Segar: 100 mL/kg for the first 10 kg, 50 mL/kg for the next 10 kg, then 20 mL/kg for each kilogram above 20.
  • For a child under 30 kg, maintenance may use D5 LR to prevent hypoglycemia.
  • Use Lactated Ringers, not Normal Saline, for large-volume resuscitation. Large-volume saline causes hyperchloremic acidosis.

A worked example

Take a 20 kg child with a 30 percent partial-thickness burn.

StepThe math
Parkland total4 times 20 times 30 = 2,400 mL LR over 24 hours
First 8 hours1,200 mL, which is 150 mL/hr (from the burn time)
Next 16 hours1,200 mL, which is 75 mL/hr
Plus maintenance100 times 10 plus 50 times 10 = 1,500 mL/day, about 62.5 mL/hr added

Titrate to urine output

Parkland is a starting point, not a fixed order. Urine output is the gold-standard endpoint.

Group Target urine output
Young children, under 30 kg1 to 2 mL/kg/hr
Older children, 30 kg or more, and adults0.5 to 1 mL/kg/hr
Electrical burns, any age1 to 2 mL/kg/hr, to flush myoglobin
  • Below target, increase the rate.
  • Above target, decrease the rate. Excess fluid worsens edema and the lungs.
  • Keep watching vitals, mental status, lactate, hemoglobin, and electrolytes.
The 8-hour clock starts at the burn

The clock starts when the burn happened, not at ED arrival. Take the 20 kg child with a 30 percent burn who arrives 3 hours late. They already owe the fluid for those hours, roughly 450 mL. Catch them up. Give 1,200 mL minus what has run, over the 5 hours left in the first window.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Urine Output Beats the Formula
Parkland is an estimate. What matters is that the child makes 1 to 2 mL/kg/hr of urine. Under that, you are behind, so increase the fluid. Well over that, you are drowning the lungs, so ease off. Check urine output hourly for the first 24 hours. Titrate.
Bottom Line: Parkland is 4 times kg times percent TBSA of LR. Half in the first 8 hours from the burn. Add maintenance. Titrate to urine of 1 to 2 mL/kg/hr.
09

After the first day

Once fluids are running, the work turns to the wound, the pain, the calories, and infection. Infection is the one that kills after the first 48 hours.

Wound care

  • Cleanse gently with sterile saline. Debride loose tissue and ruptured blisters.
  • Apply a topical antimicrobial. See the cards below.
  • Use non-adherent dressings with an outer wrap. Change 1 to 2 times daily.
  • Hydrotherapy in burn units cleans the wound and moves the joints.
  • For circumferential burns, watch for compartment syndrome. Escharotomy relieves the pressure.
  • Early excision and grafting of deep burns reduces infection and improves outcome.
Circumferential burn losing its pulse

Edema builds under rigid eschar and chokes the circulation. The signs are increasing pain, diminished pulses, pallor, and a cool extremity. This is an emergency. Call the surgeon. An escharotomy, a linear incision through the eschar, restores flow.

Wound-care medications

Topical antimicrobial

Silver sulfadiazine (Silvadene). Broad-spectrum. Apply 1 to 2 mm thick, once or twice daily.

Avoid in infants under 2 months, G6PD deficiency, sulfa allergy, and the face. Can cause transient leukopenia.

Topical antibiotic

Bacitracin or mupirocin. For the face and smaller burns. Less cosmetic concern.

Use when silver sulfadiazine is contraindicated, as in young infants.

Modern dressings

Silver-impregnated dressings. Acticoat and Aquacel Ag release silver slowly.

Fewer dressing changes and less pain.

Deep penetration

Mafenide (Sulfamylon). Penetrates eschar. Good for deep burns and cartilage such as ears.

Painful on application. Can cause metabolic acidosis.

Pain

  • IV opioids in the acute phase: morphine, fentanyl, hydromorphone. Scheduled plus as needed.
  • Procedural sedation, often ketamine, for dressing changes.
  • Step down to oral opioids, then non-opioids like acetaminophen as pain eases.
  • Non-drug tools help: distraction, music, child life, virtual reality.
  • Treat anxiety separately. A benzodiazepine can cover procedural anxiety.

Nutrition

  • Burns are hypermetabolic. Caloric needs can be 1.5 to 2 times baseline for weeks.
  • Start enteral feeding early, within 24 to 48 hours. It protects the gut and immune function.
  • Feed high protein for wound healing. Supplements are common.
  • Track nitrogen balance, prealbumin, and daily weights. Use TPN only if enteral fails.
Infection is the leading killer after 48 hours

The skin barrier is gone and the child is hypermetabolic. Watch for fever over 38.5 C and a heart rate out of proportion. Watch the wound for green or purple color, more drainage, or a foul smell. Watch for a falling temperature in severe sepsis, mental status change, and rising lactate. Common bugs are Staph including MRSA, Pseudomonas, and Klebsiella.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Shock First, Then Infection
In the first 48 hours, hypovolemic shock is the danger. After that, infection takes over. The switch is why burn units cover the wound early and cut out dead tissue. Read the wound and the vitals together. Color change, more drainage, and a foul smell all point one way.
Bottom Line: Clean, apply antimicrobial, dress. Excise and graft deep burns early. IV opioids plus distraction. Feed early and high protein. Hunt for infection.
10

The long road back

A major burn starts a recovery measured in years. There are surgeries, therapy, scar work, and psychological support. The goal is not just survival. It is function, appearance, and emotional health.

Physical and occupational therapy

  • Start early, often with passive range of motion before the child is stable.
  • Prevent contractures, where a joint sticks in the wrong position from scarring. Watch the neck, axilla, elbow, hand, hip, and knee.
  • Splint affected areas in a functional position.
  • Rebuild daily skills: eating, dressing, self-care.

Scar management

  • Pressure garments worn 23 hours a day for 12 to 24 months prevent hypertrophic scars.
  • Add massage, silicone sheets, and moisturizers.
  • Protect scars from the sun for a year or more. Use SPF 30 or higher, hats, and long sleeves.
  • Expect multiple reconstructive surgeries as the child grows.

Psychological care

PTSD, anxiety, and depression are common. Start support early.

Body image worries intensify at school age and in adolescence.

Peers may tease, stare, or exclude. That shapes development.

Burn camps build community and normalize the experience.

Child life, social work, and psychology are core team members.

Parents carry trauma, guilt, and financial stress too.

The child is more than the burn

Burned children go on to full lives. They go to school, play sports, and grow into resilient adults. The medical road is long, but outcomes are often excellent with good team care. Your job is medical excellence plus presence and advocacy. You often spend more time with the family than anyone else, and your reassurance carries weight.

Bottom Line: Rehab takes years. Prevent contractures with therapy. Pressure garments and sun protection for scars. Support the mind. The family needs care too.
11

Sending them home safely

Most pediatric burns are preventable. Scalds are the number one cause in children under 5. Prevention and clear home teaching are your highest-value work here.

Home prevention

Water heater under 120 F, which is 49 C. This prevents scalds.

Test bath water with a thermometer or the wrist before the child goes in.

Turn pot handles inward. Use back burners when you can.

Smoke detectors on every floor. Test monthly, change batteries yearly.

An escape plan. Two ways out of every room and a meeting place.

Outlet covers, secured cords, and lighters and matches locked away.

Sun protection. SPF 30 or higher, hats, and covering clothing.

Chemical safety. Lock up cleaners. Never move them to unlabeled bottles.

Home wound care for minor burns

  1. 1
    Wash the wound daily with mild soap and water.
  2. 2
    Apply the prescribed topical antibiotic or cream.
  3. 3
    Cover with a non-adherent dressing. Change it as instructed.
  4. 4
    Keep the child hydrated. Offer small protein-rich meals.
  5. 5
    Give pain medication on schedule. Do not wait for severe pain.
  6. 6
    Keep healing skin out of the sun for at least a year.

When to come back

Fever over 38.5 C or a worsening wound.

More pain, redness, warmth, odor, or drainage.

Less urine or signs of dehydration.

Trouble breathing, lethargy, or confusion.

A cold, pale, or blue limb past a circumferential burn.

Loss of a graft or new bleeding.

Supporting the family

  • Validate the fear, guilt, and grief. These are normal.
  • Connect them with social work, child life, and counseling.
  • Point to support groups such as the Phoenix Society and local burn camps.
  • Include siblings in age-appropriate conversations.
  • Plan the return to school. Many hospitals run re-entry programs.
Bottom Line: Prevent with water temperature, smoke detectors, outlet covers, and sun care. Teach home wound care and the red flags. Connect the family to support.
