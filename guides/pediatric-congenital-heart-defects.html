Learning Objectives

Differentiate acyanotic (increased pulmonary flow) from cyanotic (decreased pulmonary flow) defects.
Identify the 4 anatomical defects of Tetralogy of Fallot and their hemodynamic effects.
Recognize and manage a hypercyanotic "tet" spell at the bedside.
Identify early signs of right-sided heart failure in the pediatric patient.
Administer digoxin, diuretics, and prostaglandin E1 safely in infants.
Educate families on feeding, activity, and surgical preparation for a child with CHD.

High-Yield Summary

The 10 things you cannot miss from Chapter 41.

  • 8 per 1,000 live births — CHD is the most common congenital anomaly. Many are identified on fetal ultrasound or at the newborn pulse oximetry screen.
  • Two big categories: Acyanotic = increased pulmonary blood flow (VSD, ASD, PDA, AVSD) — left-to-right shunt. Cyanotic = decreased pulmonary blood flow (TOF, tricuspid atresia, pulmonary atresia) — right-to-left shunt.
  • Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF): 4 defects — Pulmonary stenosis, Right ventricular hypertrophy, Overriding aorta, VSD. Think "PROV" or "4 Hs": hypertrophy, hole, hole misplaced, hardened pulmonary valve.
  • Hypercyanotic "Tet" Spell = EMERGENCY: Sudden cyanosis, irritability, tachypnea. First action — knee-to-chest position. Then 100% O2, calm infant, morphine IV/IM, IV fluids, phenylephrine if refractory.
  • Right-sided heart failure in kids presents differently than adults: poor feeding, diaphoresis with feeds, failure to thrive, tachypnea at rest, hepatomegaly. Weight gain = fluid retention, not growth.
  • Digoxin (Lanoxin): Check apical HR for 1 full minute before dose. Hold if infant HR <90-110 bpm (child <70, adolescent <60). Toxic signs: vomiting, bradycardia, visual changes, arrhythmia.
  • Prostaglandin E1 (alprostadil) keeps the ductus arteriosus OPEN in ductal-dependent lesions (e.g., transposition, severe TOF) until surgical repair. Watch for apnea — keep intubation supplies ready.
  • Feeding pearl: Infants with CHD tire during feeds — use soft/preemie nipple, small frequent feeds, limit to 20-30 min, add calorie-dense formula (24-27 kcal/oz) to promote weight gain without extra fluid.
  • Pulse oximetry screening on every newborn between 24-48 hours of life. Failed screen = oxygen saturation below 90%, or a 3%+ difference between right-hand and foot readings. Prompts echocardiogram.
  • SBE (subacute bacterial endocarditis) prophylaxis with dental procedures is required for unrepaired cyanotic CHD, prosthetic valves, and the first 6 months after repair.

Fetal Circulation & Transition

Why CHD becomes obvious after birth

In fetal life, the placenta does the work of the lungs. Three shunts route blood AROUND the non-functioning lungs. At birth, these shunts must close for normal circulation — when they stay open or when structural defects exist, problems emerge.

The 3 Fetal Shunts

Shunt Location Purpose in Utero Closes Around
Ductus venosus Umbilical vein → IVC Bypasses the liver 1-2 weeks after birth
Foramen ovale Right atrium → Left atrium Bypasses the lungs Functionally within hours; anatomically by ~1 year
Ductus arteriosus Pulmonary artery → Aorta Bypasses the lungs Functionally 10-15 hours; anatomically 2-3 weeks
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
"Honeymoon Period" in the First 24-48 Hours
Many infants with CHD look completely normal at birth. Their PDA is still open, pulmonary pressures are still high, and blood is mixing "just enough." Symptoms appear over the first few days as the ductus closes and pulmonary pressure drops — this is why every newborn gets pulse oximetry screening at 24-48 hours of life.
When the Ductus Should Stay OPEN

In ductal-dependent lesions (severe TOF, transposition of the great arteries, hypoplastic left heart, pulmonary atresia), the open ductus is the only way blood can reach the lungs or body. These infants crash when the ductus closes. Treatment: IV prostaglandin E1 to keep it open until surgery.

🎯 Bottom Line: The transition from fetal to neonatal circulation unmasks most CHDs. Pulse ox screening between 24-48 hours catches many of them before discharge.

Classification of Congenital Heart Defects

Acyanotic vs. cyanotic — and why it matters

The classic teaching splits CHDs by whether they cause cyanosis. A more useful clinical framework groups them by hemodynamics — what direction blood is shunting and how pulmonary blood flow is affected. The 4-category system below is what most peds textbooks now use.

Increased Pulmonary Flow

Acyanotic

Left-to-right shunt — oxygenated blood leaks from the high-pressure left side to the low-pressure right side, flooding the lungs.

  • VSD (ventricular septal defect)
  • ASD (atrial septal defect)
  • PDA (patent ductus arteriosus)
  • AVSD (atrioventricular septal defect)

Key s/s: CHF signs, FTT, recurrent respiratory infections, murmur.

Decreased Pulmonary Flow

Cyanotic

Right-to-left shunt — deoxygenated blood bypasses the lungs and enters systemic circulation. Results in cyanosis.

  • Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF)
  • Tricuspid atresia
  • Pulmonary atresia

Key s/s: cyanosis, clubbing, polycythemia, hypercyanotic "tet" spells.

Obstructive Defects

Acyanotic

Narrowed flow downstream of a ventricle — creates back-pressure and decreased cardiac output.

  • Coarctation of the aorta
  • Aortic stenosis
  • Pulmonary stenosis

Key s/s: diminished pulses or BP differences (coarctation), murmur, CHF.

Mixed Defects

Cyanotic

Survival depends on mixing of oxygenated and deoxygenated blood at atrial, ventricular, or great-vessel level.

  • Transposition of Great Arteries
  • Truncus arteriosus
  • Hypoplastic left heart syndrome
  • Total Anomalous Pulmonary Venous Return

Key s/s: cyanosis + CHF. Usually ductal-dependent.

NCLEX Memory Trick

"The 5 T's" of cyanotic CHD: Tetralogy of Fallot · Transposition of the great arteries · Truncus arteriosus · Tricuspid atresia · Total anomalous pulmonary venous return. If a newborn is cyanotic and you see a "T" word in the answer, think CHD.

🎯 Bottom Line: Left-to-right shunt = CHF signs + murmur. Right-to-left shunt = cyanosis + tet spells. Obstructive = pressure back-up. Mixed = both.

Defects with Increased Pulmonary Blood Flow

Left-to-right shunts — VSD, ASD, PDA, AVSD

In these acyanotic defects, an abnormal opening lets oxygenated blood from the high-pressure left side leak into the low-pressure right side. The lungs are flooded, the right heart works harder, and over time the child develops signs of heart failure. Cyanosis is not typical unless the shunt reverses (Eisenmenger syndrome — late finding).

VSD

Ventricular Septal Defect

What it is: Opening in the septum between the ventricles. The most common CHD. Blood flows from LV → RV → lungs.

Signs & symptoms:

  • Loud, harsh, holosystolic murmur at the left lower sternal border — often how it's first found
  • CHF signs if defect is large: tachypnea, poor feeding, diaphoresis with feeds, FTT
  • Small VSDs often close on their own by age 2

Treatment: Small VSDs — watch and wait. Moderate/large — diuretics + ACE inhibitor + digoxin, then surgical closure (patch) between 3-12 months if not closing.

ASD

Atrial Septal Defect

What it is: Opening in the atrial septum. Blood flows LA → RA → RV → lungs. Many children are asymptomatic for years.

Signs & symptoms:

  • Soft systolic ejection murmur at the left upper sternal border with a fixed, split S2
  • Often detected incidentally; mild CHF symptoms if large
  • Adult-onset complications: atrial arrhythmias, pulmonary hypertension, stroke (paradoxical embolism)

Treatment: Small ASDs may close spontaneously. Larger defects are closed with a cardiac catheterization device (Amplatzer occluder) or surgical patch around age 4-5.

PDA

Patent Ductus Arteriosus

What it is: The fetal ductus arteriosus fails to close after birth. Blood shunts from aorta → pulmonary artery (left-to-right). Most common in premature infants.

Signs & symptoms:

  • Continuous "machinery-like" murmur at the left upper sternal border — pathognomonic
  • Wide pulse pressure, bounding pulses
  • CHF signs in premature infants

Treatment:

  • Indomethacin or ibuprofen (prostaglandin inhibitors) to close a PDA in a premature infant
  • Catheter-based closure or surgical ligation if medical therapy fails
  • Do NOT give prostaglandin inhibitors if the infant has a ductal-dependent lesion (the PDA is keeping them alive)
AVSD

Atrioventricular Septal Defect

What it is: Large hole in the center of the heart involving both atrial and ventricular septums, plus a single common AV valve. Strongly associated with Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome).

Signs & symptoms: Severe CHF in early infancy, loud murmur, FTT, frequent pulmonary infections.

Treatment: Surgical repair by age 3-6 months — patch closure of the septal defects and reconstruction of the AV valves.

Pediatric Assessment Pearl

Infants with large left-to-right shunts often feed poorly and sweat during feeds. Diaphoresis with feeding is an early and specific sign of infant CHF — the "cardiac sign" parents rarely know to report. Always ask: "How long does feeding take? Does your baby sweat while feeding?"

🎯 Bottom Line: VSD = harsh holosystolic murmur. ASD = fixed split S2. PDA = machinery murmur. AVSD = think Down syndrome. All four can cause CHF from over-circulation.

Defects with Decreased Pulmonary Blood Flow

Right-to-left shunts — cyanotic CHDs

In these defects, an obstruction to pulmonary flow is combined with a septal defect — so deoxygenated blood takes the path of least resistance and crosses into the left heart (right-to-left shunt). The result is systemic cyanosis. Tetralogy of Fallot is the most common cyanotic CHD beyond infancy.

TOF

Tetralogy of Fallot (most common cyanotic CHD)

What it is: 4 defects together (detailed in Section 5 below). Result: decreased pulmonary flow + right-to-left shunt at the VSD = cyanosis.

TA

Tricuspid Atresia

What it is: No tricuspid valve — no direct path from RA to RV. Blood must shunt right-to-left through an ASD (or PFO) to reach the left heart, then some goes to the lungs via a VSD or PDA.

Signs & symptoms: Profound cyanosis at birth, tachypnea, clubbing, poor growth. Ductal-dependent.

Treatment: Prostaglandin E1 at birth; staged surgical repair (Blalock-Taussig shunt → Glenn → Fontan by age 2-4).

PA

Pulmonary Atresia

What it is: No pulmonary valve — no flow from RV to pulmonary artery. Survival requires a PDA and usually an ASD for blood to reach the lungs.

Signs & symptoms: Severe cyanosis within hours of birth as the ductus closes.

Treatment: Emergent prostaglandin E1, surgical or catheter intervention to establish pulmonary flow.

Cyanosis That Does Not Improve With O2

If a cyanotic newborn's SpO2 does NOT improve after 100% oxygen (the "hyperoxia test"), suspect cyanotic CHD — not a pulmonary cause. In lung disease, oxygen improves saturation. In cyanotic CHD, blood is bypassing the lungs entirely, so extra oxygen has nowhere to go. Call cardiology and start prostaglandin E1.

🎯 Bottom Line: Right-to-left shunts = blue baby. Many are ductal-dependent — prostaglandin E1 is the life-saver until surgery.

Tetralogy of Fallot (TOF)

The classic cyanotic CHD — 4 defects, one picture

TOF is the most common cyanotic CHD in children. It's actually four defects that occur together. The mnemonic "PROV" (or "4 Hs") captures all four:

Pulmonary Stenosis

Narrowed pulmonary outflow tract. The severity of stenosis determines severity of cyanosis.

Right Ventricular Hypertrophy

The RV thickens from pumping against pulmonary stenosis. Creates a "boot-shaped heart" on chest X-ray.

Overriding Aorta

The aorta sits over the VSD, receiving blood from BOTH ventricles (oxygenated + deoxygenated mixed).

Ventricular Septal Defect

Large hole between the ventricles. Because pulmonary stenosis raises RV pressure, blood shunts right-to-left (unlike an isolated VSD).

Signs & Symptoms

Cyanosis: At birth if severe stenosis; otherwise develops over weeks to months.

Loud systolic murmur at the left upper sternal border (from the pulmonary stenosis).

Clubbing of fingers and toes from chronic hypoxia (older infants/children).

Polycythemia as the body compensates for hypoxia — thick blood, stroke risk.

Squatting in toddlers — increases systemic vascular resistance, forces more blood to lungs.

Hypercyanotic "tet" spells — episodes of sudden profound cyanosis (see Section 6).

Diagnostic Findings

  • Chest X-ray: "Boot-shaped" heart (coeur en sabot) from RV hypertrophy
  • Echocardiogram: Confirms all 4 defects and assesses severity
  • ABG: Decreased PaO2 that doesn't improve with 100% O2
  • CBC: Elevated hemoglobin/hematocrit from compensatory polycythemia

Surgical Management

  • Complete intracardiac repair is standard — usually between 3 and 6 months of age. Surgeon closes the VSD with a patch, relieves the pulmonary stenosis, and aligns the aorta.
  • Palliative Blalock-Taussig-Thomas (BT) shunt may be placed first in very small or very cyanotic infants — a Gore-Tex tube connects the subclavian (or innominate) artery to the pulmonary artery to increase pulmonary blood flow until definitive repair.
  • Post-repair, children generally do well with normal life expectancy, though some need re-operation for pulmonary valve regurgitation in adolescence/adulthood.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Why Toddlers with TOF Squat
Squatting is a compensatory move — not a behavior problem. When the child squats, the knees kink the femoral arteries, sharply raising systemic vascular resistance (SVR). Higher SVR forces more blood across the VSD forward to the lungs instead of right-to-left. More lung blood = better oxygenation. This is the same physiology behind the knee-chest position during a tet spell.
🎯 Bottom Line: TOF = Pulmonary stenosis + RV hypertrophy + Overriding aorta + VSD. Boot-shaped heart, squatting, tet spells. Repaired by 6 months of age.

Hypercyanotic "Tet" Spells

Recognize and treat — a pediatric cardiac emergency

A tet spell (hypercyanotic episode) is a sudden, dramatic worsening of right-to-left shunting in a child with TOF (or another cyanotic CHD). The pulmonary outflow tract spasms, blocking blood from reaching the lungs, so even more deoxygenated blood is pushed across the VSD to the body. This is a life-threatening emergency — recognize and treat immediately.

Triggers

  • Crying, feeding, straining (Valsalva)
  • Waking up in the morning — decreased SVR after sleep
  • Fever or dehydration
  • Bowel movement
  • Painful procedures (blood draws, IV starts)

Signs & Symptoms

Sudden, profound cyanosis — lips, nail beds, skin turn dusky/blue.

Tachypnea & deep labored breathing (hyperpnea).

Irritability, restlessness, inconsolable crying — OR limpness if severe.

Murmur may diminish or disappear (less flow through the pulmonary stenosis).

Loss of consciousness, seizures if prolonged and untreated.

Can last minutes to an hour; rarely fatal but risk of stroke, brain injury.

Emergency Management — In Order

  1. Knee-to-chest position. This is the first action. Flex the infant's knees up to the chest (or have an older child squat). It raises SVR and forces blood to the lungs. Do this even before calling for help.
  2. Administer 100% oxygen by blow-by or mask. Dilates pulmonary vasculature.
  3. Calm the child. Soft voice, dim lights, limit stimulation. Agitation worsens the spell.
  4. Morphine 0.1-0.2 mg/kg IV, IM, or SubQ. Reduces infundibular spasm, decreases respiratory drive, calms the child.
  5. IV fluid bolus (normal saline 10-20 mL/kg) — increases preload and pulmonary flow.
  6. If refractory: IV phenylephrine (vasoconstrictor that raises SVR) or propranolol (relieves infundibular spasm).
  7. Notify the cardiology team — severe or recurrent spells often push up the timing of surgical repair.
Do NOT Do These During a Tet Spell

Do NOT place the child flat or prone — lowers SVR and worsens the shunt. Do NOT restrain or force procedures — agitation escalates the spell. Do NOT delay the knee-chest position to get vitals or call the doctor — knee-chest comes first.

Teach the Family the Knee-Chest Maneuver

Every parent of a child with TOF needs to know the knee-chest position BEFORE discharge. Have them demonstrate it back to you. This single maneuver can stop a spell before it becomes life-threatening. Then call 911 and head to the ED.

🎯 Bottom Line: Tet spell = emergency. Knee-chest first, then O2, calm, morphine, fluids. Teach parents to do the position at home.

Obstructive Defects

Coarctation, aortic stenosis, pulmonary stenosis

These defects create a narrowing that obstructs outflow from a ventricle or through the aorta. The pressure builds up behind the obstruction, straining the heart. These are typically acyanotic (unless severe) but can cause dangerous pressure differences.

COA

Coarctation of the Aorta

What it is: Narrowing of the aorta, usually just distal to the left subclavian artery. Blood can't easily get to the lower body.

Hallmark signs (NCLEX favorite):

  • Higher BP in the arms than the legs (>20 mmHg difference)
  • Bounding brachial pulses, weak or absent femoral pulses
  • Cool lower extremities, leg pain with exercise
  • Headaches, nosebleeds in older kids (from upper-body hypertension)
  • Signs of CHF in infants if severe

Treatment: Balloon angioplasty, stent placement, or surgical resection. Infants with severe coarctation need prostaglandin E1 to keep the PDA open until repair.

AS

Aortic Stenosis

What it is: Narrowed aortic valve obstructs LV outflow. Decreases cardiac output.

Signs & symptoms: Systolic ejection murmur at the right upper sternal border radiating to the neck, chest pain or syncope on exertion, fatigue. Severe cases: poor feeding, signs of CHF in infancy.

Treatment: Balloon valvuloplasty or valve replacement. Activity restriction (no competitive sports) for moderate-to-severe stenosis.

PS

Pulmonary Stenosis

What it is: Narrowed pulmonary valve obstructs RV outflow. Often mild and asymptomatic.

Signs & symptoms: Systolic ejection murmur at the left upper sternal border. Severe cases: cyanosis, RV hypertrophy, fatigue.

Treatment: Balloon valvuloplasty is the go-to for significant stenosis.

NCLEX Alert: The 4-Extremity BP

If the question describes a child with hypertension in the arms and weak femoral pulses, or asks which assessment to prioritize in suspected coarctation, the answer is blood pressures in all four extremities. A gradient >20 mmHg between upper and lower extremities is diagnostic until proven otherwise.

🎯 Bottom Line: Coarctation = BP mismatch between arms and legs. AS & PS = ejection murmurs; balloon valvuloplasty is the workhorse.

Right-Sided Heart Failure in Children

Different presentation than adult CHF

Right-sided HF in kids with CHD looks nothing like the adult "wet cough and ankle edema" picture. Infants with CHF are poor feeders first — feeding is exercise, and a failing heart cannot meet the oxygen demand of a 20-minute feed.

Classic Signs of CHF in Infants

System What You'll See
Feeding Tires quickly, stops after 1-2 oz, diaphoresis with feeds, prolonged feeding time (>30 min)
Respiratory Tachypnea at rest (>60/min infant), retractions, grunting, nasal flaring, frequent URIs
Cardiac Tachycardia at rest, gallop rhythm, cool extremities, weak pulses, prolonged cap refill
Systemic/Right-sided Hepatomegaly (liver edge >2 cm below costal margin), periorbital edema, jugular venous distension (older child), weight gain from fluid, decreased urine output
Growth Failure to thrive — weight gain drops off the growth curve while height is preserved initially

Priority Nursing Assessment

  • Daily weights at the same time, same scale, same clothing — earliest and most sensitive indicator of fluid status. Sudden weight gain = fluid retention, not growth.
  • I&O with every diaper weighed (1 g = 1 mL).
  • Respiratory assessment q2-4 hours: rate, work of breathing, retractions, nasal flaring, grunting.
  • Cardiac assessment: apical heart rate for 1 full minute, peripheral pulses, cap refill, skin temperature.
  • Liver edge palpation at the mid-clavicular line — document distance below costal margin.
  • Feeding tolerance: time to complete feed, volume taken, diaphoresis, fatigue, cyanosis during feed.
The Infant CHF Red Flags

If parents report their baby "takes forever to eat," "falls asleep before finishing the bottle," or "sweats while eating," those are classic early CHF signs. Combined with poor weight gain and fast breathing at rest, think cardiac — not GI or respiratory.

🎯 Bottom Line: Infant CHF = poor feeding + tachypnea + diaphoresis + FTT + hepatomegaly. Daily weights are the #1 nursing indicator.

Priority Nursing Care

Conserving energy, supporting growth, preventing complications

Energy Conservation

  • Cluster care to allow long periods of undisturbed rest.
  • Limit feeding time to 20-30 minutes — if not finished, give the remainder by NG tube rather than push the baby to exhaustion.
  • Soft/preemie nipple with a larger hole requires less work.
  • Hold the infant in a semi-upright position during and after feeds to decrease reflux and reduce cardiac demand.
  • Organize cares around scheduled feeds; minimize crying episodes.

Nutrition & Growth Support

  • Small, frequent feeds (every 2-3 hours) rather than large volumes.
  • Calorie-dense formula (24-27 kcal/oz) — more calories in less volume = less fluid + more growth.
  • Add rice cereal as age-appropriate for calorie density.
  • Daily weights; weight gain of 15-30 g/day is target for infants.
  • Consider gavage (NG/OG) feeds to supplement if oral intake is poor.

Oxygenation & Respiratory Support

  • Continuous pulse oximetry; know the baseline SpO2 (may be 75-85% in unrepaired cyanotic lesions — do NOT chase 100%).
  • Supplemental O2 per order; avoid unnecessary O2 in ductal-dependent lesions (can promote ductal closure).
  • HOB elevated 30-45 degrees.
  • Suction prn; pulmonary toileting.

Preventing Infection

  • Strict hand hygiene for all caregivers and visitors.
  • Limit visitors, especially young children during RSV/flu season.
  • Ensure routine childhood vaccinations are up to date — including RSV prophylaxis (palivizumab / nirsevimab) for high-risk infants.
  • SBE prophylaxis with amoxicillin before dental and invasive procedures for unrepaired cyanotic CHD, prosthetic valves, or the first 6 months after repair.

Post-Operative Priorities (Cardiac Surgery)

  • Airway & hemodynamics first: ventilator settings, chest tube output, pacing wires, arterial line, CVP.
  • Monitor for low cardiac output syndrome — hypotension, tachycardia, oliguria, mottling, decreased cap refill.
  • Pain & sedation management; scheduled around care.
  • Chest tube assessment: amount, character, air leaks. Report sudden increase (>3 mL/kg/hr) — bleeding.
  • Watch for arrhythmias (especially junctional ectopic tachycardia, heart block after VSD closure).
  • Sternal precautions: no pulling infants up by their arms — lift under the bottom/back. Typically for 6 weeks.
🎯 Bottom Line: Conserve energy, pack in calories, prevent infection, and protect the repaired heart. Daily weights, clustered care, SBE prophylaxis.

Medications

Digoxin, diuretics, ACE inhibitors, prostaglandin E1
Digoxin (Lanoxin)
Cardiac Glycoside · Positive Inotrope

Why: Improves contractility, slows heart rate.

Check apical HR for 1 full minute before every dose.

Hold if HR <: Infant 90-110 · Child 70 · Adolescent 60.

Toxicity: N/V, bradycardia, visual "yellow-green halo," arrhythmia. Therapeutic level 0.8-2.0 ng/mL.

Hypokalemia potentiates toxicity — monitor potassium closely when on digoxin + diuretics.

If a dose is missed >4 hrs: skip it, resume next scheduled dose — do NOT double up.

If infant vomits: do NOT re-dose; give next scheduled dose.

Furosemide (Lasix)
Loop Diuretic

Why: Removes extra fluid; reduces preload in CHF.

Monitor: Daily weights, I&O, electrolytes (especially potassium), BUN/creatinine.

Adverse: Hypokalemia, dehydration, ototoxicity (rare with peds doses).

Give in the morning to avoid night-time diuresis.

Spironolactone (Aldactone)
Potassium-Sparing Diuretic

Why: Often added with furosemide to preserve potassium.

Monitor: Potassium (risk of hyperkalemia), BUN, creatinine.

Avoid salt substitutes (contain KCl).

Captopril / Enalapril
ACE Inhibitor

Why: Reduces afterload; decreases workload on the heart in large left-to-right shunts.

Monitor: BP (first-dose hypotension), serum potassium, renal function.

Adverse: Dry cough, hyperkalemia, angioedema.

Prostaglandin E1 (Alprostadil)
Ductal Patency · Critical Med

Why: Keeps the ductus arteriosus OPEN in ductal-dependent lesions until surgery.

Major adverse: Apnea — have intubation supplies at bedside. Also hypotension, fever, flushing.

Continuous IV infusion; never stop abruptly unless surgical repair is complete.

Indomethacin / Ibuprofen
Prostaglandin Inhibitor

Why: CLOSES a persistent PDA in a premature infant.

Do NOT use in ductal-dependent lesions — you'll kill the patient.

Monitor: Renal function, platelets, GI bleeding, urine output.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Digoxin + Lasix Trap
Lasix can lower potassium. Low potassium makes digoxin toxic. Watch K+ labs like a hawk whenever an infant is on both. Signs of digoxin toxicity — vomiting, bradycardia, new arrhythmia — in a baby on Lasix are a red flag. The fix is usually potassium replacement, not discontinuing the digoxin.
Prostaglandin E1 Apnea Risk

Apnea occurs in 10-12% of infants on PGE1, usually within the first hour. Always have bag-valve-mask and intubation equipment at the bedside. Never transport an infant on PGE1 without anesthesia/respiratory support available.

🎯 Bottom Line: Digoxin (check apical HR × 1 min, hold for low HR). Lasix (watch K+). PGE1 (keep ductus open, apnea risk). Indomethacin (close PDA in preemies only).

Family Education & Discharge Planning

Empowering parents to manage at home

Parents of a child with CHD carry enormous responsibility. Every teaching session should be short, repeated, and confirmed by return demonstration. The family also needs support for the emotional weight of their child's diagnosis.

Priority Teaching Topics

  • Knee-to-chest position for a tet spell — have parent demonstrate.
  • Medication administration: digoxin via syringe in the side of the mouth, exact dose, what to do if missed or vomited.
  • Signs to call the cardiologist immediately: increased cyanosis, poor feeding, lethargy, respiratory distress, weight gain, decreased wet diapers.
  • Feeding plan: small frequent feeds, calorie-dense formula, 20-30 min limit.
  • Infection prevention: hand hygiene, RSV prophylaxis schedule, avoiding crowds, SBE prophylaxis card for dental visits.
  • Activity: infants self-limit; older kids may need activity restrictions (discussed with cardiology). School/daycare notification.
  • Growth & development: follow-up with peds and cardiology, anticipate mild developmental delay which usually resolves after repair.

Emotional & Psychosocial Support

  • Acknowledge grief — families often describe the diagnosis as "losing the healthy baby they expected."
  • Encourage parents to hold and bond with the infant despite medical equipment.
  • Refer to support groups (e.g., Mended Little Hearts) and social work.
  • Sibling support: prepare siblings with developmentally appropriate information and hospital visits if safe.
  • Financial counseling — CHD care is expensive; connect with hospital financial services.
Incorporating the Child into Their Own Care

For older children, give age-appropriate choices: "Would you like to take your medicine with apple juice or water?" Let them be part of assessments (push the button on the BP cuff, listen to their own heartbeat). This reduces fear, builds trust, and develops health self-advocacy.

🎯 Bottom Line: Teach, demonstrate, confirm. Knee-chest, meds, feeding, infection prevention, when to call. Support the emotional load on the family.
