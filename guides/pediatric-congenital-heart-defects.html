Congenital Heart Defects

One axis that predicts cyanotic from acyanotic, the tet-spell response you do before you call, and the infant heart-failure signs that never look like the adult ones.

The Nursing Collective

By the end, without notes

  • Sort any defect by its one axis, then predict cyanotic or acyanotic
  • Name the four parts of Tetralogy of Fallot and why they cyanose
  • Run a tet-spell response in order, starting with knee-to-chest
  • Spot infant heart failure from feeding, not from adult signs
  • Give digoxin safely and say the exact heart rate that holds it
  • Teach a family feeding, endocarditis prophylaxis, and when to call
01

What actually gets you

There are many defects. There are only a few decisions. The exam lives in the decisions, and points get lost in three predictable ways. Read these first.

You memorized defects by name, not by axis

So a scenario gives you a lesion and you cannot say if it turns the baby blue. Sort by one axis instead. Where does the blood flow, and what happens to the lungs. Increased pulmonary flow is acyanotic. Decreased flow or mixing is cyanotic. Get the axis and the picture follows.

You froze on a tet spell instead of acting

A hypercyanotic spell is a bedside emergency. It is also a task, not a mystery. Knee-to-chest first. Then oxygen, calm, morphine. Freezing to gather vitals costs the child. The first move needs no order and no equipment.

You looked for adult heart-failure signs

Infants do not give you a wet cough and ankle edema. They give you feeding. Poor feeding, sweating with feeds, and no weight gain are the early signs. Add fast breathing at rest and a big liver. Miss those and you miss infant heart failure.

Bottom Line: Stop learning defects as a list of names. Learn three moves: sort by the axis, act on a tet spell, and read infant failure from the feed.
02

The four things

For every lesion you should say four things with the book closed. The one-sentence definition. The clue in a scenario. The priority action. The treatment and its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Work by category, because the category drives the picture.

Increased pulmonary flow (acyanotic)

Lesion Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action Treatment and teaching
VSD A hole between the ventricles shunts blood left to right into the lungs. Loud harsh holosystolic murmur at the left lower sternal border. Watch for feeding heart-failure signs. Small ones close alone; large ones get diuretic, ACE inhibitor, digoxin, then patch.
ASD A hole between the atria shunts blood left to right. Soft systolic murmur with a fixed, split S2. Often watch and monitor; many are found late. Device occluder or patch around age 4 to 5.
PDA The fetal ductus stays open, shunting aorta to pulmonary artery. Continuous machinery murmur, bounding pulses, wide pulse pressure. Confirm the lesion is not ductal-dependent first. Indomethacin or ibuprofen closes it in a preterm infant.

Obstructive (acyanotic)

Lesion Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action Treatment and teaching
Coarctation of the aorta The aorta narrows past the left subclavian, starving the lower body. Higher BP in the arms than the legs, weak femoral pulses. Blood pressures in all four extremities. Balloon, stent, or resection; severe infants need the duct kept open.
Aortic stenosis A narrow aortic valve blocks the left ventricle from emptying. Ejection murmur at the right upper sternal border, syncope on exertion. Restrict activity in moderate to severe disease. Balloon valvuloplasty or valve replacement.
Pulmonic stenosis A narrow pulmonary valve blocks the right ventricle from emptying. Systolic ejection murmur at the left upper sternal border. Track for right heart strain if severe. Balloon valvuloplasty for significant stenosis.

Cyanotic (decreased flow or mixing)

Lesion Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action Treatment and teaching
Tetralogy of Fallot Four defects push blood right to left, so it skips the lungs. Cyanosis, boot-shaped heart, squatting, tet spells. Knee-to-chest at the first sign of a spell. Full repair around 3 to 6 months; teach the family the position.
Tricuspid or pulmonary atresia A missing valve leaves no path forward, so blood must shunt. Deep cyanosis in the first hours or days of life. Start prostaglandin E1 to hold the duct open. Staged surgical repair once the infant is stable.
Mixing lesions (transposition) Two separate circuits survive only by mixing oxygenated and blue blood. Cyanosis with heart-failure signs, usually ductal-dependent. Prostaglandin E1 while cardiology is called. Surgical repair; keep intubation supplies ready.
Copy this down

Write three headers on one card. Increased flow, blue if it mixes, obstruction backs up. Under each, list the lesions and their one clue. That card is most of the exam.

03

Increased pulmonary flow

Discrimination axis: a hole lets blood shunt left to right.

These defects are acyanotic. An opening lets oxygenated blood leak from the high-pressure left side to the low-pressure right side. The lungs flood. Over time the child shows heart failure. Cyanosis is not typical unless the shunt reverses much later.

Tell them apart by the murmur
  • VSD: loud harsh holosystolic murmur, left lower sternal border. The most common defect.
  • ASD: soft systolic murmur with a fixed, split S2.
  • PDA: continuous machinery murmur, bounding pulses, wide pulse pressure.
  • AVSD: loud murmur with early severe failure. Think Trisomy 21.
What the flooding does
  • Tachypnea and fast heart rate at rest
  • Poor feeding and sweating with feeds
  • Failure to thrive and slow weight gain
  • Frequent respiratory infections

Small VSDs often close alone by age 2.

Keep the duct open, or close it

A PDA is not always the enemy. In a preterm infant with a nuisance PDA, you close it with indomethacin or ibuprofen. In a ductal-dependent lesion, that same duct is the only path to blood flow. Never give a prostaglandin inhibitor to a ductal-dependent baby. You would close their lifeline.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Diaphoresis with feeds is a cardiac sign
A baby who sweats while feeding is working too hard to eat. Feeding is the infant's exercise. Sweating with feeds, plus a long feed and poor weight gain, points to heart failure, not fussiness. Always ask how long the feed takes.
Bottom Line: VSD is harsh and holosystolic. ASD gives a fixed split S2. PDA is machinery. All flood the lungs and can cause failure.
04

Obstruction

Discrimination axis: flow is narrowed, so pressure backs up.

These lesions narrow the path out of a ventricle or through the aorta. Pressure builds behind the narrowing. They are usually acyanotic. Coarctation is the one the exam loves, because the finding is a mismatch you can measure.

Coarctation: upper body high, lower body low

The aorta narrows just past the left subclavian. Blood struggles to reach the legs. So you see higher blood pressure in the arms than the legs, a gap over 20 mmHg. Brachial pulses bound. Femoral pulses are weak or absent. The lower legs run cool. The priority assessment is blood pressures in all four extremities.

LesionClueTreatment
CoarctationArm-leg BP gap over 20 mmHg, weak femoral pulses, cool legs.Balloon, stent, or resection. Severe infants need prostaglandin E1.
Aortic stenosisEjection murmur at the right upper sternal border, chest pain or syncope on exertion.Balloon valvuloplasty or valve replacement. Restrict activity.
Pulmonic stenosisSystolic ejection murmur at the left upper sternal border.Balloon valvuloplasty for significant stenosis.
Bottom Line: Coarctation is a blood-pressure mismatch between arms and legs. The valve stenoses give ejection murmurs. Balloon valvuloplasty is the workhorse.
05

Tetralogy of Fallot

Discrimination axis: four defects together push blood right to left.

Tetralogy of Fallot is the most common cyanotic defect past infancy. It is four problems at once. The mnemonic PROV holds all four. The pulmonary stenosis is the driver; the tighter it is, the bluer the child.

LetterDefectWhat it does
PPulmonic stenosisNarrows the outflow to the lungs. Its severity sets the cyanosis.
RRight ventricular hypertrophyThe right ventricle thickens against the stenosis. Makes the boot-shaped heart.
OOverriding aortaThe aorta sits over the hole and takes blood from both ventricles.
VVentricular septal defectA large hole. High right-side pressure sends blood right to left through it.

The rest of the picture follows from chronic low oxygen. Expect a loud systolic murmur at the left upper sternal border. Older children show clubbing and thick blood from polycythemia. The chest film shows a boot-shaped heart. Oxygen does not fix the cyanosis, because blood is skipping the lungs.

Repair is usually complete around 3 to 6 months. A palliative shunt may come first in a very small or very blue infant. It routes more blood to the lungs until the full repair.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Why toddlers with TOF squat
Squatting is a fix, not a habit. It kinks the leg arteries and raises systemic vascular resistance. Higher resistance forces blood across the hole toward the lungs instead of right to left. More lung blood means better oxygen. This is the same physiology as knee-to-chest.
Bottom Line: PROV is pulmonic stenosis, right ventricular hypertrophy, overriding aorta, VSD. Boot-shaped heart, squatting, tet spells. Repaired by about 6 months.
06

The tet spell

A hypercyanotic episode is an emergency. Act, then call.

A tet spell is a sudden surge of right-to-left shunting. The pulmonary outflow spasms. More blue blood crosses the hole to the body. The child turns dusky and breathes hard. Crying, feeding, straining, waking, fever, and painful procedures all set it off.

Recognize it fast

Look for sudden deep cyanosis of the lips and nail beds. Breathing turns fast and labored. The child is irritable, then limp if it worsens. The murmur may fade, because less blood is crossing the stenosis. A long spell risks seizure and stroke.

The response, in order

  1. 1
    Knee-to-chest. Flex the infant's knees up to the chest, or let an older child squat. This raises systemic resistance and drives blood to the lungs. Do it before you call for help.
  2. 2
    100% oxygen. Blow-by or mask. It dilates the pulmonary vessels.
  3. 3
    Calm the child. Soft voice, dim lights, less handling. Agitation deepens the spell.
  4. 4
    Morphine 0.1 to 0.2 mg/kg. IV, IM, or subcutaneous. It eases the outflow spasm and the breathing drive.
  5. 5
    IV fluid bolus. Normal saline 10 to 20 mL/kg raises preload and lung flow.
  6. 6
    If it will not break. IV phenylephrine to raise resistance, or propranolol to relax the outflow. Then notify cardiology; hard spells move up the surgery date.
Do not do these during a spell

Do not lay the child flat or prone. That drops resistance and worsens the shunt. Do not restrain or force a procedure. Agitation feeds the spell. Do not delay knee-to-chest to get vitals or a phone. The position comes first.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Knee-to-chest is the free first move
In a first-action question, knee-to-chest wins. It needs no order, no equipment, and no lab. It raises resistance and pushes blood to the lungs in seconds. Teach every parent to do it at home, then call for transport.
Bottom Line: A tet spell is an emergency. Knee-to-chest first, then oxygen, calm, morphine, fluids. Teach the family the position before discharge.
07

Heart failure in the infant

It shows in the feed, not in a wet cough.

Infant heart failure looks nothing like the adult version. There is no ankle edema story. The first sign is feeding. A failing heart cannot meet the demand of a long feed, so the baby tires, sweats, and stops early.

SystemWhat you see
FeedingTires fast, stops after 1 to 2 oz, sweats with feeds, feed runs over 30 min.
RespiratoryTachypnea at rest over 60 a minute, retractions, grunting, nasal flaring.
CardiacFast rate at rest, gallop, cool limbs, weak pulses, slow capillary refill.
SystemicLiver edge over 2 cm below the costal margin, puffy eyes, weight gain from fluid, less urine.
GrowthFailure to thrive; weight drops off the curve while height holds at first.

Your priority assessments follow the same story. Daily weights catch fluid first. Weigh every diaper, where 1 g equals 1 mL. Check the apical rate for a full minute. Palpate the liver edge and document it. Time each feed and note sweating and fatigue.

The red-flag feeding history

Parents will say the baby takes forever to eat. Or falls asleep before finishing the bottle. Or sweats while eating. Pair that with poor weight gain and fast breathing at rest. Think cardiac, not reflux or a cold.

Bottom Line: Infant failure is poor feeding, sweating, tachypnea, failure to thrive, and a big liver. Daily weight is your number-one indicator.
08

Medications

You do not need every mechanism. You need what to check, what to hold for, and the traps. Digoxin and prostaglandin E1 carry the hard rules.

DrugWhyWhat you monitor or hold for
DigoxinStronger squeeze, slower rateApical rate for one full minute. Therapeutic 0.8 to 2.0 ng/mL. Low potassium turns it toxic.
FurosemidePulls fluid, drops preloadDaily weight, intake and output, potassium. Dose in the morning.
SpironolactoneSpares potassium with the loopPotassium for a high value. Avoid salt substitutes; they hold potassium chloride.
Captopril, enalaprilDrops afterload and workloadBlood pressure, potassium, renal function. Watch a dry cough.
Prostaglandin E1Keeps the duct openApnea in the first hour. Keep intubation supplies at the bedside.
Indomethacin, ibuprofenCloses a preterm PDARenal function, platelets, urine. Never in a ductal-dependent lesion.

Giving digoxin safely

  1. 1
    Count the apical rate. One full minute, before every dose. This is not optional in an infant.
  2. 2
    Hold for a low rate. Hold if the infant is under 90 to 110. Child under 70. Adolescent under 60. Then notify the provider.
  3. 3
    Know the toxic signs. Vomiting, bradycardia, a new arrhythmia, or yellow-green vision. A low potassium makes all of it more likely.
  4. 4
    Handle a missed or vomited dose. If it is missed over 4 hours, skip it and resume next time. If the infant vomits, do not re-dose. Never double up.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The digoxin and furosemide trap
Furosemide lowers potassium. Low potassium makes digoxin toxic at a normal level. So watch the potassium when a baby takes both. Toxic signs in that child are a red flag. The fix is usually potassium, not stopping the digoxin.
Prostaglandin E1 and apnea

Apnea hits about 10 to 12 percent of infants on prostaglandin E1, usually in the first hour. Keep a bag-valve-mask and intubation gear at the bedside. Run it as a continuous drip. Never stop it abruptly unless the repair is done.

Bottom Line: Digoxin needs an apical minute and a hold rule. Furosemide drops potassium. Prostaglandin E1 holds the duct and risks apnea. Prostaglandin inhibitors close a preterm PDA only.
09

Nursing care

The plan is simple to say and hard to do. Save energy, pack in calories, block infection, and protect the repaired heart.

Conserve energy and feed

  • Cluster care so the infant gets long stretches of rest.
  • Limit each feed to 20 to 30 minutes. Give the rest by tube rather than exhaust the baby.
  • Use a soft or preemie nipple; it takes less work.
  • Feed and hold semi-upright to ease reflux and cardiac demand.
  • Offer small frequent feeds every 2 to 3 hours.
  • Use calorie-dense formula, 24 to 27 kcal per oz. Aim for 15 to 30 g of weight gain a day.

Oxygen and infection

  • Know the baseline saturation. In an unrepaired cyanotic lesion it may sit at 75 to 85 percent. Do not chase 100.
  • Avoid extra oxygen in a ductal-dependent lesion; it can help close the duct.
  • Keep the head of the bed up 30 to 45 degrees.
  • Hand hygiene for every caregiver and visitor. Limit young visitors in RSV season.
  • Keep vaccines current, including RSV prophylaxis for high-risk infants.
  • Give endocarditis prophylaxis with amoxicillin before dental work for the right groups.

After cardiac surgery

  • Airway and hemodynamics first: ventilator, chest tubes, pacing wires, lines.
  • Watch for low cardiac output: low pressure, fast rate, low urine, mottling.
  • Report chest-tube output over 3 mL/kg/hr; that is bleeding.
  • Watch for arrhythmias, including heart block after a VSD closure.
  • Keep sternal precautions. Lift under the back, not by the arms, for about 6 weeks.
Who needs endocarditis prophylaxis

Not every child with a heart defect needs it. Cover unrepaired cyanotic lesions. Cover prosthetic valves. Cover the first 6 months after a repair with prosthetic material. Give it before dental and invasive procedures, and give the family a card.

Bottom Line: Save energy, pack calories, block infection, guard the repair. Daily weights, clustered care, and the right prophylaxis carry the plan.
10

Family education

Parents run this care at home. Teach in short pieces, repeat, and confirm with a return demonstration. They also carry a heavy emotional load. Support that too.

Teach these first

  • Knee-to-chest for a spell. Have the parent show you, then call 911 and go to the ED.
  • Medication. Give digoxin by syringe to the side of the mouth, exact dose. Do not re-dose after a spit-up. Skip a dose missed over 4 hours.
  • Feeding plan. Small frequent feeds, calorie-dense formula, a 20 to 30 minute limit.
  • Infection. Hand hygiene, RSV prophylaxis schedule, avoid crowds, prophylaxis card for the dentist.
  • Growth. Follow up with pediatrics and cardiology. Expect a mild delay that usually catches up after repair.

When to call the cardiologist

  • More cyanosis or a spell that does not break
  • Poor feeding, sweating with feeds, or lethargy
  • Fast or labored breathing at rest
  • Sudden weight gain or fewer wet diapers
Bring the child into their own care

Give an older child small choices. Water or juice with the medicine. Let them push the button on the cuff or hear their own heart. It lowers fear and builds trust. Support the parents too, with grief, siblings, and a referral for cost.

Bottom Line: Teach, demonstrate, confirm. Knee-to-chest, meds, feeding, infection, and the exact reasons to call. Carry the family, not just the child.
