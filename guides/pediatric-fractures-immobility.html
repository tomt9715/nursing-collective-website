Pediatric Fractures & Immobility

What makes kids' fractures unique, the 6 Ps of neurovascular assessment, cast and traction principles, and the complications of being immobilized at any age.

High-Yield Summary

Eight facts that cover almost every pediatric MSK question.

  • Pediatric bones differ from adults: more flexible, thicker periosteum, open growth plates. Kids get unique fractures (greenstick, torus/buckle) and heal faster, but growth plate (physis) injuries risk future deformity.
  • Salter-Harris classification grades growth plate fractures I–V; type II (through physis + metaphysis) is most common. Higher types = higher risk of growth arrest.
  • Neurovascular assessment: the 6 Ps — Pain, Pallor, Pulselessness, Paresthesia, Paralysis, Poikilothermia (cold). Check q1–2h after injury or new cast.
  • Compartment syndrome = emergency. Pain out of proportion to injury, unrelieved by opioids, worsened by passive stretch. Without fasciotomy, permanent nerve/muscle damage within 6–8 hours.
  • Cast care: elevate above heart, ice for first 24–48 hr, handle with PALMS (not fingers) until dry, petal edges, nothing inside the cast (no scratching tools), neurovascular checks q1–2h first day.
  • Bryant's traction (infants <2 years or <30 lb): BOTH legs elevated 90° perpendicular to bed, buttocks slightly off mattress. Used for femoral fractures and developmental hip dysplasia.
  • Traction principles: weights hang freely (never lift or remove), counter-traction is essential, body alignment maintained, pin care for skeletal traction.
  • Abuse-concerning fractures: femur in a non-ambulatory child, spiral fractures in very young, multiple fractures at different healing stages, metaphyseal corner fractures, posterior rib fractures in infants. Document thoroughly; mandatory reporting.

Pediatric Fractures: What's Different

Kid bones bend, buckle, and break differently than adult bones

Children's bones are still growing, which means they behave differently under stress than adult bones. They're more flexible, have a thicker periosteum, and contain open growth plates (physes) that are the weakest part of the bone. This biology explains the unique fracture patterns you'll see in pediatric patients.

Why Kid Bones Heal Faster — and Sometimes Worse

More collagen, less calcium — bones bend before they break.

Thicker periosteum — stabilizes fractures and speeds healing.

Open growth plates (physes) are the weakest point — injury here can arrest growth.

Remodeling is greater in children, so some imperfect alignment resolves with growth.

Common Pediatric Fracture Types

Greenstick

Incomplete
  • Incomplete fracture — one side breaks, the other bends (like a green stick)
  • Most common pediatric fracture
  • Seen in forearm, wrist
  • Reduction + casting usually sufficient

Torus (Buckle)

Compression
  • Bone buckles/compresses without breaking through
  • Common in distal radius from fall on outstretched hand
  • Stable; short-arm cast or splint x 3–4 weeks
  • Excellent prognosis

Complete / Displaced

Full Break
  • Complete break through both cortices
  • May be transverse, oblique, spiral, or comminuted
  • Spiral fractures in young kids warrant abuse screening
  • Often requires reduction; may need surgery

Salter-Harris (Growth Plate)

Physeal
  • Five types (I–V); classified by involvement of physis, epiphysis, metaphysis
  • Type II is most common (through physis + metaphysis)
  • Higher types = higher risk of growth arrest and deformity
  • Needs orthopedic follow-up — growth arrest is the big concern

Salter-Harris Grading at a Glance

Type Fracture Path Growth Arrest Risk
IStraight through physis onlyLow
IIThrough physis + metaphysis (most common)Low-moderate
IIIThrough physis + epiphysis (into joint)Moderate-high
IVThrough epiphysis + physis + metaphysisHigh
VCrush injury of the physisVery high

Common Fracture Locations

  • Distal radius (wrist) — the most common pediatric fracture. Classic "FOOSH" injury (fall on outstretched hand).
  • Forearm (radius & ulna) — common in falls and sports.
  • Clavicle — birth trauma or shoulder-impact falls. Usually heals without surgery.
  • Supracondylar humerus — playground falls. High neurovascular risk (brachial artery, median/radial nerves).
  • Tibia & fibula — toddler's fracture (spiral tibia from twisting while walking); also from sports.
  • Femur — high-energy trauma (MVA, sports). In a non-ambulatory infant, raises abuse concern.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Why Growth Plates Are the Weak Link
The physis (growth plate) is cartilaginous and mechanically weaker than the surrounding bone. Forces that would cause ligament injury in an adult (e.g., ankle sprain) can fracture the growth plate in a child. That's why any joint injury in a child with pain over the physis should be imaged — even with a "normal" looking X-ray, a Salter-Harris I is easy to miss. Follow-up in 7–10 days often reveals callus formation confirming the fracture.
🎯 Bottom Line: Greenstick and torus are classic pediatric. Salter-Harris grades growth-plate injury; higher type = higher risk of growth arrest. Kids heal fast but the physis is the vulnerability.

Neurovascular Assessment & Compartment Syndrome

The 6 Ps — and the one that matters most

After any fracture — and especially after cast or splint application — swelling or pressure can compress nerves and vessels. Missing early compromise leads to permanent nerve damage, muscle necrosis, and compartment syndrome. Systematic assessment using the 6 Ps is the universal framework.

The 6 Ps of Neurovascular Assessment

Pain

Location, severity, character. Pain out of proportion is a red flag.

Pallor

Color below injury: pale, dusky, or cyanotic = compromised circulation.

Pulselessness

Diminished or absent distal pulses = vascular compromise. Late sign.

Paresthesia

Numbness, tingling, "pins and needles" = nerve compression.

Paralysis

Inability to move fingers/toes distal to the injury = severe nerve damage.

Poikilothermia

Cold extremity — loss of thermoregulation from vascular compromise.

When to Check

  • Every 1–2 hours for the first 24 hours after injury or new cast.
  • Then every 4 hours while hospitalized, and at every outpatient follow-up.
  • Always compare the affected side to the unaffected side.
  • For young non-verbal children, observe behavior, color, swelling, capillary refill, and movement of exposed fingers/toes.

Compartment Syndrome

Compartment syndrome occurs when pressure within a fascial compartment exceeds capillary perfusion pressure, cutting off blood flow to muscles and nerves. It's a time-sensitive surgical emergency — irreversible muscle necrosis and nerve damage begin within 4–8 hours.

Pain out of proportion to injury — the EARLIEST and most sensitive sign.

Pain unrelieved by opioids — another red flag of increasing compartment pressure.

Severe pain with passive stretch — classic compartment syndrome finding.

Tense, firm, shiny swelling of the compartment.

Paresthesia — early sign of nerve compression within the compartment.

Pulselessness is LATE — by the time pulses are gone, irreversible damage has likely occurred.

Loose the Cast, Don't Wait

If you suspect compartment syndrome in a casted extremity, the cast must be bivalved (split on both sides) or removed immediately — this alone can relieve enough pressure to restore circulation. Notify the surgeon emergently; if conservative measures don't relieve the pressure, fasciotomy (surgical opening of the fascia) is required to save the limb.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Pain Out of Proportion Is the One Finding
When a child reports severe pain that doesn't make sense for the injury — pain that's worsening after a few hours in a cast, pain that opioids barely touch, pain with passive stretching of the digits — treat it as compartment syndrome until proven otherwise. By the time pulses are absent, the damage is done. Early nursing recognition saves limbs.
🎯 Bottom Line: 6 Ps q1–2h after injury/cast. Pain out of proportion = compartment syndrome. Bivalve the cast immediately; prepare for fasciotomy.

Treatment: Casts, Splints, Traction, Fixators

Picking the right immobilization and caring for it safely

Casts

  • Plaster — takes 24–48 hr to fully dry; cheaper, heavier.
  • Fiberglass — dries in 15–30 min; lighter and more durable; standard for most pediatric casts.
  • Cast duration depends on location and fracture; typically 3–6 weeks for most upper extremity, 4–8 weeks for lower.

Cast Care Priorities

  1. Elevate extremity above heart for the first 24–48 hours to minimize swelling.
  2. Ice packs over the cast for the first 24 hours (not directly on skin).
  3. Neurovascular checks q1–2h first 24 hours, then q4h.
  4. Handle a wet plaster cast with PALMS, not fingers — fingertips leave indentations that cause pressure points.
  5. Petal rough edges of the cast with adhesive tape to protect skin.
  6. Do NOT get the cast wet — cover with plastic for bathing.
  7. Nothing inside the cast — no coat hangers, pencils, or scratching tools. They cause skin breakdown and trapped objects.
  8. If itching is severe: blow cool air with a hairdryer into the cast.
  9. Teach signs to return: increasing pain, tingling, fingers/toes blue or cold, foul odor, fever, drainage.

Splints

  • Used when swelling is expected (immediately after injury, before casting).
  • Allows for expansion if swelling worsens — safer than a circumferential cast in the first 24–48 hr.
  • Often converted to cast at a follow-up visit once swelling subsides.

Traction

Traction uses a pulling force to realign bones or reduce muscle spasm. Types used in pediatrics include:

Type Description Typical Use
Bryant's Infants <2 years or <30 lb. BOTH legs elevated 90° perpendicular to bed; buttocks slightly off the mattress. Skin traction. Femoral fractures, developmental hip dysplasia
Buck's Straight leg horizontal traction with boot or skin adhesive; typical weight 5–8 lb. Hip fracture pre-op, LE muscle spasm
Russell's Skin traction with knee flexion and dual pulley system; realigns the femur. Femur fractures in older children
Skeletal (e.g., 90/90, balanced suspension) Pins or wires surgically placed through bone; heavier weights possible. Complex, unstable fractures, especially in older children

Traction Principles (NCLEX gold)

Weights hang freely — never lift, remove, or rest on the bed/floor.

Maintain counter-traction — usually the child's body weight. Don't let them slide down the bed.

Ropes in the pulleys, knots away from pulleys.

Pin care for skeletal traction — watch for signs of infection (redness, drainage, fever).

Neurovascular checks q1–2h, skin assessment q shift.

For Bryant's traction: confirm buttocks are slightly OFF the mattress — this is how you know counter-traction is appropriate.

External Fixators

  • Metal frame outside the body stabilizing bone with pins through skin into bone.
  • Used for complex, open, or comminuted fractures where cast or traction is insufficient.
  • Pin site care is critical: cleanse per protocol (often with sterile saline or chlorhexidine), monitor for infection.
Bryant's Traction Specifics

Remember: Bryant's is for very young children (<2 years or <30 lb). Both legs go up at 90 degrees — even if only one leg is fractured — because the child's own body weight provides counter-traction. The buttocks should be slightly off the mattress. If the child's weight is too high, the pull becomes dangerous. This is a favorite NCLEX detail.

🎯 Bottom Line: Casts = elevate, ice, NV checks, no scratching tools. Bryant's = both legs 90° in infants. Weights hang freely. Skeletal pins need care.

Complications of Immobility

Every body system loses when a child can't move

Immobility — from casts, traction, or any prolonged bed rest — affects every body system. Nursing priorities focus on preventing the predictable complications, because once they develop they prolong recovery and hospital stay.

System-by-System Complications

System Complications Prevention
Cardiovascular Orthostatic hypotension, decreased cardiac output, DVT (uncommon in kids but possible) Gradual position changes, isometric exercises, ROM, anti-embolic measures as ordered
Respiratory Atelectasis, pneumonia, decreased lung expansion Incentive spirometry, cough and deep breathing q2h, reposition frequently
Musculoskeletal Muscle atrophy, contractures, disuse osteoporosis (can fracture without trauma), joint stiffness Passive and active ROM, PT consult, isometric exercises, weight bearing when permitted
GI Constipation, decreased appetite, weight loss, fecal impaction High-fiber diet, adequate fluids, stool softeners, scheduled toileting
Genitourinary Urinary stasis, UTI, kidney stones, incontinence Encourage fluids, void q2–4h, perineal hygiene
Skin Pressure injuries (heels, sacrum, elbows, scalp in infants) Turn q2h, pressure-relief surfaces, keep skin clean and dry, Braden Q assessment
Psychosocial Depression, boredom, developmental regression, sleep disturbance Age-appropriate activities, child life services, family presence, school services
Disuse Osteoporosis in Kids

Prolonged immobility (weeks to months) causes bone demineralization, making the child prone to fractures without obvious trauma — turning in bed or transfer from bed to chair can break an osteopenic bone. Nursing implications: handle extremities with support, use slow position changes, and be alert for new pain reports that could represent a fracture.

Psychosocial Support for the Immobilized Child

  • Child life services for play therapy, procedural prep, normalization.
  • School continuity — hospital-based schooling or tutoring for longer stays.
  • Encourage family presence and sibling visits when possible.
  • Provide age-appropriate activities: screen time limits balanced with drawing, music, age-appropriate games, crafts.
  • Acknowledge developmental regression (thumb sucking, baby talk) as normal coping; don't shame.
🎯 Bottom Line: Immobility hits every system — cardiac, respiratory, MSK, GI, GU, skin, psychosocial. Prevent by moving what you can, turning, repositioning, and supporting the child emotionally.

Abuse-Concerning Fracture Patterns

What to recognize and report

Some fracture patterns raise concern for non-accidental trauma (NAT). Nurses are mandatory reporters and must document objectively and report suspicions to child protective services — even when the parents are present and seemingly cooperative. Reporting is about safety, not accusation.

Patterns That Warrant a Full Workup

Femur fracture in a non-ambulatory child (<1 year) — it takes enormous force to break a baby's femur.

Spiral fractures of long bones in young children, from a twisting mechanism.

Multiple fractures at different healing stages — old fractures the parent did not report.

Metaphyseal corner (bucket-handle) fractures — highly specific for abuse.

Posterior rib fractures in infants — occur from forceful squeezing; uncommon from routine falls.

Injuries inconsistent with the reported mechanism or developmental stage (e.g., 6-month-old "rolled off couch" with skull fracture).

What the Team Typically Orders

  • Skeletal survey — full-body X-ray series in children under 2 with suspicious injury.
  • Head CT/MRI if head trauma suspected.
  • Ophthalmology exam for retinal hemorrhages (shaken baby syndrome).
  • Coagulation studies to rule out bleeding disorders.
  • Social work and child protection team consultation.

Nursing Role

  • Document objectively — exact words the parent uses, time elapsed between injury and presentation, observable bruise patterns and colors, X-ray findings. Avoid accusatory language in the chart.
  • Photograph injuries per institutional protocol.
  • Report to child protective services — you are a mandatory reporter and reporting in good faith is legally protected.
  • Treat the family with respect while reporting; do not accuse or interrogate. Let the trained team handle the investigation.
  • Focus on the child's safety — that's the priority. If the hospital admits for workup, the child is safe for now.
Mandatory Reporting Is Legally Protected

Nurses are mandatory reporters in all 50 states. Reporting a reasonable suspicion of child abuse in good faith is legally protected — you cannot be sued for making a report even if the investigation ultimately finds no abuse. Not reporting a suspicion, however, can result in legal action and loss of licensure. If you're unsure, consult the hospital social worker or child protection team. Better to report and be wrong than to miss abuse.

🎯 Bottom Line: Suspicious patterns = skeletal survey, social work, mandatory report. Document objectively, photograph injuries, treat family respectfully, child safety first.

Family Education & Discharge

What parents need to know before leaving

Cast Care at Home

  • Keep cast clean and dry — cover with a plastic bag sealed with tape for bathing. Never submerge in water.
  • Elevate the extremity above heart level when sitting or sleeping for the first 48 hours.
  • Ice over the cast for the first 24 hours (30 min on, 30 min off) to reduce swelling.
  • Do not put anything inside the cast. No coat hangers, pencils, spoons, or pens for itching.
  • For itching, use a cool hair dryer directed into the cast opening — never heat.
  • Check fingers/toes every few hours in the first 24–48 hours: color, warmth, sensation, movement.
  • Return to the ED if: fingers/toes turn blue/pale/cold, severe unrelenting pain, foul smell, fever, cast breakdown, drainage, or any cast-related concern.

Traction & Immobility Teaching

  • Explain the purpose of the traction in age-appropriate terms.
  • Involve the child in care — reposition the pillow, brush their own hair, feed themselves.
  • Pain management schedule: acetaminophen ± opioids as ordered. Don't let pain get ahead of the medication.
  • Skin checks daily — look at heels, sacrum, elbows, back of head.
  • Constipation prevention: high-fiber diet, fluids, stool softeners.
  • Connect with child life, school-based services, social work as needed.

Activity & Return to School/Sports

  • Follow-up with orthopedics as scheduled. X-rays often repeated to confirm healing.
  • No full weight-bearing until cleared. Use crutches or assistive devices as prescribed.
  • School usually resumes within days to a week for upper extremity fractures; longer for LE.
  • Sports and PE restrictions until healing is confirmed — usually 6–12 weeks.
  • After cast removal: skin may look dry, pale, and weak — gentle washing and gradual return to activity.
Children Heal Remarkably Well

Most pediatric fractures heal without long-term complications — faster than adults and with more remodeling. Even fractures that are imperfectly aligned often correct themselves with growth. Emphasize hope and full recovery to anxious families while you teach the practical care steps.

🎯 Bottom Line: Teach cast care, neurovascular checks, when to return to ED. Prevent immobility complications. Reassure families that pediatric bones heal well.
