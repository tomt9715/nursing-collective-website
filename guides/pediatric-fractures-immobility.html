Pediatric Fractures & Immobility

The fracture types only kids get, the one neurovascular finding that saves a limb, and how to spot the injuries that do not match the story.

01

What actually gets you

Fractures feel like an easy topic. Kids fall, kids break bones, you cast it. The exam is not built on that. It is built on three things that go wrong in predictable ways.

Read these before anything else.

You treated the neurovascular check as routine

It is the priority. It is what catches compartment syndrome. The early red flag is pain out of proportion to the injury, and pain that opioids barely touch. Wait for the pulse to disappear and the damage is already done.

You studied adult fractures and stopped there

Kids get their own patterns. Greenstick. Buckle, also called torus. Salter-Harris injuries through the growth plate. That last group can arrest future growth. Miss the pediatric types and you miss the pediatric questions.

You took the injury story at face value

Some fractures do not fit the story. A spiral or a metaphyseal corner fracture. Posterior rib fractures. Several breaks at different healing stages. An injury a child that age could not cause. Those are the ones you screen and report.

Bottom Line: Study this as three jobs, not one disease: catch compartment syndrome early, know the fracture types kids get, and read the injury against the story.

03

Why kid bones break differently

Children's bones are still growing. They behave differently under stress than adult bones. They are more flexible. They have a thicker periosteum. They carry open growth plates, and the growth plate is the weakest part.

That biology explains the patterns you see.

Why kids heal fast, and sometimes worse

More collagen, less calcium. The bone bends before it breaks.

Thicker periosteum. It stabilizes the fracture and speeds healing.

Open growth plates (physes). The weakest point; injury here can arrest growth.

Strong remodeling. Some imperfect alignment corrects itself as the child grows.

The fracture types kids get

Greenstick
  • Incomplete; one side breaks, the other bends, like a green stick
  • Most common pediatric fracture
  • Seen in the forearm and wrist
  • Reduction plus casting is usually enough
Torus (buckle)
  • Bone compresses and buckles without breaking through
  • Common in the distal radius from a fall on an outstretched hand
  • Stable; short-arm cast or splint for 3 to 4 weeks
  • Excellent prognosis
Complete / displaced
  • A full break through both cortices
  • May be transverse, oblique, spiral, or comminuted
  • A spiral fracture in a young child warrants abuse screening
  • Often needs reduction; may need surgery
Salter-Harris (growth plate)
  • Five types, I to V, graded by what the fracture crosses
  • Type II is most common: through physis and metaphysis
  • Higher types carry higher risk of growth arrest and deformity
  • Needs orthopedic follow-up; growth arrest is the big concern

Salter-Harris at a glance

TypeFracture pathGrowth arrest risk
IStraight through the physis onlyLow
IIThrough physis and metaphysis (most common)Low to moderate
IIIThrough physis and epiphysis, into the jointModerate to high
IVThrough epiphysis, physis, and metaphysisHigh
VCrush injury of the physisVery high

Where kids break, and what to watch

  • Distal radius (wrist). The most common pediatric fracture. A classic fall on an outstretched hand.
  • Forearm (radius and ulna). Common in falls and sports.
  • Clavicle. Birth trauma or a shoulder-impact fall. Usually heals without surgery.
  • Supracondylar humerus. Playground falls. High neurovascular risk to the brachial artery and the median and radial nerves.
  • Tibia and fibula. The toddler's fracture is a spiral tibia from twisting while walking; also from sports.
  • Femur. High-energy trauma such as a crash or sport. In a non-ambulatory infant, it raises abuse concern.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Physis Is the Weak Link
The growth plate is cartilage, so it is weaker than the bone around it. A force that only sprains an adult ankle can fracture a child's growth plate. Image any joint injury with pain over the physis, even when the X-ray looks normal; a Salter-Harris I is easy to miss. Follow up in 7 to 10 days and callus often confirms it.
Bottom Line: Greenstick and torus are the classic pediatric breaks. Salter-Harris grades growth-plate injury; higher type means higher growth-arrest risk. Kids heal fast, but the physis is the vulnerability.
04

The 6 Ps and compartment syndrome

This is the assessment that saves the limb.

After any fracture, swelling and pressure can squeeze nerves and vessels. A tight cast does the same. Miss the early signs and you get permanent nerve damage, muscle death, and compartment syndrome. The 6 Ps are the framework you run every time.

The 6 Ps of neurovascular assessment

PWhat you checkWhat it means
PainLocation, severity, characterPain out of proportion is the red flag
PallorColor below the injuryPale, dusky, or cyanotic means circulation is compromised
PulselessnessDistal pulsesDiminished or absent means vascular compromise; a late sign
ParesthesiaSensation distal to the injuryNumbness or tingling means nerve compression
ParalysisMovement of fingers or toesInability to move means severe nerve damage
PoikilothermiaTemperature of the limbA cold extremity means lost thermoregulation from vascular compromise

When to check

  • Every 1 to 2 hours for the first 24 hours after injury or a new cast.
  • Then every 4 hours while hospitalized, and at every outpatient follow-up.
  • Always compare the affected side to the unaffected side.
  • For a young non-verbal child, watch behavior, color, swelling, capillary refill, and movement of exposed fingers and toes.

Compartment syndrome

Pressure builds inside a fascial compartment. It rises past the pressure that pushes blood through the capillaries. Blood flow to muscle and nerve stops. This is a time-sensitive surgical emergency. Irreversible muscle and nerve damage begins within 4 to 8 hours.

The signs come in an order. Learn the order, because acting on the early ones is the whole point.

Pain out of proportion to the injury. The earliest and most sensitive sign.

Pain unrelieved by opioids. Another warning of rising compartment pressure.

Severe pain with passive stretch. A classic compartment finding.

Tense, firm, shiny swelling over the compartment.

Paresthesia. An early sign of nerve compression inside the compartment.

Pulselessness is late. By the time pulses are gone, damage has likely occurred.

What you do, in order

  1. 1
    Loosen the cast. In a casted limb, the cast must be bivalved, split on both sides, or removed. This alone can relieve enough pressure to restore circulation.
  2. 2
    Keep the limb at heart level. Once you suspect compartment syndrome, do not elevate above the heart and do not apply cold. Both drop arterial flow to muscle that is already starving.
  3. 3
    Notify the surgeon emergently. If loosening the cast does not relieve the pressure, a fasciotomy is required to save the limb.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Pain Out of Proportion Is the One Finding
A child reports severe pain that does not fit the injury. It is worsening a few hours into a cast. Opioids barely touch it. Passive stretch of the digits makes it scream. Treat that as compartment syndrome until proven otherwise. By the time pulses are absent, the damage is done, and early nursing recognition is what saves limbs.
Bottom Line: Run the 6 Ps every 1 to 2 hours after injury or cast. Pain out of proportion means compartment syndrome. Bivalve the cast, keep the limb level, and prepare for fasciotomy.
05

When the fracture does not fit the story

Some fracture patterns raise concern for non-accidental trauma. You are a mandatory reporter. You document objectively and report a suspicion to child protective services, even when the parents are present and cooperative. Reporting is about safety, not accusation.

Patterns that warrant a full workup

Femur fracture in a non-ambulatory child under 1 year. It takes enormous force to break a baby's femur.

Spiral fractures of long bones in young children, from a twisting force.

Multiple fractures at different healing stages. Old breaks the parent did not report.

Metaphyseal corner (bucket-handle) fractures. Highly specific for abuse.

Posterior rib fractures in infants. From forceful squeezing; uncommon from routine falls.

Injury inconsistent with the story or the child's developmental stage.

What the team usually orders

  • Skeletal survey. A full-body X-ray series in children under 2 with a suspicious injury.
  • Head CT or MRI if head trauma is suspected.
  • An ophthalmology exam for retinal hemorrhages, seen in shaken baby syndrome.
  • Coagulation studies to rule out a bleeding disorder.
  • Social work and the child protection team.

Your role at the bedside

  • Document objectively. The parent's exact words. Time between injury and arrival. Bruise patterns and colors. X-ray findings. Keep accusatory language out of the chart.
  • Photograph injuries per your institution's protocol.
  • Report to child protective services. You are a mandatory reporter, and reporting in good faith is protected.
  • Treat the family with respect. Do not accuse or interrogate. Let the trained team run the investigation.
  • Keep the focus on the child's safety. If the hospital admits for workup, the child is safe for now.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Reporting in Good Faith Is Protected
Nurses are mandatory reporters in all 50 states. Report a reasonable suspicion in good faith and you cannot be sued, even if the investigation finds no abuse. Fail to report a suspicion and you risk legal action and your license. If you are unsure, consult the social worker or the child protection team. Better to report and be wrong than to miss abuse.
Bottom Line: Suspicious pattern means skeletal survey, social work, and a mandatory report. Document objectively, photograph the injuries, treat the family with respect, and put the child's safety first.
06

Casts, splints, traction, fixators

Pick the right hold, then care for it safely.

Casts

  • Plaster. Takes 24 to 48 hours to dry fully. Cheaper and heavier.
  • Fiberglass. Dries in 15 to 30 minutes. Lighter, more durable, the standard for most pediatric casts.
  • Duration depends on the site: about 3 to 6 weeks for most upper extremity, 4 to 8 weeks for lower.

Cast care, in order

  1. 1
    Elevate above the heart for the first 24 to 48 hours to limit swelling.
  2. 2
    Ice over the cast for the first 24 hours. Not directly on skin.
  3. 3
    Neurovascular checks every 1 to 2 hours the first day, then every 4 hours.
  4. 4
    Handle a wet plaster cast with your palms, not your fingers. Fingertips leave dents that become pressure points.
  5. 5
    Petal the rough edges with tape to protect the skin.
  6. 6
    Keep it dry. Cover with plastic for bathing.
  7. 7
    Nothing goes inside the cast. No hangers, pencils, or scratching tools. They break skin and get stuck.
  8. 8
    For severe itching, blow cool air from a hair dryer into the cast. Teach the family to return for increasing pain, tingling, blue or cold digits, foul odor, fever, or drainage.

Splints

  • Used when swelling is expected, right after injury and before casting.
  • They allow expansion, so they are safer than a circumferential cast in the first 24 to 48 hours.
  • Often converted to a cast at follow-up once the swelling settles.

Traction

Traction uses a pulling force to realign bone or ease muscle spasm. These are the types used in pediatrics.

TypeDescriptionTypical use
Bryant's For infants under 2 years or under 30 lb. Both legs elevated 90 degrees, perpendicular to the bed, buttocks slightly off the mattress. Skin traction. Femoral fractures, developmental hip dysplasia
Buck's Straight-leg horizontal traction with a boot or skin adhesive; typical weight 5 to 8 lb. Hip fracture before surgery, lower-extremity muscle spasm
Russell's Skin traction with knee flexion and a dual pulley system; realigns the femur. Femur fractures in older children
Skeletal Pins or wires placed surgically through bone; heavier weights possible. Includes 90/90 and balanced suspension. Complex, unstable fractures, especially in older children

Traction principles

Weights hang freely. Never lift, remove, or rest them on the bed or floor.

Maintain counter-traction. Usually the child's own body weight. Do not let them slide down the bed.

Ropes in the pulleys, knots kept away from the pulleys.

Pin care for skeletal traction. Watch for redness, drainage, and fever.

Neurovascular checks every 1 to 2 hours, skin assessment each shift.

For Bryant's, confirm the buttocks are slightly off the mattress. That is how you know counter-traction is right.

External fixators

  • A metal frame outside the body, holding bone with pins that pass through skin.
  • Used for complex, open, or comminuted fractures when a cast or traction is not enough.
  • Pin-site care is critical: cleanse per protocol, often with sterile saline or chlorhexidine, and monitor for infection.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Bryant's Is All About the Buttocks
Bryant's is for very young children, under 2 years or under 30 lb. Both legs go up at 90 degrees, even if only one leg is fractured, because the child's own body weight is the counter-traction. The buttocks should sit slightly off the mattress. Too much weight and the pull turns dangerous. This is a favorite test detail.
Bottom Line: Casts mean elevate, ice, run the 6 Ps, and put nothing inside. Bryant's means both legs at 90 degrees in infants. Weights hang freely. Skeletal pins need care.
07

What immobility costs, system by system

Immobility hits every body system. It does not matter whether it comes from a cast, traction, or bed rest. Your job is to prevent the predictable complications, because once they set in they stretch out recovery.

SystemComplicationsPrevention
Cardiovascular Orthostatic hypotension, decreased cardiac output, DVT (uncommon in kids but possible) Gradual position changes, isometric exercises, range of motion, anti-embolic measures as ordered
Respiratory Atelectasis, pneumonia, decreased lung expansion Incentive spirometry, cough and deep breathe every 2 hours, reposition often
Musculoskeletal Muscle atrophy, contractures, disuse osteoporosis, joint stiffness Passive and active range of motion, PT consult, isometrics, weight bearing when permitted
GI Constipation, decreased appetite, weight loss, fecal impaction High-fiber diet, adequate fluids, stool softeners, scheduled toileting
Genitourinary Urinary stasis, UTI, kidney stones, incontinence Encourage fluids, void every 2 to 4 hours, perineal hygiene
Skin Pressure injuries at heels, sacrum, elbows, and the scalp in infants Turn every 2 hours, pressure-relief surfaces, keep skin clean and dry, Braden Q assessment
Psychosocial Depression, boredom, developmental regression, sleep disturbance Age-appropriate activities, child life services, family presence, school services
Disuse osteoporosis

Weeks to months of immobility demineralize bone. The child can then fracture without obvious trauma. A turn in bed or a transfer to a chair can break an osteopenic bone. So support the extremities, change positions slowly, and treat any new pain as a possible fracture.

Support the immobile child

  • Child life services for play therapy, procedure prep, and normalization.
  • School continuity through hospital schooling or tutoring for longer stays.
  • Family presence and sibling visits when possible.
  • Age-appropriate activities: balance screen time with drawing, music, games, and crafts.
  • Treat regression, such as thumb sucking or baby talk, as normal coping. Do not shame it.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
A Transfer Can Break a Bone
This is the immobility fact the exam loves. After weeks in a cast or bed, a child's bone demineralizes enough to snap during ordinary handling. So you lift with support, you change positions slowly, and you take a new report of pain seriously. Move what you can, turn often, and protect the skin while you do it.
Bottom Line: Immobility touches cardiac, respiratory, MSK, GI, GU, skin, and mood. Prevent it by moving what you can, turning, repositioning, and supporting the child emotionally.
08

What the family needs before discharge

Cast care at home

  • Keep the cast clean and dry. Cover it with a plastic bag taped shut for bathing. Never submerge it.
  • Elevate the limb above heart level when sitting or sleeping for the first 48 hours.
  • Ice over the cast for the first 24 hours, 30 minutes on and 30 minutes off.
  • Nothing goes inside the cast. No hangers, pencils, spoons, or pens for itching.
  • For itching, aim a cool hair dryer into the cast opening. Never heat.
  • Check the fingers or toes every few hours the first day or two: color, warmth, sensation, movement.
  • Return to the ED for blue, pale, or cold digits, severe unrelenting pain, foul smell, fever, cast breakdown, or drainage.

Traction and immobility teaching

  • Explain the purpose of the traction in words the child can follow.
  • Involve the child in care: repositioning the pillow, brushing their own hair, feeding themselves.
  • Stay ahead of pain with a schedule: acetaminophen, with opioids as ordered.
  • Check the skin daily at the heels, sacrum, elbows, and back of the head.
  • Prevent constipation with fiber, fluids, and stool softeners.
  • Connect the family with child life, school services, and social work as needed.

Return to school and sport

  • Keep the orthopedic follow-up; X-rays are often repeated to confirm healing.
  • No full weight bearing until cleared. Use crutches or assistive devices as prescribed.
  • School usually resumes within days to a week for an upper-extremity fracture, longer for a leg.
  • Sports and PE stay restricted until healing is confirmed, usually 6 to 12 weeks.
  • After the cast comes off, the skin may look dry, pale, and weak. Wash gently and return to activity gradually.
Children heal remarkably well

Most pediatric fractures heal without long-term trouble, faster than in adults and with more remodeling. Even a slightly imperfect alignment often corrects itself with growth. Give anxious families that honest reassurance while you teach the practical steps.

Bottom Line: Teach cast care, neurovascular checks, and when to return. Prevent immobility complications. Tell families the truth: pediatric bones heal well.
