Pediatric GERD

One separation carries this whole topic: the happy spitter who is thriving, the baby whose reflux has become a disease, and the vomiting that is not reflux at all.

  • Say whether a spitty baby is a happy spitter or has GERD, and name the finding that decided it
  • Name three red flags that mean the vomiting is not reflux at all
  • Put conservative measures before any drug, and defend the first one
  • Give a PPI at the right time and say why the timing matters

01

What actually gets you

Reflux is not a hard topic. It is one separation asked three ways. Almost every infant spits up, so the exam never asks whether reflux is happening.

It asks which baby in front of you needs nothing, which needs treatment, and which needs a surgeon. Points get lost here in three predictable ways.

You treated every spitty baby the same

Spit-up alone is normal. It is reflux, not disease. The baby who is gaining weight and content is a happy spitter. The one who is failing to thrive, refusing feeds, or arching in pain has GERD. Same reflux, different meaning.

You reached for medicine before you fixed the feed

Positioning, feed size, and thickening come first. A PPI does nothing for a happy spitter. Reserve acid suppression for confirmed GERD, and only after conservative care has failed.

You called green vomit reflux

Bilious vomiting is never GERD. Neither is projectile vomiting, blood, or an infant who is not growing. Those are red flags for obstruction or worse, and they get worked up, not thickened.

Bottom Line: Stop studying GERD as spit-up. Study it as three separations: normal or disease, reflux or something surgical, feed-and-position or medicate.

The four things

For each picture below, you should be able to say four things with the book closed. The one-sentence definition, the clue you would actually see, the priority nursing action, and the first move with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Picture Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First move and teaching
Physiologic GER Stomach contents rise into the esophagus because the sphincter is immature. Frequent spit-up, gaining weight, content between feeds. Plot the weight, then reassure. Upright after feeds, small frequent feeds. No medicine.
Pathologic GERD The same reflux, now causing complications or real distress. Poor weight gain, feeding refusal, arching, cough or apnea. Confirm the growth problem, escalate the workup. Conservative measures first, then an H2 blocker or PPI.
Pyloric stenosis The pyloric muscle thickens and blocks the stomach outlet. Projectile vomiting at 2 to 8 weeks, hungry again after. Treat dehydration, notify the provider. Fluids and surgery. This is not reflux.
Malrotation with volvulus The gut twists on itself and cuts off its own blood supply. Green, bilious vomiting in a sick-looking infant. Notify the provider now; prepare an upper GI series. Surgical emergency. Never call bile reflux.

03

GER or GERD

Discrimination axis: is the baby thriving or failing.

Gastroesophageal reflux is stomach contents moving back up the esophagus. It is normal in almost every infant, because the lower esophageal sphincter is still immature. GERD is the disease version. It causes complications, distress, or both.

The whole job is telling the two apart. The growth chart usually answers it.

Physiologic GER: the happy spitter
  • Reflux without distress
  • Normal weight gain on the curve
  • Peaks at 2 to 4 months
  • Resolves by 12 to 18 months as the sphincter matures
  • No feeding refusal, no apnea, no failure to thrive

What it needs: reassurance and conservative measures. No medicine.

Pathologic GERD: the disease
  • Failure to thrive or poor weight gain
  • Feeding refusal, irritability during or after feeds
  • Esophagitis, blood in the vomit
  • Cough, wheeze, aspiration, apnea, or color change
  • Recurrent ear infections in older children

What it needs: a workup, and often medication or surgery.

The axis that separates them

Thriving or failing. Not the amount of spit-up. A baby can soak three burp cloths a feed and still be a happy spitter, as long as the weight tracks and the mood is fine. The moment growth falters or feeds become a fight, it is GERD.

Who is at higher risk for GERD

Premature infants. Immature sphincter plus other problems.

Neurologically impaired children such as cerebral palsy.

Hiatal hernia or other anatomic abnormalities.

Cystic fibrosis and Down syndrome.

Obesity in older children and adolescents.

Repaired esophageal atresia or other prior surgery.

How it looks by age

Age groupTypical presentation
Infants Frequent regurgitation, back-arching, irritability with feeds, feeding refusal, poor growth, cough or wheeze, apnea, blood in vomit if severe
Toddlers and preschoolers Vomiting, poor appetite, dental erosion, recurrent respiratory infections, hoarseness, picky eating
School age and adolescents Heartburn, regurgitation, epigastric pain, chronic cough, worsening asthma, sour breath, dental erosion
Sandifer syndrome looks like a seizure

Some infants arch the back and twist the head during reflux. It looks alarming, and it gets mistaken for a seizure. It is not epileptic. It is the baby trying to clear the esophagus, and it is tied to feeds. The tell is the timing.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Happy Spitter Rule
If the baby is gaining weight, content, and otherwise well, it is GER, not GERD. The volume on the couch cushions is irrelevant. Most reflux resolves by 12 to 18 months, and no medicine is needed. This is the most common "needs further teaching" scenario in peds.
Bottom Line: GER is normal spit-up with normal growth. GERD is spit-up plus complications. The weight curve is the answer.
04

When it is not reflux

Not all vomiting is reflux. Before you treat an infant for GERD, screen for findings that point somewhere else. Obstruction, infection, metabolic disease, and neurologic causes all vomit too.

Bilious (green) vomiting. Think malrotation with volvulus or bowel obstruction.

Projectile vomiting at 2 to 8 weeks. Think pyloric stenosis.

Blood in vomit or stool. Esophagitis, allergic colitis, or surgical bleeding.

Onset after 6 months. Physiologic reflux is winding down by then.

Still going past 18 months. It should be resolving, not worsening.

Fever or systemic signs. Think infection, sepsis, meningitis, or a UTI.

Neurologic signs. Seizures, bulging fontanelle, or lethargy point to rising pressure.

Failure to thrive. Too few calories, malabsorption, metabolic disease, or severe GERD.

Bilious vomiting is never GERD

Green vomit is bile. Bile means obstruction below the ampulla of Vater. Treat it as a surgical emergency until proven otherwise. Malrotation with volvulus can kill bowel within hours. Do not attribute it to reflux. Notify the provider and prepare for an upper GI series.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Green Means Go, To Surgery
Green vomit in an infant is an emergency, full stop. So is projectile vomiting in a 2 to 8 week old. When a stem hands you either one, the answer is never reassure, thicken, or wait. It is notify and work up.
Bottom Line: Green vomit, blood, fever, poor growth, or neuro signs are not GERD. Work up obstruction, infection, or systemic illness first.
05

Diagnosis

For uncomplicated GERD, the diagnosis is clinical. History and the growth chart carry most cases. Testing is for severe or atypical presentations, or when a conservative trial fails.

TestWhat it detectsWhen to use
Clinical assessment Feeding history, growth chart, symptom pattern First-line for all suspected GERD
Upper GI series Anatomy: hiatal hernia, malrotation, stricture Rule out an anatomic cause, not to grade GERD
24-hour pH probe Acid reflux episodes and duration Gold standard for reflux severity; atypical cases
Impedance study (MII) Both acid and non-acid reflux Symptoms persist on acid suppression
EGD with biopsy Esophagitis, eosinophilic esophagitis, Barrett changes Refractory symptoms, suspected complications
Gastric emptying scan Delayed gastric emptying GERD complicated by persistent vomiting
Bottom Line: History plus the growth chart covers most cases. The pH probe is the gold standard for severity. EGD is for refractory disease.
06

Conservative care comes first

Conservative measures fix most physiologic reflux and help even true GERD. They are cheap and safe. Try them before any drug. Priority questions ask what you do first, and the first move here is almost never a medication.

For an infant, in order

  1. 1
    Hold upright during and 30 minutes after every feed. Gravity keeps the feed down. Hold the baby or use an infant seat, not a car seat that folds the tummy.
  2. 2
    Feed small and often. Overfeeding distends the stomach and drives reflux. Small frequent feeds beat large infrequent ones.
  3. 3
    Burp often. Every half to one ounce, with gentle pats.
  4. 4
    Thicken feeds. 1 teaspoon of rice cereal per ounce of formula, with the nipple opening enlarged. Evidence is mixed, but it is commonly used.
  5. 5
    Trial a hypoallergenic formula. An extensively hydrolyzed or amino acid formula for 2 to 4 weeks. Cow's milk protein intolerance can mimic GERD.
  6. 6
    Sleep supine on a firm flat surface, always. Back to sleep governs sleep, even for a refluxing baby.

For older children

  • Manage weight. Obesity drives reflux in school-age children and teens.
  • Avoid trigger foods: spicy, acidic, fatty, chocolate, caffeine, peppermint, citrus, tomato.
  • No large meals near bedtime. Eat at least 2 to 3 hours before lying down.
  • Raise the head of the bed 6 to 8 inches on blocks under the legs, not on pillows.
  • Avoid tight clothing around the belly.
  • Keep the child away from smoke. Secondhand smoke worsens reflux.
Back to sleep beats reflux positioning

Older textbooks suggested prone or elevated sleep for reflux. Current guidance is supine sleep on a firm flat surface, no exceptions. SIDS risk outweighs any reflux benefit, and elevation is not as helpful as once believed. Upright positioning is for awake time after feeds, not for sleep.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Thicken by the Ounce
The recipe is 1 teaspoon of rice cereal per ounce of formula. Then enlarge the nipple opening so the thicker feed still flows. Teach parents to mix it right so the baby neither chokes nor takes in too many calories.
Bottom Line: Upright after feeds, small frequent meals, thickeners, weight control. Sleep supine. Fix the feed before you reach for a drug.
07

Medications and surgery

Discrimination axis: has conservative care failed, and is this confirmed GERD.

Drugs are for confirmed GERD that conservative measures did not control. They are not for happy spitters. Escalate in order: conservative care, then acid suppression, then surgery for the refractory few.

H2 Receptor Antagonist
Famotidine (Pepcid)

Blocks histamine at H2 receptors on gastric parietal cells, so acid output falls.

Dose: 1 mg/kg twice daily, up to 40 mg twice daily.

Often the first drug tried for confirmed GERD. Tolerance can build with chronic use.

Note: ranitidine (Zantac) was withdrawn in 2020 for NDMA contamination and is no longer available.

Proton Pump Inhibitor
Omeprazole, Lansoprazole

Irreversibly blocks the proton pump of parietal cells. More potent than an H2 blocker.

Give 30 to 60 minutes before a meal, usually breakfast, for full effect.

For infants, dissolve in a small amount of water or sodium bicarbonate.

Reserve for confirmed GERD. Long-term risks include low calcium, magnesium, B12, and iron, plus more respiratory and GI infections.

Symptomatic Relief
Antacids

For older children and teens with occasional heartburn.

Calcium carbonate (TUMS) or aluminum and magnesium hydroxide (Mylanta).

Not for infant GERD. Short action and electrolyte concerns.

Use With Caution
Prokinetics

Metoclopramide carries a black box warning for tardive dyskinesia and extrapyramidal symptoms. Rarely used in children.

Erythromycin is sometimes used short-term to speed gastric emptying.

Cisapride was withdrawn for fatal arrhythmias.

Surgery for Refractory GERD
Nissen Fundoplication

The fundus is wrapped around the distal esophagus, creating a physical barrier to reflux.

Indications: severe GERD that failed medical therapy, apnea or aspiration, esophageal stricture, or a neurologically impaired child. Often paired with a G-tube.

Complications: dumping syndrome, gas bloat, and an inability to vomit or belch.

Common Errors
What Not To Do

No routine metoclopramide in young children. The extrapyramidal risk is real.

No PPI for physiologic GER. Happy spitters do not need acid suppression.

No prone sleep for an infant, even with GERD.

No lifelong PPI without revisiting the need. The long-term risks add up.

The axis that separates them

Conservative care gives way to a drug only when reflux is confirmed and complications persist. A drug gives way to surgery only when medical therapy fails or the child is having life-threatening events. Move one step at a time, and never skip to a PPI for a thriving baby.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
PPIs Go Before the Meal
Give a PPI 30 to 60 minutes before eating. It binds pumps that are actively making acid, which happens during a meal. On an empty stomach or after the meal, most of the dose is wasted. This teaching point turns up again and again.
Bottom Line: Conservative first. H2 blocker, then PPI 30 to 60 minutes before meals. Nissen fundoplication for severe refractory disease. Avoid metoclopramide, prone sleep, and PPIs for happy spitters.
08

What you do at the bedside

Your priorities sort themselves once you know the separation. Track growth, watch a feed, teach the family, and know the findings that mean call now.

Nursing priorities, in order

  1. 1
    Plot weight on the growth chart every visit. This single number decides GER from GERD.
  2. 2
    Take a detailed feeding history. Volume, duration, frequency, position, burping, and formula type.
  3. 3
    Watch a feed if you can. Look for arching, crying, coughing, choking, or color change.
  4. 4
    Check hydration. Wet diapers, mucous membranes, and capillary refill.
  5. 5
    Give medications on time. Time the PPI 30 to 60 minutes before a meal.

What to teach the family

  • Hold upright during and 30 minutes after feeds. Hold the baby or use an infant seat.
  • Feed small and often. Cue-based beats schedule-based. Overfeeding worsens reflux.
  • Burp every half to one ounce with gentle pats.
  • Thicken with 1 teaspoon of rice cereal per ounce, and enlarge the nipple opening.
  • Sleep supine on a firm flat surface, no exceptions.

When to call the provider

Blood in vomit or stool.

Green (bilious) vomiting.

Projectile vomiting.

Poor weight gain or weight loss.

Apnea, cyanosis, or choking with feeds.

Feeding refusal or pain during and after feeds.

Reassurance is a nursing skill

Most parents of spitty babies are exhausted and scared. When the growth curve looks great and the baby is well, your job is to normalize the reflux. Tell them it looks dramatic, the baby is growing perfectly, and this resolves by 12 to 18 months. Pair that with upright positioning, burping, and small frequent feeds. You fix the problem without a prescription.

Bottom Line: Plot the weight, teach conservative care, time the PPI, and know the red flags. Most reflux resolves on reassurance and positioning alone.
