Pediatric GI Obstructions

Four conditions that all vomit and hurt. One signature each, two vomit colors, and the pre-op order that keeps a child safe.

Reading Time
18-22 minutes
Difficulty Level
Intermediate
Last Updated

The Nursing Collective

By the end, without notes

  • Name which of the four it is from age and vomiting alone
  • Read the vomit: non-bilious projectile versus bilious, and what each means
  • Say why you correct fluids and electrolytes before the operating room
  • Explain why the contrast enema both finds and fixes intussusception
  • Call the two silent emergencies: bilious vomiting and sudden pain relief
  • Order pre-op care: NPO, IV fluids, NG decompression, then surgery
01

What actually gets you

These four conditions all present the same way. Vomiting, a sore belly, a distressed child. The topic is not hard. The discriminations are where the points live.

Points get lost here in three predictable ways. Read these first.

You did not use the vomit to localize the problem

The color tells you where the blockage sits. Non-bilious projectile vomiting points at pyloric stenosis, a high outlet. Bilious green vomiting is a surgical emergency; it means a distal obstruction such as malrotation with volvulus. Never write green vomit off as reflux.

You blurred the four signatures together

Each condition owns one sign. An olive mass is pyloric stenosis. Currant-jelly stool is intussusception. No meconium in 48 hours is Hirschsprung. McBurney rebound is appendicitis. Learn one anchor per condition and the scenario sorts itself.

You read sudden pain relief as good news

In appendicitis it is often the opposite. The pain stops because the appendix ruptured. Peritonitis follows within hours, with high fever and a rigid belly.

Bottom Line: Stop studying four diseases. Study four signatures, two vomit colors, and one rule: fluids before the operating room.
02

The four things

For every condition below, say four things with the book closed. The one-sentence definition. The clue you would see in the child. The priority action. The treatment with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet.

Pyloric stenosis The pylorus thickens and blocks the stomach outlet. 2 to 8 week boy, projectile non-bilious vomiting, hungry again right after. Correct fluids and electrolytes first. Pyloromyotomy. Feed small, upright, burp often.

03

Pyloric stenosis

Signature: the hungry vomiter. Look-alike: reflux or gastroenteritis.

The pylorus is the outlet between the stomach and the duodenum. Here it thickens and narrows. Feeds build up and shoot back out. The window is tight: 2 to 8 weeks old.

Points to pyloric stenosis
  • Projectile non-bilious vomiting after feeds, shooting 2 to 3 feet
  • Hungry immediately after vomiting, the hungry vomiter
  • Palpable olive mass in the right upper quadrant after a feed
  • Visible peristaltic waves crossing the epigastrium
  • Poor weight gain, dehydration, fewer wet diapers
  • More common in boys, 4 to 6 times, especially first-born
Points to reflux or gastroenteritis
  • Reflux is small, effortless spit-up with no distress
  • Gastroenteritis usually brings diarrhea too
  • Both make the baby feel sick, so the baby does not want the next feed
  • Neither produces the hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis
  • No olive mass, no peristaltic waves

The classic lab pattern

Persistent vomiting of stomach acid drives one predictable disturbance. Know it cold; it is the diagnosis and it sets the treatment.

  • Hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis: low chloride, high pH, high bicarbonate
  • Hypokalemia: the kidneys waste potassium while trying to hold on to hydrogen
  • Hyponatremia can develop with prolonged vomiting

Diagnosis and treatment

  • Ultrasound is first-line, showing a thickened, elongated pylorus
  • Physical exam catches the olive mass and peristaltic waves
  • Correct the fluid and electrolyte imbalance first. You cannot safely anesthetize an alkalotic, dehydrated infant
  • Pyloromyotomy (the Ramstedt procedure) splits the thickened muscle
  • Post-op feeds usually restart within 4 to 6 hours, small and upright
The axis that separates them

Appetite and labs. A refluxing or sick baby refuses the next feed. The pyloric baby demands it. And only pyloric stenosis gives you alkalosis with low potassium and low chloride. That lab panel nails it.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Why the hungry vomiter matters
A baby with reflux or gastroenteritis feels nauseated, so they turn away from the bottle. The pyloric baby vomits and cries for more at once. The stomach is empty and they are obstructed, not sick. That one detail is worth a diagnosis.
Bottom Line: First-born boy, 2 to 8 weeks, projectile non-bilious vomiting, hungry after. Alkalosis with low potassium and low chloride. Fluids first, surgery second.
04

Intussusception

Signature: telescoping bowel with currant-jelly stool. Look-alike: colic or gastroenteritis.

One segment of bowel slides into the segment just past it, like a sock pushed into itself. Most often the terminal ileum slides into the cecum. The trapped segment loses its blood supply and starts to die.

Points to intussusception
  • Sudden, severe, intermittent pain in waves every 15 to 20 minutes
  • Child draws knees to chest and screams, then looks normal between attacks
  • Vomiting, non-bilious early and bilious as the obstruction builds
  • Currant-jelly stool, blood and mucus mixed, a late sign of ischemia
  • Sausage-shaped mass in the right upper quadrant
  • Peak age 5 to 9 months, range 3 months to 3 years
Points to colic or gastroenteritis
  • Colic pain is not this severe and has no bloody stool
  • Gastroenteritis brings steady diarrhea, not knees-to-chest waves
  • Neither gives a palpable sausage mass
  • A recent viral illness can precede intussusception, muddying the picture
  • Lethargy between attacks is a warning sign, not simple fussiness

Diagnosis and treatment

  • Ultrasound is first-line, showing the target or bulls-eye sign
  • Air or hydrostatic contrast enema both confirms the diagnosis and often fixes it
  • The enema reduces most cases without surgery
  • Surgery if the enema fails, or if there are signs of perforation or dead bowel
  • Give IV fluids, keep the child NPO, and use NG decompression before the procedure
A normal brown stool after the enema

After a contrast enema, a normal brown stool suggests the bowel reduced. Report it right away. Then watch for recurrence in the first 24 hours; the child is usually admitted for observation.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The enema does double duty
This is the detail that trips people up. In intussusception the contrast enema is not just a picture. The pressure pushes the telescoped bowel back out, so it treats as it diagnoses. No other condition here works that way.
Bottom Line: 5 to 9 month old, severe intermittent pain with knees to chest, currant-jelly stool late, right-upper-quadrant sausage mass. The enema diagnoses and treats most cases.
05

Hirschsprung disease

Signature: no meconium in 48 hours. Look-alike: feeding intolerance or simple constipation.

A segment of distal colon is born without ganglion cells, the nerves that drive peristalsis. That segment cannot relax; it stays in permanent spasm. This is a functional obstruction. Stool piles up behind it and the colon dilates.

Points to Hirschsprung
  • Newborn fails to pass meconium within 48 hours of birth, the classic flag
  • Abdominal distension and bilious vomiting
  • Older child has chronic constipation since infancy
  • Ribbon-like stools forced through the narrow spastic segment
  • Failure to thrive, poor appetite, foul breath or stool
  • More common in boys, and linked to Down syndrome
Points to feeding intolerance or constipation
  • Feeding intolerance does not stop meconium from passing
  • Simple constipation starts later, not from the first days of life
  • Diet-related constipation has no ribbon stools or distension pattern
  • Neither carries the enterocolitis risk
  • Check bowel function in every newborn with Down syndrome

Diagnosis and treatment

  • Rectal biopsy is definitive, showing absent ganglion cells in the submucosa
  • Contrast enema shows a narrow distal segment and a dilated proximal one, the transition zone
  • Anorectal manometry shows the internal sphincter failing to relax
  • Pull-through surgery (Soave, Swenson, or Duhamel) removes the aganglionic segment
  • A temporary colostomy may be placed first in severe cases
Enterocolitis is the killer

Hirschsprung-associated enterocolitis is life-threatening. Bacteria overgrow in the stagnant bowel. Watch for fever, explosive foul-smelling diarrhea, distension, lethargy, and shock. It can strike before or after surgery. Treat with IV fluids, broad-spectrum antibiotics, rectal decompression, and NPO.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The 48-hour meconium rule
Nearly all healthy term newborns pass meconium within 48 hours. When a newborn has not stooled by that mark, red flags go up. Hirschsprung sits near the top, alongside cystic fibrosis and imperforate anus. That is why nurses chart the first stool so carefully.
Bottom Line: No meconium in 48 hours, or chronic constipation with ribbon stools. Rectal biopsy confirms. Surgery fixes it. Enterocolitis kills: fever, explosive diarrhea, shock.
06

Appendicitis

Signature: pain that migrates to McBurney. Look-alike: gastroenteritis or a urinary tract infection.

The appendiceal lumen blocks. Bacteria overgrow, the wall swells, and blood supply fails. Untreated, it perforates. The pain starts vague and central, then localizes and sharpens as the inflamed appendix irritates the peritoneum.

Points to appendicitis
  • Periumbilical pain migrating to the right lower quadrant, at McBurney point
  • Anorexia, a very consistent finding
  • Nausea and vomiting, usually after the pain begins
  • Low-grade fever; a high fever suggests perforation
  • Rebound tenderness and guarding
  • Avoids jumping or bouncing, walks bent over
  • Peak age 10 to 19 years
Points to gastroenteritis or a UTI
  • Gastroenteritis usually leads with diarrhea and diffuse pain
  • Its vomiting often comes before the pain, not after
  • A UTI brings dysuria and flank pain, not McBurney rebound
  • Run a urinalysis to rule out the UTI mimic
  • Neither reproduces the periumbilical-to-right-lower-quadrant march

Peritoneal signs and workup

  • Rovsing sign: pressing the left lower quadrant causes right-lower-quadrant pain
  • Psoas sign: pain with hip extension, from a retrocecal appendix
  • Obturator sign: pain with internal rotation of the flexed right hip
  • Blumberg sign: rebound tenderness, pain when you release pressure
  • Labs show elevated white cells with a left shift, and elevated CRP
  • Ultrasound is preferred in children; CT if ultrasound is unclear
  • Treatment is appendectomy with IV antibiotics and fluids
Sudden pain relief means perforation

If a child with classic appendicitis suddenly says the pain is gone, this is not good news. The appendix likely burst and released the pressure. Within hours you will see high fever, a diffusely tender or rigid belly, and peritonitis. Notify the surgeon at once.

The axis that separates them

The pain march and rebound. Appendicitis walks the pain from the navel to the right lower quadrant, then adds rebound and anorexia. Gastroenteritis stays diffuse with diarrhea. A UTI stays low and burning.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Three things you never do pre-op
No heat on the belly, no enemas, no laxatives. Each raises the risk of rupture. Hold pain medication until the surgeon has evaluated, since it can mask the exam. Once the diagnosis is set, analgesia is appropriate.
Bottom Line: Periumbilical pain moves to the right lower quadrant, plus anorexia and rebound. No heat, no enema, no laxative pre-op. Sudden relief means rupture.
07

Pediatric GI assessment

A child's GI exam is not a smaller adult exam. Kids cannot always describe pain. They lose fluid fast and crash late. The exam needs trust and play.

Hydration, the priority

  • Weight is the number one indicator. Compare to the last well-child visit. A 10 percent loss is severe dehydration
  • Mucous membranes: tacky or dry means dehydration
  • Anterior fontanelle in infants under 18 months: sunken means dehydration, bulging means raised pressure
  • Skin turgor: slow tenting over the abdomen
  • Capillary refill over 2 seconds
  • Tears: absent when crying, in a child over 6 months
  • Urine output: fewer than one wet diaper in 6 hours suggests dehydration. Weigh diapers: 1 gram equals 1 milliliter
  • Vital signs: tachycardia is the earliest sign of volume loss. Hypotension is late and ominous

Pain

  • FLACC scale for pre-verbal or non-verbal children
  • Wong-Baker Faces for roughly ages 3 to 7
  • Numeric 0 to 10 for older children
  • Ask character, location, radiation, timing, and what changes it. Location often shifts, classically in appendicitis
Exam order matters

Inspect, then auscultate, then percuss, then palpate. Palpation changes bowel sounds. Palpate the painful area last. Use warm hands, distraction, and the parent's lap.

Characterize the vomiting

This drives the diagnosis more than any single sign. Sort every vomit before you move on.

Type Description Think of
Non-bilious projectile Forceful, shoots across the room, no bile Pyloric stenosis
Bilious (green) Contains bile, so the block is below the ampulla of Vater Malrotation or volvulus, late intussusception, bowel obstruction
Feculent Stool-like odor or look Distal bowel obstruction, Hirschsprung
Spit-up Small, effortless, no distress Physiologic reflux, overfeeding

Stabilize before the operating room

Run this order for every one of the four. Assessment and resuscitation come before the scalpel.

  1. 1
    Airway, breathing, circulation. Check perfusion, mental status, and vital signs. Tachycardia before hypotension.
  2. 2
    NPO. Nothing by mouth once obstruction or surgery is on the table.
  3. 3
    IV access and fluids. One reliable line, two if unstable. Bolus normal saline for dehydration.
  4. 4
    Fix the electrolytes. Correct the imbalance before anesthesia, above all in pyloric stenosis.
  5. 5
    NG decompression. Pass a tube for marked distension or persistent vomiting.
Bottom Line: Weigh the child, weigh the diapers, name the vomit. Tachycardia before hypotension. Warm hands, painful area last, resuscitate before you operate.
08

Medications and fluids

You do not need long mechanisms. You need what to give, what to hold, and why.

Drug or fluid Use Dose and what to watch
Normal saline bolus Dehydration, first-line 20 mL/kg over 20 to 30 minutes, repeat as needed
Maintenance fluid Ongoing hydration D5 half-normal saline. Add potassium only after urine output is confirmed
Antibiotics Appendicitis, enterocolitis Ceftriaxone plus metronidazole, or piperacillin-tazobactam
Ondansetron Nausea and vomiting 0.15 mg/kg. Watch for QT prolongation
Acetaminophen, ibuprofen Pain and fever Acetaminophen 15 mg/kg; ibuprofen 10 mg/kg if not NPO and kidneys are fine
Morphine Post-op or severe pain 0.05 to 0.1 mg/kg IV. Monitor respiratory rate and sedation
Do not give these
  • No heat, no enemas, no laxatives in suspected appendicitis; each raises rupture risk
  • No pre-op pain medication until the surgeon has evaluated
  • No IV potassium before urine output is confirmed
  • No metoclopramide in young children; it can cause extrapyramidal reactions
  • No aspirin under age 19 unless specifically indicated
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Fluids first saves lives
A dehydrated child can crash on induction. Anesthesia drops blood pressure, and a child already 10 percent volume-depleted has no reserve. That is why pyloric stenosis is fluid-corrected before surgery. The operation is definitive; the fluids are lifesaving.
Bottom Line: Bolus 20 mL/kg. Potassium only once voiding. Ondansetron for nausea, broad-spectrum antibiotics for appendicitis and enterocolitis. No metoclopramide, no pre-op heat or enemas.
09

Priority nursing care

The pre-op work is the same for all four. Stabilize, monitor, and keep the family close.

  1. 1
    NPO and IV access. Hold all feeds. Place one line, two for an unstable child.
  2. 2
    IV fluids. Give maintenance plus replacement of deficits.
  3. 3
    NG decompression. Use it for distension or persistent vomiting.
  4. 4
    Strict intake and output. Weigh diapers, take daily weights on the same scale, check vital signs at least every 4 hours.
  5. 5
    Pain and family. Assess pain with an age tool, manage once the diagnosis is set, and keep a parent present.

Post-op care

  • Check airway, breathing, and circulation on return from the operating room
  • Watch the incision for redness, drainage, swelling, or bleeding
  • Give scheduled analgesia; add ice packs and comfort positioning
  • Advance the diet as ordered, from clear fluids toward regular food
  • Get the child moving early to prevent ileus and pneumonia
  • Watch for post-op ileus, wound infection, dehiscence, or an anastomotic leak

Condition-specific pearls

Pyloric stenosis: feed small volumes, upright, with frequent burping. Some vomiting for a day or two is expected.

Intussusception: after reduction, watch for a normal brown stool and for recurrence.

Hirschsprung: watch for enterocolitis before and after surgery. Teach colostomy care if one was placed.

Appendicitis: a perforated appendix means longer antibiotics and slower recovery. Watch for an abscess.

Bottom Line: NPO, IV fluids, pain control, accurate intake and output, incision care, and family support. Then watch each condition's own complication.
10

Family education and discharge

Parents leave with the child and the plan. Teach the red flags in words they can act on.

General discharge teaching

  • Incision care: keep it clean and dry; watch for redness, swelling, drainage, or fever
  • Activity: no strenuous play, swimming, or tub baths until cleared, usually 1 to 2 weeks
  • Pain: scheduled acetaminophen with or without ibuprofen for the first day or two. No aspirin
  • Diet: start with clear fluids and advance as tolerated. Expect some nausea early
  • Bowel function: stool patterns shift after surgery and normalize over days to weeks

Condition-specific teaching

Pyloric stenosis

  • Post-op vomiting for a day or two is normal, not a failure
  • Feed small volumes, upright, and burp often
  • Call for projectile or blood-tinged vomiting, or signs of dehydration

Intussusception

  • Recurrence can happen within 24 to 72 hours. Return for severe crampy knees-to-chest episodes
  • Currant-jelly stool means return to the emergency department now

Hirschsprung

  • Enterocolitis warning signs: fever, explosive foul diarrhea, distension, lethargy. Seek care at once
  • Teach colostomy care if one was placed: skin checks, appliance changes, diet
  • Some children keep having constipation or soiling after the pull-through

Appendicitis

  • Return to school on the surgeon's timeline, usually 1 to 2 weeks for laparoscopic repair
  • Watch for an abscess: persistent fever, worsening pain, foul drainage
  • Finish the full antibiotic course even after the child feels well
Include the child

Older children do better when they hear the plan too. Say it plainly: if it hurts more, not less, or if you feel hot, tell a grown-up right away. Show them the incision in a mirror when they are ready.

Bottom Line: Incision care, activity limits, and infection watch. Each condition has its own warning signs to teach. Include the child.
