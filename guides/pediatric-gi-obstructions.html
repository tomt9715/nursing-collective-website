Pediatric GI Obstructions

Pyloric stenosis, intussusception, Hirschsprung's, and appendicitis — four pediatric GI emergencies that all present with vomiting and abdominal pain. Tell them apart, and you've earned your rotation.

10 Sections 18 Quiz Questions ~22 Min Read

Learning Objectives

Differentiate pyloric stenosis, intussusception, Hirschsprung's, and appendicitis by age, signs, and vomiting pattern.
Recognize the classic electrolyte pattern of pyloric stenosis and why fluid correction comes before surgery.
Identify the hallmark findings of intussusception and the role of the contrast enema in both diagnosis and treatment.
Understand delayed meconium passage as a red flag for Hirschsprung's and the risk of enterocolitis.
Identify the migration pattern of appendicitis pain and know the warning signs of perforation.
Apply priority nursing interventions: NPO, IV fluids, pain management, surgical prep, and family support.

High-Yield Summary

The fastest way to tell these four apart on a test.

  • Pyloric stenosis: 2–8 wk old, first-born boy. Projectile non-bilious vomiting, hungry again right after, olive-shaped RUQ mass, visible peristaltic waves.
  • Electrolytes in pyloric stenosis: hypochloremic, hypokalemic metabolic alkalosis from vomiting stomach acid. Correct fluid/electrolytes BEFORE surgery.
  • Intussusception: 3 mo–3 yr, peaks 5–9 months. Sudden severe intermittent pain, child draws knees to chest, "currant jelly" stools (late sign), sausage-shaped mass in RUQ.
  • Intussusception treatment: air or hydrostatic contrast enema is BOTH diagnostic AND therapeutic. Reduces 75–80% of cases non-surgically. Surgery if fails or signs of perforation.
  • Hirschsprung's: congenital absence of ganglion cells in distal colon. Failure to pass meconium within 48 hours of birth is the classic red flag. Older kids: chronic constipation, ribbon-like stools, FTT.
  • Hirschsprung's diagnosis: rectal biopsy is definitive (absent ganglion cells). Enterocolitis is the deadly complication: fever, explosive diarrhea, shock.
  • Appendicitis: school-age to adolescent. Periumbilical pain migrating to RLQ (McBurney's point), rebound tenderness, fever, anorexia, N/V.
  • Appendicitis rupture red flag: sudden pain relief followed by high fever and diffuse tenderness = perforation. This is a surgical emergency.
  • Pre-op for appendicitis: NPO, IV fluids, antibiotics. NO heat, NO enemas, NO laxatives — increases perforation risk.
  • Pediatric fluid & electrolyte mantra: Weight is the #1 indicator of hydration status. Weigh diapers (1 g = 1 mL). Start D5 1/2 NS; add potassium only once urine output confirmed.

Pediatric GI Assessment

What's different from adults — and what always matters

Assessing a child's GI complaint is not a smaller version of adult assessment. Kids can't always describe pain, they decompensate fast from fluid loss, and their abdominal exam requires trust and play. A few principles change how you approach every pediatric GI patient.

Hydration Assessment (the priority)

  • Weight is the #1 indicator — compare to the most recent well-child visit. A 10% weight loss = severe dehydration.
  • Mucous membranes (tongue, gums) — tacky or dry = dehydration.
  • Anterior fontanelle (infants <18 months) — sunken = dehydration, bulging = increased ICP.
  • Skin turgor — slow tenting over the abdomen in dehydration.
  • Cap refill >2 seconds.
  • Tears when crying — absent in dehydration over 6 months of age.
  • Urine output — fewer than 1 wet diaper / 6 hours suggests dehydration. Weigh diapers: 1 g = 1 mL.
  • Vital signs — tachycardia is usually the EARLIEST sign of volume depletion. Hypotension is a LATE and ominous sign in children.

Pain Assessment

  • FLACC scale (Face, Legs, Activity, Cry, Consolability) for non-verbal or pre-verbal children.
  • Wong-Baker Faces (ages ~3–7).
  • Numerical scale 0–10 for older children.
  • Ask about character, location, radiation, timing, what makes it better/worse. Localization of pain often shifts in pediatric GI disorders (classic in appendicitis).

Abdominal Exam

Order Matters

Inspect → auscultate → percuss → palpate. Palpation changes bowel sounds. Palpate the painful area LAST. Use warm hands, distraction, and the parent's lap when possible. In a young child, use the "two-hand palpation" — one hand on yours for trust, the other doing the assessment.

Vomiting Characterization (this drives the diagnosis)

Type Description Think of
Non-bilious projectile Forceful, shoots across the room, no bile Pyloric stenosis
Bilious (green) Contains bile — indicates obstruction below the ampulla of Vater Malrotation/volvulus, intussusception (late), bowel obstruction
Feculent Stool-like odor or appearance Distal bowel obstruction, Hirschsprung's
Spit-up / regurgitation Small, effortless, no distress Physiologic GER, overfeeding
🎯 Bottom Line: Weigh the child, weigh the diapers, characterize the vomiting. Tachycardia before hypotension. Warm hands, palpate painful area last.

Pyloric Stenosis

The "hungry vomiter" — 2 to 8 weeks old

Hypertrophic Pyloric Stenosis

Outlet Obstruction

Pathophysiology

The pylorus (outlet between stomach and duodenum) becomes abnormally thickened, narrowing the channel. Stomach contents build up and eventually force their way back up through the esophagus.

Who Gets It

  • Age: 2–8 weeks old (rare outside this window)
  • Sex: 4–6× more common in boys, especially first-born
  • Family history is common

Signs & Symptoms

  • Projectile non-bilious vomiting after feeds — shoots 2–3 feet
  • Hungry immediately after vomiting ("the hungry vomiter")
  • Palpable "olive" mass in the right upper quadrant / epigastrium after feeding
  • Visible peristaltic waves moving across the epigastrium
  • Failure to thrive / weight loss or poor weight gain
  • Dehydration: sunken fontanelle, poor skin turgor, decreased urine output

Classic Lab Pattern

Persistent vomiting of stomach acid (HCl) drives a predictable disturbance:

  • Hypochloremic metabolic alkalosis — low Cl-, high pH, high HCO3-
  • Hypokalemia — the kidneys waste K+ as they try to reabsorb H+
  • Hyponatremia may develop with prolonged vomiting

Diagnosis

  • Ultrasound is first-line — shows thickened, elongated pylorus ("string sign" if barium is used)
  • Physical exam: olive mass, peristaltic waves

Treatment

  • Step 1: Correct the electrolyte and fluid imbalance FIRST — cannot safely induce anesthesia in an alkalotic, dehydrated infant
  • Step 2: Pyloromyotomy (Ramstedt procedure) — laparoscopic or open incision splits the thickened pyloric muscle
  • Post-op feeding typically begins within 4–6 hours with clear fluids advancing to formula/breast milk by 24 hours
NCLEX Pattern to Memorize

"2–8 week old boy with projectile vomiting who is hungry right after" = pyloric stenosis. If the lab panel shows alkalosis + low K + low Cl, that nails it. The distractor is always "rush to surgery" — but the correct answer is always fluid and electrolyte correction first.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Why the "Hungry Vomiter" Detail Matters
A baby with gastroenteritis, formula intolerance, or GERD doesn't want to eat after vomiting — they feel sick. The pyloric stenosis baby does the opposite: they vomit and immediately cry for the next feeding, because the stomach is empty and they're not nauseated — just obstructed. That single detail is worth a diagnosis on NCLEX.
🎯 Bottom Line: First-born baby boy, 2–8 weeks old, projectile non-bilious vomiting, hungry after. Alkalosis + low K + low Cl. Fluids first, surgery second.

Intussusception

Telescoping bowel in a screaming infant

Intussusception

Telescoping Bowel

Pathophysiology

One segment of bowel telescopes into the segment directly distal to it — like pushing a sock into itself. Most commonly the terminal ileum invaginates into the cecum (ileocecal). The telescoped segment compresses mesenteric vessels, causing edema, ischemia, and eventually bowel necrosis.

Who Gets It

  • Age: 3 months to 3 years — peak incidence 5–9 months
  • More common in boys
  • Usually idiopathic; older children may have a "lead point" (Meckel's diverticulum, polyp, lymphoma)
  • Recent viral illness is a common antecedent (hypertrophied Peyer's patches)

Signs & Symptoms

Sudden, severe, intermittent abdominal pain — comes in waves every 15–20 min.

Child draws knees to chest and screams during attacks, appears normal between.

Vomiting (non-bilious early, bilious as obstruction progresses).

"Currant jelly" stools — blood and mucus mixed together. LATE sign = bowel ischemia.

Sausage-shaped mass palpable in the right upper quadrant.

Lethargy between attacks or shock-like appearance as disease progresses.

Diagnosis

  • Ultrasound first-line — "target" or "bulls-eye" sign
  • Contrast (air or hydrostatic) enema — confirms diagnosis and often fixes it
  • Abdominal X-ray may show bowel obstruction or a soft tissue mass

Treatment

  • Air or hydrostatic contrast enema — reduces 75–80% of cases non-surgically
  • Surgical reduction if enema fails, if there are signs of perforation or peritonitis, or if ischemic bowel needs resection
  • IV fluids, NG decompression, NPO pre-procedure
A Normal Stool After Enema = Possible Reduction

After a contrast enema, the passing of a normal brown stool is a sign the intussusception may have reduced. Report it right away. Also watch for signs of recurrence (up to 10% recur within 24 hours) — children are usually admitted for observation after reduction.

🎯 Bottom Line: 5–9 month old, severe intermittent pain (knees to chest), currant-jelly stool (late), RUQ sausage mass. Enema diagnoses AND treats most cases.

Hirschsprung's Disease

The baby who can't poop — congenital aganglionosis

Hirschsprung's Disease (Aganglionic Megacolon)

Functional Obstruction

Pathophysiology

Congenital absence of ganglion cells (nerve cells that control peristalsis) in a segment of the distal colon. The aganglionic segment cannot relax — it's in a state of permanent spasm, creating a functional obstruction. Stool accumulates proximal to the affected segment, and the colon dilates (megacolon).

Who Gets It

  • Typically diagnosed in the newborn period
  • More common in males (4:1)
  • Association with Down syndrome — check bowel function in all trisomy 21 newborns

Signs & Symptoms in the Newborn

  • Failure to pass meconium within 48 hours of birth — the classic red flag
  • Abdominal distension
  • Bilious vomiting
  • Feeding difficulty, poor weight gain

Signs in the Older Child

  • Chronic constipation since infancy
  • Ribbon-like stools (forced through a narrow spastic segment)
  • Abdominal distension, palpable stool in the abdomen
  • Failure to thrive, poor appetite
  • Foul-smelling breath and/or stool

Diagnosis

  • Rectal biopsy is definitive — shows absence of ganglion cells in the submucosa
  • Contrast enema — shows a narrow distal segment and a dilated proximal segment ("transition zone")
  • Anorectal manometry — failure of internal anal sphincter to relax

Treatment

  • Surgical resection of the aganglionic segment with a pull-through procedure (Soave, Swenson, or Duhamel)
  • A temporary colostomy may be placed initially in severe cases
  • Definitive pull-through typically in infancy (3–6 months or when the child is stable)
Enterocolitis — The Deadly Complication

Hirschsprung-associated enterocolitis (HAEC) is life-threatening. Bacteria proliferate in the stagnant bowel, causing severe inflammation. Signs: fever, explosive foul-smelling diarrhea, abdominal distension, lethargy, signs of shock. Can occur pre- or post-operatively. Treatment: IV fluids, broad-spectrum antibiotics, rectal decompression, NPO.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The 48-Hour Meconium Rule
99% of healthy term newborns pass meconium within 24 hours; nearly all do by 48 hours. If a newborn hasn't pooped by the 48-hour mark, red flags go up — Hirschsprung's is near the top of the differential, along with cystic fibrosis (meconium ileus) and imperforate anus. This is why nurses document the first stool so carefully.
🎯 Bottom Line: No meconium in 48 hours OR chronic constipation with ribbon stools. Rectal biopsy confirms. Surgery is the fix. Enterocolitis is the killer — fever + explosive diarrhea + shock.

Appendicitis

Migrating pain — and the silent danger of sudden relief

Appendicitis

Inflammation / Surgical

Pathophysiology

Obstruction of the appendiceal lumen (by fecalith, lymphoid hyperplasia, foreign body) leads to bacterial overgrowth, mucosal edema, ischemia, and ultimately perforation if untreated. The pain pattern reflects the inflammation's spread — starting as visceral (central, vague) and becoming parietal (localized, sharp) once the inflamed appendix irritates the parietal peritoneum.

Who Gets It

  • Most common in school-age children and adolescents (peak 10–19)
  • Rare in children <2 years (but when it happens, it's often perforated by diagnosis)
  • Slight male predominance

Classic Signs & Symptoms

  • Periumbilical pain migrating to the RLQ (McBurney's point — 1/3 of the way from the ASIS to the umbilicus)
  • Anorexia — very consistent finding
  • Nausea and vomiting (typically AFTER pain begins)
  • Low-grade fever (38–38.5 °C); high fever suggests perforation
  • Rebound tenderness at McBurney's point
  • Guarding (voluntary contraction of abdominal muscles)
  • Difficulty walking, avoids jumping/bouncing

Classic Peritoneal Signs

  • Rovsing's sign: palpation of the LLQ causes pain in the RLQ
  • Psoas sign: pain with hip extension (retrocecal appendix irritates psoas muscle)
  • Obturator sign: pain with internal rotation of the flexed right hip (pelvic appendix)
  • Blumberg's sign: rebound tenderness — pain when pressure is released

Diagnosis

  • Clinical exam + elevated WBC with left shift, elevated CRP
  • Ultrasound preferred in children (no radiation)
  • CT if ultrasound is non-diagnostic

Treatment

  • Appendectomy (laparoscopic preferred) — within 24 hours of diagnosis
  • IV antibiotics pre- and post-op (especially if perforated)
  • IV fluids, NPO, pain management
Sudden Pain Relief = Perforation

If the child with classic appendicitis symptoms suddenly reports their pain is GONE, this is NOT good news. The appendix has likely perforated, releasing pressure — but within hours, bacteria will spread and the child will develop high fever, diffuse abdominal pain, rigidity, and signs of peritonitis. Notify the surgeon immediately.

What NOT to Do Pre-Op

Do not apply heat to the abdomen — it can cause the appendix to rupture. Do not give enemas or laxatives — same reason. Do not give pain medication before the surgeon has evaluated — masking the exam delays diagnosis. (Once admitted and diagnosis confirmed, analgesia is appropriate.)

🎯 Bottom Line: Periumbilical pain → RLQ (McBurney's) + anorexia + rebound tenderness. No heat, no enemas, no laxatives pre-op. Sudden relief = rupture.

Labs & Diagnostic Tests

Knowing what each test tells you
Test Purpose Where It Matters Most
CBC WBC/left shift (infection/inflammation), Hgb (bleeding) Appendicitis, HAEC, any obstruction
BMP/CMP Electrolytes, glucose, BUN/Cr, acid-base Pyloric stenosis (alkalosis), any dehydration
CRP Inflammation marker Appendicitis, Hirschsprung enterocolitis
Urinalysis Rule out UTI (can mimic appendicitis) Appendicitis workup
Abdominal X-ray Air-fluid levels, free air, bowel gas pattern Any bowel obstruction; initial survey
Ultrasound First-line in pediatric GI — no radiation Pyloric stenosis, intussusception, appendicitis
Contrast enema Dx + Tx intussusception; transition zone in Hirschsprung's Intussusception (75–80% reduced), Hirschsprung's
Rectal biopsy Definitive dx for Hirschsprung's (absent ganglion cells) Hirschsprung's
CT abdomen Reserved for complex/unclear cases Appendicitis if US non-diagnostic
Upper GI series Visualizes GI tract flow Malrotation, pyloric stenosis (historical)
Prefer Ultrasound in Kids

Children are more sensitive to ionizing radiation than adults. Ultrasound is the go-to first imaging test for nearly every pediatric GI complaint — it's fast, radiation-free, and operator-dependent but highly accurate in trained hands. CT is reserved for when ultrasound is non-diagnostic or when surgical planning requires it.

🎯 Bottom Line: Start with ultrasound. CBC + BMP + CRP cover the labs. Contrast enema does double duty in intussusception. Rectal biopsy is the gold standard for Hirschsprung's.

Priority Nursing Care

Pre-op, post-op, and safety

Pre-Op / Admission Care (All Four Disorders)

  • NPO status — maintain strictly; clarify with surgery team when fluids can resume
  • IV access — one reliable line minimum; two for unstable children
  • IV fluids — maintenance plus replacement of deficits; see Section 8
  • NG tube for decompression if significant distension or persistent vomiting
  • Strict I&O — weigh diapers, record every intake and output
  • Daily weights on the same scale at the same time
  • VS q4h minimum, more often if unstable — watch for tachycardia, decreased BP (late), fever, tachypnea
  • Pain assessment with age-appropriate tool; manage once diagnosis is confirmed
  • Parent support and involvement — kids do better with a parent present; preserve bonding

Post-Op Care

  • Airway, breathing, circulation checks on return from OR; monitor for post-anesthesia complications
  • Incision care — observe for redness, drainage, swelling, bleeding. Laparoscopic incisions are small but can still dehisce
  • Pain management — scheduled analgesia initially (acetaminophen ± ibuprofen ± low-dose opioid); ice packs; comfort positioning
  • Advance diet as ordered — typically clear fluids → full fluids → soft food → regular. Slower for pyloromyotomy (small volumes, upright feeding)
  • Ambulation as soon as safe — reduces post-op ileus and pneumonia risk
  • Assess for complications: post-op ileus (absent bowel sounds, distension), surgical site infection, dehiscence, anastomotic leak (bilious drainage, fever, peritonitis signs)

Condition-Specific Pearls

Pyloric stenosis: post-op, feed in small volumes, upright, with frequent burping. Expect some vomiting the first 24–48 hours — usually resolves.

Intussusception: after enema reduction, watch for the passage of a normal brown stool (sign of success) and for recurrence (up to 10%).

Hirschsprung's: monitor for enterocolitis (fever, explosive diarrhea, distension) pre- AND post-op. Teach colostomy care if one was placed.

Appendicitis: if perforated, expect longer antibiotic course, possible drain, slower recovery. Monitor for abscess formation.

🎯 Bottom Line: NPO + IV fluids + pain + accurate I&O + incision care + emotional support for the family. Watch for complications specific to each disorder.

Medications & Fluids

What to give, what to avoid, why
IV Fluid Resuscitation
First-Line for Dehydration

Bolus: Normal saline 20 mL/kg IV over 20–30 minutes; repeat as needed.

Maintenance: Typically D5 1/2 NS at Holliday-Segar rate.

Potassium: Do NOT add K+ until urine output is confirmed — adequate kidneys protect against hyperkalemia.

IV Antibiotics (Appendicitis)
Pre-Op / Perforation Tx

Broad-spectrum coverage for gut flora: typically ceftriaxone + metronidazole or piperacillin-tazobactam.

Simple appendicitis: 24 hours of antibiotics.

Perforated appendicitis: 5–7 days of IV antibiotics, switch to oral to complete course.

Antiemetics
Symptom Control

Ondansetron (Zofran): preferred in children. 0.15 mg/kg IV or ODT.

Monitor for QT prolongation (check baseline EKG if at risk).

Avoid metoclopramide in young children — risk of extrapyramidal symptoms (dystonic reactions).

Analgesics
Pain Management

Acetaminophen 15 mg/kg q4–6h, ibuprofen 10 mg/kg q6–8h (if not NPO and kidneys OK).

Opioids (morphine 0.05–0.1 mg/kg IV) for post-op or severe pain — monitor respiratory rate and sedation.

Pre-op pain med AFTER surgical evaluation, not before.

Antibiotics (Enterocolitis)
Hirschsprung HAEC

Broad-spectrum IV coverage (often metronidazole + ceftriaxone or piperacillin-tazobactam).

Rectal decompression with saline irrigations.

NPO, IV fluids, close monitoring for shock.

What to Avoid
Do NOT Give

No heat, no enemas, no laxatives in suspected appendicitis — rupture risk.

No metoclopramide in young kids — EPS.

No potassium IV before urine output confirmed.

No aspirin in children <19 unless specifically indicated (Kawasaki exception).

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Fluids First Saves Lives
Dehydrated children die in the OR if you don't fix fluids first. Anesthesia drops blood pressure, and a child who's already 10% volume-depleted can crash on induction. That's why pyloric stenosis is fluid-corrected BEFORE surgery — surgery is definitive, but fluids are lifesaving. This principle applies to every pediatric GI emergency.
🎯 Bottom Line: Fluids first, potassium once voiding, Zofran for nausea, broad-spectrum antibiotics for appendicitis/HAEC. Avoid metoclopramide and pre-op heat/enemas.

Family Education & Discharge

What parents need to know when they leave the hospital

General Discharge Teaching

  • Incision care: keep clean and dry, watch for redness, swelling, drainage, or fever.
  • Activity: no strenuous play, swimming, or tub baths until cleared (typically 1–2 weeks). Laparoscopic incisions usually heal faster than open.
  • Pain management: give scheduled acetaminophen ± ibuprofen for the first 24–48 hours. Avoid aspirin in children.
  • Diet advancement: start with clear fluids, advance as tolerated. Expect some nausea in the first day or two.
  • Bowel function: expect changes in stool pattern post-op; normal regularity returns over days to weeks.

Condition-Specific Teaching

Pyloric Stenosis

  • Post-op vomiting is common for 1–2 days — not a sign of failure.
  • Feed small volumes (1–2 oz), upright, burp frequently.
  • Call if vomiting is projectile, blood-tinged, or if baby becomes dehydrated.

Intussusception

  • Recurrence can happen within 24–72 hours (up to 10%). Return to ED for severe crampy pain with knees-to-chest episodes.
  • Currant-jelly stool = call 911 / return to ED.

Hirschsprung's

  • Enterocolitis warning signs: fever, explosive foul diarrhea, abdominal distension, lethargy — immediate ED evaluation.
  • Colostomy care (if placed): skin assessment, appliance changes, dietary guidance.
  • Long-term follow-up: some children have ongoing constipation or soiling issues even after pull-through.

Appendicitis

  • Return to school per surgeon (usually 1–2 weeks for laparoscopic; 2–3 for open/perforated).
  • Watch for abscess: persistent fever, worsening abdominal pain, foul drainage.
  • Complete the full antibiotic course even if the child feels better.
Include the Child in Teaching

Older children benefit from hearing the plan too. Explain incision care and red flags in terms they can understand: "If it hurts more, not less, or if you feel hot, tell a grown-up right away." Empowering them reduces fear and improves follow-through. Show them the incision in a mirror once they're ready; demystify the surgery.

🎯 Bottom Line: Incision care, activity restriction, watch for infection. Each condition has its own warning signs to teach. Include the child.
