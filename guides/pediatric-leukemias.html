Pediatric Leukemias

Two cell lines to tell apart, three cytopenias that explain every sign, and two emergencies that decide your first move: neutropenic fever and tumor lysis.

The Nursing Collective

By the end, without notes

  • Say whether a smear points to ALL or AML, and name the finding that told you
  • Map each of the three cytopenias to the sign it makes at the bedside
  • Treat a fever in a neutropenic child as the emergency it is
  • Spot tumor lysis from four electrolyte moves, and say what you do first

01

What actually gets you

Pediatric leukemia is not a memory test. It is a small set of discriminations, and the questions live there.

The marrow fills with blasts. Normal blood cells get crowded out. Almost every sign traces back to that one event.

Points get lost here in three predictable ways. Read these first.

You learned three cytopenias as a list

Then a scenario hands you a pale, bruised, feverish child. You cannot say which low count drives which sign. They are not one list. The marrow crowds out three lines. Low red cells give pallor and fatigue. Low platelets give bruising, petechiae, and bleeding. Low neutrophils give infection.

You treated a neutropenic fever as a routine fever

It is not routine. A single temperature of 100.4°F (38°C) in a neutropenic child is an emergency. Draw two blood cultures. Give broad-spectrum antibiotics within the hour. No rectal temperatures and no suppositories. Start neutropenic precautions.

You missed tumor lysis at induction

The first chemo doses rupture cells. Their contents spill into the blood. Potassium rises. Phosphorus rises. Uric acid rises. Calcium falls. You prevent it with aggressive hydration and allopurinol or rasburicase.

Bottom Line: Stop studying leukemia as one disease. Study it as a few splits: ALL or AML, which cytopenia drives the sign, a fever that is an emergency, and the electrolyte storm at induction.
02

The four things

For ALL, for AML, and for the two emergencies below, you should be able to say four things with the book shut. The one-sentence definition. The clue you would actually see. The priority action. The first-line treatment or teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Recognition is not recall.

Thing Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line treatment
ALL Lymphoid blasts crowd the marrow; the common childhood leukemia. Child 2 to 10, pallor, bruising, bone pain, big nodes. CBC, then bone marrow biopsy. Multi-drug chemo over 2 to 3 years; intrathecal methotrexate for the CNS.
AML Myeloid blasts crowd the marrow; more in adolescents and infants. Auer rods on the smear; gum infiltration; bleeding out of proportion. CBC and smear, then bone marrow biopsy. Intense chemo; transplant is often needed.
Neutropenic fever One fever in a child with too few neutrophils to fight it. Temp 100.4°F or higher with a known low ANC. Blood cultures, then antibiotics within the hour. Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics, given empirically.
Tumor lysis syndrome Dying cells dump their contents at induction. Potassium up, phosphorus up, uric acid up, calcium down. Hydrate hard; hold IV potassium. Allopurinol or rasburicase plus IV fluids.
Copy this down

Write those four rows on one index card, by hand. ALL is lymphoid. AML is myeloid. A neutropenic fever is cultures then antibiotics within the hour. Tumor lysis is up, up, up, down. That card is most of the exam.

03

What leukemia actually is

Leukemia is a malignant overgrowth of white cell precursors, called blasts, in the bone marrow. It is the most common childhood cancer. It makes up about a third of pediatric malignancies.

The marrow normally builds two lines. The lymphoid line makes B and T cells. The myeloid line makes granulocytes, monocytes, red cells, and platelets.

In leukemia one line turns malignant. The cells stop maturing. They pile up as blasts. The blasts crowd the normal lines, so red cells, platelets, and neutrophils all fall. That is pancytopenia. The blasts also spread to the liver, spleen, lymph nodes, CNS, and testes.

Risk factors

Genetic syndromes: Down syndrome (10 to 20 times the risk), Li-Fraumeni, Fanconi anemia, neurofibromatosis.
Previous chemo or radiation. Secondary AML is a known risk.
An identical twin with leukemia. About 25% concordance.
In utero radiation and rare chemical exposures such as benzene.
Most cases have no identifiable cause. Tell the family this plainly.
Sex: boys carry a slightly higher ALL risk; AML risk is about equal.
Parents often blame themselves

Families hearing this diagnosis often ask, "Did we cause this?" Answer firmly. The vast majority of childhood leukemias have no identifiable environmental cause. Something went wrong in the DNA of a single cell. It was not a vaccine, a phone, or a missed meal. Saying so is part of first-day care.

Bottom Line: ALL is lymphoid. AML is myeloid. Either way, blasts crowd the marrow and the counts fall.
04

ALL or AML

Discrimination axis: which cell line went malignant.

This is the split the questions start from. The cell line drives the regimen, the prognosis, and whether transplant is likely.

ALL: acute lymphoblastic
  • Cell line: lymphoid, B or T precursor.
  • Frequency: about 75% of pediatric leukemias.
  • Peak age: 2 to 10 years, highest at 2 to 5.
  • Subtypes: pre-B is most common (about 85%, better prognosis); T-cell (older boys, may bring a mediastinal mass); mature B-cell, Burkitt-type (aggressive but highly curable).
  • CNS involvement is common, so intrathecal chemo is given as prophylaxis.
  • Prognosis: over 90% five-year survival.
AML: acute myelogenous
  • Cell line: myeloid precursors.
  • Frequency: about 20% of pediatric leukemias.
  • Peak age: any age, more in adolescents and infants under 2.
  • Subtypes: M0 to M7, eight in the FAB system. M3 is acute promyelocytic (APL), carries DIC risk, responds to ATRA. M5 is monoblastic, gives gum infiltration and skin lesions. M7 is megakaryoblastic, tied to Down syndrome in infants.
  • Higher relapse risk; transplant is often needed.
  • Prognosis: about 65 to 70% five-year survival.
Auer rods mean AML

Auer rods are needle-shaped inclusions inside the blast cytoplasm. They are pathognomonic for AML, especially the M3 subtype. See them in a stem, and the answer is AML, not ALL.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Auer Rods = AML
One finding settles the split on sight. Auer rods live only in myeloid blasts. If the smear shows Auer rods, pick AML. Lymphoblasts alone point to ALL.

Chronic leukemias, rare in kids

  • Chronic myelogenous leukemia (CML): rare; Philadelphia chromosome, t(9;22), positive. Treated with tyrosine kinase inhibitors such as imatinib.
  • Juvenile myelomonocytic leukemia (JMML): rare, aggressive, in infants and toddlers; usually needs transplant.

Bottom Line: ALL is B-cell or T-cell with the best prognosis. AML runs M0 to M7, and Auer rods mean AML. Down syndrome points to M7. CML carries the Philadelphia chromosome.
05

The three cytopenias, read as signs

Discrimination axis: which low count is driving what you see.

Children present with the results of marrow failure, plus signs of organ infiltration. Symptoms build over weeks. Many kids are first worked up for "another virus that will not quit" or for odd bruising.

Do not memorize the signs as one block. Map each low count to what it does. Then the scenario sorts itself.

Cell line lowWhat fallsWhat you see
Red cells Anemia Pallor, fatigue, shortness of breath on exertion, tachycardia, poor feeding in infants, pale mucous membranes
Platelets Thrombocytopenia Petechiae, purpura, ecchymoses, epistaxis, gum bleeding, prolonged bleeding from small injuries
Neutrophils Neutropenia, even when total WBC is high Fever, recurrent infections, sepsis, mouth ulcers, skin infections that will not heal

Organ infiltration

Bone pain, often legs or back; the child may limp, refuse to walk, or wake at night. Classic in ALL.
Lymphadenopathy: painless, firm, enlarged nodes.
Hepatosplenomegaly: palpable, with abdominal fullness and early satiety.
CNS: headache, vomiting, cranial nerve palsies, papilledema. More common in ALL.
Testicular enlargement: painless; a sanctuary site in ALL.
"B symptoms": fever, unintentional weight loss, drenching night sweats.

Clues by type

  • T-cell ALL: a mediastinal mass. It can compress the SVC and airway, giving facial swelling, stridor, and dyspnea. That is a respiratory emergency.
  • AML M3 (APL): bleeding out of proportion to the platelet count. Suspect DIC.
  • AML M5 (monoblastic): gum hyperplasia and skin nodules, called leukemia cutis.
  • Infant leukemia: often a high WBC, hepatosplenomegaly, and CNS disease.
Pallor, bruising, and bone pain together

One trio should trigger a CBC. Pallor and fatigue from anemia. Petechiae or odd bruising from low platelets. Bone pain or a limp. A child with all three has leukemia until proven otherwise. Other tip-offs: a fever with no source, unexplained big nodes, or early-morning bone pain that wakes the child.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Limping Toddler
A toddler who suddenly will not walk, or wakes crying about leg pain, is not just having growing pains. The differential includes septic joint, osteomyelitis, arthritis, and leukemia. These kids are often pale and bruising more than usual. Check vitals, look at the mucous membranes, and palpate the nodes. The CBC shows the diagnosis.
Bottom Line: Marrow failure is anemia plus bleeding plus infection. Add bone pain, big nodes, and hepatosplenomegaly. T-cell ALL brings a mediastinal mass. AML M3 brings DIC.
06

The workup, in order

CBC, then smear, then bone marrow, then LP.

The diagnosis starts with a CBC and ends with a bone marrow biopsy. Most of it happens in the first 48 to 72 hours. It sets the whole treatment plan.

Initial labs

TestTypical findingWhy you get it
CBC with diffAnemia and low platelets; WBC may be low, normal, or very high (> 100,000)First clue; may show blasts
Peripheral smearBlasts present; Auer rods suggest AMLSee the blast shape
Chemistry, phosphorus, uric acidMay already show a tumor lysis patternJudge tumor lysis risk
LDHElevated from high cell turnoverMarker of disease activity
Coags: PT, PTT, fibrinogen, D-dimerDIC is possible, especially AML M3Rule out DIC before procedures
Chest x-rayMediastinal mass in T-cell ALLCheck airway and SVC risk

Bone marrow biopsy, the gold standard

  • Usually aspirated from the posterior iliac crest under sedation.
  • Over 20% blasts in the marrow makes the diagnosis of acute leukemia. Written out, that is > 20%.
  • Sent for morphology, flow cytometry (the cell line), cytogenetics (chromosomes), and molecular testing.
  • Repeated after induction to confirm remission, which is under 5% blasts (< 5%).

Lumbar puncture for CNS status

  • Done at diagnosis to check for CNS leukemia.
  • Often paired with the first dose of intrathecal methotrexate as prophylaxis.
  • CNS-positive disease raises the risk group and intensifies therapy.
Positioning for the LP

Place the child on their side. Back arched, knees drawn to the chest, the fetal or C position, to open the spaces between vertebrae. In infants, hold a sitting flexed or side-lying position, and do not hyperflex the neck; it compromises the airway. Hold the child still. After intrathecal chemo, keep the child lying flat for several hours to spread the drug and reduce headache.

Staging is different here

Acute leukemia is not staged like a solid tumor. Risk is set instead by a few things.

  • Age and WBC at diagnosis.
  • Cell line and cytogenetics.
  • CNS or testicular involvement.
  • Minimal residual disease after induction.
Bottom Line: CBC with blasts, then smear, then bone marrow biopsy at over 20% blasts, then LP to check the CNS. Flow and cytogenetics steer the plan.
07

How it is treated

Three phases, intrathecal prophylaxis, and transplant for some.

Treatment is long. A child with ALL may be on therapy for 2 to 3 years. AML is more intense but shorter. Centers follow standardized Children's Oncology Group protocols.

The three phases

1. Induction, 4 to 6 weeks
  • Goal: complete remission, under 5% blasts (< 5%).
  • ALL drugs: vincristine, L-asparaginase, a corticosteroid (prednisone or dexamethasone), and an anthracycline (daunorubicin) in high-risk cases.
  • Watch for: tumor lysis in the first 48 to 72 hours, neutropenic fever, chemo reactions, mucositis.
2. Consolidation and intensification, months
  • Goal: clear residual disease, prevent relapse, block CNS spread.
  • Drugs: cyclophosphamide, cytarabine, methotrexate given IV and intrathecally; delayed intensification blocks.
  • Watch for: long cytopenias, serious infection, GI toxicity.
3. Maintenance, 2 to 3 years total in ALL
  • Goal: hold remission, keep blasts suppressed.
  • Drugs: oral 6-mercaptopurine daily and oral methotrexate weekly; periodic vincristine and steroid pulses; intermittent intrathecal methotrexate.
  • Watch for: adherence, because the schedule runs for years.
Transplant (HSCT), select cases
  • When: relapsed ALL, most high-risk AML, CML, JMML.
  • Types: allogeneic (a donor, ideally a matched sibling) or autologous. Conditioning uses high-dose chemo, sometimes with total body irradiation.
  • Watch for: graft-versus-host disease, opportunistic infections, delayed engraftment, long hospital stays.

Key chemo agents

Vincristine

A vinca alkaloid. Key toxicities: peripheral neuropathy, constipation, SIADH, jaw pain. IV only. It is fatal if given intrathecally, and that is a never event.

L-asparaginase

An enzyme that starves leukemic cells of asparagine. Watch for anaphylaxis, pancreatitis, hyperglycemia, and coagulopathy. Give IM or IV with epinephrine at the bedside.

Corticosteroids

Prednisone or dexamethasone. Cytotoxic to lymphoblasts and a backbone of ALL therapy. Side effects: hyperglycemia, mood changes, big appetite, hypertension, and avascular necrosis with long use.

Methotrexate

Given IV in high doses and intrathecally for CNS prophylaxis. Toxicities: mucositis, liver injury, kidney injury. Leucovorin rescue follows high-dose infusions.

6-mercaptopurine

Oral, daily, for years in maintenance. Adherence is critical. Give at bedtime, on an empty stomach, away from dairy.

Cytarabine (Ara-C)

The backbone of AML induction. Toxicities: deep cytopenias, mucositis, cerebellar effects at high doses, and Ara-C syndrome of fever, rash, and myalgia.

Anthracyclines

Daunorubicin and doxorubicin. Cardiotoxicity is dose-limiting, so get baseline and serial echocardiograms. They are vesicants, so a central line is preferred.

Cyclophosphamide

An alkylating agent used in consolidation and transplant conditioning. Hemorrhagic cystitis is the risk; prevent it with vigorous hydration and Mesna. Bladder cancer is a late effect.

Vincristine is IV only, never intrathecal

Intrathecal vincristine is uniformly fatal. Units use color-coded syringes, separate labeling, two-person checks, and timing that separates it from intrathecal chemo. Your part is simple. Read the label. Verify the route. Never accept a premixed syringe with no route on it.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Vincristine = IV Only
One route error kills. Vincristine goes IV and only IV. Intrathecal vincristine has no antidote and no survivors. Read the label, say the route out loud, and refuse any unlabeled syringe.

Radiation

  • Now reserved for CNS leukemia, testicular relapse, and transplant conditioning.
  • Late effects: growth failure, cognitive impairment, secondary cancers. That is why CNS radiation is minimized.

Newer therapies

  • Tyrosine kinase inhibitors (imatinib, dasatinib) for Philadelphia-positive ALL and CML.
  • Immunotherapy: blinatumomab (a CD19 bispecific), inotuzumab, and CAR-T therapy (tisagenlecleucel) for relapsed or refractory B-cell ALL.
  • ATRA (all-trans retinoic acid) for AML M3. It matures the promyelocytes and greatly improves outcomes. Watch for differentiation syndrome.
Bottom Line: Induction, then consolidation, then maintenance. ALL runs 2 to 3 years. AML is more intense and often needs transplant. Intrathecal methotrexate protects the CNS. Vincristine is IV only.
08

Neutropenic fever is not an ordinary fever

Discrimination axis: a fever with neutrophils versus a fever without them.

After chemo the white count drops. The child is functionally immunocompromised. The danger includes the child's own gut and skin flora. Neutropenic precautions guard against organisms we usually ignore.

Neutropenia by ANC

ANC (cells/mm³)CategoryWhat it means
> 1,500NormalBaseline
1,000 to 1,500MildSlightly higher risk
500 to 1,000ModerateReal infection risk
< 500SevereProfound risk; strict precautions
< 100ProfoundLife-threatening; single-organism sepsis is common
The ANC formula

ANC = WBC × (% neutrophils + % bands) ÷ 100. Example: a WBC of 2,000 with 20% neutrophils and 5% bands gives 2,000 × 25 ÷ 100, which is 500. That is severe neutropenia. Expect to calculate it, then act on the answer.

Precautions: what to do

Private room, ideally HEPA-filtered. Keep the door closed.
Strict handwashing by every person entering.
Healthy visitors only. No cough, fever, rash, or cold sores. Limit the number.
Daily oral care with a soft toothbrush or sponge. No flossing when platelets are low.
A low-microbial diet: cooked foods, no raw fruit or vegetables unless peeled, no deli meats, no unpasteurized dairy.
Daily skin checks, including the perianal area and the central line site.
Frequent vitals and temperatures. Parents report any fever at once.
Central line care per protocol, with hub scrubbing and the CLABSI bundle.

Precautions: what not to do

No rectal temperatures, rectal meds, or rectal exams. They tear mucosa and seed bacteremia.
No fresh flowers or live plants. Soil and standing water carry Aspergillus and Pseudomonas.
No raw, undercooked, or unpasteurized foods.
No live vaccines (MMR, varicella, rotavirus, nasal flu) for the child or household contacts.
No invasive procedures if avoidable: IM injections, catheterizations, dental work all raise bacteremia risk.
No aspirin or NSAIDs. They impair platelets and can mask fever. Use acetaminophen per the team.
No sick visitors, and no pets with open litter boxes.
No shared rooms. Skip the playroom until the ANC recovers.
Febrile neutropenia is an oncologic emergency

A single temperature of 100.4°F (38°C) in a neutropenic child is treated as sepsis. Inside 60 minutes: two blood cultures (peripheral and central lumen), a urine culture, CBC, CMP, lactate, and a chest x-ray if there are respiratory symptoms. Then broad-spectrum IV antibiotics, usually cefepime or piperacillin-tazobactam. Do not wait for the ANC. Treat empirically. Mortality climbs with every hour of delay.

Live vaccines and the household

Live-attenuated vaccines (MMR, varicella, rotavirus, intranasal flu, BCG, yellow fever) are not given to a child on chemo, nor to household contacts during active treatment. Inactivated vaccines (injectable flu, Tdap, pneumococcal) are allowed but may work less well. Siblings get the injectable flu shot, not the nasal spray.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Fever Now, Not Later
The whole section reduces to two habits. Any temperature of 100.4°F or higher gets cultures and antibiotics inside the hour, before the ANC even returns. And remember the four no's: no rectal temps, no flowers or plants, no live vaccines, no raw food. Everything else is just protecting an immune-compromised child.
Bottom Line: ANC under 500 is severe. A fever is an emergency, so cultures and antibiotics within the hour. No rectal temps, no flowers or plants, no live vaccines, no raw food.

The complications that decide your shift

Tumor lysis, mucositis, and transfusion reactions.

Tumor lysis syndrome

Chemo kills leukemic cells, and their contents pour into the blood. The storm hits in the first 24 to 72 hours of induction. Risk is highest with a high WBC, bulky disease, or T-cell ALL.

ElectrolyteDirectionWhy it matters
PotassiumUpArrhythmias, cardiac arrest
PhosphorusUpBinds calcium
Uric acidUpCrystallizes in the kidneys, causing acute kidney injury
CalciumDownTetany and seizures, from the high phosphorus
BUN and creatinineUp if injury occursUric acid nephropathy

Prevent and manage it

  1. Aggressive IV hydration at 2 to 3 times maintenance, to keep urine output over 3 mL/kg/hr and flush uric acid. Start before the first chemo dose.
  2. Allopurinol to cut new uric acid formation, for mild or moderate risk.
  3. Rasburicase to break down existing uric acid, for high risk. It is contraindicated in G6PD deficiency because it causes hemolysis. Screen first.
  4. Frequent labs, every 6 to 8 hours, for electrolytes, phosphorus, calcium, uric acid, and renal function.
  5. Continuous cardiac monitoring for hyperkalemia. Treat with calcium gluconate, insulin and dextrose, kayexalate, or dialysis if severe.
  6. Keep potassium and phosphorus out of the IV fluids during induction.

Mucositis

  • Chemo damages the fast-dividing lining of the GI tract.
  • It shows as painful mouth sores, poor intake, and diarrhea.
  • Care: oral care with a soft sponge or brush, alcohol-free rinses (saline or sodium bicarbonate), magic mouthwash per protocol (lidocaine, diphenhydramine, antacid), and good pain control.
  • Avoid acidic, spicy, and hot foods. Offer cold, soft, bland ones. Use parenteral nutrition if it is severe.

Nausea, vomiting, and poor appetite

  • Scheduled antiemetics: ondansetron, often with aprepitant and dexamethasone for the most emetogenic regimens.
  • Small frequent meals, the child's own preferences, and calorie supplements.
  • Watch weight and hydration. Use NG feeds or TPN if weight drops.

Transfusion reactions

These children get frequent red cell and platelet transfusions. Reactions run from mild, a febrile non-hemolytic reaction, to life-threatening, an acute hemolytic reaction, TRALI, or anaphylaxis.

Transfusion reaction: stop first

At the first sign (fever, chills, rash, hypotension, back pain, dyspnea, tachycardia), act in order. First, stop the transfusion. Then keep the line patent with normal saline through new tubing; do not flush the old tubing, since it still holds the reacting blood. Notify the provider and the blood bank. Send the unit, the tubing, and a post-reaction blood and urine sample back. Then support vitals and treat: antihistamines, antipyretics, epinephrine for anaphylaxis. Document everything.

Types of reaction

  • Acute hemolytic (ABO mismatch): fever, chills, back pain, hypotension, hemoglobinuria within minutes. Life-threatening.
  • Febrile non-hemolytic: the most common; mild fever and chills; treat with acetaminophen; use leukoreduced products.
  • Allergic or anaphylactic: hives, wheezing, hypotension. Diphenhydramine, then epinephrine and washed products for anaphylaxis.
  • TRALI: acute dyspnea within 6 hours; bilateral pulmonary edema without fluid overload. Supportive care.
  • TACO: circulatory overload, like heart failure. Slow the infusion and diurese.
  • Transfusion graft-versus-host disease: prevented by giving irradiated products to immunocompromised patients. Standard for oncology kids.

Organ toxicity and late effects

  • Anthracyclines: cardiomyopathy; follow with baseline and serial echocardiograms.
  • Cyclophosphamide: hemorrhagic cystitis; prevent with hydration and Mesna; watch for hematuria.
  • Methotrexate and vincristine: neurotoxicity; check reflexes, grip, and gait.
  • Steroids: hyperglycemia, mood swings, avascular necrosis, hypertension, weight gain.
  • Late effects: secondary cancers, late heart failure, endocrine problems (growth, thyroid, fertility), and neurocognitive and psychosocial effects.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Up, Up, Up, Down
Tumor lysis has one shape you can say fast. Potassium up, phosphorus up, uric acid up, calcium down. The potassium is what stops the heart, so it leads. Prevent the whole thing with hydration started before chemo, plus allopurinol or rasburicase.
Bottom Line: Tumor lysis is up, up, up, down, so hydrate and give allopurinol or rasburicase. A transfusion reaction means stop first, then saline through new tubing. Anthracyclines hit the heart. Late effects are real.
10

What you do first on the unit

These children carry several risks at once. Infection, bleeding, and toxic drugs. Your daily work is to keep each one in check. Order the priorities the way the risks stack up.

  1. 1
    Neutropenic precautions. Private room, strict handwashing, healthy visitors, low-microbial diet. Any temperature of 100.4°F or higher gets reported at once.
  2. 2
    Bleeding precautions. When platelets fall, transfuse near 10,000/mm³, or higher if the child is bleeding or facing a procedure. Soft toothbrush, no flossing, no aspirin or NSAIDs, no rectal procedures. Hold firm pressure after any stick for 5 to 10 minutes.
  3. 3
    Anemia support. Transfuse red cells for a hemoglobin near 7 to 8 g/dL or for symptoms. Space activity with rest. Every blood product is irradiated and leukoreduced.
  4. 4
    Safe chemo handling. Chemo is hazardous to you too. Wear chemo-rated PPE. Two certified nurses verify patient, drug, dose, route, and time. Use closed-system devices. Pregnant nurses do not administer it.
  5. 5
    Central line care. Most kids have a port, a Broviac or Hickman, or a PICC. Sterile technique for every access and dressing change. Scrub the hub 15 seconds before each access. A line infection is the top line complication.
  6. 6
    Intrathecal chemo for CNS prophylaxis. Methotrexate is given into the CSF to reach a sanctuary site. Keep the child flat afterward. And never let vincristine near that route.

Assess every day

Vitals every 4 hours, more if febrile or unstable. A fever of 100.4°F in a neutropenic child means notify now.
Full skin check each shift: line site, perianal area, mouth.
Daily weight; it catches fluid shifts and weight loss, and doses are weight-based.
Strict intake and output; the goal is over 3 mL/kg/hr during tumor lysis prevention.
Lab trends: know today's ANC and platelet count.
Pain: mucositis, bone pain, procedures. Use age-appropriate tools.
Chemo is hazardous to you, too

Use chemo-rated PPE: gown, double gloves, eye protection. Two oncology-certified nurses double-check the five rights. Use closed-system transfer devices. Keep spill kits within reach. Dispose of chemo waste per the hazardous-drug protocol. Pregnant nurses should not administer chemotherapy.

Bottom Line: Assess skin, vitals, and weight every day. Verify chemo with two nurses and full PPE. Bleeding precautions for low platelets, irradiated blood through a cared-for line, and vincristine nowhere near the intrathecal route.
11

The family is the other patient

A childhood cancer diagnosis lands on the whole family. The journey is long. Siblings, parents, and grandparents all need support. You are often the steadiest presence through months of treatment. Use that trust.

The early days

  • Normalize the guilt. When parents ask what they did, tell them nothing they did caused this.
  • Expect shock, denial, anger, and bargaining. Meet them where they are each day.
  • Use plain, concrete language. Repeat the important parts; people in crisis do not retain much.
  • Introduce the team early: oncologist, nurse, social worker, child life, chaplain, dietitian.

Home care teaching

Fever plan: 100.4°F means call the clinic and come in. Never wait and see at home.
Bleeding precautions: soft toothbrush, no aspirin or NSAIDs, an electric razor, and the signs to call about.
Medication schedule: 6-mercaptopurine at bedtime on an empty stomach, methotrexate weekly. Write it down.
Central line care: dressing and cap changes, and the signs of infection.
Sick contacts: avoid anyone with cough, fever, rash, or chickenpox. No crowds. No household contacts with recent live vaccines.
Nutrition: a neutropenic diet when the ANC is low, small frequent meals, and a call for weight loss.

Coping and support

  • Parents: acknowledge the emotional and financial toll. Social work can line up insurance help, lodging, transport, and meals.
  • Siblings: often overlooked. Watch for regression, behavior changes, and slipping grades. Include them; child life can help.
  • The child: honest, developmentally-appropriate talk. School-age kids and teens need body-image and peer support.
  • Groups: St. Jude, ACCO, and the Leukemia and Lymphoma Society reduce isolation.
  • Spiritual and cultural needs: offer the chaplain and accommodate practices.

School and survivorship

  • Most children return to school once the ANC recovers and energy allows, even during maintenance.
  • Build an individualized health plan or a 504 plan with the school nurse.
  • Coordinate absences, make-up work, and a private space for port access.
  • After therapy: off-therapy visits, then a yearly survivorship clinic for at least 10 years.
  • Monitor late effects, re-boost vaccines once immunity recovers (about 6 to 12 months off therapy), and discuss fertility at the right ages.
Hope is evidence-based

In the 1960s, childhood leukemia was almost always fatal. Today more than 90% of children with ALL are cured, and AML survival approaches 70%. The treatment is grueling, yet most children walk out cured. Your job is to hold that hope when parents cannot, to mark the small wins, and to be present on the hard days. That presence is worth as much as the chemo.

Bottom Line: The family needs support as much as the child does. Teach the fever plan, the meds, the line, sick contacts, and nutrition. Siblings, school, and survivorship all count.

Key Takeaways

ALL is lymphoid, ages 2 to 10, the best prognosis of any childhood cancer

AML is myeloid, M0 to M7; Auer rods mean AML

Marrow failure is anemia plus bleeding plus infection

Fever 100.4°F in neutropenia means cultures and antibiotics within 1 hour

Tumor lysis is K up, phos up, uric acid up, Ca down; hydrate plus allopurinol or rasburicase

Vincristine is IV only; intrathecal is fatal
