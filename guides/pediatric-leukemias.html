Pediatric Leukemias (ALL/AML)

The most common childhood cancer. ALL vs AML, bone marrow failure triad, induction-consolidation-maintenance chemo, neutropenic precautions, tumor lysis syndrome, and the long haul with the family.

9 Sections 17 Quiz Questions ~20 Min Read

Learning Objectives

Differentiate ALL and AML by age, onset, and cell line of origin.
Recognize the bone marrow failure triad: anemia, bleeding, infection.
Describe the diagnostic workup — CBC, peripheral smear, bone marrow biopsy, LP.
Apply neutropenic precautions and identify ANC-based infection risk.
Recognize and manage tumor lysis syndrome and other chemo complications.
Support the family through diagnosis, treatment, and survivorship.

High-Yield Summary

Ten facts that will carry you through any pediatric leukemia question.

  • Leukemia is the #1 childhood cancer. ALL (acute lymphoblastic) accounts for ~75% of pediatric leukemias; AML (acute myelogenous) is the second most common.
  • ALL peaks ages 2–10 (classic "toddler-to-school-age" cancer). AML occurs throughout childhood and is more common in adolescents; has 8 subtypes (M0–M7).
  • Bone marrow failure triad: anemia (pallor, fatigue), thrombocytopenia (bruising, petechiae, bleeding), neutropenia (fever, infection). Plus bone pain, lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly.
  • Diagnosis: CBC shows blasts on smear; bone marrow biopsy is the gold standard (>20% blasts = acute leukemia). LP checks for CNS involvement.
  • Chemo in three phases: Induction (achieve remission, 4–6 weeks) → Consolidation/Intensification → Maintenance (2–3 years total in ALL). BMT/HSCT for relapsed or high-risk AML.
  • Neutropenic precautions: NO rectal temps, NO fresh flowers/plants, reverse isolation, NO live vaccines, limit visitors, NO invasive procedures, handwashing, cooked food only.
  • ANC < 500 = severe neutropenia, profound infection risk. ANC = WBC × (% neutrophils + % bands) ÷ 100. Fever ≥ 100.4°F (38°C) in a neutropenic child is an oncologic emergency — blood cultures × 2, broad-spectrum antibiotics within 1 hour.
  • Tumor lysis syndrome (TLS): after chemo, cell contents dump. K+ UP, phosphorus UP, uric acid UP, calcium DOWN. Prevent with hydration + allopurinol or rasburicase.
  • Blood product transfusion reaction — first action is STOP the transfusion, keep line open with normal saline via new tubing, notify provider, send bag/tubing back to blood bank.
  • ALL has the best prognosis of any childhood cancer — >90% 5-year survival. AML survival is lower (~65–70%). Both are curable; the family journey is long but outcomes are usually good.

Leukemia Fundamentals

What goes wrong in the bone marrow

Leukemia is a malignant proliferation of abnormal white blood cell precursors (blasts) in the bone marrow. The blasts crowd out normal hematopoiesis, leading to failure of red cells, platelets, and normal WBCs. It is the most common childhood cancer, accounting for about one-third of all pediatric malignancies.

How the Normal Marrow Works

  • Bone marrow stem cells produce lymphoid (B and T cells → lymphocytes) and myeloid (granulocytes, monocytes, RBCs, platelets) lines.
  • In leukemia, one line becomes malignant — cells stop maturing and accumulate as immature blasts.
  • Blasts crowd normal cell lines → pancytopenia and systemic spread (liver, spleen, lymph nodes, CNS, testes).

ALL vs AML at a Glance

Feature ALL (Acute Lymphoblastic) AML (Acute Myelogenous)
Cell line Lymphoid (B or T precursor) Myeloid precursors
Frequency ~75% of pediatric leukemias ~20% of pediatric leukemias
Peak age 2–10 years (peak 2–5) Any age, more common in adolescents and <2 yr
Subtypes Pre-B (most common), T-cell M0–M7 (8 subtypes) by FAB classification
CNS involvement Common — prophylactic intrathecal chemo Less common
Prognosis >90% 5-year survival ~65–70% 5-year survival
Primary treatment Multi-drug chemo (2–3 years) Intense chemo; BMT/HSCT often needed

Risk Factors

Genetic syndromes: Down syndrome (10–20× risk), Li-Fraumeni, Fanconi anemia, neurofibromatosis.

Previous chemo or radiation — secondary AML is a known risk.

Identical twin with leukemia — 25% concordance rate.

In utero radiation exposure and some rare chemical exposures (benzene).

Most cases have no identifiable cause — tell the family this. It is NOT something they caused.

Sex: boys slightly higher ALL risk; roughly equal for AML.

Parents Often Blame Themselves

Families hearing a leukemia diagnosis frequently ask, "Did I do something to cause this?" Reassure them firmly: the vast majority of childhood leukemias have no identifiable environmental cause. Something went wrong at the level of the DNA in a single cell — not because of a vaccine, a cell phone, or a missed meal. Normalizing this is part of first-day nursing care.

🎯 Bottom Line: ALL = lymphoid, 2–10 yo, best prognosis. AML = myeloid, more in adolescents, 8 subtypes (M0–M7). Both = blasts crowd marrow → pancytopenia.

Classification & Types

Knowing the cell line drives the treatment

Subtype matters: it determines the chemo regimen, prognosis, and whether bone marrow transplant (BMT) is likely. You don't need to memorize every subtype, but understanding the major categories helps you anticipate the care plan.

Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia (ALL)

Most common
Peak Age

2–10 years (highest incidence age 2–5).

Subtypes
  • B-cell precursor (pre-B) — most common (~85%), better prognosis.
  • T-cell ALL — more in older boys; may present with mediastinal mass; slightly worse prognosis historically.
  • Mature B-cell (Burkitt-type) — aggressive but highly curable with modern therapy.
Key Features

CNS involvement in ~5%; prophylactic intrathecal chemo is standard.

Acute Myelogenous Leukemia (AML)

M0–M7
Peak Age

Any age; more common in adolescents and infants <2 years.

FAB Subtypes (M0–M7)
  • M0–M2: myeloblastic (least differentiated → more differentiated)
  • M3: acute promyelocytic (APL) — classic DIC risk; responds to ATRA (all-trans retinoic acid)
  • M4: myelomonocytic
  • M5: monoblastic — gum infiltration, skin lesions common
  • M6: erythroleukemia
  • M7: megakaryoblastic — strongly linked to Down syndrome in infants
Key Features

Higher relapse risk; BMT/HSCT frequently needed.

Auer Rods = AML

Auer rods are needle-shaped cytoplasmic inclusions seen on peripheral smear. Their presence is pathognomonic for AML, particularly the M3 (APL) subtype. If you see them mentioned in a question stem, AML is the answer — not ALL.

Chronic Leukemias (Rare in Kids)

  • Chronic myelogenous leukemia (CML) — rare in children; Philadelphia chromosome (t[9;22]) positive. Treated with tyrosine kinase inhibitors (imatinib) which have transformed outcomes.
  • Juvenile myelomonocytic leukemia (JMML) — rare, aggressive infant/toddler leukemia; usually requires BMT.

Risk Stratification in ALL

Feature Standard Risk High Risk
Age1–9 years<1 or ≥10 years
WBC at diagnosis<50,000/mm³≥50,000/mm³
Cell lineB-cell precursorT-cell, infant ALL, mature B
CNS involvementNonePresent at diagnosis
Response to inductionRemission by day 28Slow / minimal residual disease
🎯 Bottom Line: ALL = B-cell or T-cell, best prognosis. AML = M0–M7 subtypes; Auer rods = AML. Down syndrome → M7 risk. CML = Philadelphia chromosome.

Clinical Manifestations

The bone marrow failure triad plus organ infiltration

Children usually present with the consequences of bone marrow failure plus findings from organ infiltration by leukemic cells. Symptoms may develop over weeks; many kids are initially worked up for "another virus that won't quit" or persistent bruising.

The Bone Marrow Failure Triad

Cell Line Affected What Goes Low What You See
RBCs Anemia (Hgb down) Pallor, fatigue, SOB on exertion, tachycardia, poor feeding (infants), pale mucous membranes
Platelets Thrombocytopenia Petechiae, purpura, ecchymoses, epistaxis, gum bleeding, prolonged bleeding from minor injuries
WBCs (neutrophils) Neutropenia (despite high total WBC) Fever, recurrent infections, sepsis, mouth ulcers, skin infections that don't heal

Organ Infiltration Findings

Bone pain — particularly in the legs/back; child may limp or refuse to walk; wakes from sleep. Classic in ALL.

Lymphadenopathy — painless, enlarged, firm nodes (cervical, supraclavicular, axillary, inguinal).

Hepatosplenomegaly — palpable enlargement, abdominal distention, early satiety.

CNS involvement: headache, vomiting, cranial nerve palsies, papilledema. More common in ALL.

Testicular enlargement — painless; a sanctuary site in ALL.

"B symptoms" — fever, unintentional weight loss, drenching night sweats.

Presentation Clues by Type

  • T-cell ALL: mediastinal mass (superior mediastinum) — can cause SVC syndrome with facial swelling, stridor, dyspnea. Respiratory emergency.
  • AML M3 (APL): presents with bleeding out of proportion to platelet count — suspect DIC. Classic board question.
  • AML M5 (monoblastic): gum hyperplasia/infiltration, skin nodules ("leukemia cutis").
  • Infant leukemia: often presents with high WBC count, hepatosplenomegaly, CNS disease.
Petechiae + Fatigue + Bone Pain = Think Leukemia

The classic triad that should trigger a CBC: pallor/fatigue (anemia) + petechiae or unusual bruising (thrombocytopenia) + bone pain or limp. A child with these findings has leukemia until proven otherwise. Other tip-offs: persistent fever without a source, unexplained lymphadenopathy, or early morning bone pain that wakes them up.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Limping Toddler
A toddler who suddenly refuses to walk or wakes at night crying about leg pain is not just "growing pains." The differential includes septic joint, osteomyelitis, JIA, and leukemia. Kids with leukemia are often pale and bruising more than usual — the CBC will show the diagnosis. Always get vitals, look at the mucous membranes, and palpate the lymph nodes when bone pain and fatigue are the story.
🎯 Bottom Line: Marrow failure = anemia + bleeding + infection. Plus bone pain, big lymph nodes, hepatosplenomegaly. T-cell ALL → mediastinal mass. AML M3 → DIC.

Diagnostic Workup

CBC → smear → bone marrow → LP

The diagnosis starts with a CBC and ends with a bone marrow biopsy. The entire workup usually happens in the first 48–72 hours of admission and drives the treatment plan.

Initial Labs

Test Typical Finding Why We Get It
CBC with diff Anemia, thrombocytopenia; WBC may be LOW, NORMAL, or VERY HIGH (>100,000) First clue; may show blasts on auto-diff
Peripheral smear Blasts present; Auer rods suggest AML Visualize blast morphology
BMP / lytes / phos / uric acid May show TLS pattern at baseline Prepare for tumor lysis risk
LDH Elevated (high cell turnover) Disease activity marker
Coags (PT/PTT/fibrinogen/D-dimer) DIC possible (especially AML M3) Rule out DIC before procedures
CXR Mediastinal mass (T-cell ALL) Airway/SVC risk assessment

Bone Marrow Biopsy — The Gold Standard

  • Usually aspirated from the posterior iliac crest under sedation.
  • Diagnostic threshold: >20% blasts in the marrow = acute leukemia.
  • Sent for morphology, flow cytometry (identifies cell line), cytogenetics (chromosomal abnormalities), and molecular testing (fusion genes, mutations).
  • Repeated after induction to confirm remission (<5% blasts).

Lumbar Puncture (LP) for CNS Status

  • Performed at diagnosis to check for CNS leukemia.
  • Often combined with the first dose of intrathecal methotrexate as prophylaxis.
  • CNS-positive disease upstages the risk group and intensifies therapy.
NCLEX Point — Positioning for LP

Position the child on their side with the back arched and knees drawn up to chest (fetal/C position) to open the intervertebral spaces. In infants, hold in a sitting flexed position or side-lying — do NOT hyperflex the neck in infants (airway compromise). Hold child still during procedure; distraction and pain management afterward. Lay flat for several hours post-procedure if intrathecal chemo was given to allow even distribution and reduce post-LP headache.

Staging & Risk Group Assignment

Unlike most cancers, acute leukemia is not "staged" like a solid tumor. Instead, risk is based on:

  • Age and WBC at diagnosis
  • Cell line and cytogenetics (certain mutations → better or worse prognosis)
  • CNS or testicular involvement
  • Minimal residual disease (MRD) after induction
🎯 Bottom Line: CBC with blasts → peripheral smear → bone marrow biopsy (>20% blasts = leukemia) → LP to stage CNS. Flow + cytogenetics drive the treatment plan.

Treatment Overview

Three phases of chemo, intrathecal prophylaxis, and BMT

Leukemia treatment is long. A child with ALL may be on therapy for 2–3 years. AML is more intense but shorter. Children's oncology centers follow COG (Children's Oncology Group) protocols with standardized phases.

Three Phases of Chemotherapy

1. Induction

4–6 weeks
Goal

Achieve complete remission (<5% blasts in marrow).

Typical Drugs (ALL)

Vincristine, L-asparaginase, corticosteroid (prednisone or dexamethasone), anthracycline (daunorubicin) in high-risk.

Watch For

Tumor lysis syndrome (first 48–72 hr), neutropenic fever, chemo reactions, mucositis.

2. Consolidation / Intensification

Months
Goal

Eliminate residual disease, prevent relapse, prevent CNS spread.

Typical Approach

Cyclophosphamide, cytarabine, methotrexate (IV and intrathecal); delayed intensification blocks.

Watch For

Prolonged cytopenias; risk of serious infection; GI toxicity.

3. Maintenance

2–3 yr total (ALL)
Goal

Long-term remission; keep blasts suppressed.

Typical Approach

Oral 6-mercaptopurine (6-MP) daily + oral methotrexate weekly; periodic vincristine/steroid pulses; intermittent intrathecal methotrexate.

Watch For

Adherence; daily schedule for years; normalcy of life resumes but with frequent clinic visits.

BMT / HSCT

Select Cases
When

Relapsed ALL, most high-risk AML, CML, JMML, select high-risk subtypes.

Types

Allogeneic (donor, preferred from matched sibling) vs. autologous. Conditioning with high-dose chemo ± total body irradiation.

Watch For

Graft-versus-host disease, opportunistic infections, engraftment delays, long hospitalization.

Selected Chemotherapy Agents

Vincristine
Plant Alkaloid (Vinca)

Key toxicity: peripheral neuropathy, constipation, SIADH, jaw pain.

IV only — fatal if given intrathecally. Giving intrathecal vincristine is a never event in oncology.

L-Asparaginase
Enzyme

Depletes asparagine used by leukemic cells.

Watch for anaphylaxis, pancreatitis, hyperglycemia, coagulopathy. Given IM or IV with epinephrine at the bedside.

Corticosteroids (Prednisone / Dexamethasone)
Anti-Lymphoid

Cytotoxic to lymphoblasts; backbone of ALL therapy.

Side effects: hyperglycemia, mood changes, increased appetite, hypertension, avascular necrosis with long exposure.

Methotrexate (IV & IT)
Antimetabolite

Used IV (high-dose) and intrathecally for CNS prophylaxis.

Toxicity: mucositis, hepatotoxicity, nephrotoxicity. Leucovorin rescue after high-dose.

6-Mercaptopurine (6-MP)
Antimetabolite — Maintenance

Oral, daily, for years during maintenance.

Adherence is critical. Give at bedtime, on empty stomach, away from dairy.

Cytarabine (Ara-C)
Antimetabolite (AML backbone)

Cornerstone of AML induction.

Toxicity: profound cytopenias, mucositis, cerebellar toxicity at high doses, "Ara-C syndrome" (fever, rash, myalgia).

Anthracyclines (Daunorubicin, Doxorubicin)
Topoisomerase Inhibitor

Cardiotoxicity is dose-limiting — baseline and serial echocardiograms.

Vesicant: extravasation causes tissue necrosis. Central line preferred.

Cyclophosphamide
Alkylating Agent

Used in consolidation and BMT conditioning.

Hemorrhagic cystitis risk — vigorous hydration and Mesna. Bladder cancer is a late effect.

Vincristine is IV Only — NEVER Intrathecal

Accidental intrathecal vincristine is uniformly fatal. Oncology units use color-coded syringes, separate labeling, mandatory two-person verification, and time-separated administration from intrathecal chemo to eliminate this error. As the nurse: read the label, verify the route, and never accept a premixed syringe without a label indicating the route.

Radiation Therapy

  • Historical role larger; now reserved for CNS leukemia, testicular relapse, and conditioning for BMT.
  • Late effects: growth failure, cognitive impairment, secondary cancers — major reason CNS radiation is minimized in modern protocols.

Newer Therapies

  • Tyrosine kinase inhibitors (imatinib, dasatinib) for Philadelphia-positive ALL and CML.
  • Immunotherapy: blinatumomab (CD19 bispecific T-cell engager), inotuzumab, CAR-T cell therapy (tisagenlecleucel) for relapsed/refractory B-cell ALL.
  • ATRA (all-trans retinoic acid) for AML M3 (APL) — differentiates the promyelocytes; dramatically improves outcomes. Watch for differentiation syndrome.
🎯 Bottom Line: Induction → Consolidation → Maintenance. ALL = 2–3 yr total. AML is more intense, often needs BMT. Intrathecal methotrexate for CNS. Vincristine = IV only, never IT.

Neutropenic Precautions & Infection Control

The highest-yield section for NCLEX and the floor

After chemo, the child's white cell count plummets. They are functionally immunocompromised and at extreme risk for infection — including from their own gut flora and skin. Neutropenic precautions protect the child from organisms we don't usually worry about.

Defining Neutropenia by ANC

ANC Range (cells/mm³) Category Risk
>1,500NormalBaseline
1,000–1,500Mild neutropeniaMildly increased
500–1,000Moderate neutropeniaSignificant infection risk
<500Severe neutropeniaProfound infection risk; strict precautions
<100Profound / functional agranulocytosisLife-threatening; single-organism sepsis common
ANC Formula

ANC = WBC × (% neutrophils + % bands) ÷ 100. Example: WBC 2,000 with 20% neutrophils and 5% bands → ANC = 2,000 × (25/100) = 500. Severe neutropenia. Many NCLEX questions expect you to calculate and then apply the answer.

Neutropenic Precautions — What TO Do

Private room, ideally with HEPA filtration. Close the door.

Strict handwashing by every staff member, visitor, and parent entering the room.

Healthy visitors only — no one with cough, fever, rash, cold sores. Limit visitor numbers.

Daily oral care with soft toothbrush or oral sponges; no flossing if platelets low.

Low-microbial / neutropenic diet — cooked foods, no raw fruits/vegetables unless peeled, no deli meats, no unpasteurized dairy.

Daily skin assessment (including perianal and CVC site) for early infection.

Frequent vitals including temperature checks; parents report fever immediately.

Central line care per protocol — CLABSI prevention bundles, hub scrubbing.

Neutropenic Precautions — What NOT To Do

NO rectal temps, rectal meds, or rectal exams — can cause mucosal tears and bacteremia.

NO fresh flowers or live plants — soil and stagnant water harbor Aspergillus and Pseudomonas.

NO fresh fruits/vegetables unwashed; no raw, undercooked, or unpasteurized foods.

NO live vaccines (MMR, varicella, rotavirus, nasal flu) to child or household contacts during treatment.

NO invasive procedures if avoidable — IM injections, catheterizations, dental work all increase bacteremia risk.

NO aspirin or NSAIDs (platelet dysfunction + mask fever). Acetaminophen for pain, per oncology team.

NO sick visitors, siblings with recent live vaccines, or pets with open litter boxes.

NO group/shared rooms; avoid the playroom unless policy permits when ANC recovered.

Febrile Neutropenia Is an Oncologic Emergency

A single temperature ≥ 100.4°F (38°C) in a neutropenic child is treated as sepsis until proven otherwise. Actions within 60 minutes: two blood cultures (peripheral + central line lumen), urine culture, CBC/CMP/lactate, CXR if respiratory symptoms, and broad-spectrum IV antibiotics (typically cefepime or piperacillin-tazobactam). Do NOT wait for the ANC to come back — treat empirically. Mortality rises sharply with every hour of antibiotic delay.

NCLEX Point — Live Vaccines in the Immunocompromised

Live-attenuated vaccines (MMR, varicella, rotavirus, intranasal influenza, BCG, yellow fever) should NOT be given to a child on chemotherapy OR to household contacts during active treatment for risk of transmission. Inactivated vaccines (injectable flu, Tdap, pneumococcal) are allowed but may have blunted response. Siblings should get the injectable flu vaccine, not the intranasal spray.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Remember The Big 4 No's
The four things NCLEX loves to test about neutropenic care: NO rectal temps, NO fresh flowers/plants, NO live vaccines, NO raw foods. Memorize those four and you'll pass most neutropenic-care questions on sight. Everything else (private room, handwashing, sick visitor screening) is a generalization of "protect the immune-compromised child."
🎯 Bottom Line: ANC <500 = severe. Fever = emergency (cultures + abx within 1 hr). NO rectal temps, NO flowers/plants, NO live vaccines, NO raw food.

Treatment Complications

Tumor lysis, mucositis, transfusion reactions, late effects

Tumor Lysis Syndrome (TLS)

As chemotherapy destroys leukemic cells, their intracellular contents spill into circulation. This creates a predictable electrolyte storm in the first 24–72 hours of induction, especially in kids with high WBC, bulky disease, or T-cell ALL.

Electrolyte Direction Complication
Potassium↑ UPArrhythmias, cardiac arrest
Phosphorus↑ UPBinds calcium
Uric acid↑ UPCrystallizes in kidneys → acute kidney injury
Calcium↓ DOWNTetany, seizures (from high phos binding)
BUN/Cr↑ UP (if AKI)Uric acid nephropathy

TLS Prevention & Management

  1. Aggressive IV hydration at 2–3× maintenance to keep urine output >3 mL/kg/hr and flush uric acid through the kidneys. Start BEFORE first chemo dose.
  2. Allopurinol (xanthine oxidase inhibitor) PO/IV to reduce new uric acid formation — used for mild/moderate risk.
  3. Rasburicase (recombinant urate oxidase) — converts uric acid to allantoin. Used for high risk (high WBC, bulky disease, baseline hyperuricemia). Contraindicated in G6PD deficiency (hemolysis risk). Screen first.
  4. Frequent labs (q6–8h) monitoring lytes, phos, Ca, uric acid, BUN/Cr.
  5. Continuous cardiac monitoring for hyperkalemia effects. Treat symptomatic hyperkalemia with calcium gluconate, insulin/dextrose, kayexalate, or dialysis if severe.
  6. Avoid potassium in IV fluids during induction; avoid phosphorus-containing fluids.

Mucositis

  • Chemo damages rapidly dividing mucosal cells throughout the GI tract.
  • Presents as painful mouth sores, decreased intake, diarrhea.
  • Care: oral care with soft sponge or soft toothbrush, alcohol-free rinses (saline or sodium bicarbonate), "magic mouthwash" per protocol (typically lidocaine + diphenhydramine + antacid), adequate pain control.
  • Avoid acidic/spicy/hot foods; cold/soft/bland preferred. Consider parenteral nutrition if severe.

Nausea, Vomiting & Anorexia

  • Scheduled antiemetics (ondansetron, often + aprepitant and dexamethasone for highly emetogenic regimens).
  • Small frequent meals, child's food preferences, calorie supplements.
  • Monitor weight and hydration closely; NG feeds or TPN if weight loss.

Blood Product Transfusion Reactions

Children with leukemia receive frequent PRBC and platelet transfusions. Transfusion reactions range from mild (febrile non-hemolytic) to life-threatening (acute hemolytic, TRALI, anaphylaxis).

Transfusion Reaction — STOP FIRST

At the first sign of reaction (fever, chills, rash, hypotension, back pain, dyspnea, tachycardia): 1) STOP the transfusion immediately. 2) Keep the IV line patent with normal saline via new tubing (do not flush the existing line — it still contains the reacting blood). 3) Notify the provider and blood bank. 4) Send the unit, tubing, and a post-reaction blood sample and urine back to the blood bank. 5) Support vitals, treat symptoms (antihistamines, antipyretics, epinephrine if anaphylaxis), document everything.

Types of Reactions

  • Acute hemolytic (ABO mismatch) — fever, chills, back pain, hypotension, hemoglobinuria within minutes. Life-threatening.
  • Febrile non-hemolytic — most common; mild fever/chills; treat with acetaminophen. Use leukoreduced products.
  • Allergic / anaphylactic — hives, wheezing, hypotension. Treat with diphenhydramine; anaphylaxis → epinephrine, washed products.
  • TRALI (transfusion-related acute lung injury) — acute dyspnea within 6 hours; bilateral pulmonary edema without fluid overload. Supportive care.
  • TACO (transfusion-associated circulatory overload) — fluid overload / CHF-like; slow infusion, diuresis.
  • Graft-versus-host disease from transfusion — prevented by giving irradiated blood products to immunocompromised patients. Standard for oncology kids.

Cardiotoxicity & Other Organ Effects

  • Anthracyclines (daunorubicin, doxorubicin) → cardiomyopathy; monitor with baseline/serial echos.
  • Cyclophosphamide → hemorrhagic cystitis; prevent with hydration + Mesna; watch for hematuria.
  • Methotrexate/vincristine → neurotoxicity; assess DTRs, hand grip, gait.
  • Steroids → hyperglycemia, mood swings, AVN, HTN, weight gain.
  • L-asparaginase → pancreatitis, coagulopathy, anaphylaxis.

Late Effects (Survivorship)

  • Secondary cancers — AML after alkylators/topoisomerase inhibitors, solid tumors after radiation.
  • Cardiotoxicity — late CHF after anthracyclines.
  • Endocrine: growth hormone deficiency, hypothyroidism, infertility, early menopause.
  • Neurocognitive: learning/attention issues (especially after CNS radiation or young-age treatment).
  • Psychosocial: PTSD, anxiety, depression, "survivor guilt."
🎯 Bottom Line: TLS = K up, phos up, uric acid up, Ca down → hydrate + allopurinol/rasburicase. Transfusion reaction → STOP FIRST, saline new tubing. Anthracyclines = cardio. Late effects real.

Nursing Priorities & Safety

Daily care on the oncology unit

Daily Priority Assessment

Vital signs q4h (more if febrile/unstable); any fever ≥ 100.4°F in a neutropenic child = notify provider immediately.

Full skin assessment q shift, including CVC site, perianal area, mouth; early catch of cellulitis/typhlitis.

Daily weight — catches fluid shifts, weight loss, failure to thrive; dose recalculation often based on weight.

Strict I&O — goal output usually ≥3 mL/kg/hr during TLS prevention.

Lab trends — CBC, chemistries, uric acid, phos; know the current ANC and platelet count.

Pain assessment — mucositis, bone pain, procedure pain. Use developmentally appropriate tools.

Safe Chemotherapy Administration

Chemo Handling — Protect Yourself

Chemotherapy is hazardous to staff as well as patients. Use chemo-rated PPE (gown, double gloves, eye protection) for administration. Double-check with two oncology-certified RNs: right patient, right drug, right dose, right route, right time. Use closed-system transfer devices. Spill kits must be readily available. Dispose of chemo waste per institutional hazardous drug protocol. Pregnant nurses should not administer chemotherapy per institutional policies.

Bleeding Precautions (Thrombocytopenia)

  • Threshold for transfusion typically <10,000/mm³ (or higher if actively bleeding or pre-procedure).
  • Soft toothbrush or oral sponge; avoid flossing; electric razors.
  • No rectal procedures, IM injections, or aspirin/NSAIDs.
  • Apply firm pressure after any venipuncture/finger stick for 5–10 min.
  • Monitor for petechiae, gum bleeding, epistaxis, bloody stools, hematuria.
  • Fall precautions — dizziness from anemia plus bleeding risk = bad combo.

Anemia Support

  • PRBC transfusion typically for Hgb <7–8 g/dL (or symptomatic).
  • Expect tachycardia, pallor, fatigue; space activity with rest.
  • Remember: all blood products must be irradiated and leukoreduced for oncology patients.

Central Line Care

  • Most kids have a port-a-cath, Broviac/Hickman, or PICC.
  • Strict sterile technique for accessing, dressing changes, cap changes.
  • Scrub the hub before every access (15 seconds with alcohol).
  • Watch for redness, drainage, fever — CVC infection is the #1 line-related complication.
  • Heparin flush per protocol; teach family home care.

Nutrition & Activity

  • Calorie-dense foods; multivitamins without iron unless directed; nutritional supplements (PediaSure, Pedialyte).
  • Consult dietitian early.
  • NG tube feeds or TPN if poor intake.
  • Age-appropriate activity as tolerated; avoid contact sports during thrombocytopenia.
  • Child Life Services is essential — play therapy, procedural prep, school-age academic support.
🎯 Bottom Line: Assess skin + vitals + weight daily. Chemo = 2 RN verify, PPE. Bleeding precautions for low platelets. Central line + irradiated blood products. Child Life is your teammate.

Family Support & Education

The diagnosis hits everyone in the family

A childhood cancer diagnosis is a life-changing event for the whole family. The child's journey is long; siblings, parents, and grandparents all need support. The nurse is often the most consistent presence through the months of treatment — use that trust.

Breaking the News & Early Days

  • Normalize the parents' guilt reaction — "What did we do?" — reassure that nothing they did caused this.
  • Expect shock, denial, anger, bargaining. Meet them where they are each day.
  • Use concrete, plain language. Repeat important info; families often can't retain complex medical details in crisis.
  • Introduce the interdisciplinary team early: oncologist, oncology RN, social worker, child life, chaplain, dietitian.

Home Care Teaching Priorities

Fever plan: temp ≥ 100.4°F = call oncology clinic immediately, come to ED. Never wait and see at home.

Bleeding precautions — soft toothbrush, no aspirin/NSAIDs, electric razor, signs to call.

Medication schedule — 6-MP at bedtime on empty stomach; methotrexate weekly; write it down.

Central line care — dressing changes, cap changes, signs of infection.

Sick contacts — avoid anyone with cough/fever/rash/chickenpox; no crowds, no live-vaccine household contacts.

Nutrition — neutropenic diet when ANC low; small frequent meals; call for weight loss.

Psychosocial & Coping

  • Parents: acknowledge the emotional and financial toll. Many families take leaves of absence or lose income; social work can connect resources (insurance, lodging, transportation via RMH/local charities, meal delivery).
  • Siblings: often overlooked. Watch for regression, behavior changes, academic decline. Include them; child life can help.
  • The child: developmentally appropriate honesty. School-age and adolescents need body-image and peer support. Protect identity and independence.
  • Parent advocacy organizations: St. Jude, ACCO, Leukemia & Lymphoma Society; online communities help reduce isolation.
  • Spirituality and cultural needs — offer chaplain, accommodate religious practices.

School Re-entry

  • Most children can return to school once ANC recovers and energy allows, even during maintenance.
  • Develop an Individualized Health Plan (IHP) or 504 plan with the school nurse.
  • Coordinate with teachers for absences, make-up work, private room for port access if needed.
  • Pre-return classroom visit or peer presentation (with family consent) normalizes appearance changes (hair loss, port, mask).

Long-Term Survivorship

  • After therapy: off-therapy visits, then yearly survivorship clinic for at least 10 years.
  • Monitor for late effects: echocardiogram for anthracycline exposure, endocrine labs, growth, neurocognitive evaluation.
  • Vaccine re-boosting after immune recovery (usually 6–12 months off therapy).
  • Fertility discussion at age-appropriate points.
  • Most survivors live full, healthy lives. Emphasize hope grounded in evidence — this is no longer a universally fatal disease.
Hope Is Evidence-Based

In the 1960s, childhood leukemia was nearly uniformly fatal. Today, more than 90% of children with ALL are cured, and AML survival approaches 70%. The treatment is grueling but almost every child walks out of the clinic cured. Your job as the nurse is to hold that hope when parents can't, to celebrate the small wins, and to be present for the hard days. That presence is worth as much as the chemo.

🎯 Bottom Line: Family needs support as much as the child. Home-care priorities: fever plan, meds, line care, sick contacts, nutrition. Siblings + school + long-term survivorship matter.
