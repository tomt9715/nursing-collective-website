Pediatric Renal Disorders

Five conditions that look alike in a puffy child. Read the urine and the pressure, name the HUS triad, and know the two drugs you never give.

By the end, without notes

  • Read the urine and the pressure, and say nephrotic or nephritic
  • Name the HUS triad, and the two drugs you never give
  • Sort AKI into pre-renal, intrinsic, or post-renal, then act on it
  • State the potassium level that makes you reach for calcium first

01

What actually gets you

Pediatric renal is five conditions. The exam lives in telling them apart.

Points get lost here in three predictable ways. Read these before anything else.

You could not read the urine and the pressure

Nephrotic is massive proteinuria. Think foamy urine, low albumin, big edema, high lipids. Nephritic is glomerulonephritis. Think tea-colored urine, high blood pressure, mild edema, rising BUN. Same puffy child, opposite urine and pressure. Miss that axis and you miss the question.

You forgot the HUS rule

HUS follows bloody diarrhea. The triad is hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, and acute kidney injury. The E. coli O157:H7 picture has two traps. You do not give antibiotics. You do not give antimotility agents. Both make it worse.

You treated post-strep AGN as chronic

It usually is not. Post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis is self-limited. Most children recover in weeks. Care is supportive: watch the pressure and the fluid. Do not manage it like chronic kidney disease.

Bottom Line: Stop studying five diseases in a row. Study the axes that split them: urine color, blood pressure, and what you must not do.
02

The four things

For each condition, say four things with the book closed. The one-sentence definition. The clue in a scenario. The priority action. The first-line treatment and its teaching.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet.

Disorder Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line treatment and teaching
Nephrotic syndrome The filter leaks protein, so albumin drops and fluid floods the tissues. Preschooler, puffy eyes in the morning, foamy urine, normal BP. Daily weight, strict I&O, watch for infection. Prednisone. Low-sodium diet. Never stop steroids abruptly.
Acute glomerulonephritis (nephritic) Immune complexes inflame the glomeruli after a strep infection. School-age, tea-colored urine, high BP, 1 to 3 weeks post-strep. Monitor and control blood pressure and fluid. Supportive care. Sodium and fluid limits. Penicillin if strep persists.

Copy this down

Write five rows on one card, by hand. Foamy plus normal BP is nephrotic. Tea plus high BP is nephritic. Bloody diarrhea plus low platelets is HUS. That card is most of the exam.

03

Nephrotic syndrome

Discrimination axis: protein and edema, with a normal pressure.

This is the puffy, proteinuric preschooler. The filter loses protein. Albumin falls. Fluid leaves the vessels and swells the tissues. The liver then overmakes lipids.

The four-part picture

  • Massive proteinuria (over 40 mg/m2/hr, 3+ to 4+ on dipstick); quantify with a spot urine protein-to-creatinine ratio or 24-hour urine
  • Hypoalbuminemia (serum albumin <2.5 g/dL)
  • Edema (periorbital in the morning, generalized, ascites, pleural effusion)
  • Hyperlipidemia (elevated cholesterol and triglycerides)

What you see

  • Periorbital edema worse in the morning; overnight, lying flat sends fluid to the face
  • Generalized edema by day: ascites, scrotal or labial swelling, pleural effusion
  • Weight gain from fluid, decreased urine output
  • Foamy urine from heavy protein loss
  • Pallor, fatigue, poor appetite, irritability
  • Normal or low-normal BP, the key split from AGN

Minimal change disease causes about nine in ten pediatric cases. It responds to steroids.

Infection is the real killer

These children lose immunoglobulins in the urine along with albumin. Steroids weaken immunity further. Spontaneous bacterial peritonitis is the feared complication. It shows as fever, abdominal pain, and distension. Vaccinate against pneumococcus and varicella when off high-dose steroids. Treat fevers fast, and teach families to call for any fever above 38.5 C.

Treatment

  • Corticosteroids (prednisone) first line for minimal change disease; most respond in 2 to 4 weeks, then a long taper
  • Diuretics (furosemide) for severe edema, often paired with albumin to pull fluid back into the vessels first
  • Albumin 25% for severe edema, hypotension, or poor diuretic response
  • Low-sodium diet; keep protein intake normal, not restricted
  • Infection prevention; these children are immunocompromised from steroids and protein loss
  • Steroid-resistant or relapsing disease: cyclophosphamide, cyclosporine, mycophenolate, rituximab; renal biopsy for atypical or resistant cases
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Puffy eyes in the morning
Lying flat overnight sends fluid to the face. The child wakes with dramatic puffy eyes. By day, gravity pulls it down to legs, scrotum, and labia. The classic parent line: eyes puffy on waking, feet look fine. That is nephrotic, not allergy.
Bottom Line: Nephrotic is pale, puffy, foamy urine, low albumin, and a normal BP. Prednisone, low-sodium diet, and infection prevention.
04

Acute glomerulonephritis

Discrimination axis: blood and pressure, after a strep infection.

This is post-streptococcal glomerulonephritis. Strep comes first. One to three weeks later the urine turns. Immune complexes deposit in the glomeruli, and inflammation follows.

Who and when

  • Age 5 to 15 years, school-age, more often boys
  • Follows strep throat by 1 to 3 weeks
  • Follows impetigo or skin infection by 3 to 6 weeks

What you see

  • Tea- or cola-colored urine from gross hematuria, the hallmark
  • Hypertension from fluid retention and renin activity
  • Edema, usually facial and milder than nephrotic
  • Oliguria, fatigue, headache, anorexia, possible flank pain

The labs that nail it

  • Hematuria with RBC casts, specific for glomerular bleeding
  • Mild-to-moderate proteinuria, less than nephrotic
  • Elevated BUN and creatinine
  • Low C3 complement, consumed by immune complexes
  • Elevated ASO titer or anti-DNase B, evidence of recent strep
  • Hyperkalemia possible

Treatment

  • Mostly supportive; most children recover fully in 2 to 4 weeks
  • Blood pressure control with antihypertensives and loop diuretics
  • Fluid and sodium restriction during the acute phase
  • Penicillin if the strep infection is still active
  • Dialysis rarely, only for severe cases
  • Activity restriction until inflammation resolves

Nephrotic or nephritic

Both puff a child up. The urine and the pressure run opposite. Learn them side by side.

Nephrotic: protein and edema
  • Preschooler, 2 to 6 years
  • Foamy urine, massive proteinuria (3+ to 4+)
  • Blood pressure normal or low
  • Low albumin (<2.5 g/dL)
  • Pale, puffy, dramatic edema
  • Treatment: corticosteroids
Nephritic (AGN): blood and pressure
  • School-age, 5 to 15 years
  • Tea-colored urine, gross hematuria with RBC casts
  • Blood pressure high
  • Low C3, elevated ASO
  • Milder, mostly facial edema
  • Treatment: supportive, control BP and fluid
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Two questions, two diagnoses
Ask the urine first. Tea-colored is blood, so nephritic. Foamy is protein, so nephrotic. Then ask the pressure. High is nephritic. Normal is nephrotic. Two questions sort the puffy child every time.
Bottom Line: AGN is a school-age child with tea-colored urine, high BP, low C3, high ASO, 1 to 3 weeks post-strep. Supportive care, and most recover.
05

Hemolytic uremic syndrome

Discrimination axis: bloody diarrhea, then the triad.

HUS follows a gut infection. Then the kidney fails. Shiga toxin from E. coli O157:H7 damages small vessels. Sources are undercooked beef, unpasteurized milk or juice, and contaminated produce or water.

The triad

1. Hemolytic anemia

Red cells are shredded by fibrin strands. Schistocytes on smear, high LDH, low haptoglobin.

2. Thrombocytopenia

Platelets are consumed, below 150,000. Petechiae, bruising, bleeding tendency.

3. Acute kidney injury

Oliguria, rising BUN and creatinine. Dialysis if the kidney fails.

The course

  • 5 to 10 days before HUS: bloody diarrhea, often described as grape jelly, with abdominal pain and vomiting
  • Then: pallor, petechiae, bruising, oliguria, edema, lethargy, irritability
  • Severe cases: hypertension, seizures, altered mental status, multi-organ involvement
  • Most common in children under 5 years, especially 2 to 4 years
  • Outbreaks link to picnics, undercooked burgers, petting zoos, contaminated water

Diagnosis

  • CBC: anemia, thrombocytopenia, schistocytes on smear
  • Chemistry: elevated BUN and creatinine, hyperkalemia, metabolic acidosis
  • Hemolysis markers: elevated LDH, low haptoglobin, elevated indirect bilirubin
  • Stool culture or PCR for E. coli O157:H7 or Shiga toxin
  • Urinalysis: hematuria, proteinuria

Treatment

  • Hospitalization and supportive care; there is no specific antidote
  • Careful IV fluids, since an oliguric child overloads easily
  • Packed red cells for anemia; platelets only if actively bleeding
  • Dialysis for severe AKI with overload, electrolyte derangement, or uremia
  • Antihypertensives for blood pressure
  • Monitor for neurologic changes such as seizures
What you never give in HUS

Do not give antibiotics; killing the E. coli releases more Shiga toxin. Do not give antidiarrheals like loperamide; slowing the gut prolongs toxin exposure. Do not give NSAIDs; they injure an already-hurt kidney. Support the child and let the body clear the toxin.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Bloody diarrhea, hands off the drugs
When the stem shows bloody diarrhea in a toddler, expect an order for an antibiotic or loperamide. That order is the trap. Hold both and call the provider. Antibiotics release more toxin, and antimotility drugs trap it.
Bottom Line: HUS is bloody diarrhea, then pallor, bruising, and oliguria. The triad is anemia, low platelets, and AKI. Supportive care only, and no antibiotics, antidiarrheals, or NSAIDs.
06

Acute kidney injury

Discrimination axis: where the problem sits.

AKI is a sudden drop in kidney function, over hours to days. Creatinine rises. Urine output falls. It is often reversible if you catch the cause. Always ask where the problem comes from.

Category Where Common causes in kids
Pre-renal (most common in kids) Reduced blood flow to the kidneys Dehydration, hemorrhage, shock, sepsis, heart failure, burns
Intrinsic Damage inside the kidney AGN, HUS, acute tubular necrosis, nephrotoxic drugs (vancomycin, aminoglycosides, NSAIDs, contrast)
Post-renal Obstruction after the kidney Posterior urethral valves (infants), kidney stones, tumor, severe reflux

What you see

  • Oliguria (<1 mL/kg/hr in infants, <0.5 mL/kg/hr in a child) or anuria
  • Rising creatinine and BUN
  • Fluid overload: weight gain, edema, hypertension, crackles, hepatomegaly
  • Hyperkalemia, life-threatening, with peaked T waves on EKG
  • Metabolic acidosis, hyperphosphatemia, hypocalcemia
  • Lethargy, poor feeding, anorexia

Treatment

  • Fix the underlying cause: rehydrate, remove nephrotoxins, relieve obstruction
  • Renal ultrasound to rule out obstruction and size the kidneys
  • Fluids: replace if pre-renal dehydration, restrict if overloaded
  • Correct electrolytes; treat hyperkalemia with binding resins or IV insulin and glucose
  • Avoid nephrotoxic drugs and dose-adjust for renal function
  • Dialysis for severe cases that meet AEIOU

AEIOU: when dialysis is indicated

  • Acidosis, refractory and metabolic
  • Electrolytes, severe hyperkalemia over 6.5 not responding
  • Ingestion of a dialyzable toxin (salicylates, lithium, methanol)
  • Overload of fluid causing pulmonary edema or heart failure
  • Uremia with encephalopathy, pericarditis, or bleeding
Hyperkalemia is the emergency

Potassium above 6.0 mEq/L in an oliguric child can stop the heart. The EKG shows peaked T waves, a widening QRS, then bradycardia. Give calcium gluconate first to stabilize the cardiac membrane. Then shift potassium in with insulin, glucose, and albuterol. Remove it with sodium polystyrene sulfonate or dialysis.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Calcium protects, it does not lower
High potassium with EKG changes needs calcium gluconate first. It guards the heart while other drugs work. Insulin and glucose then shift potassium into cells. Kayexalate or dialysis actually removes it. Order matters: protect, shift, remove.
Bottom Line: AKI is a sudden drop in function. Sort it pre-renal, intrinsic, or post-renal, and fix the cause. AEIOU calls for dialysis. Hyperkalemia is the priority.
07

Chronic kidney disease

Discrimination axis: months, not hours, and it does not reverse.

CKD is progressive loss over at least three months. Nephrons drop out. The rest compensate, then fail. In children the cause is usually congenital, not diabetes.

Common pediatric causes

  • Congenital anomalies (hypoplasia, dysplasia, polycystic kidney disease)
  • Obstructive uropathy (posterior urethral valves, severe reflux)
  • Reflux nephropathy
  • Focal segmental glomerulosclerosis
  • Nephronophthisis, Alport syndrome
  • Long-term sequelae of HUS or severe AKI

Stages by GFR

StageGFRWhat it means
Stage 190 or higherKidney damage with normal GFR
Stage 260 to 89Mild loss
Stage 330 to 59Moderate loss
Stage 415 to 29Severe loss, prepare for replacement
Stage 5 (ESRD)Below 15Dialysis or transplant required

What you see

  • Growth failure, the hallmark pediatric complication
  • Anemia from low erythropoietin, causing fatigue and pallor
  • Renal osteodystrophy: low active vitamin D, high phosphate, secondary hyperparathyroidism
  • Hypertension
  • Acidosis and hyperkalemia in later stages
  • Developmental delay, anorexia, nausea, uremic symptoms

Management priorities

  • Growth: adequate calories, protein, vitamin D, and growth hormone for severe delay
  • Anemia: erythropoietin-stimulating agents with iron
  • Bone disease: phosphate binders with meals, plus active vitamin D (calcitriol)
  • Blood pressure: ACE inhibitors, kidney-protective and aggressive
  • Nutrition: renal dietitian, low phosphate, low potassium, appropriate protein
  • ESRD transition: dialysis access and transplant evaluation
Transplant is the goal

Transplant gives children the best outcomes in end-stage disease. Growth, cognition, and life expectancy all beat chronic dialysis. Living-donor transplants, often from a parent, are preferred. A pre-emptive transplant before dialysis is ideal when a donor is ready.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Phosphate binders go with food
Binders trap phosphate in the meal so it is not absorbed. Given with food, they work. Given on an empty stomach, they do nothing. When a parent says they dose it at bedtime alone, that is your teaching moment.
Bottom Line: Pediatric CKD is usually congenital. Manage growth, anemia, bone disease, BP, and nutrition. In end-stage disease, transplant is the goal.
08

What you do, in order

Every renal child gets the same core watch. Then each disorder adds one thing you cannot forget.

  1. 1
    Daily weight. Same scale, same time, same clothing. Gain is fluid, not growth, in the sick child. A 1 kg jump in a day is about 1 L retained.
  2. 2
    Strict intake and output. Measure every source. Weigh diapers, where 1 g equals 1 mL.
  3. 3
    Blood pressure every shift. Use an age-appropriate cuff, about 40 percent of the arm circumference. Check more often when it is high.
  4. 4
    Urine each void. Note color and dipstick for protein and blood. Foamy points to protein; tea-colored points to blood.
  5. 5
    Infection precautions on steroids. Hand hygiene, screen for fever, limit sick visitors. The nephrotic child has no reserve.
  6. 6
    Potassium in AKI. Watch the level and the EKG. Peaked T waves mean act now.

The one thing per disorder

Nephrotic: infection is the killer. Watch for spontaneous bacterial peritonitis: fever, abdominal pain, distension.

AGN: monitor BP closely, enforce fluid and sodium restriction, and restrict activity in the acute phase.

HUS: no antibiotics, antidiarrheals, or NSAIDs. Watch for seizures. Give platelets only if actively bleeding.

AKI: review every med for nephrotoxicity and dosing. Watch potassium and the EKG.

CKD: plot height and weight every visit. Give phosphate binders with meals.

Dialysis: protect the fistula arm. No BPs, IVs, or draws. Feel the thrill and hear the bruit each shift.

Medications

Prednisone
Corticosteroid, nephrotic

First line for minimal change disease. Dose 2 mg/kg/day, max 60 mg, until proteinuria clears, then taper.

Watch Cushingoid features, mood changes, appetite, glucose, growth, infection. Never stop abruptly.

Furosemide
Loop diuretic

For edema in nephrotic, AGN, and AKI. Often paired with albumin in nephrotic syndrome.

Monitor potassium, BP, weight, and I&O. Use caution when volume-depleted.

Albumin 25%
Volume expander

For severe hypoalbuminemia with edema or hypotension in nephrotic syndrome.

Pulls fluid into the vessels; follow with a diuretic. Watch for overload during the infusion.

Antihypertensives
Blood pressure control

ACE inhibitors are first line for AGN and CKD; they protect the kidney beyond BP.

Amlodipine helps severe acute hypertension. Monitor potassium, creatinine, BP.

Erythropoietin
Anemia of CKD

Stimulates red cell production, given SubQ or IV. Target hemoglobin 10 to 11.

Needs adequate iron, so it is paired with iron. Overshoot raises stroke risk.

Phosphate binders
Bone disease in CKD

Calcium carbonate or sevelamer bind dietary phosphate. Give with meals to work.

Between meals they give no benefit. Pair with active vitamin D.

Bottom Line: Daily weights, strict I&O, BP, and urine for all. Then one per disorder: infection for nephrotic, BP for AGN, no antibiotics for HUS, potassium for AKI, growth for CKD.
09

What families take home

Chronic renal disease needs partners, not just patients. Teach the disease, the drugs, the diet, and the red flags.

Teach the disease

  • Explain the diagnosis in plain, non-alarming words
  • Nephrotic: minimal change disease usually responds, and many children outgrow it; relapses are common but respond again
  • AGN: post-strep cases usually resolve fully in weeks to months
  • HUS: most recover, but a minority progress to CKD, so follow-up matters
  • CKD: frame it as management, not loss

Medication adherence

  • Use pill organizers, calendars, and phone reminders
  • Prednisone: give with food, never stop abruptly, taper on schedule
  • Phosphate binders: always with meals
  • Antihypertensives: daily, even when BP feels fine

Diet

  • Nephrotic and AGN: low sodium, no added salt, check labels
  • CKD: low phosphate, low potassium, appropriate protein per dietitian
  • Work with a pediatric renal dietitian for a tailored plan

When to call or return

Fever above 38.5 C, especially in a nephrotic child on steroids

Bloody or tea-colored urine

Foamy urine, weight gain, and a puffy face, which signals a nephrotic relapse

Decreased urination or no wet diaper for 6 or more hours

Headache, vision changes, or severe high BP

Abdominal pain with fever, a peritonitis risk in nephrotic children

Partner with the child

Older children and teens manage better when they take part. Explain labs, medications, and self-checks at their level. Praise small wins like taking meds on time. Connect families to support groups, a 504 plan at school, and social work. Autonomy plus understanding builds lifelong adherence.

Bottom Line: Teach the disease, drugs, diet, and red flags. Loop in school, nutrition, and social work. Long-term follow-up is the rule.
