Pediatric Skin Infections

Bacterial, viral, and fungal skin infections in children. From honey-crusted impetigo to tinea capitis to neonatal HSV — recognize, treat, and contain the spread.

6 Sections 14 Quiz Questions ~11 Min Read

Learning Objectives

Recognize the classic presentations of impetigo, cellulitis, MRSA, and folliculitis.
Differentiate HSV-1, HSV-2, warts, and molluscum contagiosum by appearance and course.
Identify tinea capitis, tinea pedis, tinea corporis, and candidiasis and their required treatments.
Apply the correct antibiotic, antiviral, or antifungal for each infection type.
Implement contact precautions and school/daycare return criteria to prevent spread.
Counsel families on home skin care, medication adherence, and when to seek urgent evaluation.

High-Yield Summary

Eight facts that cover nearly every pediatric skin infection question.

  • Impetigo: Staph aureus or GAS. Honey-colored crusts on face/extremities, highly contagious. Topical mupirocin for mild; oral cephalexin for widespread.
  • Cellulitis: deeper dermal/subq infection. Warm, red, tender, swollen; may have fever. Oral cephalexin or clindamycin; IV if systemic or periorbital.
  • MRSA: methicillin-resistant Staph. Community-acquired (CA-MRSA) common. Abscess = incision & drainage + abx (clindamycin, TMP-SMX); vancomycin for severe.
  • HSV-1 vs HSV-2: HSV-1 classically orolabial (cold sores, gingivostomatitis in toddlers); HSV-2 typically genital. Neonatal HSV is severe (skin, CNS, disseminated) — IV acyclovir immediately.
  • Warts (verruca, HPV): benign, self-limiting; cryotherapy, salicylic acid, or observation. Molluscum contagiosum = dome-shaped papules with central umbilication; usually resolves without treatment.
  • Tinea capitis: scalp ringworm; Trichophyton tonsurans. Requires ORAL antifungal (griseofulvin or terbinafine) — topical alone is ineffective. Selenium sulfide shampoo as adjunct.
  • Tinea pedis & corporis: topical antifungals (clotrimazole, terbinafine) for 2–4 weeks. Keep skin dry.
  • Candidiasis: oral thrush → nystatin suspension (swish/swallow or apply to oral mucosa). Diaper candidiasis → nystatin ointment or clotrimazole; keep area dry and clean.

Bacterial Skin Infections

Staph and Strep are the stars of the show

Most pediatric bacterial skin infections come from Staphylococcus aureus (including MRSA) or Group A Streptococcus (GAS, S. pyogenes). They range from superficial and limited (impetigo) to deep and systemic (cellulitis with sepsis). Early recognition and the correct antibiotic prevent escalation.

Impetigo

Superficial
Appearance

Honey-colored crusts on erythematous base; vesicles or pustules that rupture; classically on face (around nose/mouth) and extremities.

Cause

Staph aureus (most common) or GAS.

Treatment
  • Mild/localized: topical mupirocin (Bactroban) TID × 5–7 days
  • Widespread: oral cephalexin or dicloxacillin × 7 days
  • Keep area clean; gently wash crusts with soap and water
Spread

Highly contagious via direct contact or fomites. Return to school/daycare 24 hours after starting antibiotics and lesions are covered.

Cellulitis

Deeper
Appearance

Warm, red, tender, swollen area with poorly defined borders. May have fever, lymphangitic streaking, regional lymphadenopathy.

Cause

Staph aureus, GAS; sometimes H. influenzae type b in unvaccinated.

Treatment
  • Oral: cephalexin or clindamycin × 10 days
  • IV for systemic illness, periorbital/orbital cellulitis, infant, or failure of oral
  • Elevate the affected extremity; mark borders to monitor spread
  • Warm compresses
Red Flag

Periorbital/orbital cellulitis can spread to brain — IV antibiotics, CT imaging, ophtho consult.

MRSA

Resistant
Appearance

Often a fluctuant abscess ("spider bite" complaint), painful, red, warm. May have surrounding cellulitis.

Risk Factors

Community-acquired (CA-MRSA) is common in healthy children; gym / sports / crowded settings.

Treatment
  • Incision & drainage is primary for abscesses >5 mm
  • Oral: clindamycin or TMP-SMX (Bactrim)
  • IV vancomycin for severe/systemic disease
  • Decolonization (chlorhexidine baths, nasal mupirocin) for recurrent
Prevention

Hand hygiene, don't share towels/razors, cover wounds, clean equipment.

Folliculitis

Superficial
Appearance

Small pustules or papules at hair follicles — scalp, back, thighs. Non-tender or mildly itchy.

Cause

Usually Staph aureus; "hot tub folliculitis" from Pseudomonas.

Treatment
  • Warm compresses, gentle cleansing
  • Topical antibiotics (mupirocin) or clindamycin lotion
  • Oral antibiotics only if widespread or deep (furunculosis)
Mark the Border of Cellulitis

Use a skin-safe marker to trace the edge of the erythema when the child is admitted. Reassess every few hours. If the redness spreads past your line, escalate — the antibiotic isn't working or the infection is deeper than expected (think necrotizing fasciitis, toxic shock syndrome). Also watch for fever, tachycardia, hypotension.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Honey-Colored Crusts = Impetigo
The golden, flaking crust over a red base around the nose and mouth of a school-age child is impetigo until proven otherwise. Topical mupirocin handles most mild cases; oral antibiotics are for widespread or resistant lesions. Classic NCLEX image to recognize on sight.
🎯 Bottom Line: Impetigo = honey crusts (mupirocin). Cellulitis = mark borders (cephalexin). MRSA = abscess needs I&D + clinda/Bactrim. Folliculitis = pustules at follicles.

Viral Skin Infections

HSV, warts, and molluscum

HSV-1 (Oral / "Cold Sores")

Herpes Simplex
Appearance

Clustered vesicles on red base around lips and mouth. Prodrome of tingling. In toddlers, primary infection = gingivostomatitis (painful mouth sores + fever + drooling).

Treatment
  • Mild: supportive (pain meds, cool fluids)
  • Severe or immunocompromised: oral/IV acyclovir
  • Gingivostomatitis: hydration priority (kids refuse to drink from pain)
Precautions

Contact precautions for open lesions. Avoid kissing infants.

HSV-2 (Genital) & Neonatal

Serious
Appearance

Painful vesicles in genital/anal area in adolescents. Neonatal HSV: skin lesions, encephalitis, sepsis-like illness in a newborn.

Treatment
  • Adolescents: oral acyclovir or valacyclovir
  • Neonatal HSV: IV acyclovir immediately — delays mean death or severe neurologic damage
  • Maternal HSV at delivery: consider C-section
Red Flag

Any neonate with fever, seizures, or vesicles — rule out HSV. Don't wait for cultures to start acyclovir.

Verruca (Warts / HPV)

Benign
Appearance

Rough, cauliflower-like papules. Common warts on hands; plantar on soles (painful); flat on face.

Treatment
  • Observation — up to 2/3 resolve spontaneously in 2 years
  • Topical salicylic acid (OTC) daily × weeks
  • Cryotherapy (liquid nitrogen) in office, 2–4 week intervals
  • Duct tape occlusion is an effective adjunct
Prevention

Avoid picking/scratching; HPV vaccine starting age 9–11 protects against genital and cancer-causing types.

Molluscum Contagiosum

Benign
Appearance

Dome-shaped, skin-colored papules with central umbilication (dimple). Trunk, extremities, sometimes face.

Treatment
  • Usually self-limiting — resolves in 6–12 months without treatment
  • If bothersome: cantharidin (in office), cryotherapy, curettage
  • Avoid scratching to prevent spread (autoinoculation)
Spread

Direct contact; sharing towels, swimming pools. Keep covered; may attend school.

Neonatal HSV Is Always an Emergency

A newborn with fever, lethargy, seizures, poor feeding, or vesicular skin lesions — even if the mother had no known HSV history — must be evaluated for neonatal HSV. Start empiric IV acyclovir immediately; don't wait for PCR or culture results. Mortality and long-term neurologic outcomes depend on time to treatment.

🎯 Bottom Line: HSV-1 = cold sores / gingivostomatitis. Neonatal HSV = IV acyclovir now. Warts = salicylic acid or cryo. Molluscum = umbilicated dome, usually self-resolving.

Fungal Skin Infections

Tinea (dermatophytes) and candida (yeast)

Tinea Capitis

Scalp
Appearance

Scaly patches with hair loss on scalp; broken hair shafts ("black dot"); may have kerion (boggy inflammatory mass).

Cause

Trichophyton tonsurans (most common in US). Spread via shared combs, hats, pillows.

Treatment
  • ORAL antifungal REQUIRED — topical alone does NOT penetrate the hair shaft
  • Griseofulvin or terbinafine × 6–8 weeks
  • Selenium sulfide 2.5% shampoo 2–3×/week as adjunct (reduces shedding)
  • Monitor LFTs during long-course therapy

Tinea Corporis & Pedis

Body / Foot
Appearance

Corporis: ring-shaped red patches with clearing center and raised scaly border ("ringworm"). Pedis (athlete's foot): itching between toes, scaling, maceration.

Treatment
  • Topical antifungals: clotrimazole, terbinafine, miconazole × 2–4 weeks
  • Continue for 1–2 weeks AFTER rash clears to prevent recurrence
  • Keep skin clean and dry; change socks/shoes daily; cotton socks
  • Oral antifungals for widespread or refractory cases

Candidiasis (Oral Thrush)

Yeast
Appearance

White plaques on oral mucosa that don't scrape off easily (if they do, bleeding base). Tongue, palate, buccal mucosa.

Risk Factors

Infants, recent antibiotics, inhaled steroids, immunocompromised. Common in healthy infants up to 6 months.

Treatment
  • Nystatin oral suspension — swish and swallow (or apply to oral mucosa in infants) QID × 7–14 days
  • Treat mother's breast simultaneously if breastfeeding
  • Boil/replace pacifiers, nipples
  • Fluconazole for refractory cases or immunocompromised

Diaper Candidiasis

Diaper Area
Appearance

Beefy red patches in diaper area with satellite lesions (smaller spots outside main rash); involves skin folds (unlike irritant diaper dermatitis which spares folds).

Treatment
  • Topical nystatin or clotrimazole cream × 7–10 days
  • Apply with each diaper change; then protective barrier (zinc oxide)
  • Keep area dry — frequent diaper changes, air time when possible
  • Avoid baby wipes with fragrance/alcohol during treatment
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Tinea Capitis: Topical Alone Never Works
The fungus lives inside the hair shaft, where topical creams can't reach. Tinea capitis always requires systemic (oral) antifungal therapy for 6–8 weeks — griseofulvin or terbinafine are first-line. Selenium sulfide shampoo is an adjunct to reduce spore shedding, but it doesn't cure the infection alone. This is a common NCLEX distractor trap.
Candida vs. Irritant Diaper Rash

Candida loves skin folds — bright red, with small satellite lesions, and involves the inguinal creases. Irritant diaper dermatitis (from urine/stool contact) spares the folds and is on convex surfaces. If a rash isn't responding to barrier cream after 2–3 days, consider adding antifungal.

🎯 Bottom Line: Tinea capitis = ORAL antifungal (6–8 wk). Tinea corporis/pedis = topical. Oral thrush = nystatin swish/swallow. Diaper candidiasis = satellite lesions + skin folds → nystatin cream.

Assessment & Diagnostics

When to swab, when to scrape, when to just treat

Systematic Skin Assessment

  • Location (face, trunk, diaper area, scalp) and distribution (clustered, scattered, linear).
  • Morphology — macule, papule, vesicle, pustule, crust, plaque, scale.
  • Color, size, borders (well-defined vs. diffuse), and satellite lesions.
  • Associated symptoms: itching, pain, fever, swelling, lymphadenopathy.
  • Recent exposures: daycare, school, pets, sports, new soaps/clothing, medications, camping/hiking.

Diagnostic Tests

Test What It Detects When to Use
Wound culture & sensitivity Bacterial species + antibiotic susceptibility Non-responding cellulitis, MRSA abscess, recurrent infections
KOH prep Fungal hyphae under microscope Suspected tinea; scrape scales from active edge
Fungal culture Species identification Tinea capitis (takes 2–4 weeks); guides oral antifungal choice
Wood's lamp Fluorescence (some tinea species fluoresce) Quick office screen; T. tonsurans (US common) does NOT fluoresce — false negatives common
Tzanck smear Multinucleated giant cells (HSV, VZV) Historical; PCR is preferred now
Viral PCR Specific viral DNA (HSV, VZV, enterovirus) Suspected HSV especially in neonates
CBC with differential Leukocytosis in bacterial; eosinophilia in allergic Systemic signs, hospitalization
CRP / ESR Inflammation markers Cellulitis progression, osteomyelitis concern
IgE Allergic component Atopic dermatitis workup (not typically infection)
You Don't Always Need a Test

Many pediatric skin infections are diagnosed clinically — impetigo, folliculitis, tinea corporis, oral thrush, and molluscum are usually visible on exam. Reserve testing for atypical presentations, treatment failures, or cases with systemic involvement. Avoid over-ordering.

🎯 Bottom Line: Most skin infections = clinical diagnosis. Culture for non-responders and MRSA. KOH for tinea. PCR for neonatal HSV.

Treatment at a Glance

The common medications and their targets
Mupirocin (Bactroban)
Topical Antibiotic

Use: impetigo, folliculitis, MRSA decolonization (nasal).

Dose: apply TID × 5–10 days.

Bacitracin and triple antibiotic ointment are alternatives for minor wounds.

Cephalexin (Keflex)
Oral Antibiotic

Use: widespread impetigo, cellulitis, non-MRSA Staph.

Dose: 25–50 mg/kg/day divided QID × 7–10 days.

Watch for allergy (penicillin cross-reactivity).

Clindamycin or TMP-SMX
Oral (MRSA Coverage)

Use: CA-MRSA abscess, after I&D.

Clindamycin can cause C. diff — watch for diarrhea.

TMP-SMX contraindicated in infants <2 months.

Vancomycin IV
Severe MRSA

Use: sepsis, refractory cellulitis, severe MRSA.

Monitor trough levels, renal function, red man syndrome.

Acyclovir
Antiviral

Use: HSV (oral / IV). Neonatal HSV = IV 60 mg/kg/day.

Adequate hydration to prevent nephrotoxicity.

Nystatin
Topical Antifungal (Yeast)

Use: oral thrush (suspension), diaper candidiasis (ointment/cream).

Swish/swallow in older children; apply to oral mucosa with a clean finger or cotton swab in infants.

Clotrimazole / Terbinafine
Topical Antifungal (Dermatophyte)

Use: tinea corporis, pedis, cruris.

Apply BID × 2–4 weeks, continue 1–2 weeks after rash clears.

Griseofulvin / Terbinafine (PO)
Oral Antifungal

Use: tinea capitis (essential), extensive tinea, onychomycosis.

Griseofulvin with FATTY meal for absorption. Monitor LFTs on long courses.

Salicylic Acid / Cryotherapy
Warts

OTC salicylic acid pads or liquid nightly. Cryotherapy in office every 2–4 weeks.

Patience — results take weeks.

🎯 Bottom Line: Mupirocin for impetigo. Cephalexin for cellulitis. Clindamycin/Bactrim for MRSA. Acyclovir for HSV. Nystatin for yeast. Oral antifungal for tinea capitis.

Family Education & Prevention

Contagion control, home care, and when to return

General Home Care

  • Keep lesions clean and dry; gentle wash with soap and water.
  • Cover draining lesions with dressings to prevent spread.
  • Don't pick, squeeze, or scratch — spreads infection and causes scarring.
  • Wash hands before and after applying topical medications.
  • Apply medications for the full prescribed duration, even if rash improves.
  • Use the child's own towel, washcloth, razor, and bedding; launder in hot water.

Contagion & Return to School / Daycare

Infection When to Return
Impetigo 24 hours after starting antibiotics + lesions covered
Cellulitis Per pediatrician; usually afebrile and improving
MRSA abscess Wound covered, drainage controlled; no sports until healed
HSV (cold sores) Lesions covered or crusted; avoid contact sports/kissing
Warts / Molluscum No exclusion; cover lesions if possible
Tinea corporis / pedis 24 hours after starting antifungal; cover lesions for sports
Tinea capitis Usually can attend once treatment has started and hats/combs aren't shared
Thrush No exclusion; treatment at home

Prevention

Hand hygiene — most effective single intervention.

Don't share towels, combs, hats, razors, makeup.

Cover wounds; gym/sports wipes, clean equipment between athletes.

Keep skin dry (change wet socks/swimwear promptly) — prevents fungal.

Vaccinate: varicella, HPV, flu.

Treat household contacts if recurrent MRSA (decolonization).

When to Return to the Provider

  • Fever or worsening symptoms on appropriate therapy.
  • Spreading redness, streaking, or increasing pain (cellulitis escalation).
  • Abscess forming or enlarging.
  • Difficulty eating/drinking (gingivostomatitis, thrush).
  • No improvement after 3–5 days of appropriate treatment.
  • Any neonate with fever, lethargy, vesicles, or seizures — urgent ED evaluation.
Normalize the Itch Talk

Most skin infections are uncomfortable and itchy — and kids scratch. Shame or frustration from parents worsens the emotional impact. Reassure that scratching is normal but spreads infection, and use practical tools: keep nails short, cotton gloves at night, distraction, cool compresses. Focus on the resolution, not the child's "misbehavior."

🎯 Bottom Line: Clean and cover, finish the full course, hand hygiene, don't share personal items. Specific return-to-school rules by infection. Call for fever, spread, or neonatal symptoms.
