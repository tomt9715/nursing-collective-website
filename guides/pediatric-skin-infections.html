Pediatric Skin Infections

Three families of rash, three drug classes, and the return-to-school rule that decides who goes back Monday. Sort the rash first; the treatment falls out of the sorting.

Reading Time
15-18 minutes
Difficulty Level
Intermediate

The Nursing Collective

By the end, without notes

  • Sort a childhood rash into bacterial, fungal, or viral from its look alone
  • Match each one to an antibiotic, antifungal, or antiviral, and say why the other two classes fail
  • Say which infections need a systemic drug, not a cream: tinea capitis, cellulitis, neonatal HSV
  • State the return-to-school rule for each contagious one without looking it up
  • Recognize neonatal HSV and name the single action it demands
  • Separate Candida diaper rash from irritant dermatitis on two findings
01

What actually gets you

Pediatric skin infections are not hard to list. They are hard to tell apart. Almost every point you lose comes from one of three places.

You did not sort the rash first

Bacterial, fungal, or viral. That choice picks the drug class. A honey crust is bacterial and wants an antibiotic. A scaly ring is fungal and wants an antifungal. Grouped vesicles are herpes or varicella and want an antiviral. Pick the wrong family and every treatment answer is wrong too.

You missed the contagious one

Impetigo, tinea, HSV, and molluscum all spread. The question often is not the drug. It is when the child returns to school and what you teach the family about hand hygiene and covering the lesions. Skip that and you skip the point.

You under-called a deep infection

A cream is fine for impetigo on the surface. It is not fine for cellulitis in the dermis or an MRSA abscess. Those need a systemic drug, and the abscess needs draining. Treating deep as if it were shallow lets the infection climb.

Bottom Line: Stop memorizing skin infections as a list. Sort each rash into bacterial, fungal, or viral, then ask how deep it goes and whether it spreads. The drug and the teaching follow from those two questions.
02

The four things

For every infection below, say four things with the book closed. The one-sentence definition. The clue you would see in a child. The priority nursing action. The first-line treatment with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Here they are, grouped by family.

Bacterial

Condition Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line treatment + teaching
Impetigo A surface staph or strep infection of the skin. Honey-colored crusts around a preschooler's nose and mouth. Cover the lesions; teach hand hygiene. Topical mupirocin for mild; oral cephalexin if widespread. Highly contagious.
Cellulitis A deeper infection of the dermis and fat. Warm, red, tender, spreading area with fuzzy borders, maybe fever. Mark the border; watch for spread. Systemic antibiotics, oral cephalexin; IV if severe. Mark and reassess.
MRSA Methicillin-resistant staph, often a walled-off abscess. A painful "spider bite" that is red, warm, and fluctuant. Get it drained. Incision and drainage plus clindamycin or TMP-SMX. Do not rely on antibiotics alone.
Scalded skin (SSSS) Staph toxins split the skin so it peels in sheets. A febrile infant with red, tender skin that shears off (positive Nikolsky). Treat like a burn; protect skin and fluids. IV anti-staph antibiotics. Handle the skin gently.

Fungal

Tinea capitis Ringworm of the scalp, living inside the hair shaft. Scaly bald patches with broken "black dot" hairs, maybe a boggy kerion. Start a systemic drug; topical will not reach it. Oral griseofulvin or terbinafine; selenium sulfide shampoo as an adjunct.
Tinea corporis / pedis Ringworm of the body or feet, on the surface. A ring with a clear center and scaly raised edge, or itchy macerated toes. Keep the skin clean and dry. Topical clotrimazole or terbinafine. Keep applying after it clears.

HSV-1 Herpes simplex on the lips and mouth. Clustered vesicles on a red base, or a toddler with mouth sores, fever, and drooling. Protect hydration. Supportive care; acyclovir if severe. Avoid kissing infants.

Copy this down

Three families, three drugs. Bacterial takes an antibiotic. Fungal takes an antifungal. Viral takes an antiviral or nothing. Write that line first; it decides most of the exam.

03

Bacterial: how deep does it go

Discrimination axis: surface or deep, and drained or not.

Almost all of these are Staphylococcus aureus, including MRSA, or Group A Streptococcus. The families all look red and angry. Depth is what changes your move.

Impetigo: on the surface

Superficial, and the classic one to recognize on sight.

  • Honey-colored crusts on a red base; vesicles or pustules that rupture
  • Classically on the face, around the nose and mouth, and on extremities
  • Cause: Staph aureus most often, or GAS
  • Mild or localized: topical mupirocin (Bactroban) TID for 5 to 7 days
  • Widespread: oral cephalexin or dicloxacillin for 7 days
  • Gently wash crusts with soap and water; keep the area clean
Cellulitis: into the dermis

Deeper, and it can turn systemic.

  • Warm, red, tender, swollen with poorly defined borders
  • May bring fever, lymphangitic streaking, regional lymphadenopathy
  • Cause: Staph aureus, GAS; sometimes H. influenzae type b in the unvaccinated
  • Oral cephalexin or clindamycin for 10 days
  • IV for systemic illness, periorbital or orbital cellulitis, an infant, or oral failure
  • Elevate the limb; mark the borders; warm compresses
MRSA: resistant, walled off

Draining beats dosing here.

  • Often a fluctuant abscess, the "spider bite" complaint, painful and warm
  • May sit under surrounding cellulitis
  • Community-acquired (CA-MRSA) is common in healthy children; gym, sports, crowding
  • Incision and drainage is primary for abscesses over 5 mm
  • Oral clindamycin or TMP-SMX (Bactrim); IV vancomycin if severe or systemic
  • Decolonize recurrent cases: chlorhexidine baths, nasal mupirocin
Scalded skin (SSSS): peeling

Staph toxin, not local infection of the peeling skin.

  • Widespread redness, then superficial blistering and peeling
  • Positive Nikolsky sign; the top skin shears with gentle pressure
  • Fever and irritability; hits infants and young children
  • Manage like a burn: IV anti-staph antibiotics, fluids, gentle skin care
  • Handle the child carefully; the skin barrier is gone
Folliculitis: at the follicles

The mildest of the group.

  • Small pustules or papules at hair follicles; scalp, back, thighs
  • Non-tender or mildly itchy
  • Usually Staph aureus; "hot tub folliculitis" from Pseudomonas
  • Warm compresses and gentle cleansing
  • Topical mupirocin or clindamycin lotion
  • Oral antibiotics only if widespread or deep (furunculosis)
The axis that separates them

Depth decides the drug. Surface impetigo takes a cream. Cellulitis in the dermis takes a systemic antibiotic. An MRSA abscess takes a scalpel first, then an antibiotic. Scalded skin is toxin-driven and takes IV therapy. When the redness is spreading or the child is sick, you are past creams.

Mark the border of cellulitis

Trace the edge of the redness with a skin-safe marker on admission. Reassess every few hours.

If it spreads past your line, escalate. The antibiotic is not working, or the infection is deeper than it looked, such as necrotizing fasciitis or toxic shock syndrome. Watch for fever, tachycardia, and hypotension. Periorbital or orbital cellulitis can reach the brain; it needs IV antibiotics, CT imaging, and an ophthalmology consult.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Honey Crusts = Impetigo
The golden, flaking crust over a red base around a school-age child's nose and mouth is impetigo until proven otherwise. Topical mupirocin handles most mild cases. Oral antibiotics are for widespread or resistant lesions.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Mark, Then Reassess
The single best nursing action for admitted cellulitis is a pen line around the redness. It turns a vague "is this worse?" into a yes or no. Spread past the line means the drug is failing or the infection runs deeper than you thought.
Bottom Line: Impetigo equals honey crusts and mupirocin. Cellulitis gets a marked border and a systemic antibiotic. MRSA abscess gets drained plus clindamycin or Bactrim. Scalded skin is IV therapy and burn-style care.
04

Fungal: scalp or not, folds or not

Discrimination axis: does it need an oral drug, and is it Candida or irritant.

Two groups live here. Tinea is a dermatophyte, the ringworm mold. Candida is a yeast. The two decisions that carry points are scalp versus skin, and Candida versus irritant.

Tinea capitis: the scalp exception

The one tinea that a cream cannot cure.

  • Scaly patches with hair loss; broken shafts leave "black dots"
  • May form a kerion, a boggy inflammatory mass
  • Trichophyton tonsurans is most common in the US; shared combs, hats, pillows
  • Oral antifungal required; the fungus sits inside the hair shaft
  • Griseofulvin or terbinafine for 6 to 8 weeks
  • Selenium sulfide 2.5% shampoo 2 to 3 times a week as an adjunct; monitor LFTs on long courses
Tinea corporis and pedis: surface

Topical is enough for these.

  • Corporis: a ring with a clearing center and raised scaly border
  • Pedis: athlete's foot, itching between the toes, scaling, maceration
  • Topical antifungals: clotrimazole, terbinafine, miconazole for 2 to 4 weeks
  • Keep going 1 to 2 weeks after the rash clears to prevent recurrence
  • Keep skin clean and dry; change socks and shoes daily; cotton socks
  • Oral antifungals only for widespread or refractory cases
Oral thrush: Candida in the mouth

Yeast, not a wipe-away milk coating.

  • White plaques on the mucosa that do not scrape off easily
  • Scraping leaves a bleeding base; tongue, palate, buccal mucosa
  • Risk: infants, recent antibiotics, inhaled steroids, immunocompromise
  • Nystatin oral suspension, swish and swallow, QID for 7 to 14 days
  • Treat the mother's breast at the same time if breastfeeding
  • Boil or replace pacifiers and nipples; fluconazole if refractory
Diaper candidiasis: the folds

The rash that lives where irritant rash does not.

  • Beefy red patches with satellite lesions outside the main rash
  • Involves the skin folds; irritant dermatitis spares them
  • Topical nystatin or clotrimazole cream for 7 to 10 days
  • Apply at each change, then a barrier such as zinc oxide
  • Keep the area dry; frequent changes; air time when you can
  • Skip fragranced or alcohol wipes during treatment
The axis that separates them

Two questions do the sorting. Is the tinea on the scalp? If yes, it needs an oral drug; a cream never reaches inside the hair. Is the diaper rash in the folds with satellites? If yes, it is Candida and wants an antifungal, not a stronger barrier cream.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Tinea Capitis: Topical Alone Never Works
The fungus lives inside the hair shaft, where a cream cannot reach it. Scalp tinea always needs a systemic antifungal for 6 to 8 weeks; griseofulvin or terbinafine are first-line. Selenium sulfide shampoo only cuts spore shedding; it does not cure. This is a classic distractor.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Satellites and Folds = Candida
Candida loves warm creases, so it fills the inguinal folds and throws off small satellite spots. Irritant diaper rash rides the convex surfaces and spares the folds. If a barrier cream is not working after 2 to 3 days, add an antifungal.
Candida vs irritant diaper rash

Candida is bright red, sits in the folds, and scatters satellite lesions. Irritant dermatitis comes from urine and stool contact; it spares the folds and rides the convex surfaces. That fold pattern is the tell.

Bottom Line: Tinea capitis needs an oral antifungal for 6 to 8 weeks. Tinea corporis and pedis take a topical. Oral thrush takes nystatin swish and swallow. Diaper candidiasis shows folds and satellites and takes nystatin cream.
05

Viral: which one is an emergency

Discrimination axis: does it need an antiviral now, or will it clear on its own.

Most viral skin findings are benign and self-limiting. One is not. The job is to spot neonatal HSV fast and to reassure the family about the rest.

HSV-1: cold sores

Usually mild, but the mouth version dries kids out.

  • Clustered vesicles on a red base around the lips; a tingling prodrome
  • In toddlers, primary infection is gingivostomatitis: mouth sores, fever, drooling
  • Mild: supportive care, pain meds, cool fluids
  • Severe or immunocompromised: oral or IV acyclovir
  • Gingivostomatitis: hydration first; the child refuses to drink from pain
  • Contact precautions for open lesions; avoid kissing infants
HSV-2 and neonatal HSV

This is the one you cannot miss.

  • Adolescents: painful vesicles in the genital or anal area
  • Neonatal HSV: skin lesions, encephalitis, a sepsis-like newborn
  • Adolescents: oral acyclovir or valacyclovir
  • Neonatal HSV: IV acyclovir immediately; delay means death or neurologic damage
  • Maternal HSV at delivery: consider a C-section
  • Any neonate with fever, seizures, or vesicles: rule out HSV, do not wait for cultures
Varicella: chickenpox

The staged rash that keeps a child home.

  • Itchy vesicles on a red base, arriving in successive crops
  • Lesions in several stages at once: macules, papules, vesicles, crusts
  • Often with fever; caused by varicella-zoster virus
  • Supportive care; acyclovir for high-risk, immunocompromised, or adolescents
  • Contagious until every lesion has crusted; then return to school
  • Prevent it with the varicella vaccine
Molluscum contagiosum

The dimpled dome that resolves itself.

  • Dome-shaped, skin-colored papules with central umbilication
  • Trunk, extremities, sometimes the face
  • Usually self-limiting; clears in 6 to 12 months
  • If bothersome: cantharidin in office, cryotherapy, curettage
  • Avoid scratching to prevent autoinoculation
  • Spread by contact, shared towels, pools; keep covered, may attend school
Verruca: warts (HPV)

Benign, and patience is the plan.

  • Rough, cauliflower-like papules; common on hands
  • Plantar on the soles (painful); flat on the face
  • Observation; up to two-thirds resolve on their own within 2 years
  • Topical salicylic acid daily for weeks; duct tape occlusion helps
  • Cryotherapy in office at 2 to 4 week intervals
  • HPV vaccine from age 9 to 11 covers genital and cancer-causing types
The axis that separates them

Ask one question: is this a newborn. A neonate with fever, lethargy, poor feeding, seizures, or vesicles is neonatal HSV until proven otherwise, and the antiviral starts now. Molluscum and warts wait and watch. Most HSV-1 and varicella are supportive. The emergency is age-defined.

Neonatal HSV is always an emergency

A newborn with fever, lethargy, seizures, poor feeding, or vesicles must be worked up for HSV. This holds even when the mother has no known history.

Start empiric IV acyclovir immediately. Do not wait for PCR or culture. Mortality and long-term neurologic outcomes track with time to treatment.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Neonate + Vesicles = Acyclovir Now
A newborn with fever plus vesicles, or a sepsis-like picture, gets empiric IV acyclovir before any result comes back. Oral will not do, and topical will not do. Waiting for the PCR is the wrong answer; the drug is time-sensitive.
Bottom Line: HSV-1 is cold sores and toddler gingivostomatitis. Neonatal HSV is IV acyclovir now. Varicella stays home until every lesion crusts. Molluscum is an umbilicated dome that self-resolves; warts take patience.
06

Assessment and diagnostics

Most of these are diagnosed by looking. Testing is for the atypical rash, the treatment failure, and the sick child.

Systematic skin assessment

  • Location (face, trunk, diaper area, scalp) and distribution (clustered, scattered, linear).
  • Morphology: macule, papule, vesicle, pustule, crust, plaque, scale.
  • Color, size, borders (well-defined vs diffuse), and satellite lesions.
  • Associated symptoms: itching, pain, fever, swelling, lymphadenopathy.
  • Recent exposures: daycare, school, pets, sports, new soaps or clothing, medications, camping.

Diagnostic tests

Test What it detects When to use it
Wound culture & sensitivity Bacterial species plus antibiotic susceptibility Non-responding cellulitis, MRSA abscess, recurrent infections
KOH prep Fungal hyphae under the microscope Suspected tinea; scrape scales from the active edge
Fungal culture Species identification Tinea capitis (takes 2 to 4 weeks); guides the oral antifungal choice
Wood's lamp Fluorescence (some tinea species glow) Quick office screen; T. tonsurans does not fluoresce, so false negatives are common
Tzanck smear Multinucleated giant cells (HSV, VZV) Historical; PCR is preferred now
Viral PCR Specific viral DNA (HSV, VZV, enterovirus) Suspected HSV, especially in neonates
CBC with differential Leukocytosis in bacterial; eosinophilia in allergic Systemic signs, hospitalization
CRP / ESR Inflammation markers Cellulitis progression, osteomyelitis concern
IgE Allergic component Atopic dermatitis workup, not typically infection
You do not always need a test

Impetigo, folliculitis, tinea corporis, oral thrush, and molluscum are usually diagnosed on the exam. Save the tests for atypical presentations, treatment failures, and systemic involvement. Do not over-order.

Bottom Line: Most skin infections are a clinical diagnosis. Culture the non-responders and the MRSA. KOH the tinea. PCR the neonatal HSV.
07

Treatment at a glance

Match the class to the family, then match the route to the depth. Here are the drugs you will see and their teaching points.

Topical Antibiotic

Mupirocin (Bactroban). Impetigo, folliculitis, MRSA nasal decolonization.

Apply TID for 5 to 10 days. Bacitracin or triple antibiotic ointment for minor wounds.

Oral Antibiotic

Cephalexin (Keflex). Widespread impetigo, cellulitis, non-MRSA Staph.

25 to 50 mg/kg/day, divided QID, for 7 to 10 days. Watch penicillin cross-reactivity.

Oral, MRSA Coverage

Clindamycin or TMP-SMX. CA-MRSA abscess, after incision and drainage.

Clindamycin can cause C. diff; watch for diarrhea. TMP-SMX is off-limits under 2 months.

Severe MRSA

Vancomycin IV. Sepsis, refractory cellulitis, severe MRSA.

Monitor trough levels, renal function, and red man syndrome.

Antiviral

Acyclovir. HSV, oral or IV. Neonatal HSV is IV at 60 mg/kg/day.

Keep the child hydrated to protect the kidneys.

Topical Antifungal (Yeast)

Nystatin. Oral thrush suspension; diaper candidiasis ointment or cream.

Swish and swallow in older children; wipe onto the mucosa in infants.

Topical Antifungal (Dermatophyte)

Clotrimazole or terbinafine. Tinea corporis, pedis, cruris.

Apply BID for 2 to 4 weeks. Keep going 1 to 2 weeks after it clears.

Oral Antifungal

Griseofulvin or terbinafine (PO). Tinea capitis, extensive tinea, nails.

Give griseofulvin with a fatty meal. Monitor LFTs on long courses.

Warts

Salicylic acid or cryotherapy. OTC acid nightly; cryo every 2 to 4 weeks.

Set expectations; results take weeks.

When a cream is not enough

Escalation follows depth. Move up only as far as the infection forces you.

  1. 1
    Topical, for the surface. Localized impetigo and folliculitis clear with mupirocin. No systemic drug needed.
  2. 2
    Drain, if it is walled off. A fluctuant MRSA abscess over 5 mm gets incision and drainage first. Antibiotics alone will not empty it.
  3. 3
    Oral, for the dermis. Cellulitis and widespread impetigo take a systemic antibiotic. Add MRSA coverage when the story fits.
  4. 4
    IV, for the sick or the very young. Systemic illness, periorbital cellulitis, scalded skin, an infant, or neonatal HSV all move straight to IV.
Bottom Line: Mupirocin for impetigo. Cephalexin for cellulitis. Clindamycin or Bactrim for MRSA. Acyclovir for HSV. Nystatin for yeast. Oral antifungal for tinea capitis.
08

Family education and prevention

The exam loves the teaching, because families carry the plan home. Two things dominate: stopping the spread and knowing when to come back.

Teach it in this order

  1. 1
    Hand hygiene. The single most effective step. Wash before and after applying any topical medication.
  2. 2
    Do not share personal items. Own towel, washcloth, razor, comb, hat, bedding. Launder in hot water.
  3. 3
    Cover the lesions. Cover draining lesions with dressings. Do not pick, squeeze, or scratch; that spreads it and scars.
  4. 4
    Finish the full course. Apply or take the medication for the whole prescribed duration, even after the rash looks better.
  5. 5
    Keep skin dry. Change wet socks and swimwear promptly. Dry skin starves fungus.

Prevention that matters

  • Hand hygiene is the most effective single intervention.
  • Do not share towels, combs, hats, razors, or makeup.
  • Cover wounds; wipe down gym and sports equipment between athletes.
  • Keep skin dry to head off fungal infections.
  • Vaccinate: varicella, HPV, flu.
  • Decolonize household contacts for recurrent MRSA.

When to return to the provider

  • Fever or worsening symptoms on appropriate therapy.
  • Spreading redness, streaking, or increasing pain.
  • An abscess forming or enlarging.
  • Difficulty eating or drinking with gingivostomatitis or thrush.
  • No improvement after 3 to 5 days of treatment.
  • Any neonate with fever, lethargy, vesicles, or seizures; urgent ED.
Normalize the itch talk

These rashes itch, and kids scratch. Shame from a frustrated parent only makes it worse.

Tell the family that scratching is normal but spreads the infection. Give them tools: short nails, cotton gloves at night, distraction, cool compresses. Aim at the resolution, not at the child's behavior.

Bottom Line: Clean and cover, finish the full course, wash hands, do not share personal items. Learn the return-to-school rule per infection. Call for fever, spread, or any neonatal symptom.
