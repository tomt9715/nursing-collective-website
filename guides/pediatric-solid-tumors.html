Pediatric Solid Tumors

Two abdominal masses you must not confuse, one brain tumor that hides as a stomach bug, and the single rule that comes before any assessment.

01

What actually gets you

Pediatric solid tumors are not a memory test. They are a set of look-alikes. The questions live where two tumors overlap.

After leukemia, the tumors you see most are brain tumors and neuroblastoma. Brain tumors are the top solid tumor in children. Neuroblastoma is the most common cancer of infancy. This guide works the two prototypes. It also sits Wilms tumor next to neuroblastoma, because that is the pair you will mix up.

Points get lost here in three predictable ways. Read these first.

You palpated the abdominal mass

A firm abdominal mass in a young child can be neuroblastoma or Wilms tumor. Both have a fragile capsule. Pressing on it can rupture the capsule and seed tumor cells. This is the single most tested rule here. Do not palpate. Post a sign at the bedside. Inspect and listen only.

You could not tell neuroblastoma from Wilms

Both are an abdominal mass in a young child. That is the trap. They split on three things. Where the tumor starts. Whether it crosses the midline. What the urine shows. Miss the split and you miss the diagnosis the question wants.

You missed the brain tumor picture

Morning headache, vomiting on waking, and a clumsy gait is not a stomach bug. It is rising pressure inside the skull. That triad is medulloblastoma until imaging says otherwise. Treat the pressure as the emergency it is.

Bottom Line: Stop studying these as three diseases. Study them as three splits: neuroblastoma or Wilms, an abdominal mass or a brain tumor, and image before you tap.

For each tumor below, you should be able to say four things with the book shut. The one-sentence definition. The clue you would see in a real child. The priority action. The first-line treatment with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

03

Neuroblastoma, and how it differs from Wilms

Discrimination axis: where the tumor starts and whether it crosses the midline.

Neuroblastoma is the most common solid tumor outside the skull in children. It is the most common cancer in infants. The median age is about 18 months, and most children are under 5.

It grows from neural crest cells of the sympathetic nervous system. So it can start anywhere the sympathetic chain runs. About 65 percent begin in the adrenal gland or the retroperitoneum. The chest, neck, and pelvis are also possible.

Neuroblastoma: the sympathetic chain
  • Starts in the adrenal gland or along the sympathetic chain
  • Firm, irregular mass that often crosses the midline
  • Under 5 years, most common in infancy
  • Urine VMA and HVA are elevated
  • Often already spread at diagnosis
Wilms tumor: the kidney
  • Starts in one kidney; the name is nephroblastoma
  • Smooth flank mass that stays on one side and does not cross midline
  • Young child, usually 2 to 5 years
  • No urine catecholamines
  • Generally a better outlook
The axis that separates them

Three things split them. Origin: neuroblastoma is nerve tissue, Wilms is kidney. Midline: neuroblastoma crosses it, Wilms stays on one side. Markers: neuroblastoma raises urine VMA and HVA, Wilms does not.

Both share one rule. Do not palpate either abdomen.

The classic presentation

Firm, irregular, non-tender abdominal mass that often crosses the midline. A parent often finds it at bath time.

Weight loss, anorexia, fatigue, fever, irritability, and pallor from marrow infiltration.

Catecholamine release causing hypertension, tachycardia, flushing, and diarrhea.

Raccoon eyes (periorbital ecchymosis) from spread to the orbit. A classic late sign.

Horner's syndrome (one-sided ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis) from a neck or chest tumor.

Opsoclonus-myoclonus, the "dancing eyes, dancing feet" sign. Rare and paraneoplastic.

Where it spreads

  • Bone and bone marrow, the most common site.
  • Liver, especially in infants, sometimes with massive hepatomegaly.
  • Orbital bones, causing raccoon eyes.
  • Skin, as blue-purple nodules, the "blueberry muffin" look in infants.
  • Distant lymph nodes.

How it gets diagnosed

  • Abdominal ultrasound is often the first image.
  • CT or MRI of the primary site: abdomen, chest, neck, or pelvis.
  • 24-hour urine for catecholamines. VMA and HVA are elevated in over 90 percent.
  • MIBG scan to find the primary tumor and any metastases.
  • Bone marrow biopsy from both iliac crests.
  • Tissue biopsy for histology and cytogenetics, including MYCN status.
  • Labs: CBC, LDH, ferritin, and chemistries.

Risk drives everything

Risk group Typical child What it means
Low Infant, localized, favorable biology, no MYCN Sometimes surgery or watchful waiting alone; over 90 percent survive
Intermediate Localized or locally advanced, unfavorable biology Surgery plus moderate chemo; good outlook
High Metastatic, older than 18 months, MYCN amplified Intensive chemo, surgery, stem cell transplant, radiation, and immunotherapy; about half survive
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Follow the sympathetic chain
Neuroblastoma grows from sympathetic nervous tissue. So it can appear anywhere the chain runs: adrenal gland, retroperitoneum, chest, neck, or pelvis. That is why the picture shifts. Horner's syndrome from a neck tumor, an abdominal mass from the adrenal, catecholamine surges from anywhere. Tumor from neural crest cells in the adrenal medulla means neuroblastoma.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Crosses the midline
When a young child has an abdominal mass, ask one question. Does it cross the midline. Neuroblastoma crosses it and raises urine VMA and HVA. Wilms stays on one side and does not. Same first move for both: do not palpate.
Bottom Line: Neuroblastoma is nerve tissue, crosses the midline, and raises urine VMA and HVA. Wilms is kidney and stays on one side. Never palpate either. MYCN amplified means high risk.
04

Medulloblastoma

Discrimination axis: a brain tumor hiding as a stomach bug.

Medulloblastoma is the most common malignant brain tumor in children under 14. The peak is age 5 to 9. It grows in the cerebellum, in the posterior fossa.

It often blocks the flow of spinal fluid, which causes hydrocephalus. Catch it early and outcomes are far better.

The classic presentation

Morning headache. Worse on waking, better after vomiting or sitting up.

Vomiting on arising. Often projectile, with no nausea first. It relieves the headache.

Ataxia and clumsiness. Wide-based gait, bumping into things, truncal sway.

Nystagmus, head tilt, double vision. From cranial nerve involvement.

Rising pressure. Lethargy, irritability, personality change, papilledema.

In infants: bulging fontanel, a growing head, and setting-sun eyes.

Rising pressure looks different by age

Infant, under 1 year Child, over 1 year
Bulging fontanel Morning headache, worse with coughing or straining
Widened sutures, rapid head growth Projectile vomiting on arising, no nausea
High-pitched cry Irritability, personality or school change
Poor feeding, lethargy Ataxia, diplopia, papilledema
Late: setting-sun eyes Late: Cushing's triad (HTN, bradycardia, irregular breathing)
Cushing's triad is a late warning

Hypertension, bradycardia, and irregular (Cheyne-Stokes) breathing together is the late triad of rising pressure and coming herniation. This is an emergency. Notify the provider, raise the head of the bed to 30 degrees, protect the airway, and prepare mannitol or hypertonic saline. Watch for a widening pulse pressure too.

Why the vomiting relieves the headache

Vomiting briefly lowers the pressure inside the skull. It stimulates the vagus nerve and empties the stomach. This is not a stomach bug.

Gut vomiting follows nausea. Here the vomiting comes suddenly, often in the morning, with no nausea, and the headache eases after. That history is a strong clue.

How it gets diagnosed

  • MRI of the brain with contrast is the gold standard. It shows the posterior fossa mass and any hydrocephalus.
  • MRI of the spine rules out spinal fluid drop metastases.
  • Lumbar puncture, after imaging, checks the fluid for tumor cells.
  • Tissue diagnosis is made at surgery. Molecular subtyping guides treatment.
  • Baseline labs: CBC, chemistries, hearing before cisplatin, and endocrine studies.
Image before you tap

Never do a lumbar puncture on a child with signs of rising pressure until imaging clears a posterior fossa mass. Pulling fluid from below a mass can make the brain herniate. Morning vomiting and headache means MRI first. The lumbar puncture waits until it is safe.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Vomiting that relieves
A stomach bug makes you nauseated, then you vomit, and you still feel sick. A posterior fossa tumor is different. The vomiting hits in the morning, with no warning nausea, and the headache eases after. Morning headache plus painless morning vomiting plus a clumsy gait points to the cerebellum.
Bottom Line: Medulloblastoma lives in the cerebellum. Morning headache, painless morning vomiting, and ataxia are rising pressure. Image before you tap. Watch for Cushing's triad.
05

Treatment

Surgery first, then chemo, radiation, and sometimes transplant.

Treatment is multi-modal and matched to risk. It runs long, often a year or more. Families meet many specialists on the way.

Surgery

Medulloblastoma. Maximal safe resection through a posterior fossa craniotomy. A VP shunt or external drain may be needed for hydrocephalus.

Neuroblastoma. Complete resection when localized. Debulking, then chemo, when widespread.

Chemotherapy

Medulloblastoma. Cisplatin, vincristine, cyclophosphamide, and lomustine over 6 to 12 months.

Neuroblastoma. Cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, cisplatin, etoposide, and topotecan. Intensive in high-risk disease.

Radiation

Medulloblastoma. Craniospinal radiation after surgery, usually for children over 3. It causes cognitive late effects, so it is limited in the very young.

Neuroblastoma. Radiation to the primary site or metastases in high-risk disease.

Autologous BMT

Medulloblastoma. Stem cell rescue lets high-risk or young children get high-dose chemo and spares radiation.

Neuroblastoma. Autologous stem cell transplant is standard for high risk. Immunotherapy with anti-GD2 antibody (dinutuximab) follows.

Chemo effects to watch

Drug Effect What you do
Cisplatin Ototoxic, hearing loss Baseline and serial audiology; watch the kidneys too
Doxorubicin Cardiotoxic Baseline and serial echocardiograms
Cyclophosphamide Hemorrhagic cystitis Aggressive hydration and Mesna
Vincristine Neuropathy, constipation, jaw pain Give IV only; intrathecal is fatal
Any chemo Neutropenia Fever at or above 100.4°F: cultures and antibiotics within 1 hour
Any chemo Mucositis, nausea, anorexia Oral care and scheduled antiemetics
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Fever is the emergency
In a child on chemo, a fever at or above 100.4°F is febrile neutropenia. That is an oncologic emergency. Draw cultures, peripheral and from the line, then start broad-spectrum antibiotics within the hour. Do not stop to treat the fever first. Delay raises the danger.
Bottom Line: Surgery first, then chemo, radiation, and sometimes transplant. High-risk neuroblastoma adds immunotherapy. Remember cisplatin ears, doxorubicin heart, cyclophosphamide bladder, and vincristine IV only.
06

What you do first

The bedside priorities that questions test.

These questions hand you four reasonable actions and ask which comes first. Safety leads. The action you can take now beats the one that needs an order.

Do not palpate

A suspected neuroblastoma or Wilms abdomen has a fragile capsule. Palpation can rupture it and spread tumor cells. Post a sign at the bedside. Tell every staff member and every parent. Document it on the plan of care. Inspect and listen only, until oncology says otherwise.

Before posterior fossa surgery

  1. 1
    Neuro checks every 1 to 2 hours. LOC, pupils, cranial nerves, motor, reflexes. Catch rising pressure early.
  2. 2
    Head of bed at 30 degrees, head midline. Avoid Trendelenburg. Limit coughing, straining, and suctioning.
  3. 3
    Watch fluid balance. Avoid overload, keep strict intake and output, and avoid hypotonic fluids.
  4. 4
    Prepare the child and family. Age-appropriate words, child life, and a tour of the PICU.

After posterior fossa surgery

  1. 1
    Position flat, side-lying or prone, on the un-operated side. Never Trendelenburg; it raises pressure. Confirm head-of-bed orders with the surgeon.
  2. 2
    Neuro checks every 15 minutes at first, then space out. Watch for rising pressure, new deficits, seizure, or Cushing's triad.
  3. 3
    Monitor a VP shunt or external drain. Keep the chamber at the ordered level. Document the amount, color, and clarity of fluid.
  4. 4
    Manage pain carefully. Opioids can sedate and mask neuro changes. Use scheduled acetaminophen.
  5. 5
    Watch for cerebellar mutism. Muteness, trouble swallowing, and emotional swings can start 1 to 2 days out.
  6. 6
    Care for the wound. Watch the suture line for fluid leak, infection, or opening.
  7. 7
    Start PT and OT early. Expect balance and coordination problems.
Cerebellar mutism syndrome

This follows up to 25 percent of posterior fossa surgeries. The child is alert right after surgery. Then, 24 to 96 hours later, speech drops or stops. Swallowing is hard and mood swings. It usually resolves over weeks to months.

Prepare the family. It is frightening but not permanent. Speech therapy, reassurance, and other ways to communicate all help.

Caring for a child with neuroblastoma

Do not palpate the abdomen. Post the sign. Educate staff and parents.

Monitor blood pressure. Catecholamines can drive hypertension, tachycardia, flushing, and sweating.

Protect weight and nutrition. Many are underweight. Use a dietitian, NG feeds, or TPN.

Neutropenic precautions. No rectal temperatures, fresh flowers, live vaccines, or raw food.

Watch for tumor lysis at induction. Hydrate and give allopurinol or rasburicase.

Control bone pain. Scheduled opioids, PCA for older children, heat, and distraction.

After abdominal surgery for neuroblastoma

  • Vital signs every 15 to 30 minutes at first, with cardiac monitoring.
  • Pain control, wound checks, bowel sounds, and strict intake and output.
  • Early mobilizing once stable, to prevent ileus, atelectasis, and clots.
  • Watch for blood pressure swings as catecholamines shift after resection.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The do-not-palpate tumors
Three pediatric tumors spread when you palpate them: neuroblastoma, Wilms tumor, and hepatoblastoma. If a stem gives you any pediatric abdominal mass, your first move is almost always the same. Do not palpate, and tell the provider. Inspect and listen only.
Bottom Line: Posterior fossa post-op is side-lying or prone, never Trendelenburg. Watch for cerebellar mutism. For neuroblastoma, never palpate, and watch blood pressure from catecholamines.
07

Family and home teaching

A long road for the whole family.

A brain tumor or metastatic neuroblastoma reshapes a family for years. Long stays, rehab, delays, and money stress are common. Your steady presence matters through all of it.

What to teach before discharge

Spot rising pressure or recurrence. Morning headaches, vomiting, personality change, balance trouble.

Fever at or above 100.4°F in a neutropenic child means the ED now. Never wait.

Keep every chemo dose and appointment. Missed doses lower the chance of cure.

Follow the feeding and hydration plan. Weight loss is dangerous; NG or TPN may be needed.

Avoid sick contacts and live vaccines in the household during therapy.

Keep long-term follow-up. The late-effects clinic tracks growth, hearing, cognition, hormones, and heart.

Support around the family

  • Child life specialists for procedure prep, play, and development.
  • Social work for insurance, money, lodging (Ronald McDonald House), rides, and meals.
  • Siblings, who are often overlooked. Include them and watch their mental health.
  • School re-entry help for absences, missed work, peers, and a 504 plan or IEP.
  • Neuropsychology for children who had CNS treatment.
  • Support groups like the Children's Oncology Group and the American Childhood Cancer Organization.

Finishing therapy

  • Many centers hold an end-of-chemo bell ceremony.
  • Care moves to a survivorship clinic, yearly for at least 10 years, or for life in high-risk disease.
  • Fear of recurrence is normal. Offer mental health support.
Survivorship is the goal

Childhood cancer survival has tripled over the past 50 years. Most children with low or intermediate-risk neuroblastoma and medulloblastoma become long-term survivors.

Your job is to protect the whole child. Through treatment, and back into school, sport, friendships, and adult life.

Bottom Line: A long road. Teach the family to spot rising pressure and recurrence. Fever means the ED. Siblings need support too. Survivorship care tracks late effects.
