High-Yield Summary

Eight facts to remember about pediatric solid tumors.

  • Medulloblastoma = most common CNS (brain) tumor in children <14 and the prototype for pediatric brain cancers. Arises in the cerebellum / posterior fossa.
  • Classic presentation of medulloblastoma: morning headache, vomiting on arising (projectile, improves after), ataxia/clumsiness, head tilt, signs of increased ICP, hydrocephalus.
  • Neuroblastoma = most common EXTRACRANIAL solid tumor of childhood and the most common cancer of infancy. Arises from neural crest cells of the sympathetic nervous system.
  • Neuroblastoma most common location = abdomen / adrenal gland (~65%). Can also occur anywhere along the sympathetic chain — chest, neck, pelvis, retroperitoneum.
  • Never palpate a suspected neuroblastoma abdomen! Palpation can rupture the tumor capsule and spread cancer cells. Post a sign at the bedside.
  • Diagnostic workup: MRI is gold standard for CNS tumors; neuroblastoma diagnosed by CT/MRI + elevated urine catecholamines (VMA and HVA) + biopsy. MYCN gene amplification = high risk.
  • Treatment: Surgical resection + chemotherapy + radiation (craniospinal for medulloblastoma) ± autologous BMT. Radiation of the developing brain has significant late effects — used cautiously in kids <3.
  • Post-op posterior fossa care: position flat side-lying or prone (NOT trendelenburg — increases ICP); monitor for cerebellar mutism syndrome (transient muteness + emotional lability 1–2 days post-op).

Pediatric Solid Tumor Fundamentals

The two tumors every peds nursing student needs to know

After leukemia, the next most common pediatric cancers are brain tumors (#1 solid tumor overall in kids) and neuroblastoma (the most common extracranial solid tumor in children and the most common cancer in infants). This guide focuses on the two prototypes tested in pediatric nursing: medulloblastoma and neuroblastoma.

Why These Two?

  • Medulloblastoma is the classic childhood brain tumor, located in the cerebellum. Nurses must recognize increased ICP and manage post-op posterior fossa care.
  • Neuroblastoma presents with an abdominal mass in a baby or toddler — and a huge NCLEX pearl: do NOT palpate the abdomen.
Feature Medulloblastoma Neuroblastoma
Origin Cerebellum / posterior fossa; primitive neuroectodermal cells Neural crest cells of the sympathetic nervous system (adrenal medulla most often)
Typical age Young school-age, <14 years (peak 5–9) <5 years; most common cancer of infancy; median age ~18 months
Most common location Cerebellum Abdomen / adrenal gland (~65%), also chest, neck, pelvis
Classic presentation Morning HA, vomiting on arising, ataxia, increased ICP Firm, irregular abdominal mass, crosses midline; may have systemic symptoms
Tumor markers None specific; MRI + biopsy Urine catecholamines: VMA & HVA elevated
Key nursing rule Post-op posterior fossa positioning; monitor for ICP DO NOT PALPATE abdomen
Most Solid Tumors Are Highly Curable

Even though the diagnoses are frightening, long-term survival for most pediatric solid tumors has improved dramatically. Medulloblastoma 5-year survival is ~70–80% with current therapy; low/intermediate-risk neuroblastoma has >90% survival. Your reassurance grounded in evidence supports family coping.

🎯 Bottom Line: Medulloblastoma = brain (cerebellum), school-age. Neuroblastoma = abdomen (adrenal), infants/toddlers. Both are serious but often curable.
2

Medulloblastoma

The brain tumor of childhood

Medulloblastoma is the most common malignant CNS tumor in children under 14. It grows in the cerebellum within the posterior fossa and frequently obstructs CSF flow, causing hydrocephalus. Early recognition dramatically improves outcomes.

Classic Presentation

Morning headache — worse on awakening; improves after vomiting or upright posture.

Vomiting on arising — classically projectile, NOT preceded by nausea. Relieves headache.

Ataxia / clumsiness — bumping into things, wide-based gait, truncal sway.

Nystagmus, head tilt, diplopia — from cranial nerve involvement.

Increased ICP — lethargy, irritability, personality change, papilledema.

In infants: bulging fontanel, macrocephaly, setting-sun eyes, sunsetting sign.

Signs of Increased ICP by Age

Infant (<1 yr) Child (>1 yr)
Bulging fontanel Morning headache, worsens with coughing/straining
Widened sutures / rapid head circumference growth Projectile vomiting on arising (no nausea)
High-pitched cry Irritability, personality/school performance change
Poor feeding, lethargy Ataxia, diplopia, papilledema
Late: setting-sun eyes Late: Cushing's triad (HTN, bradycardia, irregular respirations)
Cushing's Triad = Late Herniation Warning

Hypertension + bradycardia + irregular (Cheyne-Stokes) respirations is the classic late triad of increased ICP with impending herniation. This is an emergency — notify provider immediately, elevate HOB 30\u00b0, ensure airway, prepare mannitol or hypertonic saline. Also watch for widening pulse pressure.

Why Vomiting "Relieves" the Headache

Vomiting temporarily reduces intracranial pressure by stimulating the vagus nerve and reducing gastric volume. This is distinct from GI vomiting (which follows nausea). In medulloblastoma, vomiting happens suddenly, often in the morning, with no nausea, and relieves the headache — a very specific history clue.

Treatment Overview

  1. Maximal safe surgical resection — neurosurgery removes as much tumor as possible; often requires VP shunt or external ventricular drain if hydrocephalus.
  2. Craniospinal radiation — reduces CSF-disseminated tumor; typically given to children >3 years (risk of cognitive late effects makes it cautious in younger children).
  3. Adjuvant chemotherapy — cisplatin, vincristine, cyclophosphamide, lomustine, others; for 6–12 months.
  4. Autologous stem cell rescue (BMT) — sometimes used in high-risk disease to allow high-dose chemo.
  5. Surveillance imaging every few months for the first 2 years, then less frequently.

Long-Term Concerns

  • Neurocognitive late effects from surgery and radiation — especially in younger children.
  • Growth hormone deficiency, hypothyroidism from CNS radiation.
  • Hearing loss from cisplatin.
  • Secondary cancers at the radiation site.
  • Need for school accommodations, IEP, neuropsychology services.
🎯 Bottom Line: Medulloblastoma = cerebellum = morning HA + projectile AM vomiting + ataxia + ↑ICP. MRI then surgery + radiation + chemo. Watch Cushing's triad.

Neuroblastoma

The abdominal tumor of infancy

Neuroblastoma is the most common extracranial solid tumor in children and the most common cancer in infants. It arises from neural crest cells of the sympathetic nervous system — so it can develop anywhere along the sympathetic chain. Most cases (~65%) start in the adrenal medulla / retroperitoneum, but chest, neck, and pelvic tumors also occur.

Classic Presentation

Firm, irregular, non-tender abdominal mass — often crosses the midline. Parent may find it at bath time.

Weight loss, anorexia, fatigue, fever, irritability, pallor (anemia from marrow infiltration).

Catecholamine release can cause hypertension, tachycardia, flushing, diarrhea.

Raccoon eyes / periorbital ecchymosis — from metastasis to the orbit (classic late sign).

Horner's syndrome (unilateral ptosis, miosis, anhidrosis) — from a cervical/thoracic tumor.

Opsoclonus-myoclonus syndrome ("dancing eyes, dancing feet") — rare, paraneoplastic.

Why "Do NOT Palpate"

Never Palpate a Suspected Neuroblastoma Abdomen

The neuroblastoma tumor capsule is fragile. Palpation can rupture the capsule and spread malignant cells to other sites. Post a bedside sign (\u201cDo Not Palpate Abdomen\u201d), educate all staff and parents, and document clearly on the plan of care. If abdominal assessment is essential, inspection and auscultation only — NO palpation until the oncology team directs otherwise.

Sites of Metastasis

  • Bone & bone marrow — most common metastasis site.
  • Liver — especially in infants (may cause massive hepatomegaly).
  • Orbital bones — causing raccoon eyes.
  • Skin — blue-purple nodules ("blueberry muffin" in infants).
  • Distant lymph nodes.

Diagnosis & Tumor Markers

  • Urinary catecholamines — elevated VMA (vanillylmandelic acid) and HVA (homovanillic acid) in >90% of patients. 24-hour urine collection.
  • Imaging — abdominal CT or MRI to identify mass; MIBG scan for metastases.
  • Bone marrow biopsy — rule out marrow involvement.
  • Tissue biopsy — confirmatory; sent for cytogenetics including MYCN gene amplification (present = high risk).

Risk Stratification Drives Treatment

Risk Group Typical Patient Prognosis
Low risk Infants, localized tumor, favorable biology; no MYCN amplification >90% survival; sometimes just surgery or observation
Intermediate Localized or locally advanced; unfavorable biology Good; surgery + modest chemo
High risk Metastatic, >18 months old, MYCN amplified ~50% survival; intensive multi-modal therapy (chemo + surgery + HSCT + radiation + immunotherapy)
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Follow the Sympathetic Chain
Neuroblastoma arises from sympathetic nervous system tissue, so it can grow anywhere the chain travels: adrenal medulla (most common), retroperitoneum, thorax, neck, or pelvis. That\u2019s why presentations vary — Horner\u2019s for cervical/thoracic tumors, abdominal mass for adrenal, and hypertension/tachycardia from catecholamine release anywhere. If NCLEX says "tumor arising from neural crest cells in the adrenal medulla" → neuroblastoma.
🎯 Bottom Line: Neuroblastoma = infant/toddler, adrenal abdominal mass crossing midline. NEVER palpate. Urine VMA/HVA + MYCN testing. Look for raccoon eyes, Horner\u2019s.

Diagnostic Workup

Imaging, tumor markers, staging

Medulloblastoma Workup

  1. MRI brain with contrast — gold standard imaging; identifies posterior fossa mass and hydrocephalus.
  2. MRI spine — rules out CSF drop metastases (essential before treatment plan).
  3. Lumbar puncture (after imaging confirms no mass that would cause herniation) — cytology for malignant cells in CSF.
  4. Histopathologic diagnosis made at the time of surgical resection; molecular subtyping guides treatment.
  5. Baseline labs: CBC, chemistries, hearing (pre-cisplatin), endocrine evaluation.
NCLEX Point — Why Image Before LP?

Never perform a lumbar puncture on a child with signs of increased ICP until imaging rules out a posterior fossa mass. Removing CSF from below a mass can cause cerebral herniation. In a child with morning vomiting and headache, MRI FIRST, LP later (if safe).

Neuroblastoma Workup

  1. Abdominal ultrasound — often first-line imaging when abdominal mass is suspected.
  2. CT or MRI of the primary site (abdomen, chest, neck, or pelvis depending on findings).
  3. 24-hour urine for catecholamines (VMA and HVA) — elevated in >90%.
  4. MIBG scan — identifies primary tumor and metastatic deposits.
  5. Bone marrow biopsy (bilateral iliac crest) — assess marrow involvement.
  6. Tumor biopsy — tissue for histology, cytogenetics, and critically MYCN amplification status.
  7. Labs: CBC (anemia from marrow involvement), LDH, ferritin, chemistries.
🎯 Bottom Line: Medulloblastoma → MRI brain & spine + LP. Neuroblastoma → imaging + urine VMA/HVA + MIBG + biopsy + MYCN. Image BEFORE LP.

Treatment Overview

Surgery, chemotherapy, radiation, and BMT

Pediatric solid tumor therapy is multi-modal and risk-stratified. Treatment is long (months to a year or more), and families will meet many specialists along the way.

Surgery

First Step
Medulloblastoma

Maximal safe resection via posterior fossa craniotomy. May need VP shunt or EVD for hydrocephalus.

Neuroblastoma

Complete resection for localized disease; debulking for widespread disease followed by chemo.

Chemotherapy

Multi-Agent
Medulloblastoma

Cisplatin, vincristine, cyclophosphamide, lomustine; 6–12 months.

Neuroblastoma

Combinations of cyclophosphamide, doxorubicin, cisplatin, etoposide, topotecan; intensive in high-risk disease.

Radiation

Age-Dependent
Medulloblastoma

Craniospinal radiation after surgery (for children >3 years). Causes cognitive late effects — minimized in young children.

Neuroblastoma

Radiation to primary site or metastases in high-risk disease.

Autologous BMT

High Risk
Medulloblastoma

Stem cell rescue for high-risk or young children to enable high-dose chemo and spare radiation.

Neuroblastoma

Autologous stem cell transplant is standard for high-risk disease; followed by immunotherapy (anti-GD2 antibody dinutuximab).

Common Chemo Toxicities to Watch

Cisplatin → ototoxicity (hearing loss). Baseline + serial audiology.

Doxorubicin → cardiotoxicity. Baseline + serial echocardiograms.

Cyclophosphamide → hemorrhagic cystitis. Aggressive hydration + Mesna.

Vincristine → peripheral neuropathy, constipation, jaw pain. IV ONLY — fatal intrathecal.

All chemo → neutropenia. Fever ≥100.4\u00b0F = emergency (cultures + abx within 1 hr).

All chemo → mucositis, nausea, anorexia. Oral care + scheduled antiemetics.

🎯 Bottom Line: Surgery first, then chemo ± radiation ± autologous BMT. Neuroblastoma high-risk adds immunotherapy. Watch cisplatin ears, doxorubicin heart, cyclophosphamide bladder.

Nursing Priorities & Safety

Pre-op, post-op, and beyond

Medulloblastoma: Pre-Op Priorities

Neuro checks q1–2 hours — LOC, pupils, cranial nerves, motor, reflexes. Detect rising ICP early.

HOB elevated 30°, head midline; avoid Trendelenburg; minimize activities that raise ICP (coughing, straining, suctioning).

Fluid balance — avoid fluid overload; strict I&O. Avoid hypotonic fluids.

Prep child & family for surgery — age-appropriate explanations, child life involvement, tour of PICU.

Medulloblastoma: Post-Op Priorities (Posterior Fossa Craniotomy)

  1. Positioning: flat side-lying or prone on the UN-operated side to prevent pressure on the surgical site. NOT Trendelenburg (raises ICP). HOB is usually flat or slightly elevated per surgeon orders — verify.
  2. Neuro checks q15 minutes initially, spacing out as stable. Watch for return of increased ICP, new focal deficits, seizure, or Cushing's triad.
  3. Monitor VP shunt or EVD if present; keep level of drip chamber per order; document output, color, clarity of CSF.
  4. Pain management — opioids cautiously (opioid-induced sedation may mask neuro changes); scheduled acetaminophen.
  5. Watch for cerebellar mutism syndrome — transient muteness, dysphagia, emotional lability, irritability 1–2 days post-op. Usually resolves over weeks to months; provide reassurance and communication supports.
  6. Skin & wound care — suture/staple line care; monitor for CSF leak, infection, dehiscence.
  7. Begin PT/OT early; anticipate balance/coordination deficits; involve child life and rehab.
Cerebellar Mutism Syndrome

A post-operative complication seen in up to 25% of posterior fossa surgeries. The child is initially alert after surgery, then develops decreased speech or complete muteness, dysphagia, and emotional lability 24–96 hours later. Often resolves in weeks to months. Prepare families — it\u2019s frightening but not permanent. Speech therapy, reassurance, and alternative communication strategies help.

Neuroblastoma: Priorities

DO NOT PALPATE the abdominal mass. Post sign at bedside; educate all staff and parents.

Monitor blood pressure — catecholamine release can cause HTN; watch for tachycardia, flushing, sweating.

Weight and nutrition — kids may be underweight; consult dietitian, NG feeds, or TPN as needed.

Neutropenic precautions during chemo — no rectal temps, fresh flowers, live vaccines, raw food; prompt fever response.

Tumor lysis monitoring — high tumor burden at induction; hydrate + allopurinol/rasburicase.

Pain control for bone metastases; scheduled opioids, PCA for older children, heat, distraction.

Post-Op Abdominal Surgery (Neuroblastoma)

  • Vital signs q15–30 min initially, cardiac monitoring.
  • Pain control, wound assessment, bowel sounds, I&O.
  • Early mobilization when stable; prevent ileus, atelectasis, VTE.
  • Monitor for BP swings from catecholamine changes post-resection.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Three "Do Not Palpate" Tumors
Three pediatric tumors where palpation can spread cancer cells: neuroblastoma, Wilms tumor, and hepatoblastoma. If the NCLEX stem mentions any pediatric abdominal mass, the first intervention is almost always do not palpate + notify the provider. Inspection and auscultation only.
🎯 Bottom Line: Medulloblastoma post-op = side-lying/prone, NOT Trendelenburg. Watch for cerebellar mutism. Neuroblastoma: NEVER palpate. BP swings from catecholamines.

Family Coping & Education

A long journey for the whole family

A brain tumor or metastatic neuroblastoma diagnosis upends family life for years. Extended hospitalizations, rehab, developmental delays, and financial stress are common. Nursing presence through the journey is significant.

Key Education Points

Recognize signs of ICP or recurrence — morning headaches, vomiting, personality changes, balance issues.

Fever ≥ 100.4°F in a neutropenic child = ED immediately — never delay.

Chemotherapy adherence and follow-up schedules — missing doses reduces cure rates.

Feeding & hydration plan — weight loss is dangerous; NG or TPN may be needed.

Avoid sick contacts, live vaccines in household during active therapy.

Long-term follow-up — late effects clinic; growth, hearing, cognition, endocrine, cardiac.

Psychosocial Supports

  • Child life specialists — procedure preparation, play therapy, developmental support.
  • Social work — insurance, financial assistance, lodging (Ronald McDonald House), transportation, meal support.
  • Siblings — often overlooked; include in age-appropriate conversations, plan for their own mental health.
  • School re-entry programs — help with absences, missed content, peer preparation, 504 plan / IEP.
  • Neuropsychology services for children with CNS treatment; cognitive rehabilitation.
  • Support organizations — Children\u2019s Oncology Group family resources, American Childhood Cancer Organization, disease-specific foundations.

End-of-Therapy Milestones

  • End-of-chemo "bell ringing" ceremonies at many centers — celebrate!
  • Transition to survivorship clinic visits; yearly follow-up for at least 10 years or lifelong for high-risk disease.
  • Address fear of recurrence — normalize ongoing anxiety; refer for mental health support.
Survivorship Is the Goal

Childhood cancer survival rates have tripled in the past 50 years. Most children with low/intermediate-risk neuroblastoma and medulloblastoma become long-term survivors. Your role as the nurse is to protect the whole child — not just through treatment, but through the transition back to school, sports, friendships, and eventually independent adulthood.

🎯 Bottom Line: Long journey. Recognize recurrence/ICP signs. Fever = ED. Siblings need support too. Survivorship clinic for late effects.
