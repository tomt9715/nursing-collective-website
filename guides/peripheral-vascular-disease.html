Vascular Anatomy & Physiology

The peripheral vascular system consists of arterial and venous networks that transport blood to and from the body's tissues. Understanding normal structure and function is essential for recognizing pathological changes in peripheral vascular disease.

Arterial System

Arteries carry oxygenated blood away from the heart to the tissues. They have thick, muscular walls that can withstand high pressures and regulate blood flow through vasoconstriction and vasodilation.

Layer Structure Function
Tunica Intima Single layer of endothelial cells Smooth surface for blood flow; site of atherosclerosis
Tunica Media Smooth muscle and elastic fibers Vasoconstriction/vasodilation; maintains pressure
Tunica Adventitia Connective tissue Structural support; anchors vessel to surrounding tissue

Venous System

Veins return deoxygenated blood to the heart. They have thinner walls than arteries and operate under low pressure. Veins contain one-way valves that prevent backflow and rely on the muscle pump mechanism to assist blood return.

Clinical Pearl: The Muscle Pump

Contraction of the calf muscles (the "second heart") squeezes deep veins and propels blood toward the heart. This is why prolonged immobility increases DVT risk - the muscle pump isn't working! Early ambulation and leg exercises activate this mechanism.

Lower Extremity Circulation

Arterial Supply

  • Superficial femoral artery â†’ continues down the thigh
  • Popliteal artery â†’ behind the knee
  • Anterior tibial â†’ dorsalis pedis (top of foot)
  • Posterior tibial â†’ behind medial malleolus
  • Peroneal artery â†’ lateral leg

Venous Drainage

  • Deep veins: Run alongside arteries (femoral, popliteal, tibial); primary return pathway
  • Superficial veins: Great saphenous (medial), small saphenous (posterior); connect to deep system via perforators
  • Perforating veins: Connect superficial and deep systems; valves prevent blood from flowing superficially

Arterial vs Venous Insufficiency

Differentiating between arterial and venous insufficiency is one of the most important clinical skills in vascular nursing. The two conditions have distinctly different pathophysiology, presentations, and treatments.

Arterial Insufficiency

Pathophysiology

  • Decreased blood flow TO tissues
  • Caused by atherosclerosis, embolism, or spasm
  • Results in tissue ischemia and hypoxia

Key Assessment Findings

  • Cool extremities
  • Pale or dusky color; pallor with elevation
  • Absent or diminished pulses
  • Intermittent claudication (pain with activity)
  • Rest pain (severe disease)
  • Shiny, thin, hairless skin
  • Thick, brittle nails
Venous Insufficiency

Pathophysiology

  • Impaired blood return FROM tissues
  • Caused by valve incompetence or obstruction
  • Results in venous hypertension and congestion

Key Assessment Findings

  • Warm extremities
  • Brown discoloration (hemosiderin staining)
  • Pulses present (may be hard to palpate due to edema)
  • Heaviness/aching worse with standing
  • Relieved by elevation
  • Edema (pitting)
  • Stasis dermatitis, lipodermatosclerosis
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Position Test
Position makes a difference! Here's a quick way to differentiate: Arterial disease pain WORSENS with elevation (less blood flow against gravity) and IMPROVES when legs are dependent (dangling). Venous disease pain IMPROVES with elevation (helps blood return) and WORSENS with dependency (blood pools). Also remember: arterial ulcers are usually on toes and pressure points, while venous ulcers are typically on the medial malleolus ("gaiter area"). Understanding the pathophysiology makes the differences logical!

Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)

Peripheral arterial disease is a manifestation of systemic atherosclerosis affecting arteries outside the heart and brain, most commonly in the lower extremities. It is a marker of significant cardiovascular risk.

Risk Factors

Major Risk Factors Contributing Factors
Smoking (strongest modifiable risk) Hyperhomocysteinemia
Diabetes mellitus Chronic kidney disease
Hypertension Elevated CRP/inflammation
Hyperlipidemia Obesity
Age >50 years Sedentary lifestyle
PAD = Systemic Disease

PAD is not just a leg problem! Patients with PAD have a 6-7x higher risk of cardiovascular death. They are at high risk for heart attack and stroke because the same atherosclerotic process affects coronary and cerebral arteries. Always assess for CAD and stroke risk in PAD patients!

Clinical Stages of PAD (Fontaine Classification)

Progressive Stages of PAD

I
Asymptomatic

No symptoms despite measurable disease. May have abnormal ABI. Often discovered incidentally.

II
Intermittent Claudication

Muscle pain, cramping, or fatigue with walking that is relieved by rest. IIa: claudication >200m; IIb: claudication <200m.

III
Rest Pain

Pain at rest, especially at night. Often relieved by dangling leg over bed. Indicates severe ischemia - limb-threatening!

IV
Tissue Loss

Ulceration or gangrene. Critical limb ischemia (CLI). High risk for amputation without revascularization.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Understanding Claudication
Claudication is exercise-induced ischemia of the leg muscles! The word comes from Latin "claudicare" meaning "to limp." Key features: (1) it's predictable - same distance causes symptoms, (2) it's reproducible - happens with each walk, (3) it's relieved by standing still (doesn't require sitting), and (4) the location indicates the level of disease (buttock/hip = aortoiliac, thigh = common femoral, calf = superficial femoral/popliteal - most common). Don't confuse it with neurogenic claudication from spinal stenosis, which is relieved by sitting/bending forward, not just standing.

Assessment of PAD

  • History: Walking distance, pain characteristics, risk factors, functional limitations
  • Inspection: Skin color, hair distribution, nail changes, muscle atrophy, ulcers, gangrene
  • Palpation: Skin temperature, capillary refill (>3 sec abnormal), pulse quality bilaterally
  • Special tests: ABI, segmental pressures, pulse volume recordings, duplex ultrasound
  • Positional color changes: Pallor with elevation (Buerger's test), dependent rubor
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: PAD is a systemic disease â€” patients have 6â€“7Ã— higher cardiovascular death risk. Claudication is predictable and reproducible. Rest pain and tissue loss are limb-threatening emergencies.

Ankle-Brachial Index (ABI)

The ankle-brachial index is a simple, non-invasive test that compares blood pressure in the ankle to blood pressure in the arm. It is the primary screening and diagnostic tool for PAD.

How to Perform ABI

ABI Measurement Steps

1
Patient Preparation

Have patient rest supine for 10 minutes. Room should be warm. Remove constrictive clothing. Explain the procedure.

2
Measure Brachial Pressures

Place appropriate-sized cuff on upper arm. Use Doppler to locate brachial artery. Inflate cuff until signal disappears, then slowly deflate. Record the systolic pressure when signal returns. Measure both arms.

3
Measure Ankle Pressures

Place cuff just above the ankle. Use Doppler to locate dorsalis pedis (DP) and posterior tibial (PT) arteries. Measure systolic pressure at each. Use the HIGHER of DP or PT for each ankle.

4
Calculate ABI

ABI = Highest ankle pressure (that leg) Ã· Highest brachial pressure (either arm). Calculate separately for each leg.

1.00-1.30 Normal No significant PAD
0.91-0.99 Borderline May have early disease; consider exercise ABI
0.41-0.90 Mild to Moderate PAD Claudication typical; lifestyle modification, medications

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
ABI Clinical Pearls
ABI has some important limitations! In diabetic patients and those with CKD, arteries can become calcified and incompressible, giving falsely elevated ABI readings (>1.30). In these patients, use toe-brachial index (TBI) instead - toe arteries don't calcify as much. Also, a normal resting ABI doesn't rule out PAD - if you suspect disease, order an exercise ABI. The patient walks on a treadmill, then ABI is remeasured. A drop of >20% post-exercise indicates significant PAD even with normal resting values.

Chronic Venous Insufficiency

Chronic venous insufficiency (CVI) occurs when leg veins cannot efficiently return blood to the heart, usually due to damaged valves or previous DVT. It leads to venous hypertension and characteristic skin changes.

Pathophysiology

  • Valve incompetence: Damaged or weakened valves allow blood to flow backward (reflux)
  • Obstruction: Previous DVT can cause chronic venous obstruction
  • Muscle pump failure: Reduced calf muscle function decreases venous return
  • Venous hypertension: Elevated pressure in leg veins causes fluid extravasation, inflammation, and tissue damage

Clinical Manifestations

Finding Description Pathophysiology
Edema Pitting edema, worse at end of day, improves overnight Increased capillary filtration from venous hypertension
Varicose Veins Dilated, tortuous superficial veins Valve failure and venous dilation
Hemosiderin Staining Brown/reddish discoloration of skin RBC extravasation and hemoglobin breakdown
Stasis Dermatitis Red, scaly, itchy skin; may weep Inflammatory response to venous hypertension
Lipodermatosclerosis Hardened, fibrotic skin; "inverted champagne bottle" leg Chronic inflammation and fibrosis
Venous Ulcers Shallow, irregular ulcers at medial malleolus Tissue breakdown from chronic venous hypertension

Management of CVI

  • Compression therapy: Graduated compression stockings (30-40 mmHg); cornerstone of treatment
  • Leg elevation: Above heart level, 3-4 times daily for 30 minutes
  • Exercise: Walking activates calf muscle pump; avoid prolonged standing/sitting
  • Skin care: Moisturize dry skin; treat dermatitis; avoid trauma
  • Weight management: Reduce abdominal pressure on venous return
  • Procedures: Ablation, sclerotherapy, or surgery for severe cases
Compression Therapy Tips

Apply compression stockings in the morning before getting out of bed (before edema develops). Ensure proper fit - improper sizing can cause harm. Stockings should be replaced every 3-6 months as elasticity decreases. Contraindicated in significant arterial disease (ABI <0.5) - always check ABI before prescribing!

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Compression therapy is the cornerstone of CVI management. Apply stockings in the morning before getting out of bed. Always check ABI first â€” compression is contraindicated if ABI < 0.5.

Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)

Deep vein thrombosis is a blood clot that forms in the deep veins, most commonly in the lower extremities. DVT is a serious condition that can lead to pulmonary embolism, a potentially fatal complication.

Virchow's Triad

The three factors that predispose to thrombus formation:

Factor Definition Clinical Examples
Venous Stasis Slowed or stagnant blood flow Immobility, paralysis, long flights, prolonged surgery, heart failure
Endothelial Injury Damage to vessel wall lining Surgery, trauma, IV catheters, previous DVT, vasculitis
Hypercoagulability Increased tendency to clot Malignancy, pregnancy, oral contraceptives, inherited disorders (Factor V Leiden), dehydration
Memory Aid: DVT Risk Factors

THROMBOSIS

  • Trauma, Travel (long distance)
  • Hypercoagulable states, HRT/Hormones
  • Recent surgery, Restricted mobility
  • Old age, Obesity
  • Malignancy
  • Birth control pills, Blood disorders
  • Orthopedic surgery
  • Smoking, Stroke
  • Immobility, IV catheters
  • Sepsis, prior clot history

Clinical Presentation

  • Unilateral leg swelling - most common finding
  • Pain or tenderness - often in calf
  • Warmth - affected leg warmer than unaffected
  • Erythema - redness along course of vein
  • Palpable cord - thrombosed vein may be felt
  • Homan's sign - calf pain with dorsiflexion (not reliable - don't rely on this!)
Critical Warning: PE Signs

DVT can lead to pulmonary embolism (PE) if clot dislodges and travels to the lungs. Watch for:

  • Sudden dyspnea
  • Pleuritic chest pain
  • Tachycardia, tachypnea
  • Hypoxemia
  • Hemoptysis
  • Anxiety, sense of impending doom

PE is a medical emergency! Activate rapid response if suspected.

Diagnosis

  • Wells Score: Clinical prediction rule to estimate DVT probability
  • D-dimer: High sensitivity but low specificity; useful for ruling OUT DVT if negative
  • Duplex ultrasound: Gold standard for diagnosis; shows non-compressible vein with thrombus
  • Venography: Rarely used; invasive contrast study
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Virchow's triad (stasis + injury + hypercoagulability) predisposes to DVT. Duplex ultrasound is the gold standard. Watch for PE: sudden dyspnea + chest pain + tachycardia = medical emergency.

DVT Prevention Strategies

Prevention of DVT is a critical nursing responsibility, especially in hospitalized patients. Evidence-based prophylaxis significantly reduces the incidence of venous thromboembolism (VTE).

Risk Assessment

All hospitalized patients should be assessed for VTE risk. Common tools include the Padua Prediction Score (medical patients) and Caprini Score (surgical patients).

Risk Level Examples Recommended Prophylaxis
Low Risk Minor surgery, ambulatory patients Early ambulation
Moderate Risk General surgery, medical illness with bed rest Pharmacologic OR mechanical
High Risk Hip/knee replacement, major trauma, cancer surgery Pharmacologic AND mechanical
High Bleeding Risk Active bleeding, recent neurosurgery Mechanical prophylaxis only

Mechanical Prophylaxis

Mechanical Prevention Methods

1
Sequential Compression Devices (SCDs)

Intermittent pneumatic compression of calves simulates walking and enhances venous return. Must be worn consistently - removing for >2 hours significantly reduces effectiveness.

2
Graduated Compression Stockings (GCS)

Anti-embolism stockings (TEDs) provide graduated compression (highest at ankle, decreasing up leg). Ensure proper fit and smooth application. Check skin integrity daily.

3
Venous Foot Pumps

Inflatable devices that compress the plantar venous plexus. Alternative when leg devices can't be used.

4
Early Ambulation

The most natural form of VTE prevention! Get patients out of bed and walking as soon as safely possible. Even standing and marching in place helps.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
SCDs Work - If They're On!
Compliance is everything with SCDs! Studies show SCDs reduce DVT by 60% - but only if worn consistently. Every time you enter a patient's room, check: Are the SCDs plugged in? Are they actually cycling? Are they on the patient's legs (not hanging off the bed)? Document SCD compliance. Educate patients and families on why they're important. The most expensive DVT prevention device in the world is worthless sitting on the floor. Make SCD checks part of your routine!

Pharmacologic Prophylaxis

Medication Dose (Typical) Key Nursing Considerations
Unfractionated Heparin (UFH) 5,000 units SQ q8-12h Short half-life; reversible with protamine; monitor platelets for HIT
Enoxaparin (Lovenox) 40 mg SQ daily LMWH; more predictable; dose adjust for renal impairment (CrCl <30)
Fondaparinux (Arixtra) 2.5 mg SQ daily Factor Xa inhibitor; contraindicated if CrCl <30; no HIT risk
Rivaroxaban (Xarelto) 10 mg PO daily (post-surgery) Oral factor Xa inhibitor; convenient; limited reversal options
Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia (HIT)

HIT is a serious complication of heparin therapy (UFH > LMWH). It paradoxically causes thrombosis, not bleeding!

  • Monitor platelet count baseline and every 2-3 days during heparin
  • Suspect HIT if platelets drop >50% from baseline or below 100,000
  • Timing: typically 5-14 days after starting heparin (or sooner if prior exposure)
  • If suspected: STOP all heparin immediately, send HIT antibodies, start alternative anticoagulant

Nursing Interventions for VTE Prevention

  • Assess VTE risk on admission and with changes in condition
  • Implement ordered prophylaxis - both mechanical and pharmacologic
  • Early mobility: Ambulate patients as soon as safely possible; consult PT/OT
  • Hydration: Prevent dehydration which increases blood viscosity
  • Positioning: Avoid positions that impede venous return (crossing legs, prolonged hip flexion)
  • Leg exercises: Teach ankle pumps, foot circles for immobile patients
  • Monitor for DVT: Assess legs for swelling, pain, warmth each shift
  • Patient education: Teach signs of DVT/PE; importance of compliance with prophylaxis
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: SCDs reduce DVT by 60% but only if worn consistently. Every room visit: check they're plugged in, cycling, and actually on the patient. Early ambulation is the most natural DVT prevention.

Treatment & Nursing Care

Management of peripheral vascular disease requires a comprehensive approach including risk factor modification, pharmacotherapy, and interventional procedures when indicated.

Treatment of DVT

Treatment Purpose Nursing Considerations
Anticoagulation (initial) Prevent clot extension and PE LMWH, UFH infusion, or DOACs; monitor for bleeding; aPTT for UFH
Anticoagulation (long-term) Prevent recurrence Warfarin (INR 2-3), DOACs; duration 3+ months; patient education essential
Thrombolysis Dissolve large clots Catheter-directed for iliofemoral DVT; high bleeding risk; ICU monitoring
IVC Filter Prevent PE if anticoagulation contraindicated Does NOT treat DVT; does NOT replace anticoagulation when possible
Compression Therapy Reduce edema, prevent PTS Start after acute phase; graduated compression stockings for 2+ years
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
DVT and Ambulation - Myth Busted!
Old teaching: strict bed rest for DVT to prevent PE. NEW evidence: early ambulation is safe and beneficial! Multiple studies show that once therapeutic anticoagulation is established, early walking does NOT increase PE risk and actually reduces leg symptoms faster. Current guidelines recommend early ambulation with compression for most DVT patients. The exception: massive iliofemoral DVT or unstable patients may need brief initial rest. But for routine DVT, get them moving! Always verify your facility's protocol, but don't keep patients unnecessarily bedridden.

Treatment of PAD

  • Risk factor modification: Smoking cessation (MOST important), BP control, diabetes management, lipid therapy
  • Exercise therapy: Supervised walking program; walk until claudication, rest, repeat; 30-45 min, 3x/week
  • Antiplatelet therapy: Aspirin or clopidogrel reduces cardiovascular events
  • Cilostazol (Pletal): PDE inhibitor; improves walking distance; contraindicated in heart failure
  • Revascularization: Angioplasty/stenting or surgical bypass for severe/refractory disease

Nursing Care for PAD

Key Nursing Interventions

1
Protect Extremities

Avoid trauma to ischemic limbs. Use heel protectors. Inspect feet daily. Proper footwear. No hot water bottles or heating pads (decreased sensation).

2
Position for Perfusion

For arterial disease: keep legs level or slightly dependent (NOT elevated). Elevating ischemic limbs worsens pain and perfusion. Reverse Trendelenburg may help rest pain.

3
Promote Circulation

Encourage prescribed exercise. Avoid crossing legs. Keep environment warm. Avoid vasoconstrictors (caffeine, tobacco, cold). Maintain hydration.

4
Monitor for Complications

Assess pulses, skin color, temperature, capillary refill. Watch for signs of critical limb ischemia: rest pain, non-healing wounds, gangrene. Report changes promptly.

5
Patient Education

Smoking cessation resources. Foot care instruction (especially diabetics). Signs of worsening disease. Medication adherence. Exercise prescription. When to seek care.

NCLEX Tip: Position Matters!

Arterial disease: Keep legs DOWN (level or dependent) to promote blood flow TO the tissues. Elevation worsens ischemia.

Venous disease: Keep legs UP (elevated) to promote blood return FROM the tissues. Dependency worsens edema.

Remember: Arteries = blood going OUT = keep down. Veins = blood coming BACK = raise up.

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: For arterial disease: keep legs DOWN, protect from trauma, NO heating pads. For venous disease: keep legs UP, compression therapy, early mobilization. Position is the simplest and most tested intervention.

1 Single Best Answer Knowledge

A nurse is assessing two patients â€” one with peripheral arterial disease (PAD) and one with chronic venous insufficiency (CVI). Which finding is correctly matched to the condition?

  1. A. PAD: warm skin, brown discoloration around the ankles
  2. B. CVI: absent pedal pulses, intermittent claudication
  3. C. PAD: cool/pale extremity, thin shiny skin, hair loss
  4. D. CVI: rest pain that worsens with leg elevation

Correct Answer: C

Why C is correct:

PAD involves reduced arterial blood flow to the extremities. Classic findings include cool/pale skin (poor perfusion), thin shiny skin, hair loss on the legs, thickened toenails, and diminished or absent pulses. These are all signs of chronic tissue ischemia from inadequate arterial supply.

Why the others are wrong:

A. Warm skin and brown discoloration (hemosiderin staining) are characteristics of CVI, not PAD. PAD causes cool, pale skin.

B. Absent pulses and claudication are arterial (PAD) findings, not venous. CVI has palpable pulses because the arterial system is intact.

D. Rest pain that worsens with elevation is an arterial finding. Venous disease pain is relieved by elevation and worsened by dependency.

Test-Taking Tip

Arterial vs Venous at a glance: Arterial = 5 P's (Pain, Pallor, Pulselessness, Paresthesia, Paralysis), cool, thin skin, hair loss. Venous = warm, edematous, brown staining, dermatitis, aching relieved by elevation.

3 Select All That Apply Application

A nurse is caring for a patient with a newly diagnosed deep vein thrombosis (DVT) in the left leg. Which nursing interventions are appropriate? (Select all that apply.)

  1. A. Elevate the affected leg above heart level
  2. B. Apply warm, moist compresses to the affected leg
  3. C. Massage the affected calf to relieve discomfort

Why A, B, D, E are correct:

A. Elevation promotes venous return and reduces edema. B. Warm compresses promote vasodilation and comfort (cold is for arterial). D. C. NEVER massage a leg with a known DVT. Massage can dislodge the clot, causing it to travel to the lungs as a pulmonary embolism â€” a potentially fatal complication.

Test-Taking Tip

DVT rule: No massage, no vigorous exercise of the affected leg. For temperature application: venous = warm (promotes flow), arterial = avoid heat (increases O2 demand in ischemic tissue). This is a commonly tested concept.

4 Priority Analysis

A patient 3 days post-hip replacement suddenly develops acute onset of dyspnea, tachycardia (HR 126), chest pain, and an SpO2 of 84% on room air. The nurse suspects this complication and should FIRST:

  1. A. Elevate the head of the bed and apply high-flow oxygen
  2. B. Prepare for a STAT CT pulmonary angiography
  3. C. Administer sublingual nitroglycerin for chest pain
  4. D. Apply compression stockings to both legs

Correct Answer: A

Why A is correct:

This presentation â€” sudden dyspnea, tachycardia, chest pain, and hypoxemia in a post-surgical patient â€” is classic for pulmonary embolism (PE), the most dangerous complication of DVT. The immediate priority is oxygenation: elevate HOB to optimize breathing and apply high-flow O2 to address the life-threatening hypoxemia (SpO2 84%).

Why the others are wrong:

B. CT pulmonary angiography is the gold standard for PE diagnosis, but stabilizing the patient (airway, breathing, circulation) always comes before diagnostics.

C. NTG is for cardiac chest pain (angina). PE chest pain is caused by a mechanical obstruction, not coronary ischemia. NTG would not help and could cause hypotension.

D. Compression stockings are for DVT prevention, not PE treatment. Applying them during an acute PE event does not address the immediate life threat.

Test-Taking Tip

PE = sudden onset of the "3 D's": Dyspnea, Distress, Desaturation. Post-surgical patients and immobile patients are highest risk. Priority: ABCs first (oxygenate), then diagnose (CT-PA), then treat (anticoagulation or thrombolytics).

  3. C. "Cross your legs when sitting to improve blood flow."
  4. D. "Wear compression stockings during the day to improve circulation."

Correct Answer: B

Why B is correct:

In PAD, the goal is to promote arterial blood flow TO the ischemic extremities. Keeping legs level or slightly dependent (dangling) uses gravity to assist arterial perfusion. This is the opposite of venous disease positioning.

Why the others are wrong:

A. Leg elevation is for VENOUS disease. Elevating legs in PAD would further reduce arterial blood flow to already ischemic tissues, worsening pain and tissue damage.

C. Crossing legs compresses both arterial and venous vessels, reducing blood flow. This is harmful for both PAD and CVI patients.

D. Compression stockings are for VENOUS disease. In PAD, they would compress already narrowed arteries, further reducing blood flow and potentially causing tissue necrosis.

Test-Taking Tip

This is one of the most commonly tested PVD concepts: Arterial = legs DOWN (blood needs to get there). Venous = legs UP (blood needs to get back). Mix these up on NCLEX and you'll get it wrong every time.

6 Single Best Answer Application

A patient on heparin therapy for DVT has the following lab results: aPTT 120 seconds (therapeutic range 60-80 seconds), platelets 58,000/mmÂ³ (baseline 210,000). The nurse should FIRST:

  1. A. Continue the heparin drip and recheck labs in 6 hours
  2. B. Stop the heparin infusion and notify the provider immediately
  3. C. Administer protamine sulfate to reverse the heparin
  4. D. Reduce the heparin rate by 50% and recheck aPTT in 2 hours

Correct Answer: B

Why B is correct:

This patient shows two critical findings: a supratherapeutic aPTT (120 sec, well above the 60-80 range) AND a platelet drop >50% from baseline (210,000 â†’ 58,000). The platelet drop strongly suggests heparin-induced thrombocytopenia (HIT) â€” a life-threatening immune reaction. Heparin must be stopped IMMEDIATELY and the provider notified. HIT paradoxically causes thrombosis, not bleeding.

Why the others are wrong:

A. Continuing heparin with suspected HIT and supratherapeutic aPTT is dangerous and could cause fatal thrombosis.

C. Protamine sulfate reverses heparin's anticoagulant effect but does not treat HIT. The priority is stopping the heparin and notifying the provider, who will order alternative anticoagulation (e.g., argatroban).

D. Dose reduction is inadequate. With suspected HIT, ALL heparin products must be completely discontinued â€” not just reduced.

Test-Taking Tip

HIT red flags: Platelet drop >50% from baseline, typically 5-10 days after starting heparin. Remember: HIT causes CLOTS (not bleeding) despite low platelets. Stop ALL heparin (including flushes). Antidote for heparin = protamine. Antidote for warfarin = vitamin K.

7 Select All That Apply Application

Which components make up Virchow's Triad for venous thromboembolism risk? (Select all that apply.)

  1. A. Venous stasis (immobility, prolonged bed rest)
  2. B. Arterial plaque formation (atherosclerosis)
  3. C. Endothelial injury (trauma, surgery, IV catheters)
  4. D. Hypercoagulability (cancer, oral contraceptives, genetic disorders)

Correct Answer: A, C, D

Why A, C, D are correct:

Virchow's Triad describes the three factors that contribute to venous thrombosis: A. Venous stasis â€” blood pooling from immobility. C. Endothelial injury â€” damage to the vessel wall triggers clotting. D. Hypercoagulability â€” increased tendency for blood to clot. All three factors increase DVT risk, and they're additive.

Why B is wrong:

B. Arterial plaque formation (atherosclerosis) is the pathophysiology of peripheral ARTERIAL disease, not venous thrombosis. Virchow's Triad specifically addresses venous thromboembolism risk factors.

Test-Taking Tip

Remember SEH: Stasis + Endothelial injury + Hypercoagulability = Virchow's Triad. Common NCLEX scenarios: post-operative patients (stasis + injury), cancer patients on birth control (hypercoagulability x2), long-haul flights (stasis).

8 Priority Analysis

A patient with PAD reports a sudden onset of severe leg pain with a cold, pale, pulseless left foot. The nurse recognizes this as acute arterial occlusion. What is the priority nursing action?

  1. A. Apply warm blankets to the affected extremity
  2. B. Position the leg in a dependent position and notify the provider STAT
  3. C. Administer a dose of prescribed warfarin
  4. D. Prepare for an ABI measurement

Correct Answer: B

Why B is correct:

Acute arterial occlusion is a vascular emergency â€” the 6 P's (Pain, Pallor, Pulselessness, Paresthesia, Paralysis, Poikilothermia). The limb is at risk for irreversible ischemic damage within 4-6 hours. Position the leg dependent (gravity assists arterial flow) and notify the provider STAT for emergent intervention (embolectomy, thrombolytics, or surgical bypass).

Why the others are wrong:

A. Do NOT apply external heat to an ischemic limb. Heat increases metabolic demand in tissue that already has inadequate blood supply, accelerating tissue death. Protect with light covering only.

C. Warfarin takes 3-5 days to reach therapeutic effect and will not help in an acute emergency. Heparin or surgical intervention is needed immediately.

D. ABI is a non-emergent diagnostic tool. In acute arterial occlusion, time is tissue â€” the priority is immediate provider notification and preparation for emergent intervention.

Test-Taking Tip

Acute arterial occlusion = 6 P's = emergency. Think "time is tissue" (like "time is brain" for stroke). No heat, no elevation, no delay. Keep leg DOWN and call for help immediately. This has a 4-6 hour window before irreversible damage.

Key Takeaways

Arterial: Cool, pale, pulseless, painful with activity. Venous: Warm, brown, edematous, aching relieved by elevation.

ABI 0.9-1.3 is normal. â‰¤0.4 indicates critical limb ischemia. >1.3 suggests calcified vessels.

Intermittent claudication = PAD symptom. Predictable leg pain with walking, relieved by standing still.

Virchow's Triad: Stasis + Endothelial injury + Hypercoagulability = DVT risk.

DVT prevention: SCDs, compression stockings, pharmacologic prophylaxis, and EARLY AMBULATION.

DVT complication: Pulmonary embolism. Watch for sudden dyspnea, chest pain, tachycardia.

Position: Arterial disease - keep legs DOWN. Venous disease - keep legs UP.
