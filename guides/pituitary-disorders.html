High-Yield Summary — Review This in 60 Seconds

The must-know facts from this guide, distilled for quick review.

  • SIADH = too much ADH → water retention → dilutional hyponatremia. Think "SIADH = Serum Is Abnormally Diluted and Hyponatremic." Treatment: fluid restriction (800-1000 mL/day) + tolvaptan. Correct sodium SLOWLY.
  • DI = not enough ADH (or kidneys ignore it) → massive water loss → hypernatremia. Think "DI = Dry Inside." Output 3-20 L/day of dilute urine. Treatment: desmopressin (DDAVP) for central DI + fluid replacement.
  • SIADH and DI are OPPOSITES: SIADH has concentrated urine + low sodium. DI has dilute urine + high sodium. Lab values mirror each other.
  • Tolvaptan (Samsca) for SIADH: Blocks V2 receptors, causes water excretion. Black box warning for liver toxicity. Monitor sodium closely — don't correct >10-12 mEq/L in 24 hours (osmotic demyelination risk).
  • Post-transsphenoidal surgery #1 complication: CSF leak. Test nasal drainage for glucose (CSF is glucose-positive). Look for halo sign. No coughing, sneezing, nose blowing, bending, or straining. No toothbrushing for 2 weeks.
  • Transient DI is common after pituitary surgery. Watch for sudden urine output >200 mL/hr with low specific gravity. May need desmopressin.
  • Acromegaly: Excess GH in adults from pituitary adenoma. Enlarged hands, feet, jaw, coarsened features. Treatment: transsphenoidal surgery + octreotide (somatostatin analog).
  • Prolactinoma: Most common pituitary tumor. Women: galactorrhea + amenorrhea. Men: erectile dysfunction + decreased libido. Treatment: cabergoline (dopamine agonist).
  • Pituitary adenomas cause bitemporal hemianopsia (loss of peripheral vision bilaterally) from optic chiasm compression.
  • Sheehan syndrome: Postpartum pituitary necrosis from hemorrhage/shock during delivery → inability to lactate is often the first sign.

Hypothalamus & Pituitary Anatomy

The Master Gland System

The hypothalamus and pituitary gland form the central command center of the endocrine system. The hypothalamus, located at the base of the brain, connects the nervous system to the endocrine system. It receives signals from the brain and translates them into hormonal commands sent to the pituitary gland via the hypothalamic-hypophyseal portal system (a specialized blood vessel connection) and via direct neural connections.

The pituitary gland (hypophysis) sits in the sella turcica, a bony pocket at the base of the skull, directly below the hypothalamus. Despite being only the size of a pea (~1 cm), it controls virtually every other endocrine gland in the body. It has two functionally distinct lobes:

Anterior Pituitary (Adenohypophysis)

The anterior pituitary produces six major hormones, regulated by hypothalamic releasing and inhibiting hormones delivered through the portal blood system:

Hormone Abbreviation Target & Function Hypothalamic Control
Growth Hormone GH Liver, bones, tissues — promotes growth and metabolism GHRH (stimulates) / Somatostatin (inhibits)
Prolactin PRL Mammary glands — stimulates milk production Dopamine (inhibits) / TRH (stimulates)
Adrenocorticotropic Hormone ACTH Adrenal cortex — stimulates cortisol production CRH (stimulates)
Thyroid-Stimulating Hormone TSH Thyroid — stimulates T3/T4 production TRH (stimulates)
Follicle-Stimulating Hormone FSH Gonads — follicle development, sperm production GnRH (stimulates)
Luteinizing Hormone LH Gonads — ovulation, testosterone production GnRH (stimulates)
Memory Aid: Anterior Pituitary Hormones — "FLAT PiG"

FSH, LH, ACTH, TSH, Prolactin, Growth Hormone. These six hormones are produced by the anterior pituitary. Remember: the anterior pituitary is the "factory" that makes hormones.

Posterior Pituitary (Neurohypophysis)

The posterior pituitary does not produce hormones. Instead, it stores and releases two hormones that are manufactured in the hypothalamus and transported down nerve axons:

Hormone Other Name Function Clinical Significance
ADH Vasopressin / Arginine vasopressin (AVP) Acts on kidney collecting ducts → increases water reabsorption → concentrates urine → decreases serum osmolality Deficiency = DI. Excess = SIADH.
Oxytocin — Stimulates uterine contractions during labor; promotes milk letdown during breastfeeding Used therapeutically (Pitocin) for labor induction

ADH Regulation: The Key to SIADH & DI

ADH (antidiuretic hormone) is the single most important hormone for understanding pituitary water balance disorders. Its regulation involves:

ADH Feedback Loop

1
Osmoreceptors Detect High Serum Osmolality

Specialized neurons in the hypothalamus detect rising blood concentration (dehydration, high sodium). This triggers ADH release from the posterior pituitary.

2
ADH Acts on Kidney Collecting Ducts

ADH binds to V2 receptors on kidney collecting ducts, inserting aquaporin channels that allow water to be reabsorbed back into the bloodstream. This produces concentrated, low-volume urine.

3
Blood Volume Increases, Osmolality Decreases

As water is retained, blood becomes more dilute. Serum osmolality drops. Blood pressure rises slightly.

4
Negative Feedback Stops ADH Release

When osmolality returns to normal (~275-295 mOsm/kg), osmoreceptors signal the hypothalamus to stop ADH release. The kidneys then excrete more dilute urine.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
ADH = "Anti-Diuretic" = Anti-Peeing Hormone
Break down the name: "Anti" = against, "Diuretic" = producing urine. So ADH's job is to reduce urine output by holding onto water. When ADH is HIGH (SIADH), urine output is LOW and concentrated. When ADH is LOW or ineffective (DI), urine output is HIGH and dilute. The name tells you everything: more ADH = less pee. Less ADH = more pee.
Bottom Line: The hypothalamus makes ADH and oxytocin; the posterior pituitary just stores and releases them. The anterior pituitary produces 6 hormones (FLAT PiG). ADH controls water balance by acting on kidney collecting ducts. ADH problems cause SIADH (too much) or DI (too little/ineffective).

Diabetes Insipidus (DI)

Diabetes insipidus is a condition of ADH deficiency or resistance resulting in the kidneys' inability to concentrate urine. The result is massive output of dilute, "tasteless" urine (the Latin "insipidus" means "tasteless," as opposed to diabetes "mellitus" meaning "honey-sweet"). Patients can produce 3-20 liters of urine per day.

Memory Aid: DI = "Dry Inside"

DI = Dry Inside. The body is losing all its water through massive urination, leading to dehydration. Think of a faucet that won't turn off — water is constantly draining out.

Types of Diabetes Insipidus

Central (Neurogenic) DI

Brain Can't Make/Release ADH

  • Hypothalamus or posterior pituitary fails to produce or secrete adequate ADH
  • Causes: Head trauma, neurosurgery (esp. transsphenoidal), brain tumors, meningitis, encephalitis, aneurysm
  • Most common type overall
  • Responds to desmopressin (DDAVP) — providing synthetic ADH replaces what's missing
  • May be transient (post-surgical) or permanent
  • Water deprivation test: urine concentrates after DDAVP given
Nephrogenic DI

Kidneys Ignore ADH

  • Kidneys are resistant to ADH despite adequate ADH production
  • Causes: Lithium (#1 drug cause), demeclocycline, chronic kidney disease, hypercalcemia, hypokalemia, genetic/hereditary
  • Less common than central DI
  • Does NOT respond to desmopressin — kidneys won't listen to ADH regardless
  • Treatment: thiazide diuretics (paradoxical effect), low-sodium diet, remove offending drug
  • Water deprivation test: urine stays dilute even after DDAVP
Nursing Alert: Lithium and Nephrogenic DI

Lithium is the #1 drug cause of nephrogenic DI. Up to 40% of patients on long-term lithium develop some degree of concentrating defect. Monitor I&O and serum sodium in all patients on lithium. Educate patients to report sudden increases in thirst or urination. If nephrogenic DI develops, the prescriber must weigh the risk of continuing lithium versus the DI.

Signs and Symptoms

  • Polyuria: Large volumes of dilute urine (3-20 L/day) — the hallmark symptom
  • Polydipsia: Extreme, unrelenting thirst (compensatory mechanism)
  • Nocturia: Frequent nighttime urination disrupting sleep
  • Dehydration signs: Dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor, sunken eyes, tachycardia, hypotension
  • Hypernatremia: Serum sodium >145 mEq/L (water loss concentrates sodium)
  • Elevated serum osmolality: >295 mOsm/kg
  • Low urine specific gravity: <1.005 (very dilute urine)
  • Low urine osmolality: <200 mOsm/kg (very dilute)
  • Fatigue, irritability, muscle weakness (from dehydration and electrolyte imbalance)
  • Weight loss (from fluid loss)
  • In infants/children: Irritability, poor feeding, failure to thrive, high-pitched cry, fever, preference for water over milk

Treatment

Central DI: Desmopressin (DDAVP)

Desmopressin is a synthetic analog of ADH and is the primary treatment for central DI. It replaces the missing hormone.

  • Routes: Intranasal spray/drops (most common outpatient), oral tablets, subcutaneous injection, IV (hospital setting)
  • MOA: Binds to V2 receptors on kidney collecting ducts → inserts aquaporin channels → increases water reabsorption → concentrates urine
  • Dosing: Individualized based on urine output, usually 1-2 times daily
  • Side effects:
    • Hyponatremia (most serious — from excessive water retention)
    • Water intoxication (headache, nausea, confusion, seizures, coma)
    • Nasal congestion, irritation, epistaxis (intranasal route)
    • Headache, abdominal cramping, nausea
    • Flushing
    • Hypertension (at high doses)
  • Contraindications: Type 2B von Willebrand disease, hyponatremia, heart failure
Nursing Alert: Desmopressin Monitoring

Monitor for water intoxication and hyponatremia — the most dangerous complication of desmopressin.

  • Strict I&O (hourly initially in acute settings)
  • Daily weights (same time, same scale — report sudden gain >2 lbs/day)
  • Urine specific gravity (should increase toward normal 1.010-1.025)
  • Serum sodium levels (watch for hyponatremia — overcorrection)
  • Signs of fluid overload (edema, weight gain, hypertension, crackles)
  • Urine output (should decrease toward normal with treatment)
Patient Teaching: Desmopressin
  • Take as prescribed — do NOT skip doses (symptoms return rapidly)
  • Drink to satisfy thirst, but do not overhydrate (risk of water intoxication)
  • Weigh daily and report sudden weight gain >2 lbs/day
  • Carry medication at all times (refrigerate unused vials)
  • Wear medical alert identification
  • Avoid alcohol (interferes with ADH action)
  • Intranasal route: Prime spray before first use, blow nose gently before administering, rotate nostrils
  • Report headache, confusion, weight gain, or decreased urine output immediately
  • Lifelong therapy is usually required for permanent central DI

Nephrogenic DI Treatment

  • Remove the offending agent (discontinue lithium if possible)
  • Thiazide diuretics (hydrochlorothiazide) — paradoxically reduces urine output by promoting sodium and water reabsorption in the proximal tubule
  • Low-sodium, low-protein diet — reduces solute load on kidneys
  • NSAIDs (indomethacin) — may reduce urine output in some cases
  • Adequate fluid replacement — patient must have free access to water

Nursing Care for DI

Priority Nursing Interventions

1
Strict Intake and Output (I&O)

Monitor and record all fluid intake and urine output every 1-2 hours in acute settings. Report urine output >200 mL/hr or >4 L/day.

2
Daily Weights

Weigh at the same time daily with same clothing and scale. A sudden weight loss indicates fluid loss. 1 kg = approximately 1 liter of fluid.

3
Monitor Urine Specific Gravity

Check specific gravity with each void or per protocol. In untreated DI it will be <1.005. With effective treatment, it should rise toward normal (1.010-1.025).

4
Assess Hydration Status

Monitor for dehydration: dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor, tachycardia, hypotension, decreased LOC. Provide free access to fluids — keep water at bedside at all times.

5
Monitor Serum Sodium and Osmolality

Check labs per protocol. Report sodium >145 mEq/L or rising trend. Correct hypernatremia gradually to prevent cerebral edema.

6
Fall Precautions and Skin Care

Implement fall precautions (orthostatic hypotension from dehydration). Ensure easy access to both fluids and bathroom. Prevent skin breakdown from dehydration — apply moisturizer to dry skin and mucous membranes.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Post-Surgical DI: The 200 mL/hr Rule
After any brain surgery (especially transsphenoidal), watch for sudden onset DI. If urine output suddenly jumps to >200 mL/hr for 2+ consecutive hours with low specific gravity (<1.005), suspect DI. This is often transient (resolves in days to weeks) but may require desmopressin. Report immediately — the patient can become dangerously dehydrated within hours at these output rates.
Bottom Line: DI = massive dilute urine output + dehydration + hypernatremia. Central DI responds to desmopressin; nephrogenic DI does not (kidneys ignore ADH). Lithium is the #1 drug cause of nephrogenic DI. Nursing priorities: strict I&O, daily weights, urine specific gravity, hydration assessment, serum sodium monitoring.

SIADH (Syndrome of Inappropriate ADH)

SIADH is a condition of excess ADH secretion unrelated to the body's actual need. The result: the kidneys retain too much water, diluting the blood and causing dilutional hyponatremia — the serum sodium drops because there is too much water, not too little sodium.

Memory Aid: SIADH Mnemonics

"SIADH = Serum Is Abnormally Diluted and Hyponatremic"

"SIADH → SINK the sodium (low)" vs "DI → DRAIN the water (polyuria)"

Think of SIADH as a sponge that won't stop absorbing water — the body holds onto everything.

Pathophysiology

In SIADH, ADH is released continuously regardless of serum osmolality. The kidneys respond by reabsorbing excessive water from the collecting ducts. This causes:

  • Water retention → expanded fluid volume (but usually NO edema because the excess water distributes intracellularly)
  • Dilutional hyponatremia → sodium is diluted by the excess water (total body sodium may actually be normal)
  • Concentrated urine → urine osmolality and specific gravity are inappropriately HIGH
  • Low serum osmolality → blood is too dilute
  • High urine sodium → kidneys excrete sodium in an attempt to match the dilute blood

Causes

Category Specific Causes Key Details
Malignancies Small cell lung cancer (SCLC) #1 malignant cause. Tumor cells produce ectopic ADH. Always check chest X-ray.
CNS Disorders Head trauma, stroke, brain tumors, meningitis, encephalitis, subarachnoid hemorrhage Any brain injury can disrupt hypothalamic regulation of ADH
Medications SSRIs, carbamazepine, vincristine, cyclophosphamide, opioids, oxytocin, NSAIDs SSRIs are the #1 drug cause. Very common in elderly patients on antidepressants.
Pulmonary Pneumonia, TB, positive pressure ventilation (PPV/PEEP), lung abscess Pulmonary conditions can trigger ADH release
Post-operative General anesthesia, pain, nausea, stress response Transient SIADH is common after surgery — pain and anesthesia both stimulate ADH
NCLEX Connection: Small Cell Lung Cancer + Hyponatremia

If an NCLEX question describes a patient with lung cancer and low serum sodium, think SIADH. Small cell lung cancer is the classic ectopic ADH-producing tumor. This is a very high-yield connection.

Signs and Symptoms

Symptoms correlate with the severity and rate of sodium decline. Gradual hyponatremia may be asymptomatic; rapid drops are dangerous.

  • Mild hyponatremia (Na+ 130-135): Nausea, headache, fatigue, muscle cramps
  • Moderate hyponatremia (Na+ 120-130): Confusion, disorientation, muscle cramps, weakness, personality changes, irritability
  • Severe hyponatremia (Na+ <120): Seizures, coma, altered mental status, obtundation, respiratory arrest, death
  • Weight gain WITHOUT edema (the excess water distributes intracellularly, not interstitially)
  • Concentrated urine: Dark, low volume, specific gravity >1.030, high urine osmolality
  • Decreased urine output (body is retaining water)
Nursing Alert: Seizure Precautions

Hyponatremia <120 mEq/L = HIGH seizure risk. Implement seizure precautions immediately: padded side rails, bed in lowest position, suction equipment at bedside, IV access maintained, frequent neuro checks. Seizures from severe hyponatremia can be fatal. Never discontinue seizure precautions until sodium is stable above 125 mEq/L. Also watch for MDMA/ecstasy and tricyclic antidepressants as medication causes of SIADH.

Treatment

1. Fluid Restriction (PRIMARY Treatment)

Fluid restriction to 800-1000 mL/day is the cornerstone of SIADH management. By limiting water intake, you allow the kidneys to gradually excrete the excess water and sodium levels to normalize.

Clinical Pearl: Enforcing Fluid Restriction

Fluid restriction is one of the hardest nursing interventions to enforce. Tips: (1) Divide the allowed volume across the shift (e.g., 400 mL day, 300 mL evening, 300 mL night). (2) Use small cups. (3) Provide ice chips (count as half-volume). (4) Offer mouth care and lip moisturizer to reduce oral discomfort. (5) Educate the patient WHY restriction is necessary. (6) Include all fluids: IV fluids, ice chips, foods that melt (gelatin, ice cream), and medication flushes.

2. Tolvaptan (Samsca) — Oral Vasopressin Antagonist

  • MOA: Selective V2 receptor antagonist — blocks ADH at kidney collecting ducts → promotes excretion of free water WITHOUT sodium loss ("aquaresis" not diuresis)
  • Indication: Clinically significant euvolemic or hypervolemic hyponatremia (Na+ <125 mEq/L or symptomatic) resistant to fluid restriction
  • Dosing: Start 15 mg PO daily, can titrate to 30 mg then 60 mg based on response
  • Administration: Must be initiated IN THE HOSPITAL (REMS program requirement) for sodium monitoring. Can be taken with or without food.
  • Side effects:
    • Thirst (most common and bothersome)
    • Dry mouth, xerostomia
    • Polyuria, frequent urination, nocturia
    • Hypernatremia (if sodium rises too quickly or inadequate fluid intake)
    • Hepatotoxicity — BLACK BOX WARNING (severe liver injury reported)
    • Dehydration, hyperglycemia, constipation
  • Monitoring:
    • Serum sodium every 6-8 hours initially, then daily
    • LFTs (ALT, AST, bilirubin) at baseline and periodically
    • Monitor for overly rapid sodium correction
    • Fluid intake (patient should drink to thirst — do NOT restrict fluids on tolvaptan)
    • Urine output, signs of dehydration, mental status
  • Maximum duration: 30 days due to hepatotoxicity risk
  • Avoid grapefruit juice (CYP3A4 interaction increases drug levels)
  • Contraindications: Urgent need to raise Na+ (use hypertonic saline instead), hypovolemic hyponatremia, anuric patients, hepatic impairment, strong CYP3A4 inhibitors
Patient Teaching: Tolvaptan
  • Drink water in response to thirst (do NOT restrict fluids while on tolvaptan)
  • Report dark urine, fatigue, nausea, abdominal pain, or jaundice (liver problems)
  • Report excessive thirst or excessive urination
  • Not for urgent sodium correction (takes several hours to work)
  • Bring medication to all follow-up appointments
Nursing Alert: Osmotic Demyelination Syndrome (ODS)

CRITICAL: Do NOT correct sodium faster than 10-12 mEq/L in 24 hours. Rapid sodium correction can cause osmotic demyelination syndrome (ODS), previously called central pontine myelinolysis. This causes irreversible brain damage: dysarthria, dysphagia, quadriparesis, locked-in syndrome. It is worse than the hyponatremia itself. Correct SLOWLY and monitor sodium frequently. If sodium rises too fast, the provider may need to give hypotonic fluids (D5W) to slow correction.

3. Conivaptan (Vaprisol) — IV Vasopressin Antagonist

  • MOA: Dual V1A/V2 vasopressin receptor antagonist — IV option for hospitalized patients
  • Loading dose: 20 mg IV over 30 minutes
  • Maintenance: 20 mg continuous infusion over 24 hours (can increase to 40 mg/day)
  • Maximum duration: 4 days
  • Used for euvolemic or hypervolemic hyponatremia
  • Must be given through a large vein (risk of phlebitis at peripheral sites)
  • Hospital use only with same sodium correction limits as tolvaptan
  • Contraindications: Hypovolemic hyponatremia, anuric patients

4. Hypertonic Saline (3% NaCl) — Severe Symptomatic Hyponatremia

  • Indication: Severe symptomatic hyponatremia (seizures, coma, Na+ <120)
  • Administration: IV via infusion pump; often given as 100-150 mL boluses
  • Monitoring: Serum sodium every 1-2 hours; stop when symptoms resolve or Na+ rises 4-6 mEq/L
  • Goal: Do NOT exceed 10-12 mEq/L correction in 24 hours (ODS risk)
  • ALWAYS given in ICU setting with continuous monitoring

5. Additional Treatments

  • Treat underlying cause (e.g., treat pneumonia, remove offending medication, treat cancer)
  • Loop diuretics (furosemide) with salt replacement — promotes water excretion
  • Demeclocycline — older treatment that induces nephrogenic DI (blocks ADH at kidney)
  • High-sodium diet if chronic SIADH

Nursing Care for SIADH

Priority Nursing Interventions

1
Enforce Fluid Restriction

Strictly limit fluids to ordered amount (usually 800-1000 mL/day). Distribute across shifts. Include ALL fluid sources in the count.

2
Strict I&O Monitoring

Record all intake and output accurately. In SIADH, output should be LOW relative to a normal patient (the body is retaining water).

3
Daily Weights

Weight gain without edema is a hallmark of SIADH. Expect weight to decrease as treatment is effective.

4
Neurological Assessment

Monitor LOC, orientation, headache, confusion, muscle cramps, and seizure activity. Hyponatremia affects the brain first. Implement seizure precautions if Na+ <125.

5
Monitor Serum Sodium Frequently

Every 4-6 hours during acute management. Ensure sodium is not correcting too rapidly (<10-12 mEq/L per 24 hours). Report trends to provider.

The #1 SIADH vs DI Distinction
If you remember only one thing: SIADH = the body holds onto water (concentrated urine, low sodium, fluid restriction). DI = the body loses all its water (dilute urine, high sodium, replace fluids). They are exact opposites in every lab value and every treatment approach. SIADH restricts fluids. DI replaces fluids.
Bottom Line: SIADH = excess ADH → water retention → dilutional hyponatremia + concentrated urine. Treatment: fluid restriction first, then tolvaptan or hypertonic saline for severe cases. Correct sodium SLOWLY (<10-12 mEq/L/24hr) to prevent osmotic demyelination. #1 malignant cause: small cell lung cancer. #1 drug cause: SSRIs.

SIADH vs DI Comparison

SIADH and DI are opposite disorders of the same hormone (ADH). This comparison is one of the highest-yield topics for NCLEX. Every lab value, symptom, and treatment is reversed.

SIADH (Too Much ADH)

Water Retention & Dilution

  • Urine output: ↓ Decreased (oliguric)
  • Urine concentration: ↑ Concentrated (dark, high SG >1.030)
  • Serum sodium: ↓ LOW (<135 mEq/L)
  • Serum osmolality: ↓ LOW (<275 mOsm/kg)
  • Urine osmolality: ↑ HIGH
  • Weight: Gain (without edema)
  • Thirst: Not prominent
  • Key symptoms: Confusion, seizures, N/V
  • Treatment: RESTRICT fluids
  • Medications: Tolvaptan, hypertonic saline
DI (Not Enough ADH)

Water Loss & Dehydration

  • Urine output: ↑↑ Massively increased (3-20 L/day)
  • Urine concentration: ↓ Very dilute (clear, SG <1.005)
  • Serum sodium: ↑ HIGH (>145 mEq/L)
  • Serum osmolality: ↑ HIGH (>295 mOsm/kg)
  • Urine osmolality: ↓ LOW
  • Weight: Loss (dehydration)
  • Thirst: Extreme polydipsia
  • Key symptoms: Polyuria, dehydration, thirst
  • Treatment: REPLACE fluids
  • Medications: Desmopressin (DDAVP)

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
NCLEX Quick Decision: "Is This SIADH or DI?"
On the NCLEX, check TWO things: (1) Urine output — high or low? (2) Serum sodium — high or low? If urine is LOW and sodium is LOW = SIADH (holding onto water). If urine is HIGH and sodium is HIGH = DI (losing water). Then match the treatment: SIADH = restrict fluids (stop adding water). DI = give fluids + desmopressin (replace the water). Everything else follows logically from these two data points.
Bottom Line: SIADH and DI are mirror images. Every lab value is opposite. SIADH = water retention, low sodium, concentrated urine, restrict fluids. DI = water loss, high sodium, dilute urine, replace fluids. On the NCLEX: check urine output + serum sodium to differentiate instantly.

NCLEX Strategy: Serum and Urine Move in Opposite Directions

In both SIADH and DI, the serum and urine values are OPPOSITES of each other. In SIADH: serum osmolality is LOW while urine osmolality is HIGH (body retains water in blood, concentrates urine). In DI: serum osmolality is HIGH while urine osmolality is LOW (body is dehydrated, urine is dilute). This "opposite" pattern is consistent across all lab values.

Serum and urine always move in opposite directions. IGF-1 is the best screening test for acromegaly. Prolactin levels >200 ng/mL are virtually diagnostic of prolactinoma.

Growth Hormone Disorders

Acromegaly (GH Excess in Adults)

Acromegaly occurs when excessive growth hormone (GH) is produced after growth plate closure in adults, almost always caused by a GH-secreting pituitary adenoma. Since bones can no longer grow in length, the excess GH causes thickening and enlargement of soft tissues, cartilage, and bone.

Key Features of Acromegaly

  • Enlarged hands and feet — rings and shoes no longer fit (gradual onset over years)
  • Coarsened facial features: Broad nose, prominent brow ridge (frontal bossing), enlarged jaw (prognathism), thickened lips, widened spacing between teeth
  • Enlarged tongue (macroglossia) — contributes to sleep apnea and voice deepening
  • Thick, oily skin; excessive sweating; skin tags
  • Joint pain and arthritis (osteoarthritis from cartilage overgrowth)
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome (from soft tissue swelling)
  • Sleep apnea (from macroglossia and soft tissue enlargement)
  • Hypertension and cardiomegaly → eventually heart failure
  • Insulin resistance / diabetes mellitus (GH is counter-regulatory to insulin)
  • Headaches and visual disturbances (if adenoma is large enough to compress optic chiasm)
  • Deepened voice (vocal cord thickening)
Clinical Pearl: Slow Onset Recognition

Acromegaly develops so gradually that patients and families may not notice the changes. Average time from symptom onset to diagnosis is 7-10 years. Comparing old photographs is one of the most effective ways to recognize the progressive facial changes. On the NCLEX, look for: "Patient reports rings and shoes feel tight" or "spouse noticed facial features have changed."

Diagnosis

  • IGF-1 level (Somatomedin C): Most reliable screening test; elevated in acromegaly. IGF-1 is produced by the liver in response to GH and has a stable level (unlike GH, which is pulsatile).
  • Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT): Confirmatory test. Normally, a glucose load suppresses GH to <1 ng/mL. In acromegaly, GH fails to suppress (remains elevated despite glucose administration).
  • MRI of the pituitary: Identifies the adenoma size and location.

Treatment

  • Transsphenoidal surgery — first-line treatment to remove the pituitary adenoma

Octreotide (Sandostatin) — Somatostatin Analog

Used when surgery is unsuccessful or the patient is not a surgical candidate:

  • MOA: Mimics somatostatin → suppresses GH and IGF-1 release from anterior pituitary
  • Routes: Subcutaneous injection (short-acting, 2-3x daily) OR IM injection (LAR — long-acting release, once monthly)
  • Side effects:
    • GI upset (nausea, diarrhea, abdominal cramping) — most common
    • Gallstones/cholelithiasis (decreased gallbladder motility — very common with long-term use)
    • Hyperglycemia (suppresses insulin secretion — monitor blood glucose)
    • Bradycardia, arrhythmias
    • Injection site pain
    • Hypothyroidism (monitor TSH)
  • Monitoring: GH and IGF-1 levels, blood glucose, gallbladder ultrasound (baseline and periodic), ECG, thyroid function
  • Administration: Rotate injection sites, refrigerate medication, bring to room temperature before injection

Lanreotide (Somatuline Depot)

  • Same drug class as octreotide (somatostatin analog) with similar effects
  • Deep subcutaneous injection every 4 weeks
  • Same side effects and monitoring as octreotide

Pegvisomant (Somavert) — GH Receptor Antagonist

  • MOA: Blocks GH receptors at target tissues → normalizes IGF-1 levels
  • Route: Daily subcutaneous injection
  • Note: Does NOT shrink the pituitary tumor (only blocks GH action peripherally)
  • Side effects: Elevated liver enzymes (monitor LFTs), injection site reactions, flu-like symptoms
  • Monitoring: LFTs (baseline and monthly for 6 months), IGF-1 levels, periodic MRI of pituitary (tumor may grow since GH feedback is disrupted)

Other Options

  • Cabergoline — dopamine agonist; may help if tumor co-secretes prolactin (adjunct therapy, less effective than somatostatin analogs)
  • Radiation therapy — adjunct if surgery and medications are insufficient

Gigantism (GH Excess in Children)

Gigantism occurs when excess GH is produced before growth plate closure in children and adolescents. Because the growth plates are still open, the bones grow excessively in length, resulting in extreme height (some patients reaching 7-8+ feet).

  • Same causes as acromegaly (usually pituitary adenoma)
  • Proportional overgrowth of the entire body (unlike acromegaly's localized thickening)
  • Treatment: Same as acromegaly — surgery, somatostatin analogs, radiation
  • Early treatment is critical to prevent extreme height and complications

Growth Hormone Deficiency

GH Deficiency in Children

Growth Failure

  • Short stature (falling below growth curve percentiles)
  • Delayed puberty
  • Proportional body — short but normal proportions
  • "Cherubic" (baby-faced) appearance
  • Delayed bone age on X-ray
  • Normal intelligence
  • Treatment: Somatropin (recombinant GH)
GH Deficiency in Adults

Metabolic Changes

  • Decreased muscle mass and strength
  • Increased body fat (especially abdominal/visceral)
  • Fatigue and decreased exercise tolerance
  • Decreased bone density (osteoporosis risk)
  • Elevated LDL cholesterol
  • Decreased quality of life, depressed mood
  • Treatment: Somatropin if symptomatic

Somatropin (Recombinant Human Growth Hormone)

  • Route: Subcutaneous injection, usually given at bedtime (mimics natural GH secretion pattern, which peaks during sleep)
  • Dosing: Weight-based, individualized; requires periodic adjustment as child grows
  • Mechanism: Replaces deficient GH → stimulates IGF-1 production → promotes linear growth (children) and metabolic function
  • Side effects:
    • Joint and muscle pain, arthralgias
    • Peripheral edema, fluid retention
    • Carpal tunnel syndrome
    • Insulin resistance, hyperglycemia (monitor blood glucose)
    • Increased intracranial hypertension (headaches, papilledema, vision changes)
    • Hypothyroidism (GH can unmask hypothyroidism — monitor TSH)
    • Slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE) in children — hip/knee pain, limp
    • Injection site reactions
    • Pancreatitis (rare)
  • Monitoring:
    • Growth rate and height (children) — measure every 3-6 months
    • IGF-1 levels (target normal range for age)
    • Blood glucose, HbA1c
    • Thyroid function tests (TSH, free T4)
    • Bone age X-rays (children — assess growth plate status)
    • Fundoscopic exam (check for papilledema — intracranial hypertension)
    • Hip X-rays if child reports hip/knee pain (SCFE screening)
    • Monitor for scoliosis progression in children
  • Contraindications: Active malignancy (GH can stimulate tumor growth), acute critical illness, diabetic retinopathy, closed epiphyses (for growth indication in children)
Patient/Family Teaching: Somatropin
  • Proper subcutaneous injection technique (teach child self-injection when age-appropriate)
  • Rotate injection sites (abdomen, thighs, buttocks, upper arms)
  • Store in refrigerator (do not freeze), protect from light
  • Once reconstituted, use within specified time per manufacturer
  • Administer at the same time daily (preferably bedtime)
  • Report immediately: Hip/knee pain or limp (SCFE), severe headache or vision changes (intracranial hypertension)
  • Monitor blood glucose (especially if diabetic or family history)
  • Regular follow-up appointments are essential for growth monitoring
  • In children: treatment may continue for years until growth plates close
  • This is an expensive medication — explore financial assistance programs
Nursing Alert: SCFE in Children on Growth Hormone

Slipped capital femoral epiphysis (SCFE) is an orthopedic emergency that can occur in children receiving growth hormone therapy. Watch for: new onset limp, hip/knee/groin pain, limited hip range of motion, external rotation of the affected leg. Report immediately — requires surgical intervention. Risk is higher during rapid growth periods.

Bottom Line: Acromegaly = GH excess in adults (enlarged hands, feet, facial features, organ enlargement). Gigantism = GH excess in children (extreme height). IGF-1 is the best screening test; OGTT is confirmatory. Treatment: surgery first, then octreotide. GH deficiency: short stature in children, metabolic changes in adults. Treat with somatropin at bedtime.

Hyperprolactinemia

Hyperprolactinemia is excess prolactin in the blood, most commonly caused by a prolactinoma (prolactin-secreting pituitary adenoma). Prolactinomas are the most common type of pituitary tumor, accounting for about 40% of all pituitary adenomas.

Causes

  • Prolactinoma — most common cause of pathological hyperprolactinemia
  • Medications: Antipsychotics (haloperidol, risperidone), metoclopramide, methyldopa — these block dopamine, which normally inhibits prolactin
  • Stalk effect: Any pituitary mass that compresses the pituitary stalk can prevent dopamine from reaching prolactin-secreting cells, causing mild hyperprolactinemia
  • Hypothyroidism: Elevated TRH stimulates prolactin release (always check TSH)
  • Pregnancy and breastfeeding: Physiological (normal) cause of elevated prolactin

Signs and Symptoms

Women Men Both Sexes (Large Tumors)
Galactorrhea (spontaneous milky nipple discharge) Erectile dysfunction Headaches (mass effect)
Amenorrhea or oligomenorrhea Decreased libido Visual field defects (bitemporal hemianopsia)
Infertility Gynecomastia (rare) Hypopituitarism (compression of normal pituitary)
Decreased bone density Infertility, decreased sperm count Cranial nerve palsies (very large tumors)
NCLEX Connection: Galactorrhea + Amenorrhea

A woman presenting with milky nipple discharge and absent menstrual periods = think prolactinoma. Always rule out pregnancy first, then check prolactin level. This is a classic NCLEX presentation. In men, prolactinomas are often diagnosed later because symptoms (ED, decreased libido) are less specific, so tumors tend to be larger at diagnosis.

Treatment

Cabergoline (Dostinex) — Preferred Dopamine Agonist

  • MOA: Dopamine D2 receptor agonist — activates dopamine receptors on pituitary lactotroph cells → inhibits prolactin secretion AND shrinks the tumor
  • Dosing: 0.25 mg twice weekly, can increase gradually based on prolactin levels
  • Advantages: More effective and better tolerated than bromocriptine; shrinks tumors in up to 90% of cases; twice-weekly dosing improves adherence
  • Side effects:
    • Nausea, vomiting (take with food to reduce)
    • Orthostatic hypotension, dizziness (rise slowly)
    • Headache, fatigue
    • Cardiac valvulopathy (rare at prolactinoma doses, more concern with high Parkinson's doses)
    • Impulse control disorders (gambling, hypersexuality — rare but report immediately)
  • Monitoring: Prolactin levels, pregnancy test (fertility may return), blood pressure, echocardiogram (if long-term high-dose therapy)
Patient Teaching: Cabergoline
  • Take with food to reduce nausea
  • Rise slowly from sitting/lying (orthostatic hypotension)
  • Use contraception if pregnancy is not desired (fertility may return as prolactin normalizes)
  • May take weeks to months for symptoms to improve
  • Report severe headache, vision changes, or unusual urges/behaviors

Bromocriptine (Parlodel) — Alternative Dopamine Agonist

  • MOA: Same as cabergoline (dopamine D2 agonist)
  • Dosing: Daily (1-2 times daily) — more frequent than cabergoline
  • More side effects than cabergoline: nausea, vomiting, orthostatic hypotension, headache, fatigue, nasal congestion
  • Less preferred due to more side effects and more frequent dosing
  • May be preferred in pregnancy (has more safety data than cabergoline in pregnant patients)
  • Patient teaching: Take with food to reduce GI side effects; rise slowly from sitting/lying; may take weeks to see full effect

Surgery

  • Transsphenoidal surgery is reserved for cases that fail medical therapy or cannot tolerate dopamine agonists
  • Also indicated for large tumors causing acute visual loss
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Prolactinoma: Medication First, Surgery Second
Unlike most pituitary tumors, prolactinomas are treated with MEDICATION first, not surgery. Dopamine agonists (cabergoline) are remarkably effective at both normalizing prolactin AND shrinking the tumor. Surgery is second-line. This is the opposite approach from acromegaly or Cushing's disease where surgery is first-line. Remember: prolactinoma = pills first, acromegaly = surgery first.
Bottom Line: Prolactinoma is the most common pituitary tumor. Women: galactorrhea + amenorrhea + infertility. Men: ED + decreased libido. Treatment: dopamine agonists first (cabergoline preferred over bromocriptine). Medication shrinks the tumor in most cases. Surgery is reserved for medication failure.

Cushing's Disease & Hypopituitarism

Cushing's Disease (Pituitary ACTH Excess)

Cushing's disease specifically refers to excess cortisol caused by an ACTH-secreting pituitary adenoma. The tumor produces too much ACTH, which drives the adrenal glands to overproduce cortisol. This is a subset of Cushing's syndrome (which includes all causes of excess cortisol).

Clinical Pearl: Cushing's Disease vs Syndrome

Cushing's disease = pituitary cause (ACTH-secreting adenoma). ACTH is HIGH because the pituitary tumor is producing it. Cushing's syndrome = any cause of excess cortisol (including exogenous steroids, adrenal tumors). If ACTH is LOW, the cause is adrenal (ACTH is suppressed by the high cortisol). If ACTH is HIGH, the cause is pituitary or ectopic. See the Adrenal Disorders guide for comprehensive Cushing's syndrome coverage.

Classic Cushing's Features (Review)

  • Moon face (rounded facial fullness)
  • Buffalo hump (fat pad on upper back)
  • Truncal obesity with thin extremities
  • Purple striae on abdomen, thighs, breasts
  • Easy bruising, thin fragile skin
  • Hyperglycemia / diabetes (cortisol raises blood glucose)
  • Hypertension
  • Osteoporosis (cortisol breaks down bone)
  • Immunosuppression (increased infection risk)
  • Muscle wasting and weakness (protein catabolism)
  • Emotional lability, depression, psychosis

Diagnosis

  • 24-hour urine free cortisol: Elevated
  • Late-night salivary cortisol: Elevated (normally cortisol is lowest at midnight)
  • Low-dose dexamethasone suppression test: Cortisol fails to suppress
  • ACTH level: HIGH in Cushing's disease (pituitary origin)
  • MRI pituitary: Identifies the adenoma

Treatment

Transsphenoidal surgery to remove the ACTH-secreting pituitary adenoma is the first-line treatment. Post-operatively, patients will need temporary cortisol replacement (the remaining pituitary and adrenals are suppressed from chronic excess).

Hypopituitarism (Panhypopituitarism)

Hypopituitarism is the decreased or absent function of one or more pituitary hormones. When ALL anterior pituitary hormones are deficient, it is called panhypopituitarism.

Causes

  • Pituitary tumors — most common cause (tumor compresses normal tissue)
  • Pituitary surgery or radiation
  • Sheehan syndrome — postpartum pituitary necrosis from massive hemorrhage/shock during delivery
  • Traumatic brain injury
  • Sarcoidosis, hemochromatosis, autoimmune hypophysitis
Nursing Alert: Sheehan Syndrome

Sheehan syndrome is postpartum pituitary necrosis. The pituitary gland enlarges during pregnancy and is vulnerable to ischemia if severe hemorrhage occurs during or after delivery. The first sign is often inability to lactate (due to prolactin deficiency). Other signs develop gradually: fatigue, amenorrhea, hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency. If a postpartum patient reports she "can't produce milk" after significant birth hemorrhage, suspect Sheehan syndrome.

Symptoms Depend on Which Hormones Are Deficient

Deficient Hormone Resulting Condition Key Symptoms
ACTH Secondary adrenal insufficiency Fatigue, weakness, hypotension, hypoglycemia. No hyperpigmentation (unlike primary Addison's).
TSH Secondary hypothyroidism Fatigue, cold intolerance, weight gain, constipation
GH GH deficiency Children: growth failure. Adults: decreased muscle mass, increased body fat
FSH/LH Hypogonadism Amenorrhea, infertility, decreased libido, erectile dysfunction
Prolactin Prolactin deficiency Inability to lactate (often the first sign in Sheehan syndrome)
ADH Diabetes insipidus Polyuria, polydipsia (posterior pituitary involvement)

Treatment: Hormone Replacement

Treatment is lifelong replacement of the deficient hormones. Critical order: Always replace cortisol FIRST before thyroid hormone. Starting thyroid hormone in a cortisol-deficient patient can precipitate adrenal crisis (thyroid hormone increases metabolic demand, exhausting the already insufficient adrenal glands).

Bottom Line: Cushing's disease = pituitary ACTH-secreting adenoma causing cortisol excess (ACTH is HIGH). Treatment: transsphenoidal surgery. Hypopituitarism = deficiency of one or more pituitary hormones. Sheehan syndrome = postpartum pituitary necrosis (inability to lactate is the first sign). Replace cortisol BEFORE thyroid hormone.

Pituitary Adenomas & Tumors

Pituitary adenomas are benign tumors of the pituitary gland. They are classified by size and function:

Classification

By Size By Function Most Common Types
Microadenoma: <10 mm Functioning: Secretes excess hormone 1. Prolactinoma (40% of all pituitary adenomas)
Macroadenoma: ≥10 mm Non-functioning: Does not secrete hormones; causes symptoms by mass effect 2. GH-secreting (acromegaly) 3. ACTH-secreting (Cushing's disease)

Mass Effect Symptoms

As a pituitary tumor grows, it can compress surrounding structures:

Bitemporal Hemianopsia (Classic Finding)

The optic chiasm sits directly above the pituitary gland. An upward-growing tumor compresses the optic chiasm from below, causing bitemporal hemianopsia — loss of the outer (temporal) visual fields in both eyes. Patients describe "tunnel vision" — they can see what's directly in front of them but lose peripheral vision bilaterally.

NCLEX Connection: Visual Field Defects

If an NCLEX question describes a patient with "bumping into things on the side" or "loss of peripheral vision in both eyes," think pituitary tumor compressing the optic chiasm. Bitemporal hemianopsia is virtually pathognomonic for a pituitary macroadenoma. This is a high-yield connection. The nurse should perform visual field testing (confrontation test) as part of the neurological assessment.

Other Mass Effect Symptoms

  • Headaches (from stretching of the diaphragma sellae)
  • Cranial nerve palsies (if tumor extends into cavernous sinus — CN III, IV, VI involvement causing diplopia)
  • Hypopituitarism (compression of normal pituitary tissue)
  • CSF leak (if tumor erodes through the floor of the sella)
  • Pituitary apoplexy: Sudden hemorrhage into a pituitary adenoma — causes sudden severe headache, visual loss, altered consciousness, and acute hypopituitarism. This is a medical emergency requiring emergent surgery and corticosteroid administration.

Diagnosis

  • MRI with gadolinium contrast — imaging modality of choice
  • Comprehensive hormone panel (GH/IGF-1, prolactin, ACTH, cortisol, TSH, free T4, FSH, LH, testosterone/estradiol)
  • Visual field testing (formal perimetry)
Bottom Line: Pituitary adenomas are benign but cause problems through hormone excess or mass effect. Bitemporal hemianopsia (loss of peripheral vision bilaterally) from optic chiasm compression is the classic finding. Prolactinomas are the most common type. Pituitary apoplexy (hemorrhage into tumor) is an emergency. MRI is the diagnostic imaging of choice.

Transsphenoidal Hypophysectomy

Transsphenoidal hypophysectomy is the surgical removal of pituitary tumors through the nose and sphenoid sinus — a minimally invasive approach that avoids craniotomy. This is the first-line surgical treatment for most pituitary adenomas (except prolactinomas, which respond to medication first).

Pre-Operative Care

  • Complete hormone level panel (baseline for post-op comparison)
  • Visual field testing and ophthalmologic exam (baseline visual acuity and fields)
  • Baseline neurological assessment — document LOC, cranial nerve function, and current symptoms
  • MRI/CT review with surgical team
  • NPO status per protocol
  • Stress-dose steroids may be needed if cortisol deficiency is suspected (prevent adrenal crisis perioperatively)
  • Patient teaching (CRITICAL):
    • Explain surgical approach (through nose, no visible incision)
    • Post-op expectations: nasal packing, mouth-breathing, activity restrictions
    • Instruct on the "5 No's": no coughing, sneezing, nose blowing, bending, or straining
    • Teach open-mouth sneezing technique if they must sneeze
    • Importance of reporting any clear nasal drainage, headache, or vision changes

Post-Operative Positioning

  • Head of bed elevated 30-45 degrees at all times (reduces intracranial pressure and promotes drainage)
  • Avoid hip flexion >90 degrees
  • No flat positioning
  • Turn side to side carefully

Post-Operative Complications

Nursing Alert: CSF Leak — The Most Serious Complication

CSF leak is the most dangerous post-transsphenoidal surgery complication. It creates a direct pathway for bacteria to enter the CNS, causing meningitis.

  • Signs: Clear, watery nasal drainage; constant postnasal drip; severe headache that worsens when upright; patient reports "salty taste" in throat
  • Test drainage for glucose: CSF contains glucose (test with glucometer or Dextrostix). Mucus does NOT contain glucose.
  • Halo sign: If drainage is bloody, look for a yellowish ring surrounding the blood on gauze or linen — this indicates CSF mixed with blood.
  • Action: Report IMMEDIATELY. Patient may need surgical repair. Keep HOB elevated. Avoid any action that increases ICP.
Nursing Alert: Transient Diabetes Insipidus

DI occurs in up to 30% of patients after transsphenoidal surgery due to manipulation of the posterior pituitary or stalk. Monitor for:

  • Sudden large urine output (>200 mL/hr for 2+ consecutive hours)
  • Very dilute urine (specific gravity <1.005)
  • Rising serum sodium
  • Excessive thirst

Often transient (resolves within days to weeks). May require temporary desmopressin. Some cases become permanent requiring lifelong DDAVP.

Clinical Pearl: The Triphasic Pattern

Some patients experience a triphasic endocrine response after pituitary surgery: (1) DI phase (days 1-5): transient polyuria from pituitary stalk swelling → (2) SIADH phase (days 5-10): unregulated ADH release from damaged cells → (3) Permanent DI: if the stalk damage is severe. Monitor sodium and urine output closely through all three phases. The SIADH phase is the most dangerous because clinicians may not expect the sudden shift from polyuria to hyponatremia.

Other Post-Operative Complications

3. Hemorrhage

  • Rare but can cause airway compromise or increased ICP
  • Signs: Increased bleeding from nose, facial/nasal swelling, tachycardia, hypotension, decreased LOC
  • Actions: Notify surgeon stat, prepare for potential airway management, apply ice packs

4. Meningitis

  • From CSF leak providing a pathway for bacteria, or contamination during surgery
  • Signs: Fever, severe headache, nuchal rigidity (stiff neck), photophobia, altered mental status
  • Classic signs: Positive Brudzinski's sign (neck flexion causes hip flexion) and Kernig's sign (resistance to knee extension with hip flexed)
  • Actions: Notify MD immediately, blood cultures, lumbar puncture, start antibiotics emergently
  • Prevention: Aseptic technique, prophylactic antibiotics if CSF leak present

5. Increased Intracranial Pressure (ICP)

  • From cerebral edema, hematoma, or hydrocephalus
  • Signs: Decreased LOC, headache, vomiting, papilledema
  • Cushing's triad (late, ominous sign): Bradycardia + hypertension + irregular respirations
  • Actions: Maintain HOB elevation, notify MD stat, prepare for emergency imaging

6. Visual Changes

  • Vision should improve post-op if tumor was compressing optic chiasm
  • New or worsening vision changes are concerning (hemorrhage, edema, nerve damage)
  • Assess visual acuity and visual fields regularly and compare to pre-operative baseline

7. Hormonal Deficiencies (Hypopituitarism)

  • Can occur from surgical damage to remaining normal pituitary tissue
  • May develop immediately or over weeks to months
  • Monitor for:
    • Adrenal insufficiency (hypotension, hypoglycemia, fatigue) — most dangerous
    • Hypothyroidism (fatigue, bradycardia, cold intolerance)
    • GH deficiency, gonadal deficiency (amenorrhea, decreased libido)
  • Treatment: Lifelong hormone replacement therapy as needed
  • Important: Patients with ACTH deficiency need stress-dose steroids for illness, surgery, or physiologic stress

Post-Operative Nursing Care

Priority Nursing Interventions

1
Monitor for CSF Leak

Assess nasal drainage frequently. Test clear drainage for glucose. Look for halo sign. Report immediately if suspected. Place a mustache dressing under the nose and document drainage amount and character.

2
Strict I&O and Urine Specific Gravity

Monitor urine output hourly. Check specific gravity every void or per protocol. Watch for sudden polyuria (DI) or oliguria (SIADH). Weigh daily.

3
Neurological Assessment

Assess LOC, orientation, pupil response, visual acuity, visual fields, and cranial nerve function every 1-2 hours initially. Compare to pre-operative baseline.

4
Nasal Packing Care

Nasal packing is usually placed during surgery and removed in 2-3 days. Do NOT remove or manipulate. Monitor for bleeding. Patient must mouth-breathe while packing is in place.

5
Oral Care

No toothbrushing upper teeth for approximately 2 weeks (to avoid disrupting the surgical site in the upper gum line/sphenoid area). Use gentle mouth rinses only (saline or dilute mouthwash). Soft diet initially. Avoid hot liquids initially. No drinking through straws for the first week (creates negative pressure that can disrupt surgical site). Provide lip care for mouth-breathing dryness.

6
Prevent ICP Elevation

Keep HOB elevated 30-45°. Provide stool softeners (prevent straining/Valsalva). Remind patient: NO coughing, sneezing, nose blowing, bending forward at waist, or heavy lifting (>10 lbs for 2-4 weeks). Teach open-mouth sneezing technique.

7
Pain Management

Headache is common (from surgery and sinus pressure). Administer acetaminophen or opioids as ordered. Ice packs to face and forehead. Avoid aspirin and NSAIDs initially (increased bleeding risk).

8
Emergency Equipment

Keep suction equipment at bedside (gentle oral suctioning only — NEVER nasal suctioning). Supplemental oxygen available. Monitor glucose if on corticosteroids.

Discharge Teaching: Post-Transsphenoidal Surgery
  • Activity restrictions with timeframes (no nose blowing for 2-4 weeks, no heavy lifting for 2-4 weeks, no bending at waist for 2 weeks)
  • Oral care instructions (when toothbrushing can resume)
  • Report immediately: Clear nasal drainage (CSF leak), fever/stiff neck (meningitis), sudden increase in urination (DI), vision changes, severe headache
  • Hormone replacement medications as prescribed (may be lifelong)
  • Medical alert identification (especially if ACTH-deficient — needs stress-dose steroids)
  • Follow-up appointments with endocrinologist, neurosurgeon, and ophthalmologist
  • May need lifelong hormone monitoring and periodic MRI surveillance
  • Steroid stress dosing education if ACTH-deficient (double dose for illness, injectable for emergencies)

Pediatric Considerations

Growth Hormone Deficiency in Children

GH deficiency is the most common pituitary disorder in children. Key pediatric considerations:

  • Growth velocity is the most sensitive indicator — a declining growth rate crossing percentile lines is more concerning than absolute short stature
  • Bone age X-ray (left wrist) shows delayed bone maturation — key diagnostic tool
  • GH stimulation testing required for diagnosis (single random GH levels are unreliable due to pulsatile secretion)
  • Somatropin dosing is weight-based and adjusted as the child grows — requires frequent dose adjustments
  • Monitor for SCFE (slipped capital femoral epiphysis) — hip/knee pain, limping during growth hormone therapy
  • Treatment is most effective when started early, before growth plates close
  • Psychosocial support is essential — short stature can cause bullying, social isolation, and low self-esteem in school-age children
  • Adolescent adherence may decline — involve them in self-management and explain the importance of consistent injections
DI and SIADH in Children
  • Central DI in children is most commonly caused by CNS tumors (craniopharyngioma is the classic pediatric pituitary region tumor), head trauma, or neurosurgery
  • Infants with DI cannot express thirst — present with irritability, poor feeding, failure to thrive, fever, and dehydration. They may have a preference for water over formula/milk.
  • Desmopressin dosing in children requires careful titration — children are more susceptible to water intoxication and hyponatremia
  • SIADH in children can occur with meningitis, pneumonia, or post-operatively — hyponatremia may cause seizures more quickly in children due to smaller body water reserves
  • School-age children with DI need bathroom access plans — frequent urination should not be restricted. Communicate with school nurses about the condition.
  • Nocturnal enuresis (bedwetting) may be a presenting symptom of DI in older children
Craniopharyngioma — The Classic Pediatric Pituitary Tumor

Craniopharyngioma is the most common pituitary region tumor in children (peak age 5-14 years). Unlike adult pituitary adenomas, these arise from embryonic tissue near the pituitary stalk. Key features:

  • Often causes growth failure and delayed puberty as first signs
  • Visual field defects (bitemporal hemianopsia) from optic chiasm compression
  • Diabetes insipidus commonly develops either from the tumor or after surgical removal
  • Headaches and signs of increased ICP
  • Treatment: surgical resection (often incomplete due to location) ± radiation
  • Post-operative panhypopituitarism is common — lifelong hormone replacement therapy
Bottom Line: GH deficiency is the most common pediatric pituitary disorder — growth velocity decline is the key red flag. Craniopharyngioma is the classic pediatric pituitary tumor. Infants with DI present with irritability and failure to thrive (they can't tell you they're thirsty). Children on desmopressin need careful monitoring for water intoxication.

Memory Tricks & Quick Review

Use these mnemonics and memory tricks to quickly recall key concepts during exams.

SIADH vs DI Mnemonics
  • "SIADH = Serum Is Abnormally Diluted and Hyponatremic"
  • "DI = Dry Inside" (dehydration) — "Drinks like crazy" (polydipsia)
  • "SIADH → SINK the sodium (low). DI → DRAIN the water (polyuria)"
  • ADH = "Anti-Peeing Hormone" — More ADH = less pee (SIADH). Less ADH = more pee (DI).
Anterior Pituitary Hormones: "FLAT PiG"

FSH, LH, ACTH, TSH, Prolactin, Growth Hormone — the 6 hormones produced by the anterior pituitary.

Post-Transsphenoidal Surgery: "5 No's + 1"

No nose blowing, No coughing, No sneezing (open mouth), No bending, No straining + No toothbrushing (upper teeth for 2 weeks). Plus: HOB UP, check for CSF (clear drainage + glucose positive), monitor for DI.

Medication-Disorder Quick Match
  • Desmopressin = DI (replaces ADH)
  • Tolvaptan = SIADH (blocks ADH at V2 receptor)
  • Octreotide = Acromegaly (suppresses GH)
  • Somatropin = GH deficiency (replaces GH)
  • Cabergoline = Prolactinoma (dopamine agonist inhibits prolactin)

Remember: each drug is the opposite action of the disease process.

Post-Surgical Complication Quick Table

Complication Key Sign to Watch For Immediate Action
CSF Leak Clear nasal drainage, halo sign, glucose-positive drainage Notify MD stat, HOB up, no ICP-raising activities
Diabetes Insipidus Urine >200 mL/hr, specific gravity <1.005 Notify MD, prepare desmopressin, replace fluids
SIADH Decreased urine output, falling serum sodium, confusion Fluid restriction, check sodium, seizure precautions
Meningitis Fever, nuchal rigidity, photophobia, Brudzinski's/Kernig's signs Notify MD stat, cultures, emergent antibiotics
Hemorrhage Increased nasal bleeding, tachycardia, hypotension Notify surgeon stat, prepare for airway management
Increased ICP Decreased LOC, Cushing's triad (bradycardia, HTN, irregular resp) Maintain HOB elevation, notify MD, prepare for imaging
Bottom Line: Use "FLAT PiG" for anterior pituitary hormones. SIADH = SINK sodium (low), DI = DRAIN water (polyuria). Post-surgery: 5 No's + glucose test for CSF. Match each medication to its disorder by remembering each drug reverses the disease process.

Key Takeaways

SIADH = water retention. Low sodium, concentrated urine. Treatment: fluid restriction + tolvaptan. Correct Na+ SLOWLY.

DI = water loss. High sodium, dilute urine. Treatment: fluid replacement + desmopressin (central DI).

SIADH and DI are opposites. Every lab value, symptom, and treatment is reversed between them.

ODS risk: Don't correct sodium >10-12 mEq/L in 24 hours. Irreversible brain damage.

Post-transsphenoidal #1: CSF leak. Test nasal drainage for glucose. Halo sign. 5 No's.

Acromegaly: Enlarged hands, feet, jaw. IGF-1 screening. OGTT confirms. Surgery first-line.

Prolactinoma: Galactorrhea + amenorrhea. Most common pituitary tumor. Cabergoline first-line (not surgery).

Bitemporal hemianopsia: Loss of peripheral vision bilaterally = pituitary tumor compressing optic chiasm.

Sheehan syndrome: Postpartum pituitary necrosis. Inability to lactate is often the first sign.

Med matching: Desmopressin=DI, Tolvaptan=SIADH, Octreotide=Acromegaly, Somatropin=GH deficiency, Cabergoline=Prolactinoma.
