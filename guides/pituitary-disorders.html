01

What actually gets you

This looks like a topic with a dozen disorders in it. Most of it is one hormone, ADH, running in two directions.

Learn that pair properly and the rest is short. A few tumours, and the drug each one needs. Points get lost here in three predictable ways.

You memorised two disorders instead of one axis

SIADH and DI are not two diseases to learn separately. They are too much ADH and too little, and every value in one is the reverse of the other.

Two numbers separate them: the urine output and the serum sodium. Learn one column and invert it.

You fixed the sodium too fast

A sodium of 118 looks like something to correct urgently. Correcting it faster than 10 to 12 mEq/L in 24 hours causes osmotic demyelination.

That is irreversible brain injury, and it is worse than the hyponatremia you were treating. In this topic the speed of the fix is the thing being tested.

You saw clear nasal drainage and called it a runny nose

After transsphenoidal surgery, clear drainage from the nose is cerebrospinal fluid until proven otherwise.

That is an open route from the nose to the brain, and the complication it causes is meningitis. Test it for glucose, because CSF has glucose and mucus does not.

Bottom Line: Learn ADH in both directions and most of the topic falls out. Then correct slowly, and treat clear nasal drainage as a leak.
02

The four things

For every disorder below, you should be able to say four things with the book closed: the one-sentence definition, the clue you would actually see in a patient, the priority nursing action, and the first-line drug with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

03

Two lobes, two jobs

The hypothalamus sits at the base of the brain and connects the nervous system to the endocrine system. It turns brain signals into hormonal commands.

The pituitary sits just below it, in a bony pocket called the sella turcica. It is about the size of a pea, and it controls nearly every other endocrine gland.

The two lobes do completely different things, and confusing them is the first mistake available here.

Anterior lobe: a factory

It makes six hormones, under instructions arriving through the portal blood system.

FLAT PiG: FSH, LH, ACTH, TSH, Prolactin, Growth hormone.

Each one is switched on or off by a hypothalamic releasing or inhibiting hormone.

Posterior lobe: a warehouse

It makes nothing. It stores and releases two hormones that the hypothalamus made and sent down nerve axons.

ADH and oxytocin.

So a posterior pituitary problem is really a hypothalamic delivery problem.

HormoneTarget and functionHypothalamic control
Growth hormoneLiver, bone and tissue. Growth and metabolismGHRH stimulates, somatostatin inhibits
ProlactinMammary glands. Milk productionDopamine inhibits, TRH stimulates
ACTHAdrenal cortex. Cortisol productionCRH stimulates
TSHThyroid. T3 and T4 productionTRH stimulates
FSHGonads. Follicle development, sperm productionGnRH stimulates
LHGonads. Ovulation, testosterone productionGnRH stimulates
Prolactin runs backwards

Every other anterior hormone is switched on by its releasing hormone. Prolactin is held back by dopamine instead.

So anything that blocks dopamine raises prolactin. That is why antipsychotics and metoclopramide cause galactorrhea, and why a dopamine agonist treats a prolactinoma.

It is also why a tumour pressing on the pituitary stalk raises prolactin mildly. The dopamine cannot get down to switch it off.

ADH, which is the whole first half of this guide

HormoneWhat it doesWhat goes wrong
ADH, also called vasopressin Acts on the kidney collecting ducts, reabsorbs water, concentrates the urine and dilutes the blood Too little is DI. Too much is SIADH.
Oxytocin Uterine contraction in labour, milk letdown in breastfeeding Given therapeutically as Pitocin
  1. 1
    Osmoreceptors notice. Neurons in the hypothalamus detect rising blood concentration, from dehydration or a high sodium. They trigger ADH release from the posterior pituitary.
  2. 2
    ADH reaches the kidney. It binds V2 receptors on the collecting ducts and inserts aquaporin channels, so water is reabsorbed. The urine becomes concentrated and low volume.
  3. 3
    The blood dilutes. Volume rises, osmolality falls, blood pressure rises slightly.
  4. 4
    Feedback stops it. Once osmolality is back near 275 to 295 mOsm/kg, ADH release stops and the kidneys pass dilute urine again.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Name Tells You Everything
Anti-diuretic. Against urine production. More ADH means less urine, and it will be concentrated. Less ADH means more urine, and it will be dilute. You can derive both diseases from the name alone.
Bottom Line: Anterior makes six hormones, FLAT PiG. Posterior only stores two. Prolactin is the one held back rather than switched on. ADH controls water, so ADH controls this whole topic.
04

Diabetes insipidus

ADH is missing, or the kidneys will not respond to it. Either way the kidneys cannot concentrate urine, and water pours out.

Insipidus means tasteless, as opposed to mellitus, which means honey-sweet. The urine is dilute rather than sugary. Patients can pass 3 to 20 litres a day.

Two types, and only one responds to the drug

Discrimination axis: is the hormone missing, or is the kidney ignoring it.

Central, or neurogenic

The hypothalamus or posterior pituitary cannot make or release enough ADH.

  • Causes: head trauma, neurosurgery especially transsphenoidal, brain tumours, meningitis, encephalitis, aneurysm.
  • The commoner type overall.
  • May be temporary after surgery, or permanent.

Responds to desmopressin, because you are replacing what is missing.

Water deprivation test: the urine concentrates once DDAVP is given.

Nephrogenic

ADH is present in normal amounts. The kidneys will not listen to it.

  • Causes: lithium is the commonest drug cause, then demeclocycline, chronic kidney disease, hypercalcemia, hypokalemia, and inherited forms.
  • Less common than central.

Does not respond to desmopressin. More ADH does not help a kidney that ignores ADH.

Water deprivation test: the urine stays dilute even after DDAVP.

Lithium is the drug to remember

Up to 40 percent of patients on long-term lithium develop some degree of concentrating defect.

Monitor intake, output and serum sodium in anyone on it. Teach the patient to report a sudden increase in thirst or urination.

If nephrogenic DI does develop, continuing the lithium becomes a risk-versus-benefit decision for the prescriber.

What you see

  • Polyuria. 3 to 20 litres a day of dilute urine. The hallmark.
  • Polydipsia. Extreme unrelenting thirst, which is the body compensating.
  • Nocturia, disrupting sleep every night.
  • Dehydration. Dry mucous membranes, poor turgor, sunken eyes, tachycardia, hypotension.
  • Hypernatremia, over 145 mEq/L, because losing water concentrates the sodium.
  • Serum osmolality over 295 mOsm/kg.
  • Urine specific gravity under 1.005 and urine osmolality under 200 mOsm/kg.
  • Fatigue, irritability and muscle weakness from the dehydration.
  • Weight loss, which is fluid loss.
  • In infants: irritability, poor feeding, failure to thrive, a high-pitched cry, fever, and a preference for water over milk.

Treating central DI

Desmopressin is synthetic ADH. It binds the same V2 receptors, inserts the same aquaporins, and concentrates the urine.

It comes intranasally, orally, subcutaneously and intravenously. Dosing is individualised against the urine output, usually once or twice daily.

The danger is the opposite problem

Give too much desmopressin and you have made SIADH. Hyponatremia and water intoxication are the serious side effects. They present as headache, nausea, confusion, seizures and coma.

So monitor several things. Strict intake and output, hourly at first. Daily weights, same time and same scale, reporting a gain over 2 lb in a day. Urine specific gravity, which should climb toward 1.010 to 1.025. Serum sodium. And signs of overload: edema, hypertension, crackles.

Other effects are milder. Nasal irritation and epistaxis with the spray. Headache, cramping, nausea, flushing. Hypertension at high doses.

Teaching desmopressin
  • Take it as prescribed and never skip a dose. Symptoms return quickly.
  • Drink to satisfy thirst, but do not push fluids beyond that.
  • Weigh daily and report a gain over 2 lb in a day.
  • Carry the medication at all times. Refrigerate unused vials.
  • Wear medical alert identification.
  • Avoid alcohol, which interferes with ADH action.
  • With the spray, prime it before first use, blow the nose gently first, and rotate nostrils.
  • Report headache, confusion, weight gain, or a drop in urine output immediately.

For permanent central DI this is lifelong therapy.

Treating nephrogenic DI

Desmopressin will not work, so the approach is different.

  • Remove the cause. Stop the lithium if that is possible.
  • Thiazide diuretics. Hydrochlorothiazide paradoxically reduces urine output, by promoting sodium and water reabsorption proximally.
  • Low sodium, low protein diet, which reduces the solute the kidney has to excrete.
  • NSAIDs such as indomethacin may reduce output in some cases.
  • Free access to water, always. This is not optional.

What you do

  1. 1
    Strict intake and output. Every 1 to 2 hours in an acute setting. Report output over 200 mL/hr, or over 4 litres a day.
  2. 2
    Daily weights. Same time, same clothing, same scale. One kilogram is about one litre of fluid, so the scale measures the loss directly.
  3. 3
    Urine specific gravity. With each void or per protocol. Untreated it sits under 1.005. On effective treatment it should climb toward 1.010 to 1.025.
  4. 4
    Hydration status. Mucous membranes, turgor, heart rate, blood pressure, level of consciousness. Keep water at the bedside, within reach, at all times.
  5. 5
    Sodium and osmolality. Report a sodium over 145 or a rising trend. Correct hypernatremia gradually, because correcting it fast causes cerebral edema.
  6. 6
    Falls and skin. Dehydration means orthostatic hypotension, and the patient is going to the toilet constantly. Make both the water and the bathroom easy to reach.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The 200 mL an Hour Rule
After any brain surgery, and especially transsphenoidal, watch for output jumping over 200 mL/hr for two consecutive hours with a specific gravity under 1.005. That is DI. Report it immediately, because at those rates the patient dehydrates within hours.
Bottom Line: Massive dilute urine, dehydration, high sodium. Central responds to desmopressin; nephrogenic does not, and lithium is the commonest drug cause. Watch output, weight, specific gravity and sodium.
05

SIADH

ADH is released continuously, whether the body needs it or not. The kidneys keep reabsorbing water, and the blood becomes dilute.

The sodium falls because there is too much water, not because sodium was lost. Total body sodium may be entirely normal. That is dilutional hyponatremia. It is also why you remove water rather than add salt.

Five things follow from that one mechanism:

  • Water retention, expanding volume. Usually with no edema, because the excess water goes inside cells rather than into the interstitium.
  • Dilutional hyponatremia.
  • Concentrated urine, so urine osmolality and specific gravity are inappropriately high.
  • Low serum osmolality, because the blood is too dilute.
  • High urine sodium, as the kidney tries to excrete sodium to match the dilute blood.

What causes it

CategoryCausesWhat to remember
Malignancy Small cell lung cancer The leading malignant cause. The tumour makes its own ADH. Check the chest x-ray.
CNS disorders Head trauma, stroke, brain tumours, meningitis, encephalitis, subarachnoid hemorrhage Any brain injury can disturb hypothalamic control of ADH
Medications SSRIs, carbamazepine, vincristine, cyclophosphamide, opioids, oxytocin, NSAIDs SSRIs are the leading drug cause, and very common in older patients
Pulmonary Pneumonia, TB, positive pressure ventilation, lung abscess Lung pathology triggers ADH release
Post-operative Anesthesia, pain, nausea, the stress response Transient SIADH after surgery is common
Lung cancer with a low sodium

If a scenario pairs lung cancer with hyponatremia, it is testing SIADH. Small cell lung cancer is the classic tumour that makes ADH where no ADH should be made.

Learn the pairing as one fact. It appears in oncology questions as often as endocrine ones.

What you see

Symptoms track the sodium, and they track how fast it fell. A gradual drop may be silent. A rapid one is dangerous at the same number.

SodiumWhat the patient shows
130 to 135Nausea, headache, fatigue, muscle cramps
120 to 130Confusion, disorientation, weakness, personality change, irritability
Under 120Seizures, coma, obtundation, respiratory arrest, death

Alongside that: weight gain without edema. Dark concentrated urine, specific gravity over 1.030. And reduced output.

Under 120 means seizure precautions

Padded side rails, bed in the lowest position, suction at the bedside, IV access maintained, frequent neurological checks.

Do not discontinue them until the sodium is stable above 125. Seizures from severe hyponatremia can be fatal.

The numbers that confirm it

ValueIn SIADH

Treatment, in order

First, fluid restriction. 800 to 1000 mL a day is the cornerstone. Limit the water and the kidneys gradually clear the excess, and the sodium comes up on its own.

Making a fluid restriction actually work

This is one of the hardest orders to carry out. The patient is thirsty and the water is right there.

  • Divide the allowance across the shifts, for example 400, 300 and 300 mL.
  • Use small cups, so the same volume looks like more.
  • Ice chips count as half their volume.
  • Offer mouth care and lip moisturiser, which treats the discomfort without the fluid.
  • Explain why. A patient who understands the sodium will keep to it.
  • Count everything: IV fluids, ice chips, gelatin and ice cream, and medication flushes.

Second, tolvaptan, an oral V2 receptor antagonist. It blocks ADH at the collecting duct so free water is excreted without losing sodium. That is aquaresis rather than diuresis.

  • When: significant euvolemic or hypervolemic hyponatremia, sodium under 125 or symptomatic, that fluid restriction has not fixed.
  • Dose: start at 15 mg daily, titrate to 30 then 60 mg.
  • Started in hospital, as a programme requirement, so the sodium can be watched.
  • Black box warning for liver injury. Maximum 30 days for that reason.
  • Do not fluid restrict on tolvaptan. The patient drinks to thirst.
  • Monitor: sodium every 6 to 8 hours at first then daily, liver function at baseline and periodically, urine output, mental status.
  • Avoid grapefruit juice, which raises drug levels through CYP3A4.
  • Side effects: thirst is the commonest and most bothersome. Also dry mouth, polyuria, nocturia, dehydration, hyperglycemia, constipation.

Third, conivaptan, the intravenous option. A dual V1A and V2 antagonist. 20 mg over 30 minutes, then 20 mg over 24 hours, maximum 4 days. Use a large vein; it causes phlebitis peripherally.

Fourth, hypertonic saline, 3 percent. For severe symptomatic hyponatremia with seizures, coma, or a sodium under 120. Given by pump in an ICU, often as 100 to 150 mL boluses. Check sodium every 1 to 2 hours. Stop when symptoms resolve, or the sodium has risen 4 to 6 mEq/L.

Why you correct it slowly

Never faster than 10 to 12 mEq/L in 24 hours.

Brain cells adapt to a low sodium by shedding their own solutes. Raise the serum sodium quickly and water is pulled out of those adapted cells, and the myelin is destroyed.

That is osmotic demyelination syndrome, once called central pontine myelinolysis. It causes dysarthria, dysphagia, quadriparesis and locked-in syndrome, and it is irreversible.

It is worse than the hyponatremia you were treating. If the sodium is rising too fast, the provider may give hypotonic fluid to slow it down.

Alongside all of that, treat the cause. The pneumonia, the offending drug, the tumour.

What you do

  1. 1
    Enforce the restriction. Distribute it across the shifts and count every source.
  2. 2
    Strict intake and output. Output here is low, which is the opposite of DI and is what you expect.
  3. 3
    Daily weights. Weight gain without edema is the hallmark. Effective treatment brings the weight down.
  4. 4
    Neurological assessment. Level of consciousness, orientation, headache, confusion, cramps, seizure activity. Hyponatremia hits the brain first. Seizure precautions under 125.
  5. 5
    Sodium every 4 to 6 hours during acute management. Check it is not correcting too fast as well as not too slowly.
Bottom Line: Excess ADH holds water, dilutes the sodium and concentrates the urine. Restrict fluid first, then tolvaptan. Correct slowly, because osmotic demyelination is worse than the hyponatremia.

The mirror

Discrimination axis: one hormone, two directions. Learn one column and invert it.

These are not two disorders. They are one hormone at both extremes, and every single value reverses.

Two numbers decide it

Look at the urine output and the serum sodium, and nothing else.

Low output and low sodium is SIADH. The body is holding water.

High output and high sodium is DI. The body is losing water.

Then the treatment follows without any further thought. SIADH means stop adding water. DI means give the water back and replace the hormone.

Serum and urine always oppose each other

This is the pattern that catches people, because it feels wrong.

In SIADH the serum is dilute and the urine is concentrated. In DI the serum is concentrated and the urine is dilute.

That makes sense once you see it. Water is either in the blood or in the toilet. Whichever compartment has it is the dilute one.

Urine output high or low. Serum sodium high or low. Both low is SIADH. Both high is DI. Everything else in the question follows from those two data points, so do not read the rest until you have them.
Write this down

Blank paper. Two columns. Write only the SIADH column from memory, thirteen rows, then fill DI by inverting each one. Check afterwards. Any row that did not invert is a row you have not learned.

Bottom Line: Every value reverses. Check urine output and serum sodium, and the diagnosis and the treatment both fall out of those two numbers.

Why IGF-1 beats a growth hormone level

Growth hormone is secreted in pulses, so a single random level tells you almost nothing. It could be high or low in the same patient an hour apart.

IGF-1 is made by the liver in response to GH and its level is stable through the day. It averages out the pulses.

That is why IGF-1 is the screening test and a random GH is not.

Bottom Line: Serum and urine values move in opposite directions in both disorders. IGF-1 screens for acromegaly because growth hormone is pulsatile. A prolactin over 200 ng/mL is effectively diagnostic of a prolactinoma.
08

Growth hormone, too much and too little

Discrimination axis: are the growth plates still open.

Excess growth hormone does two different things, depending on the age of the skeleton. In a child with open plates the bones grow longer. That is gigantism. In an adult they cannot, so the tissue thickens instead. That is acromegaly.

Acromegaly

Almost always a growth hormone secreting pituitary adenoma. The bones cannot lengthen. So soft tissue, cartilage and bone thicken instead.

  • Enlarged hands and feet. Rings and shoes stop fitting, gradually, over years.
  • Coarsened face. Broad nose, prominent brow, enlarged jaw, thickened lips, teeth spreading apart.
  • Macroglossia, an enlarged tongue, which causes sleep apnea and deepens the voice.
  • Thick oily skin, heavy sweating, skin tags.
  • Joint pain and arthritis, from cartilage overgrowth.
  • Carpal tunnel syndrome, from soft tissue swelling.
  • Hypertension and cardiomegaly, eventually heart failure.
  • Insulin resistance and diabetes, because growth hormone opposes insulin.
  • Headache and visual change if the adenoma reaches the optic chiasm.
It is invisible to the people who see them daily

Acromegaly develops so slowly that neither the patient nor the family notices. The average gap between first symptom and diagnosis is 7 to 10 years.

Comparing old photographs is one of the most effective diagnostic manoeuvres available, and it costs nothing.

In a question, the clue arrives as something small and practical. Rings that no longer fit. A spouse who says the face has changed.

Diagnosis: IGF-1 as the screening test. Then a glucose tolerance test to confirm. Glucose normally suppresses GH below 1 ng/mL, and in acromegaly it does not. MRI finds the adenoma.

Treating acromegaly

Transsphenoidal surgery is first-line. Drugs are for when surgery fails or is not possible.

DrugHow it worksWhat you watch
Octreotide A somatostatin analogue. Somatostatin normally inhibits GH, so this mimics the off switch GI upset is commonest. Gallstones, because gallbladder motility falls. Hyperglycemia, because insulin is suppressed too. Bradycardia. Hypothyroidism. Monitor glucose, gallbladder ultrasound, ECG and TSH.
Lanreotide The same class, deep subcutaneous every 4 weeks Same effects and same monitoring as octreotide
Pegvisomant Blocks GH receptors at the tissue, so IGF-1 normalises It does not shrink the tumour, and the tumour may grow because the feedback is gone. Monitor liver enzymes and repeat the MRI.
Cabergoline A dopamine agonist, useful if the tumour also secretes prolactin An adjunct, and less effective here than the somatostatin analogues

Octreotide comes two ways. A short-acting subcutaneous injection two or three times daily, or a long-acting intramuscular one monthly. Rotate sites, refrigerate it, and warm it to room temperature first.

Gigantism

The same excess, before the growth plates close. The plates are open, so the bones lengthen. Patients can reach extreme height.

The overgrowth is proportional across the whole body. Acromegaly thickens locally instead. Treatment is the same, and starting early is what prevents the height.

Growth hormone deficiency

In children: growth failure
  • Short stature, falling across growth percentiles
  • Delayed puberty
  • Proportional body, short but normally shaped
  • A young, baby-faced appearance
  • Delayed bone age on x-ray
  • Normal intelligence
In adults: metabolic change
  • Reduced muscle mass and strength
  • Increased body fat, especially abdominal
  • Fatigue and poor exercise tolerance
  • Reduced bone density
  • Raised LDL cholesterol
  • Low mood and reduced quality of life

Somatropin is recombinant human growth hormone. Subcutaneous, at bedtime, because natural GH peaks during sleep. Weight-based dosing, adjusted as a child grows.

What somatropin can cause
  • Joint and muscle pain, peripheral edema, carpal tunnel syndrome
  • Insulin resistance and hyperglycemia, so monitor glucose
  • Raised intracranial pressure, presenting as headache, papilledema and vision change
  • Hypothyroidism, which growth hormone can unmask, so monitor TSH
  • Slipped capital femoral epiphysis in children, presenting as hip or knee pain and a limp

Contraindicated in active malignancy, because growth hormone can drive tumour growth. Also in acute critical illness, diabetic retinopathy, and closed epiphyses when the indication was growth.

Hip or knee pain on growth hormone

A slipped capital femoral epiphysis is an orthopedic emergency. It can happen in a child on growth hormone.

Look for a new limp. Hip, knee or groin pain. Limited hip movement, and external rotation of that leg. Knee pain is the trap; the problem is the hip.

Report it immediately, because it needs surgery. The risk is highest during rapid growth.

Monitoring is growth rate and height every 3 to 6 months. Then IGF-1, glucose and HbA1c, and thyroid function. Bone age x-rays for the growth plates. A fundoscopic exam for papilledema. Hip x-rays if there is hip or knee pain. And watch for scoliosis.

Teaching somatropin
  • Subcutaneous technique, and teach the child to self-inject when they are old enough.
  • Rotate sites: abdomen, thighs, buttocks, upper arms.
  • Refrigerate, do not freeze, protect from light. Once reconstituted, use within the stated time.
  • Same time daily, preferably bedtime.
  • Report hip or knee pain or a limp, and severe headache or vision change, immediately.
  • Monitor glucose, especially with diabetes in the family.
  • Treatment may continue for years, until the growth plates close.
  • It is expensive. Ask about financial assistance programmes early rather than late.
Bottom Line: Open plates means gigantism; closed plates means acromegaly. IGF-1 screens, glucose tolerance confirms, surgery is first-line. Somatropin goes in at bedtime, and hip or knee pain in a child on it is an emergency.
09

Too much prolactin

A prolactinoma is the commonest pituitary tumour, about 40 percent of all adenomas.

It is not the only cause of a high prolactin. The others are worth ruling out first.

  • Prolactinoma. The commonest pathological cause.
  • Medications. Antipsychotics such as haloperidol and risperidone, metoclopramide, methyldopa. All of them block dopamine, and dopamine is what holds prolactin down.
  • Stalk effect. Any pituitary mass compressing the stalk stops dopamine reaching the lactotrophs, so prolactin rises mildly.
  • Hypothyroidism. A raised TRH stimulates prolactin, so always check the TSH.
  • Pregnancy and breastfeeding. Physiological, and the first thing to exclude.
In womenIn menIn either, with a large tumour
Galactorrhea, spontaneous milky dischargeErectile dysfunctionHeadache, from mass effect
Amenorrhea or oligomenorrheaReduced libidoBitemporal hemianopsia

Why men present later

Galactorrhea and absent periods are hard to ignore, so women present relatively early with small tumours.

Erectile dysfunction and low libido are common and non-specific, so men are worked up later. By then the tumour is usually larger. It is also more likely to be pressing on the chiasm.

Rule out pregnancy first in any woman, then check the prolactin.

Treating it

Cabergoline is the preferred dopamine agonist. It activates D2 receptors on the lactotrophs, which both suppresses prolactin and shrinks the tumour.

  • Dose: 0.25 mg twice a week, increased against the prolactin level.
  • Why it is preferred: more effective and better tolerated than bromocriptine, shrinks tumours in up to 90 percent of cases, and twice weekly dosing is easier to keep to.
  • Side effects: nausea and vomiting, so take it with food. Orthostatic hypotension, so rise slowly. Headache and fatigue. Cardiac valvulopathy, rare at these doses. Impulse control disorders such as gambling or hypersexuality, rare but reportable.
  • Monitor: prolactin, blood pressure, a pregnancy test since fertility may return, and an echocardiogram with long-term high-dose therapy.
  • Teach: take with food, rise slowly, use contraception if pregnancy is not wanted because fertility returns, expect weeks to months for improvement, and report severe headache, vision change or unusual urges.

Bromocriptine is the alternative, same mechanism, daily dosing. More side effects, so less preferred. It has more safety data in pregnancy, which is when it may be chosen.

Surgery is reserved for medication failure, intolerance, or a large tumour causing acute visual loss.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Pills First, Surgery Second
Prolactinoma is the exception among pituitary tumours. A dopamine agonist both normalises the prolactin and shrinks the tumour, so surgery is second-line. Acromegaly and Cushing's disease go the other way round. Prolactinoma is pills first; the rest are surgery first.
Bottom Line: The commonest pituitary tumour. Galactorrhea with amenorrhea in a woman, erectile dysfunction in a man. Cabergoline first, and it shrinks the tumour. Surgery only if the drug fails.
10

Too much ACTH, and not enough of everything

Cushing's disease

Discrimination axis: disease means the pituitary; syndrome means any cause.

Cushing's disease is specifically an ACTH-secreting pituitary adenoma. The tumour makes ACTH, the ACTH drives the adrenals, and the adrenals make too much cortisol.

The ACTH level tells you which

Cushing's disease is the pituitary cause, and ACTH is high, because the tumour is producing it.

Cushing's syndrome is any cause of excess cortisol, including prescribed steroids and adrenal tumours.

If ACTH is low, the source is adrenal or exogenous. The high cortisol has correctly suppressed the pituitary. If ACTH is high, the source is the pituitary or an ectopic tumour.

The full picture is in the Adrenal Disorders guide. What belongs here is the pituitary end of it.

The clinical picture is the familiar one. Moon face, buffalo hump, truncal obesity with thin limbs. Purple striae and easy bruising. High glucose, high pressure, osteoporosis. Immunosuppression, muscle wasting, mood change.

Diagnosis: 24-hour urine free cortisol and late-night salivary cortisol. A low-dose dexamethasone suppression test, where cortisol fails to suppress. An ACTH level, high here. Then an MRI to find the adenoma.

Treatment: transsphenoidal surgery to remove the adenoma. Afterwards the patient needs temporary cortisol replacement, because chronic excess has suppressed the remaining pituitary and the adrenals.

Hypopituitarism

Reduced or absent function of one or more pituitary hormones. When every anterior hormone is deficient it is called panhypopituitarism.

The commonest cause is a pituitary tumour compressing normal tissue. Then surgery or radiation. Then Sheehan syndrome, traumatic brain injury, sarcoidosis, hemochromatosis and autoimmune hypophysitis.

Sheehan syndrome

The pituitary enlarges during pregnancy, which makes it vulnerable to ischemia. A severe hemorrhage during or after delivery can infarct it.

The first sign is usually an inability to lactate, because prolactin is lost. Everything else develops gradually: fatigue, amenorrhea, hypothyroidism, adrenal insufficiency.

A postpartum patient who says she cannot produce milk, after a significant birth hemorrhage, is describing Sheehan syndrome.

Cortisol goes in before thyroid hormone

Treatment is lifelong replacement of whatever is missing. The order is not optional.

Always replace cortisol first. Thyroid hormone raises the metabolic rate, and a raised metabolic rate raises the demand for cortisol. Give it to a cortisol-deficient patient and you exhaust adrenals that were already failing, which precipitates adrenal crisis.

This is a sequencing question, and sequencing questions are exactly what this topic is tested with.

Bottom Line: Disease means pituitary and a high ACTH; syndrome means any cause. Sheehan syndrome shows up as an inability to lactate after a birth hemorrhage. Replace cortisol before thyroid hormone.
11

Adenomas and what they press on

Pituitary adenomas are benign. They cause harm two ways: by making a hormone they should not, or by taking up space.

By sizeBy functionCommonest types
Microadenoma: under 10 mm Functioning: secretes excess hormone 1. Prolactinoma, 40 percent of all pituitary adenomas
Macroadenoma: 10 mm or more Non-functioning: no hormone, symptoms from mass effect alone 2. GH-secreting, causing acromegaly. 3. ACTH-secreting, causing Cushing's disease
Bitemporal hemianopsia

The optic chiasm sits directly above the pituitary. A tumour growing upward presses on it from below.

The fibres crossing at the chiasm carry the outer visual fields. So those are the fields that go. The patient loses peripheral vision on both sides and keeps the centre. They call it tunnel vision.

In a question this arrives as bumping into things at the sides, or catching door frames. It is close to specific for a pituitary macroadenoma. Test the visual fields by confrontation as part of your neurological assessment.

Other mass effects. Headache, from stretching of the diaphragma sellae. Cranial nerve palsies with diplopia if the tumour reaches the cavernous sinus, affecting the third, fourth and sixth nerves. Hypopituitarism, from compression of normal tissue. And a CSF leak if it erodes through the floor of the sella.

Pituitary apoplexy

Sudden hemorrhage into a pituitary adenoma. It presents as sudden severe headache, visual loss, altered consciousness and acute hypopituitarism.

This is a medical emergency needing urgent surgery and corticosteroids. The corticosteroids matter as much as the surgery, because the patient has just lost their ACTH.

Diagnosis: MRI with gadolinium is the imaging of choice. A full hormone panel: GH and IGF-1, prolactin, ACTH, cortisol, TSH, free T4, FSH, LH, and testosterone or estradiol. Plus formal visual field testing.

Bottom Line: Benign, but they either secrete or they compress. Bitemporal hemianopsia means the chiasm. Prolactinomas are commonest. Apoplexy is an emergency that needs steroids as well as surgery.
12

Surgery through the nose

Transsphenoidal hypophysectomy removes a pituitary tumour through the nose and the sphenoid sinus, avoiding a craniotomy. It is the first-line surgery for most pituitary adenomas, prolactinomas excepted.

The route explains all of the nursing. The surgeon has made an opening between the nasal cavity and the base of the brain. Your job for the next few days is to keep anything from raising the pressure across it.

Before

  • A full baseline hormone panel, for comparison afterwards.
  • Visual field testing and an ophthalmic exam, as a baseline.
  • A baseline neurological assessment: level of consciousness, cranial nerves, current symptoms.
  • Nothing by mouth per protocol.
  • Stress-dose steroids if cortisol deficiency is suspected, to prevent adrenal crisis around the surgery.
  • Teaching, which matters more here than in most surgery. Explain the route, so they are not expecting an incision. Warn them about nasal packing and mouth breathing. Teach the restrictions before they are sedated, because afterwards is too late.

After: the two complications you are watching for

CSF leak, the most dangerous one

A leak creates a direct route for bacteria into the central nervous system, and the result is meningitis.

Signs: clear watery nasal drainage. A constant postnasal drip. A severe headache that is worse sitting up. A patient who reports a salty taste at the back of the throat.

Test it for glucose. CSF contains glucose. Nasal mucus does not. A glucometer or test strip answers the question at the bedside.

Halo sign: if the drainage is bloody, look for a yellowish ring spreading around the blood on the gauze. That ring is CSF separating out.

Action: report immediately, keep the head of the bed up, and avoid anything that raises intracranial pressure. It may need surgical repair.

Transient diabetes insipidus

DI occurs in up to 30 percent of patients after this surgery. The cause is handling of the posterior pituitary or the stalk.

Watch for a sudden large urine output. Over 200 mL/hr for two consecutive hours. Very dilute, at a specific gravity under 1.005. Rising sodium, and excessive thirst.

It usually resolves in days to weeks and may need temporary desmopressin. Some cases are permanent.

The triphasic pattern, and why it catches people out

Some patients run through three phases after pituitary surgery.

Days 1 to 5: DI. Transient polyuria, from swelling of the stalk.

Days 5 to 10: SIADH. Damaged cells release their stored ADH all at once, with no regulation.

Then permanent DI, if the stalk damage was severe.

The middle phase is the dangerous one. The team has spent a week treating polyuria. Then the patient swings to hyponatremia while everyone is still watching for dehydration. Monitor sodium and output through all three.

The other complications

What you do

  1. 1
    Watch for CSF. Assess nasal drainage often. Test anything clear for glucose. Look for the halo sign. A mustache dressing under the nose lets you document the amount and character.
  2. 2
    Hourly output and specific gravity. Watch for sudden polyuria meaning DI, or oliguria meaning SIADH. Weigh daily.
  3. 3
    Neurological assessment every 1 to 2 hours at first. Consciousness, orientation, pupils, visual acuity and fields, cranial nerves. Compare against the pre-operative baseline.
  4. 4
    Nasal packing. Placed in surgery, removed in 2 to 3 days. Do not remove or manipulate it. Monitor for bleeding. The patient breathes through the mouth until it comes out.
  5. 5
    Mouth care, carefully. No brushing the upper teeth for about 2 weeks, because the surgical site sits behind the upper gum line. Gentle rinses only. Soft diet, no hot liquids at first, no straws for a week because the suction can disturb the site. Lip care, since they are mouth breathing.
  6. 6
    Head of bed at 30 to 45 degrees at all times. No lying flat, no hip flexion beyond 90 degrees, turn side to side carefully. Stool softeners, so they never strain.
  7. 7
    Pain. Headache is expected, from the surgery and the sinus pressure. Acetaminophen or opioids as ordered, ice to the face and forehead. Avoid aspirin and NSAIDs at first, for the bleeding risk.
  8. 8
    Suction at the bedside, for the mouth only. Never nasal suction. That route now leads somewhere it should not.
The five things they must not do
  1. No nose blowing.
  2. No coughing.
  3. No sneezing. If they must, with the mouth open.
  4. No bending forward at the waist.
  5. No straining, which is what the stool softeners are for.

Add no toothbrushing of the upper teeth for 2 weeks. Every one of these raises pressure across a surgical site that separates the nose from the brain.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Every Restriction Is About Pressure
Blowing, coughing, sneezing, bending, straining. All five spike the pressure across a fresh opening between nose and brain, and all five can start a CSF leak. If you understand that, you do not have to memorise the list.
Going home
  • No nose blowing and no heavy lifting for 2 to 4 weeks. No bending at the waist for 2 weeks.
  • When toothbrushing can resume.
  • Report immediately: clear nasal drainage, fever or a stiff neck, a sudden increase in urination, vision change, or severe headache.
  • Hormone replacement as prescribed, which may be lifelong.
  • Medical alert identification, particularly if ACTH-deficient.
  • Follow-up with endocrinology, neurosurgery and ophthalmology.
  • Steroid stress dosing if ACTH-deficient: double for illness, injectable for emergencies.
Bottom Line: Clear nasal drainage is CSF until glucose says otherwise. Watch output for DI, and expect the triphasic swing. Head up, five restrictions, no nasal suction, no upper toothbrushing for 2 weeks.

Discrimination axis: each drug does the opposite of what the disease is doing.

That is the whole pattern. Once you see it, this table becomes derivable rather than memorised.

Each Drug Reverses the Disease
Desmopressin replaces the ADH that DI is missing. Tolvaptan blocks the ADH that SIADH has too much of. Octreotide suppresses the GH of acromegaly. Somatropin replaces the GH that is missing. Cabergoline restores the dopamine brake on prolactin. Five drugs, one pattern.
Bottom Line: Desmopressin for DI, tolvaptan for SIADH, octreotide for acromegaly, somatropin for GH deficiency, cabergoline for prolactinoma. Every one is the opposite of the disease.

Children

Growth hormone deficiency

The commonest pituitary disorder in children.

  • Growth velocity is the sensitive measure. A rate that is falling across percentile lines matters more than a short child who is tracking steadily.
  • A bone age x-ray of the left wrist shows delayed maturation, and it is a key diagnostic tool.
  • GH stimulation testing is needed to diagnose it, because random levels are useless when secretion is pulsatile.
  • Somatropin dosing is weight-based and needs adjusting as the child grows.
  • Watch for slipped capital femoral epiphysis: hip or knee pain, a limp, during therapy.
  • Starting early, before the growth plates close, is what makes it work.
  • Psychosocial support is part of the treatment. Short stature causes bullying, isolation and low self-esteem at school age.
  • Adherence often falls in adolescence. Involve them in managing it themselves and explain why consistency matters.
DI and SIADH in children
  • Central DI in a child is usually caused by a CNS tumour, most classically a craniopharyngioma, or by head trauma or neurosurgery.
  • Infants cannot tell you they are thirsty. They present with irritability, poor feeding, failure to thrive, fever and dehydration. Some show a clear preference for water over milk.
  • Desmopressin needs careful titration in children, who are more susceptible to water intoxication and hyponatremia.
  • SIADH in a child can follow meningitis, pneumonia or surgery. Hyponatremia causes seizures sooner, because body water reserves are smaller.
  • A school-age child with DI needs a bathroom access plan. Frequent urination must not be restricted, so talk to the school nurse.
  • New bedwetting in an older child can be the presenting symptom of DI.
Craniopharyngioma

The commonest tumour of the pituitary region in children, peaking between 5 and 14 years. Unlike adult adenomas these arise from embryonic tissue near the stalk.

  • Growth failure and delayed puberty are often the first signs.
  • Bitemporal hemianopsia, from the same chiasm compression as in adults.
  • Diabetes insipidus commonly develops, either from the tumour or after removing it.
  • Headache and signs of raised intracranial pressure.
  • Treatment is surgical resection, often incomplete because of where it sits, sometimes with radiation.
  • Panhypopituitarism afterwards is common, needing lifelong replacement.
Bottom Line: GH deficiency is the commonest paediatric pituitary disorder, and falling growth velocity is the red flag. Craniopharyngioma is the classic tumour. An infant with DI cannot ask for water, so they present as failure to thrive.
