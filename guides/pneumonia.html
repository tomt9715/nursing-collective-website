Respiratory Anatomy & Physiology Review

Understanding normal pulmonary anatomy and defense mechanisms is essential for comprehending how pneumonia develops and why certain populations are at higher risk for infection.

Lower Respiratory Tract Structures

  • Trachea: Main airway; lined with ciliated epithelium and mucus-secreting cells for particle trapping
  • Bronchi: Branch from trachea into left and right; contain cartilage rings for support
  • Bronchioles: Smaller airways without cartilage; terminal bronchioles lead to respiratory zone
  • Alveoli: 300 million air sacs where gas exchange occurs; thin walls allow O2/CO2 diffusion
  • Pulmonary Capillaries: Surround alveoli; site of gas exchange between air and blood

Pulmonary Defense Mechanisms

Defense Mechanism Location Function
Mucociliary Escalator Airways (trachea to bronchioles) Cilia beat upward to move trapped particles out of lungs
Cough Reflex Irritant receptors in airways Forceful expulsion of foreign material and secretions
Alveolar Macrophages Alveoli Engulf and destroy pathogens that reach alveoli
IgA Antibodies Mucosal surfaces Neutralize pathogens before they invade tissue
Surfactant Alveolar surface Contains antimicrobial proteins; prevents alveolar collapse
Clinical Pearl: Defense Mechanism Impairment

Pneumonia develops when pathogens overwhelm or bypass pulmonary defenses.

  • Smoking: Paralyzes cilia, damages mucociliary escalator
  • Intubation: Bypasses upper airway defenses, provides direct bacterial access
  • Sedation/Anesthesia: Suppresses cough reflex and mucociliary function
  • Immunosuppression: Impairs macrophage function and antibody production

Normal Gas Exchange

In healthy lungs, oxygen diffuses from alveoli into pulmonary capillaries while carbon dioxide moves from blood into alveoli. This process requires:

  • Adequate Ventilation: Air reaching alveoli (V)
  • Adequate Perfusion: Blood flow through pulmonary capillaries (Q)
  • Intact Alveolar-Capillary Membrane: Thin barrier for diffusion
Key Concept: V/Q Mismatch in Pneumonia

Pneumonia causes V/Q mismatch: Infected alveoli fill with inflammatory exudate and cannot participate in gas exchange. Blood flows past these non-ventilated alveoli without picking up oxygen (shunting), leading to hypoxemia that may not respond well to supplemental oxygen.

Pneumonia Pathophysiology

Pneumonia is an acute infection of the lung parenchyma (alveoli and surrounding tissue) that causes inflammation, consolidation, and impaired gas exchange. Understanding the pathophysiology helps guide assessment and treatment.

Definition

Pneumonia is an inflammatory condition of the lung parenchyma caused by infectious agents (bacteria, viruses, fungi) or aspiration of gastric contents. It results in alveolar filling with inflammatory exudate, impairing gas exchange and causing characteristic clinical and radiographic findings.

How Pneumonia Develops

Pathophysiologic Sequence

1
Pathogen Entry

Organisms reach lower respiratory tract via inhalation of droplets, aspiration of oropharyngeal secretions, or hematogenous spread from another infection site.

2
Defense Failure

Pathogens overcome or evade pulmonary defense mechanisms. Large inoculum, virulent organisms, or impaired host defenses allow infection to establish.

3
Inflammatory Response

Alveolar macrophages trigger inflammatory cascade. Cytokines released cause vasodilation, increased capillary permeability, and neutrophil recruitment to infection site.

4
Alveolar Filling (Consolidation)

Alveoli fill with inflammatory exudate: fluid, WBCs, cellular debris, and bacteria. This "consolidation" appears as opacity on chest X-ray and impairs gas exchange.

5
Impaired Gas Exchange

Consolidated alveoli cannot participate in oxygen exchange. Blood passing through these areas returns to circulation without being oxygenated (intrapulmonary shunting), causing hypoxemia.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Understanding Consolidation
Consolidation is the hallmark of pneumonia! When alveoli fill with inflammatory exudate instead of air, the lung tissue becomes "solid" or consolidated. This produces characteristic findings: dullness to percussion (like tapping on a solid object instead of a drum), bronchial breath sounds where you'd normally hear vesicular sounds, and increased tactile fremitus (vibrations are transmitted better through solid than air-filled tissue). On chest X-ray, consolidation appears as a white opacity where you'd normally see black air-filled lung.

Types of Pneumonia by Etiology

Type Common Pathogens Key Features
Bacterial Streptococcus pneumoniae (#1), H. influenzae, Staph aureus, Klebsiella High fever, productive cough with purulent sputum, lobar consolidation
Viral Influenza, RSV, SARS-CoV-2, Adenovirus Gradual onset, dry cough, diffuse interstitial infiltrates, may precede bacterial superinfection
Atypical Mycoplasma, Legionella, Chlamydophila "Walking pneumonia," dry cough, extrapulmonary symptoms, patchy infiltrates
Fungal Pneumocystis jirovecii, Aspergillus, Histoplasma Immunocompromised patients, gradual onset, may need specific antifungals
Aspiration Oral anaerobes, gram-negatives from GI tract Foul-smelling sputum, right lower lobe common, risk with dysphagia/LOC changes
Memory Aid: #1 Bacterial Pathogen

"Strep pneumo is #1 in the community!"

Streptococcus pneumoniae (pneumococcus) is the most common cause of community-acquired bacterial pneumonia. This is why pneumococcal vaccination is so important for high-risk populations!

CAP vs HAP vs VAP

Pneumonia is classified by where the infection was acquired because this determines the likely pathogens and guides antibiotic selection. The distinction between community-acquired and healthcare-associated pneumonia is clinically crucial.

Community-Acquired (CAP)

Definition

Acquired outside healthcare settings or within 48 hours of hospital admission

Common Pathogens

  • S. pneumoniae (#1 bacterial)
  • H. influenzae
  • Mycoplasma pneumoniae
  • Respiratory viruses
  • Legionella (exposure history)

Treatment

  • Often outpatient treatment
  • Macrolides or doxycycline
  • Fluoroquinolones if severe
Hospital-Acquired (HAP)

Definition

Develops ≥48 hours after hospital admission (not incubating at admission)

Common Pathogens

  • Pseudomonas aeruginosa
  • MRSA
  • Klebsiella, E. coli
  • Acinetobacter
  • Multi-drug resistant organisms

Treatment

  • Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics
  • Anti-pseudomonal coverage
  • Consider MRSA coverage
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Why CAP vs HAP Matters
The 48-hour rule determines antibiotic choice! CAP pathogens are typically susceptible to standard antibiotics. HAP pathogens are often resistant "hospital bugs" like Pseudomonas and MRSA that require broad-spectrum, sometimes multiple antibiotics. If you give CAP antibiotics for HAP, treatment may fail. If you give HAP antibiotics for CAP, you contribute to antibiotic resistance. Always document the timeline of symptom onset relative to admission!

Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia (VAP)

VAP: A Serious Hospital-Acquired Infection

VAP develops ≥48-72 hours after endotracheal intubation.

  • Most common ICU-acquired infection
  • Mortality rate 20-50%
  • Same resistant pathogens as HAP (Pseudomonas, MRSA, Acinetobacter)
  • Largely PREVENTABLE with VAP bundle interventions

Detailed Comparison Table

Feature CAP HAP VAP
Onset Before or <48h after admission ≥48h after admission ≥48-72h after intubation

#1 Pathogen S. pneumoniae Pseudomonas, MRSA Pseudomonas, MRSA

VAP Prevention Bundle

The "VAP Bundle" is a set of evidence-based interventions that significantly reduce VAP rates:

  • Elevate HOB 30-45 degrees - Reduces aspiration risk
  • Daily sedation vacation - Assess readiness to extubate
  • Daily spontaneous breathing trial - Minimize ventilator days
  • Peptic ulcer prophylaxis - Prevent stress ulcers
  • DVT prophylaxis - Prevent thromboembolism
  • Oral care with chlorhexidine - Reduce oral bacteria
  • Subglottic suctioning - Remove secretions above cuff

High-Risk Patient Populations

Identifying patients at high risk for pneumonia is essential for prevention, early recognition, and appropriate management. Risk factors relate to impaired host defenses, increased pathogen exposure, or both.

Risk Factor Categories

Category Risk Factors Mechanism
Age Age >65 years, infants/young children Weakened immune response, immature immune system
Chronic Lung Disease COPD, asthma, cystic fibrosis, bronchiectasis Impaired mucociliary clearance, structural damage
Immunosuppression HIV/AIDS, chemotherapy, transplant, steroids Impaired cellular and humoral immunity
Chronic Disease Diabetes, heart failure, renal failure, liver disease Impaired immune function, fluid overload
Lifestyle Smoking, alcohol abuse, malnutrition Damaged airways, impaired cough, poor immunity
Aspiration Risk Dysphagia, GERD, decreased LOC, NG tube Direct pathogen entry into lower airways
Healthcare Exposure Recent hospitalization, nursing home, dialysis Exposure to resistant organisms
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The High-Risk Patient Profile
Think about who needs extra vigilance! Your highest-risk pneumonia patients often have multiple risk factors: an elderly patient with COPD who smokes, or a diabetic patient on steroids with swallowing difficulties. When you see these patients, prioritize: aspiration precautions, incentive spirometry, early mobilization, meticulous oral care, and close monitoring for early signs of respiratory infection. Prevention is key - pneumonia in these patients can be devastating.

Special Populations

Patients Requiring Special Consideration

1
Elderly (>65 years)

May present atypically - confusion, falls, decreased appetite instead of classic symptoms. Lower baseline temperature means fever may be "normal" temp. Higher mortality risk.

2
Immunocompromised

Consider opportunistic pathogens (Pneumocystis, fungal). May have minimal inflammatory response. Requires broader antibiotic coverage and possibly prophylaxis.

3
Post-Surgical Patients

Pain limits deep breathing and coughing. Anesthesia effects on mucociliary function. Immobility increases risk. Incentive spirometry and early mobilization critical.

4
Stroke/Neurological Impairment

Dysphagia increases aspiration risk dramatically. May have impaired cough reflex. Requires swallow evaluation before oral intake. HOB elevation essential.

5
Nursing Home Residents

Exposed to resistant organisms. Often have multiple comorbidities. May present atypically. Sometimes classified as healthcare-associated pneumonia requiring broader coverage.

Atypical Presentation in Elderly

Elderly patients may NOT present with classic pneumonia symptoms!

  • May be afebrile or have only low-grade temperature
  • Confusion or altered mental status may be the first sign
  • Falls, weakness, decreased appetite, incontinence
  • May lack productive cough or have only mild symptoms
  • Don't wait for "classic" presentation - have high index of suspicion!

Vaccination Recommendations

Vaccine Who Should Receive Schedule
Pneumococcal (PCV20 or PCV15+PPSV23) Adults ≥65, immunocompromised, chronic disease See current CDC guidelines for sequence
Influenza Everyone ≥6 months, especially high-risk groups Annually before flu season
COVID-19 Everyone eligible, especially high-risk groups Per current CDC recommendations

Clinical Assessment

Thorough assessment is crucial for early recognition of pneumonia, determining severity, and monitoring response to treatment. Assessment includes history, physical examination, and recognition of complications.

Classic Pneumonia Presentation

System Signs & Symptoms Clinical Significance
Respiratory Cough (productive or dry), dyspnea, tachypnea, pleuritic chest pain Cough with purulent sputum suggests bacterial infection
Systemic Fever, chills, rigors, fatigue, malaise High fever with rigors classic for bacterial pneumonia
Auscultation Crackles (rales), bronchial breath sounds, decreased breath sounds Bronchial sounds over peripheral lung = consolidation
Percussion Dullness over affected area Consolidation replaces air-filled lung
Tactile Fremitus Increased over consolidated area Sound transmits better through solid tissue
Oxygenation Hypoxemia, SpO2 <94% V/Q mismatch from consolidation
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Consolidation Triad
Three findings that indicate lung consolidation: (1) Dullness to percussion - solid tissue sounds dull, not resonant like air-filled lung. (2) Bronchial breath sounds - normally heard only over trachea, but heard over peripheral lung when consolidated. (3) Increased tactile fremitus - have patient say "99" while you palpate chest; vibrations are stronger over consolidated lung. When you find this triad, you've found the pneumonia!

Severity Assessment: CURB-65 Score

CURB-65: Pneumonia Severity Score

Each criterion scores 1 point. Higher scores = higher mortality risk.

  • Confusion (new onset)
  • Urea >7 mmol/L (BUN >19 mg/dL)
  • Respiratory rate ≥30/min
  • Blood pressure: SBP <90 or DBP ≤60 mmHg
  • 65 years of age or older

Score 0-1: Outpatient treatment | Score 2: Consider admission | Score 3-5: Hospitalize (consider ICU if 4-5)

Signs of Severe Pneumonia/Respiratory Failure

Warning Signs - Escalate Care Immediately
  • Severe hypoxemia: SpO2 <90% despite supplemental O2
  • Respiratory distress: RR >30, accessory muscle use, inability to speak full sentences
  • Hemodynamic instability: SBP <90, MAP <65, requiring vasopressors
  • Altered mental status: Confusion, lethargy, agitation
  • Multi-lobar involvement: Bilateral or >2 lobes on chest X-ray
  • Rapidly progressing infiltrates: >50% increase in 48 hours

Diagnostics & Chest X-Ray Interpretation

Diagnostic testing confirms the diagnosis of pneumonia, identifies the causative pathogen when possible, and assesses severity. Chest X-ray is the cornerstone of pneumonia diagnosis.

Key Diagnostic Tests

Test Purpose Expected Findings
Chest X-Ray Confirm diagnosis, identify pattern Infiltrates, consolidation, air bronchograms
CBC with Differential Assess inflammatory response Leukocytosis with left shift (bacterial); may be normal or lymphocytic (viral)
Basic Metabolic Panel Assess hydration, kidney function BUN elevation (dehydration, severity marker)
Sputum Culture Identify pathogen, guide antibiotics Organism identification and sensitivities
Blood Cultures Detect bacteremia (severe cases) Positive in ~10-20% of hospitalized patients
Procalcitonin Distinguish bacterial vs viral Elevated in bacterial infection; guides antibiotic use
ABG/SpO2 Assess oxygenation status Hypoxemia; may show respiratory alkalosis early

Chest X-Ray Interpretation

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Reading a Pneumonia Chest X-Ray
Normal lung = black (air-filled). Pneumonia = white opacity (consolidation). Look for: (1) Location - which lobe(s) are affected? (2) Pattern - lobar (one segment, sharp borders) suggests typical bacterial; patchy/diffuse suggests atypical or viral. (3) Air bronchograms - dark branching airways visible within white consolidation (air in bronchi surrounded by fluid-filled alveoli). (4) Pleural effusion - blunting of costophrenic angle suggests parapneumonic effusion.

Chest X-Ray Patterns by Pneumonia Type

Pattern Appearance Typical Cause
Lobar Consolidation Dense opacity filling entire lobe with air bronchograms; sharp borders at fissures Typical bacterial (S. pneumoniae, Klebsiella)
Bronchopneumonia Patchy, multifocal opacities; often bilateral; follows bronchial distribution S. aureus, gram-negatives, aspiration
Interstitial Pattern Diffuse, reticular (net-like) opacities; ground glass appearance Viral, Pneumocystis, atypical bacteria
Cavitation Air-filled cavity within consolidation (necrosis) S. aureus, Klebsiella, TB, anaerobes
Pleural Effusion Blunted costophrenic angle; layering on decubitus film Parapneumonic effusion; may need drainage if empyema

Clinical Pearl: CXR Limitations

Chest X-ray findings may lag behind clinical presentation!

  • Early pneumonia may have normal CXR - infiltrates take time to develop
  • Severely dehydrated patients may have minimal infiltrates until rehydrated
  • Neutropenic patients may not mount enough inflammatory response for visible infiltrates
  • CT scan is more sensitive if clinical suspicion high with negative CXR

Treatment & Antibiotics

Antibiotic therapy is the cornerstone of bacterial pneumonia treatment. Selection depends on the classification (CAP vs HAP/VAP), severity, local resistance patterns, and patient factors.

Empiric Antibiotic Selection

Setting Recommended Antibiotics Duration
CAP - Outpatient (healthy) Amoxicillin OR Doxycycline OR Macrolide (azithromycin) 5-7 days
CAP - Outpatient (comorbidities) Respiratory fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin) OR Amoxicillin-clavulanate + macrolide 5-7 days
CAP - Inpatient (non-ICU) Beta-lactam (ceftriaxone) + macrolide OR Respiratory fluoroquinolone alone 5-7 days
CAP - ICU Beta-lactam + macrolide OR Beta-lactam + fluoroquinolone 7 days or until stable
HAP/VAP Anti-pseudomonal beta-lactam (piperacillin-tazobactam, cefepime) ± MRSA coverage (vancomycin) 7 days (may extend)
Aspiration Pneumonia Ampicillin-sulbactam OR Clindamycin (anaerobic coverage) 7-14 days
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Antibiotic Timing Matters
First dose of antibiotics should be given within 4 hours of diagnosis! For severe pneumonia or sepsis, aim for within 1 hour. Studies show that delayed antibiotic administration increases mortality. As a nurse, once pneumonia is diagnosed and antibiotics are ordered, prioritize getting that first dose administered promptly. Don't wait for cultures to result before starting empiric therapy - cultures can guide adjustments later.

Supportive Treatment

Beyond Antibiotics

1
Oxygen Therapy

Maintain SpO2 ≥92% (≥88-92% for COPD with CO2 retention). Use nasal cannula, high-flow, or non-invasive ventilation as needed. Monitor closely for respiratory failure.

2
IV Fluids

Treat dehydration from fever, decreased intake. Use caution in heart failure. Adequate hydration helps thin secretions.

3
Antipyretics/Analgesics

Acetaminophen or NSAIDs for fever and pleuritic chest pain. Improved comfort promotes deep breathing and mobility.

4
Airway Clearance

Encourage coughing and deep breathing. Chest physiotherapy if indicated. Suctioning for patients unable to clear secretions.

Watch for Treatment Failure

Signs that antibiotic therapy is not working (after 48-72 hours):

  • Persistent or worsening fever
  • No improvement in respiratory status
  • Increasing oxygen requirements
  • Worsening infiltrates on repeat chest X-ray
  • Development of new complications (effusion, empyema)

Action: Notify provider for possible antibiotic adjustment, additional cultures, or imaging.

Nursing Interventions

Nursing care for pneumonia patients focuses on optimizing oxygenation, promoting airway clearance, administering treatments, preventing complications, and providing patient education.

Priority Nursing Interventions

Key Interventions

1
Maintain Oxygenation

Monitor SpO2 continuously. Administer oxygen as prescribed to maintain SpO2 ≥92%. Position in high Fowler's to maximize lung expansion. Report desaturation or increased O2 requirements promptly.

2
Promote Airway Clearance

Encourage coughing, deep breathing, and use of incentive spirometer every 1-2 hours while awake. Assist with position changes. Ensure adequate hydration to thin secretions. Suction if unable to clear.

3
Administer Antibiotics Promptly

Give first dose within 4 hours of diagnosis (1 hour if severe/sepsis). Obtain cultures before antibiotics if possible without delaying treatment. Monitor for allergic reactions and effectiveness.

4
Monitor for Complications

Watch for respiratory failure (increasing RR, desaturation, altered LOC), sepsis (fever, tachycardia, hypotension), and pleural effusion (decreased breath sounds, dullness). Report changes immediately.

5
Prevent Aspiration

HOB elevation ≥30 degrees. Assess swallow function before oral intake. Provide oral care every 4 hours. Supervise meals for patients at risk. Keep suction available.

6
Promote Mobility

Early mobilization reduces complications and improves outcomes. Assist with ambulation as tolerated. Progress activity gradually. Out of bed to chair for meals when able.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Incentive Spirometry Technique
Proper technique makes incentive spirometry effective! Teach patients to: (1) Sit upright or at 45+ degrees. (2) Exhale normally, then seal lips around mouthpiece. (3) Inhale slowly and deeply - aim for 3-5 seconds to raise the indicator. (4) Hold breath for 5-10 seconds at maximum inspiration. (5) Remove mouthpiece and exhale normally. (6) Repeat 10 times every 1-2 hours while awake. The goal is sustained maximal inspiration, not speed - slow and steady opens collapsed alveoli!

Patient Education

Topic Key Teaching Points
Medications Complete full antibiotic course even if feeling better; take as prescribed; report allergic reactions
Activity Gradual return to activity; fatigue may persist for weeks; rest as needed but stay mobile
Follow-up Follow-up chest X-ray often recommended in 6-8 weeks to confirm resolution
Prevention Pneumococcal and influenza vaccinations; hand hygiene; avoid smoking; manage chronic conditions
Warning Signs Return for worsening symptoms: increasing dyspnea, persistent high fever, confusion, chest pain
Memory Aid: Pneumonia Nursing Priorities

"BREATHE" for Pneumonia Care:

  • Breathing - Monitor respiratory status, oxygenation
  • Rest and activity balance
  • Encourage coughing, deep breathing, IS
  • Antibiotics - Give promptly, monitor effectiveness
  • Turn and reposition regularly
  • Hydration - Encourage fluids to thin secretions
  • Educate - Prevention, medications, warning signs

Key Takeaways

CAP = community pathogens (S. pneumoniae #1); HAP = hospital pathogens (Pseudomonas, MRSA)

48-hour rule: Pneumonia onset ≥48h after admission = HAP, requiring broader antibiotic coverage

High-risk: Elderly, immunocompromised, chronic disease, smokers, aspiration risk

CXR: Consolidation = white opacity with air bronchograms; lobar vs patchy patterns

Antibiotics within 4 hours of diagnosis (1 hour if severe); don't delay for cultures

Nursing priorities: Oxygenation, airway clearance, early mobility, aspiration prevention
