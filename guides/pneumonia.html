The Nursing Collective

By the end, without notes

  • Classify a pneumonia from the admission timeline alone, and say what that changes
  • Separate bacterial from atypical using onset, sputum and the film
  • Name the three findings that prove consolidation at the bedside
  • Explain why confusion in an older patient is a respiratory finding

01

What actually gets you

Pneumonia is an infection in a place that is supposed to hold air. Everything follows from that. The exam questions cluster in four places, and none of them are the definition.

You waited for the classic presentation in an older patient

An 84 year old with pneumonia may have no fever, no cough and no chest pain. What they have is confusion, or a fall, or they stopped eating.

Confusion in an older adult is a vital sign. If you wait for the textbook picture in this patient, you find the pneumonia late.

You gave the antibiotic before you took the culture

One dose can sterilise the specimen and you lose the organism for good. Then the patient stays on broad-spectrum guesswork for the rest of the admission.

Sputum first, then antibiotic. It costs about two minutes.

You treated a hospital pneumonia with community antibiotics

Where the patient caught it predicts which organism they have, and hospital organisms are the resistant ones.

The whole classification section exists to answer one question: which drug. Get the timeline and you have the answer.

You treated aspiration as bad luck

It is not. Aspiration pneumonia is largely a nursing outcome, and three nurse-driven actions prevent most of it.

Head of the bed, swallow status, oral care. Those three sit at the centre of more exam questions than any antibiotic in this guide.

Bottom Line: Study this as three discriminations: where they caught it, what kind of organism it is, and whether this patient is deteriorating.
02

The four things

For every type below, you should be able to say four things with the book closed: the one-sentence definition, the clue you would see in a patient, the priority nursing action, and the first-line drug with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Type Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line drug
CAP Caught outside hospital, or within 48 hours of admission. Came in from home unwell. Usually susceptible organisms. Score the severity; it decides admission or home. Amoxicillin, doxycycline or a macrolide. Beta-lactam with a macrolide if admitted.

Copy this down

Four rows on one index card, by hand. Forty-eight hours is the line. Community organisms are susceptible, hospital organisms are not. Foul-smelling and right lower lobe means aspiration. That card is most of the exam.

03

How the alveolus fills

The lung is not sterile because it is sealed. It is sterile because it is constantly cleaning itself. Pneumonia starts when that cleaning fails.

The defences, and what breaks them

DefenceWhereWhat it does
Mucociliary escalatorTrachea down to the bronchiolesCilia beat upward and carry trapped particles out
Cough reflexIrritant receptors in the airwaysBlows foreign material and secretions out
Alveolar macrophagesThe alveoliEat whatever gets that far
IgA antibodiesMucosal surfacesNeutralise pathogens before they invade
SurfactantThe alveolar surfaceCarries antimicrobial proteins, and keeps alveoli open
Every risk factor is one of these breaking
  • Smoking paralyses the cilia, so the escalator stops
  • Intubation bypasses the upper airway entirely and holds the door open
  • Sedation and anesthesia suppress the cough and slow the cilia
  • Immunosuppression takes out the macrophages and the antibodies

Learn the four defences and you can derive the risk factor list rather than memorise it.

Five steps to consolidation

  1. 1
    The pathogen arrives. Inhaled in droplets, aspirated from the oropharynx, or carried in the blood from an infection elsewhere.
  2. 2
    The defences fail. Too many organisms, too virulent, or a host whose defences were already down.
  3. 3
    Inflammation fires. Macrophages release cytokines. Vessels dilate, capillaries leak, neutrophils pour in.
  4. 4
    The alveoli fill. Fluid, white cells, debris and bacteria replace the air. This is consolidation, and it is what you see on the film.
  5. 5
    Gas exchange fails. Blood flows past alveoli that hold no air and comes back unoxygenated. That is a shunt, and it is why the oxygen sometimes does not help much.
Consolidation explains every physical finding

Solid tissue behaves differently from air, and that is the whole assessment.

  • Dull to percussion, because you are tapping something solid instead of a drum
  • Bronchial breath sounds out in the periphery, because solid tissue carries sound from the large airways instead of muffling it
  • Increased tactile fremitus, for the same reason; vibration travels better through solid than through air
  • White on the film, where black air-filled lung should be

You do not have to memorise those four. Derive them from the word solid.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Understanding Consolidation
Air-filled lung is resonant, muffles sound and looks black. Consolidated lung is dull, carries sound and looks white. Dullness, bronchial breath sounds and increased fremitus are not three facts; they are one fact stated three ways.
Bottom Line: Defences fail, inflammation fills the alveoli, and air is replaced by exudate. Consolidation is solid lung, and every finding follows from that word.
04

Bacterial, viral or atypical

Discrimination axis: how fast it started, and whether the film matches the patient.

Three patterns, and the scenario almost always tells you which one it wants.

Bacterial

Onset: sudden, with a high fever and shaking chills.

  • Productive cough, purulent or rusty sputum
  • Pleuritic chest pain, tachypnea
  • Lobar consolidation on the film
  • White count up with a left shift

Organisms: S. pneumoniae is number one overall. Then H. influenzae, S. aureus after influenza, and Klebsiella with alcohol use or diabetes.

Treatment: empiric antibiotics within 4 hours. Expect improvement in 48 to 72 hours.

Viral

Onset: gradual, over several days.

  • Dry, non-productive cough
  • Muscle aches, headache, fatigue
  • Diffuse interstitial infiltrates, usually bilateral
  • White count normal or low

Organisms: influenza A and B, SARS-CoV-2, RSV in children and older adults, adenovirus, parainfluenza.

Treatment: mostly supportive. Antivirals where they exist. Watch for a bacterial infection layering on top.

Atypical

Onset: insidious. This is the walking pneumonia.

  • Persistent dry, hacking cough
  • Symptoms outside the chest: headache, sore throat, gut upset
  • Patchy infiltrates, and the film looks worse than the patient
  • White count normal or barely raised

Organisms: Mycoplasma pneumoniae is the commonest. Then Legionella from water sources, Chlamydophila, and Coxiella.

Treatment: a macrolide such as azithromycin. Doxycycline or a fluoroquinolone as alternatives.

The axis that separates them

Bacterial arrives suddenly and looks as sick as the film. Atypical creeps in, and the patient looks better than their chest x-ray. That mismatch is the single most useful clue in the section.

There is a reason for it, and it is worth knowing. Mycoplasma has no cell wall. So it produces no organisms on a Gram stain, and beta-lactams, which work by attacking the cell wall, do nothing at all. That is why atypical pneumonia gets a macrolide rather than a penicillin.

TypeOrganismsWhat marks it out
BacterialS. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, S. aureus, KlebsiellaHigh fever, purulent cough, lobar consolidation
ViralInfluenza, RSV, SARS-CoV-2, adenovirusGradual, dry cough, diffuse interstitial pattern. May be followed by bacterial infection
AtypicalMycoplasma, Legionella, ChlamydophilaWalking pneumonia, dry cough, symptoms outside the chest, patchy film

Bottom Line: Sudden and matching the film is bacterial. Gradual and diffuse is viral. Insidious, dry, and worse on the film than in the bed is atypical, and it needs a macrolide.
05

Where they caught it

Discrimination axis: how many hours since admission.

This section exists to answer one question, and the question is which antibiotic. Where the infection was acquired predicts the organism, and the organism decides the drug.

Community-acquired

Definition: caught outside a healthcare setting, or within 48 hours of admission.

Organisms: S. pneumoniae first, then H. influenzae, Mycoplasma, respiratory viruses, and Legionella where there is an exposure history.

Resistance: usually susceptible to standard antibiotics.

Treatment: often at home. Macrolide or doxycycline, or a fluoroquinolone if it is severe.

Hospital-acquired

Definition: develops 48 hours or more after admission, and was not incubating on arrival.

Organisms: Pseudomonas aeruginosa, MRSA, Klebsiella, E. coli, Acinetobacter.

Resistance: frequently multi-drug resistant.

Treatment: broad-spectrum IV. Antipseudomonal cover, and MRSA cover where indicated.

Why 48 hours

It takes roughly that long for a hospital organism to colonise a patient and cause disease. Anything sooner, they brought with them.

The consequence runs both ways. Give community antibiotics for a hospital pneumonia and the treatment fails. Give hospital antibiotics for a community pneumonia and you have exposed the patient and the ward to broad-spectrum drugs for nothing. So document when the symptoms started relative to admission.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The 48-Hour Rule
Under 48 hours from admission is community, and the organisms are susceptible. Forty-eight or more is hospital, and the organisms are Pseudomonas and MRSA. The timeline is not administrative detail; it picks the drug.

Ventilator-associated pneumonia

VAP

Develops 48 to 72 hours or more after intubation.

  • The commonest infection acquired in intensive care
  • Mortality of 20 to 50 percent
  • The same resistant organisms as HAP: Pseudomonas, MRSA, Acinetobacter
  • Largely preventable, which is what makes it a nursing topic

The VAP bundle
  • Head of bed at 30 to 45 degrees, to reduce aspiration
  • Daily sedation vacation, to assess readiness to extubate
  • Daily spontaneous breathing trial, to cut ventilator days
  • Peptic ulcer prophylaxis
  • DVT prophylaxis
  • Oral care with chlorhexidine, to cut the oral bacterial load
  • Subglottic suctioning, to clear secretions sitting above the cuff

Notice how much of that is about getting the tube out sooner. The tube is the risk factor.

Bottom Line: Forty-eight hours splits community from hospital, and that split picks the antibiotic. VAP carries the highest mortality and is the most preventable.

Who gets it

Every risk factor below is a defence mechanism from section 03 being disabled. Read it that way and the list stops being arbitrary.

Chronic lung diseaseCOPD, asthma, cystic fibrosis, bronchiectasisMucociliary clearance and airway structure
ImmunosuppressionHIV, chemotherapy, transplant, steroidsMacrophages and antibodies

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The High-Risk Profile
The patients who get into trouble stack risk factors: an older smoker with COPD, or a diabetic on steroids who struggles to swallow. For them the work is preventive; aspiration precautions, incentive spirometry, early mobility, meticulous oral care, and watching for the first sign.

Populations that need something different

  1. 1
    Older adults. They present atypically. Confusion, a fall, or a lost appetite instead of fever and cough. Their baseline temperature is lower, so a fever may read as normal.
  2. 2
    Immunocompromised patients. Consider opportunistic organisms such as Pneumocystis and fungi. They may mount almost no inflammatory response, so the usual markers stay quiet.
  3. 3
    Post-surgical patients. Pain stops them breathing deeply and coughing. Anesthesia slows the cilia. Immobility does the rest. Incentive spirometry and early mobilisation are the answer.
  4. 4
    Stroke and neurological impairment. Dysphagia raises the aspiration risk sharply, and the cough reflex may be gone. Nothing by mouth until swallowing has been assessed.
  5. 5
    Nursing home residents. Exposed to resistant organisms, usually with several comorbidities, and often presenting atypically.
The older patient does not read the textbook
  • They may have no fever, or only a low-grade one
  • Confusion or altered mental status may be the first thing anybody notices
  • Falls, weakness, poor appetite, new incontinence
  • Cough may be absent or barely productive

A change in mental status in an older adult should send you looking for infection, not to a delirium diagnosis.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The Consolidation Triad
Dullness to percussion, bronchial breath sounds where they should not be, and increased tactile fremitus. Have the patient say ninety-nine while you palpate; the vibration is stronger over consolidated lung. Find all three and you have found the pneumonia.

Scoring the severity: CURB-65

This score answers one question, and it is not which antibiotic. It is whether this patient goes home.

One point each
  • Confusion, newly
  • Urea, meaning a BUN over 19 mg/dL
  • Respiratory rate of 30 or more
  • Blood pressure, systolic under 90 or diastolic 60 or under
  • 65 years of age or older

Collecting it properly
  1. Collect before the first antibiotic. This is the step that matters most
  2. Rinse the mouth with water first, not mouthwash, to cut oral flora
  3. Several deep breaths, then a cough from the chest rather than a throat clear
  4. Into a sterile container, aiming for 5 to 10 mL
  5. Label it and get it to the lab within 1 to 2 hours, or refrigerate
  6. Document the time, the appearance, the amount, and whether antibiotics had started

A good specimen has over 25 white cells and under 10 epithelial cells per low-power field. Too many epithelial cells means you collected saliva.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Sputum Before Antibiotics
A single dose can sterilise the culture and cost you the organism. When you have orders for both a culture and a stat antibiotic, collect first, then give it; the sequence takes two minutes. If antibiotics already went in, still collect, and mark the requisition post-antibiotic.
Escalate on any of these
  • Saturation under 90 percent despite supplemental oxygen
  • Respiratory rate over 30, accessory muscle use, cannot finish a sentence
  • Systolic under 90, mean arterial pressure under 65, or needing vasopressors
  • Confusion, lethargy or agitation
  • More than two lobes, or bilateral involvement, on the film
  • Infiltrates growing by more than half in 48 hours
Bottom Line: The consolidation triad finds it. CURB-65 decides where the patient goes. Sputum goes to the lab before the antibiotic goes in.

Reading the film

Four things to look at

Air is black. Fluid and solid tissue are white. Pneumonia is white where black should be.

  • Location. Which lobe, and how many
  • Pattern. Lobar with sharp borders suggests typical bacterial. Patchy or diffuse suggests atypical or viral
  • Air bronchograms. Dark branching airways visible inside white lung, because the bronchi still hold air while the alveoli around them do not
  • Pleural effusion. A blunted costophrenic angle
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Reading a Pneumonia Chest X-Ray
Normal lung is black because it is full of air. Consolidation is white. An air bronchogram is a dark airway inside a white patch, and it is the giveaway that the alveoli around it have filled. A blunted costophrenic angle means fluid has collected.

A clear film does not rule it out
  • Early pneumonia can look normal. The infiltrate takes time to appear
  • A severely dehydrated patient may show almost nothing until they are rehydrated
  • A neutropenic patient may never mount enough inflammation to make a visible infiltrate
  • CT is more sensitive when suspicion is high and the plain film is clear

The film lags the patient. Treat the patient.

Bottom Line: Black is air, white is pneumonia, and an air bronchogram proves the alveoli filled. A normal film early on does not rule anything out.

Antibiotics and the clock

Two decisions. Which drug, which comes from the classification. And how fast, which is always faster than feels necessary.

SettingAntibioticDuration
CAP, outpatient, otherwise healthyAmoxicillin, doxycycline, or a macrolide such as azithromycin5 to 7 days
CAP, outpatient with comorbiditiesA respiratory fluoroquinolone such as levofloxacin, or amoxicillin-clavulanate with a macrolide5 to 7 days
CAP, admitted, not ICUA beta-lactam such as ceftriaxone with a macrolide, or a respiratory fluoroquinolone alone5 to 7 days
CAP, ICUBeta-lactam with a macrolide, or beta-lactam with a fluoroquinolone7 days, or until stable
HAP and VAPAntipseudomonal beta-lactam such as piperacillin-tazobactam or cefepime, with vancomycin if MRSA cover is needed7 days, sometimes longer

The clock

First dose within 4 hours of diagnosis. Within 1 hour if the pneumonia is severe or the patient is septic.

Delay raises mortality, so the first dose is a nursing priority rather than a task for later. Do not wait for the culture to result; empiric therapy starts now and gets narrowed when the organism is known.

The one thing that does come before the antibiotic is the sputum specimen.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Antibiotic Timing
Four hours from diagnosis, one hour if severe or septic. Every hour of delay costs. Culture first, then the drug; that ordering takes minutes and does not breach the window.

Everything that is not an antibiotic

  1. 1
    Oxygen. Target 92 percent or above, or 88 to 92 percent if they are a COPD patient who retains CO2. Nasal cannula, high flow, or non-invasive ventilation as needed.
  2. 2
    Fluids. Fever and poor intake dehydrate them, and dehydration thickens secretions. Use caution in heart failure.
  3. 3
    Antipyretics and analgesia. Acetaminophen or an NSAID for fever and pleuritic pain. A comfortable patient breathes deeply and moves; a patient in pain does neither.
  4. 4
    Airway clearance. Coughing and deep breathing. Chest physiotherapy where indicated. Suction for a patient who cannot clear their own secretions.
When it is not working

Expect improvement in 48 to 72 hours. If it has not come, something is wrong.

  • Fever persisting or climbing
  • No improvement in the respiratory status
  • Rising oxygen requirement
  • Worsening infiltrates on a repeat film
  • A new complication such as an effusion or empyema

Notify the provider. The antibiotic may be wrong, there may be a resistant organism, or there may be something to drain.

Bottom Line: Classification picks the drug. Four hours, or one hour if septic. Reassess at 48 to 72 hours and escalate if nothing has changed.
10

What you do first

Priority questions give you four defensible actions and ask which comes first. All four are things a nurse would do. That is the trick. You are being tested on ordering, not on correctness.

The ordering rules
  • Oxygenation beats everything else. Position and oxygen before anything that needs a pharmacy.
  • The culture goes before the first antibiotic, and the antibiotic goes before almost everything else.
  • Prevention counts as an intervention. Head of the bed and oral care prevent the next pneumonia.
  • A change in mental status outranks a number on the monitor.
  • Anything that needs no order beats anything that does.
  1. 1
    Oxygenate. Monitor the saturation continuously. Oxygen as prescribed to hold 92 percent or above. High Fowler's to open the lung. Report a rising oxygen requirement early.
  2. 2
    Clear the airway. Coughing, deep breathing and incentive spirometry every 1 to 2 hours while awake. Reposition them. Keep them hydrated so secretions stay thin. Suction if they cannot clear it.
  3. 3
    Give the antibiotic. Within 4 hours of diagnosis, or 1 hour if severe. Get cultures first if it does not delay treatment. Watch for reaction and for effect.
  4. 4
    Watch for the complications. Respiratory failure as the rate climbs and the saturation falls. Sepsis as fever, tachycardia and hypotension arrive together. Effusion as breath sounds drop on one side.
  5. 5
    Prevent aspiration. Head of the bed at 30 degrees or more. Swallow assessed before anything by mouth. Oral care every 4 hours. Supervise meals for anyone at risk, and keep suction to hand.
  6. 6
    Get them moving. Early mobilisation reduces complications and shortens the stay. Out to a chair for meals as soon as they can manage it.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Incentive Spirometry Technique
Sit upright, breathe out normally, seal the lips, then breathe in slowly over 3 to 5 seconds and hold it for 5 to 10 at the top. Ten repetitions every 1 to 2 hours while awake. The goal is a slow sustained breath in, not a fast one; slow is what reopens collapsed alveoli.

Preventing aspiration pneumonia

This is the part of the topic that is genuinely in your hands.

Who is at risk
  • Neurological: stroke with dysphagia, reduced consciousness, sedation, waking from anesthesia
  • Mechanical: NG or OG tube, endotracheal tube, tracheostomy, enteral feeding
  • Gastrointestinal: reflux, gastroparesis, vomiting, bowel obstruction
  • Functional: poor dentition, weak cough, advanced age, debility
  • Medications: opioids, sedatives, anticholinergics, neuromuscular blockers
The prevention bundle
  • Head of bed at 30 to 45 degrees. During feeding and for 30 to 60 minutes after. For continuous tube feeding, keep it up all the time
  • Swallow screen before anything by mouth. Nothing by mouth until the bedside screen is passed. If it fails, a formal speech and language evaluation comes before the diet advances
  • Oral care every 2 to 4 hours. Fewer bacteria in the mouth means fewer bacteria to aspirate. Chlorhexidine for ventilated patients
  • Nothing by mouth until assessed after a stroke, an extubation or sedation
  • Suction at the bedside, working, with a Yankauer, for every high-risk patient
  • Check gastric residuals. Hold the feed above the local threshold, typically 250 to 500 mL, and notify
  • Medications given upright, crushed only when it is safe to crush them, liquid formulations where they exist
Position, before and during
  • To prevent: high Fowler's, 60 to 90 degrees, for eating and for 30 minutes after. Semi-Fowler's, at least 30 degrees, the rest of the time for tube-fed patients
  • If they are aspirating now: turn them onto their side immediately, so it drains rather than going further in
  • Afterwards: suction, assess the respiratory status, notify, and expect a chest x-ray and possibly antibiotics

It is nearly always the right lung, and usually the right lower lobe. The right mainstem bronchus is wider, shorter and more vertical, so gravity sends everything down it.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Three Non-Negotiables
Head of the bed, checked every time you walk into the room rather than once a shift, because beds get flattened for procedures and stay flat. Swallow status, with the diet order matched to the evaluation and signage up for nothing by mouth. Oral care, which cuts aspiration pneumonia rates by up to 40 percent in high-risk patients.

11

Children are not small adults

Pneumonia is a leading cause of admission in children. Two things change: they cannot tell you what is wrong, and the respiratory rate is the earliest thing that moves.

What it looks like when they cannot say it
  • Tachypnea, usually the first and most sensitive sign
  • Nasal flaring, grunting, head bobbing
  • Retractions, subcostal, intercostal or suprasternal
  • Feeding poorly, refusing to eat or drink
  • Irritability, or lethargy
  • Fever may be absent in a neonate, and hypothermia can mean sepsis
Normal respiratory rate by age
  • Newborn: 30 to 60 a minute
  • Infant, 1 to 12 months: 25 to 50
  • Toddler, 1 to 3 years: 20 to 30
  • Preschool, 3 to 5 years: 20 to 25
  • School age, 6 to 12 years: 18 to 22
  • Adolescent: 12 to 20

Count for a full 60 seconds, while the child is calm. A rate counted on a crying child is worth nothing.

Watch for Dehydration
Three things stack up: fast breathing loses fluid, fever raises demand, and a sick child refuses to drink. Watch urine output under 1 mL/kg/hr, dry membranes, a sunken fontanelle, poor skin turgor and lethargy. Small frequent drinks, and daily weights.
When a child gets admitted
  • Under 3 to 6 months with suspected bacterial pneumonia
  • Saturation under 90 to 92 percent on room air
  • Moderate or severe respiratory distress
  • Dehydrated, or unable to keep fluids down
  • Outpatient antibiotics have already failed
  • Toxic appearance, or signs of sepsis
  • Complicated pneumonia with effusion, empyema or abscess
  • Underlying cardiac, pulmonary or immune disease
  • Concerns about follow-up or care at home
What the parents take home
  • Medication: finish the whole course, and measure it with the dosing device, never a kitchen spoon
  • Fluids: ice lollies, clear soups, oral rehydration solution
  • Comfort: head elevated for sleep, cool mist humidifier, saline drops and suction for congestion
  • Activity: rest, but quiet play is fine. Strict bed rest is not needed
  • Come back if: breathing gets harder, fever persists past 3 days on antibiotics, they refuse fluids, they become hard to rouse, or they look blue
  • Prevention: vaccines, hand hygiene, no smoke exposure
Bottom Line: Respiratory rate is the earliest sign, so know the normals by age. Flaring, grunting and retractions mean real distress, and children dehydrate fast.
