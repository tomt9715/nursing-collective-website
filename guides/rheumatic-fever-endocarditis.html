Rheumatic Fever & Endocarditis

Two post-infection heart syndromes — one autoimmune, one direct. Jones criteria, peripheral signs, long-course antibiotics, and SBE prophylaxis.

8 Sections 14 Quiz Questions ~12 Min Read

Learning Objectives

Differentiate rheumatic fever (autoimmune, post-strep) from infective endocarditis (direct infection).
Apply the Jones criteria (CASES mnemonic) to diagnose acute rheumatic fever.
Recognize the classic peripheral signs of infective endocarditis: Osler, Janeway, Roth, splinter hemorrhages.
Administer long-course IV antibiotics and monitor for valve complications.
Identify which pediatric patients need SBE prophylaxis before dental procedures.
Educate families on secondary prophylaxis compliance and lifelong cardiac follow-up.

High-Yield Summary

What separates RF from IE on any exam question.

  • Rheumatic Fever = autoimmune. Triggered by untreated Group A Strep pharyngitis. Antibodies cross-react with heart, joints, skin, and brain. Peaks 2–3 weeks after strep throat.
  • Infective Endocarditis = direct infection. Bacteria colonize the endocardium/valves, forming vegetations. Usually Strep viridans (subacute) or Staph aureus (acute).
  • Jones Criteria (CASES): Carditis, Arthritis, Subcutaneous nodules, Erythema marginatum, Sydenham chorea. Dx = 2 major OR 1 major + 2 minor + evidence of recent strep.
  • IE classic peripheral signs: Osler nodes (painful fingertips), Janeway lesions (painless palms/soles), Roth spots (retinal hemorrhages), splinter hemorrhages under nails.
  • Both treated with long-course antibiotics. RF = penicillin to eradicate strep + anti-inflammatories. IE = 4–6 weeks of IV antibiotics (often PCN + gentamicin, or vancomycin).
  • Rheumatic Heart Disease (RHD) is the long-term sequela of RF — scarring of the mitral and aortic valves. Mitral stenosis/regurgitation is the classic finding.
  • Secondary RF prophylaxis = monthly IM benzathine PCN (or daily oral) for 5 years / until age 21 if no carditis; 10 years / until age 21 if carditis without residual damage; lifetime if RHD.
  • SBE prophylaxis with amoxicillin 50 mg/kg PO before dental work is required for: prosthetic valves, unrepaired cyanotic CHD, history of IE, first 6 months after CHD repair with prosthetic material, residual defects adjacent to prosthetic material.

RF vs. IE: Two Ways Infection Hits the Heart

Same end-organ, completely different mechanism

Both diseases are triggered by bacteria, both can damage heart valves, but the mechanism is opposite. Rheumatic fever is an autoimmune over-reaction to a throat infection. Infective endocarditis is the bacteria themselves setting up shop inside the heart. Treatment is different and so are the nursing priorities — so know which one you're dealing with.

Rheumatic Fever

Autoimmune
  • Trigger: Untreated Group A Strep pharyngitis
  • Mechanism: Antibody cross-reaction (molecular mimicry)
  • Timing: 2–3 weeks after strep throat
  • Age: 5–15 years typically
  • Target: Heart valves (mitral > aortic), joints, skin, brain
  • Long-term: Rheumatic heart disease (valve scarring)

Infective Endocarditis

Direct Infection
  • Trigger: Bacteremia seeding the endocardium
  • Mechanism: Bacterial vegetations on valves
  • Timing: Insidious (subacute) or rapid (acute)
  • Risk: Structural heart disease, prosthetic valves, dental work, IV drugs
  • Target: Native or prosthetic valves
  • Long-term: Valve destruction, embolic stroke, death
🎯 Bottom Line: RF = the body attacking itself after strep throat. IE = bacteria camping on the valves directly. Both can wreck valves; the paths there are different.

Rheumatic Fever: The Autoimmune Cascade

Why treating strep throat actually matters

Acute Rheumatic Fever (ARF) is a delayed autoimmune response following Group A Strep (GAS) pharyngitis. Antibodies produced against the streptococcal M protein cross-react with the body's own tissue that looks similar — a phenomenon called molecular mimicry. The targets: heart valves, joints, skin, basal ganglia.

Epidemiology

  • Age: 5–15 years — school-age kids. Rare before age 3.
  • Timing: 2–3 weeks after untreated or inadequately treated strep pharyngitis.
  • Geography: Uncommon in the US now (thanks to antibiotic treatment), but a major cause of pediatric heart disease in developing countries.
  • Recurrence: High without prophylaxis — each recurrence worsens heart damage.

The Key Principle

Why We Treat Strep Throat Aggressively

Treating GAS pharyngitis with a full 10-day course of penicillin (or amoxicillin) within 9 days of symptom onset prevents rheumatic fever in almost all cases. The NCLEX-favorite teaching point: parents should NOT stop antibiotics when their child feels better. Incomplete treatment = the #1 preventable cause of RF.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Molecular Mimicry in One Sentence
The strep M protein looks structurally similar to proteins in the heart valve, synovium, skin, and brain — so antibodies made to kill strep end up attacking those tissues too. That one concept explains every symptom of rheumatic fever. Heart = carditis. Joints = arthritis. Skin = nodules + rash. Brain = chorea.
🎯 Bottom Line: RF is a delayed autoimmune response. Prevent it by treating strep completely. Each attack worsens valve damage — so secondary prophylaxis is the long game.

Jones Criteria: Diagnosing Rheumatic Fever

CASES + evidence of strep

Diagnosis is clinical. You need 2 major criteria, OR 1 major + 2 minor criteria, PLUS evidence of recent Group A Strep infection. The major criteria fit into the mnemonic CASES.

Carditis

New murmur (most often mitral regurgitation), pericarditis, or signs of CHF. Most common major criterion and the one that causes permanent damage.

Arthritis

Migratory polyarthritis of large joints (knees, ankles, elbows, wrists). Painful, swollen, warm. Responds dramatically to aspirin.

Subcutaneous Nodules

Small, painless, firm nodules over bony prominences (elbows, knees, scalp). Rare but highly specific for RF.

Erythema Marginatum

Pink, non-pruritic, ring-shaped rash with clear centers. Trunk and proximal extremities. Evanescent — comes and goes.

Sydenham Chorea

Involuntary, purposeless, jerky movements (face, hands, feet). Emotional lability, clumsiness, poor handwriting. Can appear months after the triggering strep infection.

Plus: Minor Criteria

Fever, arthralgia, elevated ESR/CRP, prolonged PR interval on EKG. Support the diagnosis but can't establish it alone.

Evidence of Recent Strep

  • Positive throat culture or rapid strep test
  • Rising ASO (anti-streptolysin O) titer — the classic serologic marker
  • Elevated anti-DNase B
  • History of scarlet fever in preceding weeks
Classic NCLEX Patterns

Migratory polyarthritis + new mitral murmur 2 weeks after strep throat = RF. Sydenham chorea = "the child can't sit still, handwriting got messy" — a single major criterion that raises strong suspicion. A positive throat culture or rising ASO titer is the evidence-of-strep piece — don't miss that in the stem.

🎯 Bottom Line: 2 major OR 1 major + 2 minor + proof of recent strep. Carditis is the one that causes lasting damage.
4

Infective Endocarditis: Vegetations on Valves

Who gets it and why

Infective endocarditis is an infection of the endocardium, most commonly the heart valves. Bacteria in the bloodstream adhere to damaged or abnormal valve surfaces and form vegetations — clumps of bacteria, fibrin, and platelets that destroy the valve and can embolize.

Common Pathogens

Type Usual Pathogen Typical Source
Subacute (SBE) Streptococcus viridans Oral flora — dental procedures, poor oral hygiene
Acute Staphylococcus aureus IV drug use, indwelling catheters, skin breakdown
Prosthetic valve (early) Staphylococcus epidermidis Surgical contamination within 60 days
Bowel/GU source Enterococcus GI/GU instrumentation

Who's at Risk

Structural heart disease: CHD (especially cyanotic), valvular defects, prior RF with RHD.

Prosthetic valves — lifetime elevated risk.

Indwelling central lines (chemo ports, dialysis catheters).

IV drug use — classically causes right-sided IE affecting the tricuspid valve.

Recent dental work, GI/GU procedures without prophylaxis in a high-risk patient.

Poor dental hygiene — chronic low-grade bacteremia from periodontal disease.

Presentation

  • Subacute: Weeks of low-grade fever, fatigue, weight loss, anorexia, night sweats. Easy to mistake for viral illness.
  • Acute: High fever, rigors, rapidly progressive valve destruction, septic shock.
  • New or changing heart murmur is a key finding.
  • Embolic phenomena: Stroke, splenic infarct, renal infarct, pulmonary emboli (right-sided IE).
  • Constitutional: Anorexia, weight loss, arthralgia, splenomegaly.
🎯 Bottom Line: IE = bacteria on a valve. Strep viridans from the mouth is the classic subacute pathogen. Watch for new murmur + persistent fever + embolic events.

Classic Peripheral Signs of Endocarditis

The named findings that show up on exams

These peripheral findings are less common in pediatric IE than adults, but they are NCLEX gold — every time they appear in a stem, IE is the answer. Most are caused by microemboli or immune complex deposition.

Osler Nodes

Painful, tender red-purple nodules on fingertips and toes. Immune-complex deposition. Remember: "Ow!ssler" = painful.

Janeway Lesions

Painless macules on palms and soles. Caused by septic microemboli. Remember: "Janeway = Junk away" — painless.

Roth Spots

Retinal hemorrhages with pale centers, seen on fundoscopy. Microemboli in the retina.

Splinter Hemorrhages

Thin red/brown streaks under the fingernails — look like tiny splinters. Microemboli.

Petechiae

Pinpoint hemorrhages on skin, conjunctivae, and palate. Non-specific but common.

Splenomegaly

Enlarged spleen from chronic immune stimulation. Classic in subacute IE.

NCLEX Pain-vs-Painless Rule

When distinguishing Osler and Janeway on exam: Osler = Ow (painful, fingertips) vs. Janeway = "Jan-who?" (painless, palms/soles). Both point to infective endocarditis. Seeing either should trigger blood cultures and an echo.

Diagnosis

  • Blood cultures × 3 from different sites BEFORE antibiotics — positive in ~90% of cases.
  • Echocardiogram: TTE first, TEE if negative and suspicion remains high — visualizes vegetations.
  • Elevated ESR, CRP, WBC; mild anemia of chronic disease.
  • Urinalysis: microscopic hematuria (glomerular microemboli).
  • Duke criteria combine clinical, lab, and imaging findings for definitive diagnosis.
🎯 Bottom Line: Osler = painful fingertips. Janeway = painless palms/soles. Roth = retina. Splinter = nails. Blood cultures + echo clinch the diagnosis.

Treatment: Antibiotics & Inflammation

Different diseases, long courses, different drug classes
Penicillin for Rheumatic Fever
Eradication · Primary Tx

Goal: Eliminate residual strep from the throat.

IM benzathine PCN G single dose (preferred), OR oral penicillin V or amoxicillin for 10 days.

Use erythromycin or azithromycin for PCN-allergic patients.

Aspirin / NSAIDs for RF
Anti-Inflammatory

For arthritis and mild carditis. High-dose aspirin (75–100 mg/kg/day) during acute phase.

Naproxen often used in children to avoid aspirin's Reye risk outside the Kawasaki exception.

Continue until inflammation markers normalize (usually weeks).

Corticosteroids for Severe Carditis
Immune Modulator

Prednisone for severe carditis with CHF. Tapered over weeks.

Monitor: BP, glucose, mood changes, GI side effects.

IV Antibiotics for IE
4–6 Week IV Course

Strep viridans: IV PCN G ± gentamicin × 4 weeks.

Staph aureus (MSSA): Nafcillin (or cefazolin) × 6 weeks.

MRSA or PCN allergy: Vancomycin × 6 weeks. Monitor troughs and renal function.

PICC or central line commonly placed for home IV therapy.

Surgery for IE
Valve Replacement/Repair

Indicated for: severe valve destruction, heart failure, persistent bacteremia despite treatment, large vegetations, recurrent embolization.

Prosthetic valve patients may need valve replacement.

Carditis Support
Supportive Care

Bed rest during acute phase (weeks) for RF carditis and IE.

CHF management: diuretics, afterload reduction, digoxin as indicated.

Monitor for arrhythmias, especially heart block in RF (prolonged PR).

Cultures Before Antibiotics

For suspected IE, draw three sets of blood cultures from different sites before starting antibiotics. Empiric antibiotics can sterilize the blood quickly and cost you the pathogen identification that guides the 6-week course. Unless the patient is septic, hold antibiotics until cultures are drawn.

🎯 Bottom Line: RF = penicillin + NSAIDs + bed rest. IE = cultures first, then 4–6 weeks of IV antibiotics, valve surgery if needed.

Prevention & Prophylaxis

Two different strategies for two different diseases

Secondary RF Prophylaxis (Preventing Recurrence)

Once a child has had RF, they are at high risk for recurrence with each new strep infection — and each attack worsens valve damage. Long-term prophylaxis is therefore essential.

Situation Duration of Prophylaxis
RF without carditis 5 years OR until age 21 (whichever is longer)
RF with carditis, no residual valve damage 10 years OR until age 21 (whichever is longer)
RF with residual rheumatic heart disease 10 years OR until age 40, sometimes lifetime
  • First-line: Benzathine penicillin G IM every 4 weeks (every 3 weeks in high-risk areas).
  • Alternative: Oral penicillin V BID or sulfadiazine daily.
  • Compliance is the main challenge — the IM dose hurts, but it's more reliable than oral adherence.

SBE Prophylaxis (Preventing Endocarditis)

Not every child needs antibiotics before the dentist. The 2021 AHA guidelines limit prophylaxis to those at highest risk for a bad outcome if they develop IE.

Who Needs SBE Prophylaxis?

Yes (before dental work involving gingival manipulation or respiratory mucosa):

• Prosthetic cardiac valves or prosthetic material used for valve repair
• Previous infective endocarditis
• Unrepaired cyanotic CHD
• Completely repaired CHD with prosthetic material, for first 6 months post-repair
• Repaired CHD with residual defects at or adjacent to prosthetic material
• Cardiac transplant recipients with valvulopathy

No: Mitral valve prolapse, bicuspid aortic valve, isolated ASD, fully repaired CHD past the 6-month window without residual defect.

Prophylaxis Antibiotic

  • Amoxicillin 50 mg/kg PO (max 2 g) 30–60 min before procedure.
  • PCN allergy: clindamycin 20 mg/kg PO (max 600 mg) OR azithromycin 15 mg/kg.
  • If unable to take oral: ampicillin IM/IV, or cefazolin.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Two Kinds of "Prophylaxis" — Don't Mix Them Up
Secondary RF prophylaxis is a long-term monthly PCN to prevent RF recurrence. SBE prophylaxis is a one-time dose of amoxicillin before dental or respiratory procedures to prevent endocarditis in high-risk hearts. Same drug class, completely different purposes. Don't confuse them on NCLEX.
🎯 Bottom Line: RF prophylaxis = monthly IM PCN for years. SBE prophylaxis = single amoxicillin dose 30–60 min before high-risk procedure in high-risk patient.

Nursing Care & Family Education

Long-course IV antibiotics, bed rest, compliance

Rheumatic Fever — Acute Phase

  • Bed rest during active carditis (weeks) — progressive activity as ESR/CRP normalize.
  • Assess cardiac status: HR, rhythm, new murmurs, signs of CHF (tachypnea, hepatomegaly, edema).
  • Check EKG for prolonged PR interval (minor Jones criterion).
  • Joint care for migratory polyarthritis: warm packs, positioning, anti-inflammatories on schedule.
  • Sydenham chorea care: safety (padded rails, assist with feeding if dropping utensils), quiet environment, emotional support (children are aware and embarrassed).
  • Administer and educate on penicillin — check for allergy history.

Infective Endocarditis — Acute Phase

  • Obtain blood cultures BEFORE antibiotics (3 sets from different sites).
  • Long-term IV antibiotic administration — often via PICC or central line.
  • Monitor drug levels (vancomycin trough, gentamicin peak/trough) and renal function.
  • Monitor for embolic events: new focal neuro deficits, flank pain (renal), LUQ pain (splenic), chest pain/dyspnea (pulmonary in right-sided IE).
  • Assess for signs of heart failure.
  • Serial echocardiograms and clinical exams as ordered.
  • Line care: dressing changes, signs of line infection, flushing.

Family Teaching

  • For RF: Complete the full antibiotic course for ANY strep throat (10 days, no stopping early). Report sore throats promptly.
  • Monthly PCN injections are life-saving — compliance is the single most important factor. Consider setting a calendar reminder or syncing with school visits.
  • Dental hygiene: brush and floss daily, regular cleanings. For SBE-prophylaxis patients, show caregivers the amoxicillin protocol to hand to the dentist.
  • Carry an SBE prophylaxis card listing the diagnosis, valve/repair history, and the prophylaxis regimen.
  • Watch for signs that should trigger immediate contact with the cardiologist: persistent fever, fatigue/weight loss, new rash or joint pain, new neurologic symptoms.
  • Live with a cardiology team — lifelong follow-up if there's residual valve damage.
Empowering Kids & Teens

Adolescents often resist monthly injections. Explain the "why" in concrete terms: "Your heart valves can't take another attack. One shot a month keeps the strep away." Involve them in scheduling and let them choose the injection site. Autonomy + understanding improves compliance better than lectures.

🎯 Bottom Line: Bed rest for carditis. Cultures first for IE. Teach the family to finish antibiotics, go to monthly injections, and carry the SBE card.
