Rheumatic Fever & Endocarditis

Two ways an infection reaches the heart. One is the body attacking itself after strep. One is bacteria living on the valve. This guide is about telling them apart and acting on each.

  • Sort the four peripheral signs by where they sit and whether they hurt

01

What actually gets you

This topic is two diseases that both damage heart valves. They arrive by opposite routes. Points get lost when you blur them together, so read these three traps first.

You did not tie the fever back to the strep throat

Rheumatic fever follows an untreated group A strep throat. That is the trigger. A full antibiotic course for the throat prevents almost every case. When a family stops the antibiotic early, the strep lingers and the immune attack begins. Miss that link and the teaching question falls apart.

You treated the sore joints as the main event

The arthritis is loud, but it moves and it clears. The carditis is quiet, and it scars the valve for life. A migratory joint pain settles in days. A damaged mitral valve does not. Watch the heart, not the knee.

You confused the two diseases

Rheumatic fever is an immune reaction after strep. You diagnose it with the Jones criteria and a rising ASO titer. Infective endocarditis is a live infection on the valve. You diagnose it with positive blood cultures and embolic signs. Same organ, different proof.

Bottom Line: Stop studying these as one card. Study them as three discriminations: immune reaction or active infection, carditis or arthritis, and cultures or Jones criteria.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

03

Rheumatic fever or endocarditis

Discrimination axis: an immune reaction, or an active infection.

Both diseases start with bacteria and both can wreck a valve. The mechanism is opposite. Rheumatic fever is the immune system overreacting to a throat infection. Endocarditis is the bacteria themselves living inside the heart. The nursing priorities differ, so sort them first.

Rheumatic fever: the body after strep
  • Trigger: untreated group A strep pharyngitis
  • Mechanism: antibody cross-reaction, called molecular mimicry
  • Timing: 2 to 3 weeks after strep throat
  • Age: 5 to 15 years, rare before age 3
  • Target: heart valves (mitral over aortic), joints, skin, brain
  • Long term: rheumatic heart disease, valve scarring
Endocarditis: bacteria on the valve
  • Trigger: bacteria in the blood seeding the endocardium
  • Mechanism: bacterial vegetations on the valve
  • Timing: slow and subacute, or fast and acute
  • Risk: structural heart disease, prosthetic valves, dental work, IV drug use
  • Target: native or prosthetic valves
  • Long term: valve destruction, embolic stroke, death

Why the strep throat matters

Acute rheumatic fever is a delayed immune response after group A strep pharyngitis. The body makes antibodies against the strep M protein. That protein looks like proteins in the heart, joints, skin, and basal ganglia. So the antibodies attack those tissues too. This cross-reaction is called molecular mimicry.

It shows up in school-age children, 5 to 15 years, and rarely before age 3. The attack peaks 2 to 3 weeks after an untreated or undertreated strep throat. Each recurrence worsens the valve damage, so prevention is the long game.

Treat strep throat completely

A full 10-day course of penicillin or amoxicillin, started within 9 days of symptom onset, prevents rheumatic fever in almost every case. The teaching point families get wrong: do not stop the antibiotic when the child feels better. Incomplete treatment is the leading preventable cause of rheumatic fever.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Finish the Course
One concept explains every symptom. The strep M protein mimics the heart valve, the joints, the skin, and the brain. So heart equals carditis, joints equal arthritis, skin equals nodules and rash, brain equals chorea. Clear the strep fully and the antibodies never get started.
Bottom Line: Rheumatic fever is a delayed immune reaction. Prevent it by treating strep completely. Each attack scars the valve further, so prophylaxis is the long game.
04

The Jones criteria

Discrimination axis: major criteria, minor criteria, and proof of strep.

Diagnosis is clinical. You need two major criteria, or one major plus two minor criteria. Either way, you also need evidence of a recent group A strep infection. The five major criteria fit the mnemonic CASES.

Major criterionWhat you see
C - CarditisNew murmur, usually mitral regurgitation, pericarditis, or heart failure signs. The most common major criterion, and the one that causes permanent damage.
A - ArthritisMigratory polyarthritis of large joints (knees, ankles, elbows, wrists). Painful, swollen, warm, and it responds fast to aspirin.
S - Subcutaneous nodulesSmall, painless, firm nodules over bony prominences (elbows, knees, scalp). Rare but highly specific.
E - Erythema marginatumPink, non-itchy, ring-shaped rash with clear centers on the trunk and proximal limbs. It comes and goes.
S - Sydenham choreaInvoluntary, jerky, purposeless movements of the face, hands, and feet, with emotional lability and messy handwriting. Can appear months later.
The minor criteria and the strep evidence

Minor criteria: fever, arthralgia, elevated ESR or CRP, and a prolonged PR interval on the EKG. They support the diagnosis but cannot make it alone.

Evidence of recent strep: a positive throat culture or rapid strep test, a rising ASO (anti-streptolysin O) titer, elevated anti-DNase B, or scarlet fever in the preceding weeks. The ASO titer is the classic serologic marker.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Two Major, or One Plus Two
Migratory arthritis plus a new mitral murmur two weeks after strep is two major criteria, so it is rheumatic fever. Sydenham chorea alone raises strong suspicion. Do not skip the strep evidence in the stem; the positive culture or rising ASO titer is the piece that seals it.
Bottom Line: Two major, or one major plus two minor, plus proof of recent strep. Carditis is the criterion that causes lasting damage.
05

Infective endocarditis

Discrimination axis: who gets it, and which bug did it.

Infective endocarditis is an infection of the endocardium, most often the heart valves. Bacteria in the blood stick to a damaged or abnormal valve surface. There they build vegetations, clumps of bacteria, fibrin, and platelets. The vegetations destroy the valve and can break off and embolize.

The usual bugs and their source

TypeUsual pathogenTypical source
SubacuteStreptococcus viridansOral flora, from dental work or poor oral hygiene
AcuteStaphylococcus aureusIV drug use, indwelling catheters, skin breakdown
Prosthetic valve, earlyStaphylococcus epidermidisSurgical contamination within 60 days
Bowel or GU sourceEnterococcusGI or GU instrumentation
Match the bug to the story

A dental procedure or bad oral hygiene points to strep viridans and a subacute course. IV drug use points to staph aureus, an acute course, and right-sided disease on the tricuspid valve. The source in the stem usually names the organism.

Who is at risk

  • Structural heart disease: cyanotic congenital defects, valve defects, prior rheumatic heart disease
  • Prosthetic valves, which carry a lifelong elevated risk
  • Indwelling central lines, such as chemo ports and dialysis catheters
  • IV drug use, classically right-sided disease on the tricuspid valve
  • Recent dental, GI, or GU procedures without prophylaxis in a high-risk patient
  • Poor dental hygiene, from chronic low-grade bacteremia

How it presents

  • Subacute: weeks of low-grade fever, fatigue, weight loss, anorexia, night sweats. Easy to mistake for a viral illness.
  • Acute: high fever, rigors, fast valve destruction, septic shock
  • A new or changing heart murmur is a key finding
  • Embolic events: stroke, splenic infarct, renal infarct, pulmonary emboli in right-sided disease
  • Constitutional signs: anorexia, weight loss, arthralgia, splenomegaly
Bottom Line: Endocarditis is bacteria on a valve. Strep viridans from the mouth is the classic subacute bug. Watch for a new murmur, a fever that will not quit, and embolic events.
06

The peripheral signs

Discrimination axis: where the sign sits, and whether it hurts.

These peripheral findings are less common in children than in adults. When they appear in a scenario, though, endocarditis is the answer. Most come from microemboli or immune-complex deposition. Sort them by location and by pain.

SignWhereHurts?Cause
Osler nodesFingertips and toesPainful, tenderImmune-complex deposition. Think "Ow-sler."
Janeway lesionsPalms and solesPainless maculesSeptic microemboli
Roth spotsRetina, on fundoscopyNot painfulRetinal hemorrhages with pale centers
Splinter hemorrhagesUnder the fingernailsNot painfulMicroemboli, look like tiny splinters
PetechiaeSkin, conjunctivae, palateNot painfulPinpoint hemorrhages, non-specific
SplenomegalyLeft upper abdomenNot painfulChronic immune stimulation, classic in subacute disease
Osler hurts, Janeway does not

Osler equals Ow: painful nodules on the fingertips. Janeway is painless: flat macules on the palms and soles. Both point to endocarditis. Either one should trigger blood cultures and an echo.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Sort by Pain, Then Place
Osler is painful and sits on the tips. Janeway is painless and sits on the palms and soles. Roth is the retina, splinters are the nails. Erythema marginatum is the ring rash, but that one belongs to rheumatic fever, not endocarditis. Do not let it wander into the wrong disease.

How it is diagnosed

  • Three sets of blood cultures from different sites, drawn before antibiotics. Positive in about 90 percent of cases.
  • Echocardiogram: TTE first, then TEE if suspicion stays high. It shows the vegetations.
  • Elevated ESR, CRP, and WBC, with a mild anemia of chronic disease
  • Urinalysis showing microscopic hematuria from glomerular microemboli
  • The Duke criteria combine clinical, lab, and imaging findings for the definitive diagnosis
Bottom Line: Osler is painful fingertips. Janeway is painless palms and soles. Roth is retina, splinters are nails. Blood cultures and an echo clinch it.
07

First-line treatment

Different diseases, both with long courses. Rheumatic fever clears the strep and calms the inflammation. Endocarditis needs weeks of targeted antibiotics. Draw the cultures first.

Rheumatic fever

RoleRegimenNotes
Eradicate strepIM benzathine penicillin G once (preferred), or oral penicillin V or amoxicillin for 10 daysUse erythromycin or azithromycin if penicillin-allergic
Anti-inflammatoryHigh-dose aspirin, 75 to 100 mg/kg/day, in the acute phaseNaproxen is often used in children to avoid the Reye risk outside the Kawasaki exception. Continue until markers normalize.
Severe carditisPrednisone, tapered over weeksReserved for severe carditis with heart failure. Monitor BP, glucose, mood, and GI effects.

Infective endocarditis

OrganismAntibioticDuration
Strep viridansIV penicillin G, with or without gentamicin4 weeks
Staph aureus (MSSA)Nafcillin, or cefazolin6 weeks
MRSA or penicillin allergyVancomycin, with trough and renal monitoring6 weeks

A PICC or central line is usually placed for home IV therapy. Surgery to repair or replace the valve is indicated for severe valve destruction, heart failure, persistent bacteremia despite treatment, large vegetations, or recurrent embolization.

Carditis support runs alongside both. Bed rest during the acute phase. Diuretics, afterload reduction, and digoxin for heart failure as needed. Watch for arrhythmias, especially the heart block that a prolonged PR interval warns of.

Cultures before antibiotics

For suspected endocarditis, draw three sets of blood cultures from different sites before starting antibiotics. An early antibiotic dose can sterilize the blood and cost you the pathogen. Without the organism, you cannot target the 6-week course. Unless the patient is septic, hold antibiotics until the cultures are drawn.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Cultures First
When the stem gives you a new murmur, a fever, and an order for antibiotics, the priority is the cultures. Three sets, different sites, before the first dose. The only exception is an unstable, septic patient who cannot wait.
Bottom Line: Rheumatic fever is penicillin plus an anti-inflammatory plus rest. Endocarditis is cultures first, then 4 to 6 weeks of IV antibiotics, and valve surgery if the valve is failing.
08

Two kinds of prophylaxis

Discrimination axis: preventing a recurrence, or covering a procedure.

These two are the classic mix-up. Same drug class, opposite purpose. One runs for years to stop rheumatic fever coming back. One is a single dose before a procedure to protect a high-risk heart.

Secondary rheumatic fever prophylaxis

Once a child has had rheumatic fever, each new strep infection can trigger a recurrence. Each attack scars the valve further. So long-term prophylaxis is essential, and the duration depends on the heart.

SituationDuration of prophylaxis
Rheumatic fever without carditis5 years, or until age 21, whichever is longer
Rheumatic fever with carditis, no residual valve damage10 years, or until age 21, whichever is longer
Rheumatic fever with residual rheumatic heart disease10 years, or until age 40, sometimes lifetime
  • First-line: benzathine penicillin G IM every 4 weeks, or every 3 weeks in high-risk areas
  • Alternative: oral penicillin V twice daily, or sulfadiazine daily
  • Compliance is the main challenge. The IM dose hurts, but it beats oral adherence for reliability.

Endocarditis prophylaxis before procedures

Not every child needs antibiotics before the dentist. Current AHA guidance limits prophylaxis to the patients at highest risk of a bad outcome if they develop endocarditis.

Who needs prophylaxis before dental work

Yes, before dental work involving the gums or the respiratory mucosa:

  • Prosthetic cardiac valves, or prosthetic material used for valve repair
  • Previous infective endocarditis
  • Unrepaired cyanotic congenital heart disease
  • Repaired CHD with prosthetic material, for the first 6 months after repair
  • Repaired CHD with residual defects at or next to prosthetic material
  • Cardiac transplant recipients with valvulopathy

No: mitral valve prolapse, bicuspid aortic valve, isolated ASD, or fully repaired CHD past the 6-month window without a residual defect.

  • Amoxicillin 50 mg/kg PO, max 2 g, given 30 to 60 minutes before the procedure
  • Penicillin allergy: clindamycin 20 mg/kg PO (max 600 mg), or azithromycin 15 mg/kg
  • Cannot take oral: ampicillin IM or IV, or cefazolin
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Two Prophylaxis, Do Not Blend Them
Secondary rheumatic fever prophylaxis is monthly penicillin for years, to stop a recurrence. Endocarditis prophylaxis is one dose of amoxicillin before a procedure, to protect a high-risk heart. Same drug family, different job. When a stem asks about the dentist, it wants the single pre-procedure dose.
Bottom Line: Rheumatic fever prophylaxis is monthly IM penicillin for years. Endocarditis prophylaxis is a single amoxicillin dose 30 to 60 minutes before a high-risk procedure in a high-risk patient.
09

What you do first

Priority questions hand you four reasonable actions and ask which comes first. Order matters more than completeness. Here is the sequence that answers most of them.

  1. 1
    Cultures before antibiotics. For suspected endocarditis, draw three sets of blood cultures from different sites first. Hold antibiotics until they are drawn, unless the patient is septic.
  2. 2
    Give the antibiotic and teach adherence. For strep and for rheumatic fever, the full course clears the organism. Teach the family to finish it even after the child feels well.
  3. 3
    Rest the heart during carditis. Bed rest through the active phase, then progressive activity as ESR and CRP normalize. Assess heart rate, rhythm, new murmurs, and heart failure signs. Check the EKG for a prolonged PR interval.
  4. 4
    Set up secondary prophylaxis. The monthly injection is the single biggest factor in preventing recurrence. Coordinate it with the school nurse or clinic so appointments are not missed.
  5. 5
    Cover the high-risk heart before procedures. For a patient who qualifies, give amoxicillin 30 to 60 minutes before dental or invasive work. Send them home with a prophylaxis card for the dentist.
  6. 6
    Watch for emboli. During IV therapy, monitor for new focal neuro deficits, flank pain, left-upper-quadrant pain, and chest pain or dyspnea. Monitor drug levels and renal function on vancomycin and gentamicin. Care for the line.
Sydenham chorea care

The movements are involuntary, so no restraints. Pad the side rails and keep the environment quiet. Assist with feeding if the child is dropping utensils. Give emotional support; children are aware and embarrassed. The movements resolve over weeks to months.

Bringing teens along

Adolescents resist the monthly injection. Explain the why in concrete terms: your heart valves cannot take another attack, and one shot a month keeps the strep away. Let them help schedule it and choose the injection site. Autonomy plus understanding beats a lecture.

Bottom Line: Cultures first for endocarditis. Rest the heart for carditis. Teach the family to finish the antibiotics, keep the monthly injections, and carry the prophylaxis card.
