Seizures & Epilepsy

01

What actually gets you

A seizure is dramatic, so the instinct is to do something. Most of what people instinctively do is wrong, and the exam is built on that gap.

You put something in their mouth

Everyone has heard that a seizing patient can swallow their tongue. They cannot. It is anatomically impossible.

What a spoon or an oral airway actually does is break teeth, tear the mouth, and give them something to aspirate. Nothing goes in, ever.

You held them down

Restraining a convulsing patient does not shorten the seizure. Muscle is contracting with enormous force, and holding a limb against it dislocates shoulders and breaks bones.

You protect them from what is around them. You do not fight the movement.

You did not look at a clock

Five minutes is the line between a seizure and a neurological emergency, and five minutes at the bedside feels like twenty.

Note the time it started. Without that number you cannot tell whether this is settling or whether you should be calling for help.

You treated the absence seizure with the wrong drug

Carbamazepine and phenytoin are good drugs for focal and tonic-clonic seizures. In absence seizures they make things worse.

This is one of the few places in pharmacology where the standard drug is actively harmful, which is exactly why it appears on exams.

Bottom Line: Study this as three questions. Where did it start, was the patient aware, and how long has it been running.
02

The four things

For every type below, you should be able to say four things with the book closed: the one-sentence definition, the clue you would see in a patient, the priority nursing action, and the first-line drug with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Type Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line drug
Focal aware Starts in one area, and awareness is kept throughout. They can describe it afterwards. A strange smell, a twitching hand. Document exactly what they felt and where it started. Levetiracetam or carbamazepine. Long-term therapy.
Focal impaired awareness Starts in one area and takes awareness with it. Blank, lip smacking, fumbling. No memory of it afterwards. Keep them safe while they are not in control. Do not restrain. Levetiracetam or carbamazepine. The commonest adult focal type.
Tonic-clonic Both hemispheres from the start, stiffening then rhythmic jerking. Cry out, fall, stiffen, jerk. Then confusion for a long time afterwards. Protect, position on the side, time it. Valproic acid or levetiracetam. Valproate is teratogenic.
Absence Brief generalised episodes of stopped awareness, with no convulsion. A child who stares for 10 seconds and carries on. No postictal phase. Recognise it. It is usually mistaken for daydreaming for months. Ethosuximide. Carbamazepine and phenytoin make it worse.
Status epilepticus A seizure past 5 minutes, or repeated seizures with no recovery between. The clock. That is the whole diagnosis. Airway, then call for help. This is an emergency now. IV lorazepam 4 mg. A benzodiazepine always comes first.
Copy this down

Five rows on one index card, by hand. Focal starts in one place, generalised starts everywhere. Absence has no postictal phase. Five minutes makes it an emergency. That card is most of the exam.

03

Why a brain seizes

Neurons normally fire in a regulated, staggered way. A seizure is what happens when a group of them fires at once, far too much, and drags its neighbours along.

  • The balance tips. Too much glutamate, which excites, or too little GABA, which inhibits. Either way the brain becomes easier to set off.
  • An epileptogenic focus. A patch of abnormal neurons with a lower firing threshold than the tissue around them.
  • Kindling. Each seizure lowers the threshold for the next. Seizures beget seizures, which is part of why untreated epilepsy worsens.
  • Spread. A focal seizure can propagate until both hemispheres are involved, which is a focal to bilateral tonic-clonic.
Seizure or epilepsy

These are not the same word, and the exam separates them carefully.

A seizure is a single event. It may have been provoked by something you can fix, in which case the patient may never need a long-term drug.

Epilepsy is a chronic condition: two or more unprovoked seizures more than 24 hours apart, or one seizure with a high risk of recurrence. This is a brain with a standing predisposition, and it needs treating as such.

So the first question after a first seizure is not which drug. It is what provoked it.

FeatureSeizureEpilepsy
What it isA single event of abnormal electrical activityTwo or more unprovoked seizures over 24 hours apart
CauseProvoked by fever, drugs or a metabolic upset, or unprovokedAn underlying neurological predisposition
TreatmentTreat the cause. Long-term drugs may never be neededLong-term antiepileptic therapy, and lifestyle changes
RecurrenceLow, if the provoking cause is correctedHigh without treatment

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Missed Medication Is the Leading Trigger
When a patient with known epilepsy arrives after a breakthrough seizure, ask about the tablets first. People stop them because they feel cured, or cannot afford them, or hate the side effects. The line worth teaching is that seizure-free does not mean epilepsy-free.
Alcohol withdrawal seizures

They come 12 to 48 hours after the last drink, and they are usually generalised tonic-clonic.

They matter because of where they can go next: status epilepticus, or delirium tremens. Treatment is a benzodiazepine, which is also what treats the withdrawal itself.

Bottom Line: Excitation beats inhibition and a group of neurons fires together. A seizure is an event; epilepsy is a predisposition. Look for a reversible cause before you diagnose the second one.
04

Focal or generalized

Discrimination axis: did it start in one place, or everywhere at once.

The classification is not academic. Where a seizure starts decides which drug works, and in absence seizures the wrong drug makes it worse.

Focal: starts in one area

Focal aware, once called simple partial.

  • Awareness kept throughout
  • Motor, sensory or autonomic symptoms
  • They can describe it afterwards
  • This is what an aura actually is

Focal impaired awareness, once called complex partial.

  • Awareness impaired or lost
  • Automatisms: lip smacking, fumbling, picking at clothes
  • Confused afterwards, with no memory of it
  • The commonest focal seizure in adults

Focal to bilateral tonic-clonic. Starts in one place, then spreads to both hemispheres. Previously called secondarily generalised, and the aura is the focal part before it spreads.

Generalized: both hemispheres from the start

Tonic-clonic, once called grand mal.

  • Tonic phase: stiffening, loss of consciousness, sometimes a cry
  • Clonic phase: rhythmic jerking
  • Lasts 1 to 3 minutes, then a long confused recovery
  • Risks: bitten tongue, incontinence, injury from the fall

Absence, once called petit mal.

  • Brief staring, 5 to 30 seconds
  • Starts and stops abruptly
  • No postictal phase at all
  • Common in children aged 4 to 12

The rest. Myoclonic gives brief shock-like jerks. Atonic is the drop attack, a sudden loss of tone. Tonic is sustained stiffening with no clonic phase.

The postictal phase is the tell

A tonic-clonic seizure leaves the patient confused, drowsy and sore for minutes to hours. That is the brain recovering from a whole-hemisphere event.

An absence seizure has none of that. They stop, they stare, and then they carry on mid-sentence as though nothing happened.

So if a scenario describes an episode with no recovery period afterwards, it is telling you absence. That single feature separates it from almost everything else.

Absence seizures, and why they get missed

They look like daydreaming. A child can have dozens a day, and the person who notices is usually the teacher rather than the parent.

First line is ethosuximide if absence is the only seizure type, or valproic acid if there are others alongside it.

Carbamazepine and phenytoin can make absence seizures worse. Learn that as a rule, because it turns a reasonable-looking answer into the wrong one.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
An Aura Is Already the Seizure
It is not a warning that a seizure is coming. It is a focal aware seizure, happening in one area before it spreads. So document what it felt like; that description helps localise where the seizure starts. Common ones are an odd smell or taste, a visual disturbance, a rising feeling in the stomach, or deja vu.
Write this down

Blank paper. Two columns, focal and generalized. Put every seizure type in the right one, then mark which have a postictal phase and which do not.

Bottom Line: Focal starts in one area, generalized starts in both. Absence has no postictal phase, and carbamazepine makes it worse.
05

What you watch, and what you write

You are frequently the only person who sees the seizure. The neurologist classifies it from your description, so your observation is a diagnostic test.

The three phases

PhaseWhat is happeningWhat you see
Pre-ictal, the auraA focal seizure before it spreadsDeja vu, an odd smell or taste, anxiety, visual change
IctalThe seizure itselfMotor, sensory or behavioural change, depending on where it started
Post-ictalThe brain recoveringConfusion, drowsiness, headache, aching muscles. Minutes to hours

Four things to record

  1. 1
    Timing. When it started, how long it ran, and how long to full recovery. Past 5 minutes, call for help.
  2. 2
    Movement. Tonic, clonic or myoclonic. Which body parts. And critically, whether it was symmetric or started on one side, because that is what tells you focal or generalized.
  3. 3
    Awareness. Were they aware. Did they respond to voice. Where were the eyes looking. Any automatisms.
  4. 4
    Airway. Cyanosis, apnea, secretions, breathing pattern. Tongue biting, incontinence, vomiting.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Your Observation Is the Diagnostic Test
The neurologist was not in the room. What body part moved first. Did it start on one side. Did it spread. Was the patient aware. How long did it run, and how long to recover. Those answers decide the classification, and the classification decides the drug.

The workup

TestWhat it is forWhat to know
EEGThe gold standard for diagnosisRecords electrical activity. It can be normal between seizures, so a normal EEG does not exclude epilepsy. Video EEG is the most useful version
MRI brainFinding a structural causeTumours, sclerosis, malformations, old stroke. Preferred over CT for the epilepsy workup
CT headThe emergency scanRules out hemorrhage and mass effect. This is the first scan in the emergency department for a new seizure
BloodsFinding a metabolic causeSodium, calcium, glucose, magnesium. CBC, liver function, toxicology, and drug levels
Lumbar punctureRuling out CNS infectionIf meningitis or encephalitis is suspected. It goes after the CT, to exclude a mass and the risk of herniation
Bottom Line: EEG diagnoses, MRI finds the cause, CT is the emergency scan. Your bedside description is what classifies the seizure.
06

When it does not stop

Discrimination axis: the clock.

Status epilepticus is a seizure lasting longer than 5 minutes, or two or more seizures without the patient returning to baseline in between. It is a medical emergency.

Why a long seizure is dangerous in itself

The convulsion is not just a symptom at this point. It is doing the damage.

  • Hypoxia, as the respiratory muscles fatigue and stop ventilating
  • Hyperthermia, from the sheer amount of muscle activity
  • Rhabdomyolysis, as muscle breaks down
  • Metabolic acidosis
  • Cerebral edema
  • Permanent neuronal death

And it gets harder to stop the longer it runs, because the GABA receptors that benzodiazepines act on start withdrawing from the cell surface. Early treatment is not just kinder; it works better.

The protocol

  1. 1
    Airway and safety. Maintain the airway, suction, oxygen. Position on the side. Get IV access.
  2. 2
    A benzodiazepine. Lorazepam 4 mg IV is first line, or diazepam 10 mg IV. It can be repeated once after 5 to 10 minutes.
  3. 3
    Load an antiepileptic. If it is still going: fosphenytoin 20 mg PE/kg IV, or valproic acid, or levetiracetam.
  4. 4
    Refractory status. Still seizing means intubation and a continuous infusion of midazolam, propofol or pentobarbital.
Why a benzodiazepine always goes first

Benzodiazepines work in seconds to minutes by boosting GABA, the brain's own brake. Antiepileptics like fosphenytoin take much longer to load.

So the benzodiazepine stops the seizure and the antiepileptic keeps it stopped. Reaching for the loading dose first wastes the window.

Lorazepam is preferred over diazepam because it stays in the brain longer, even though diazepam gets there faster. Watch the respirations, and have a bag-valve-mask and intubation equipment at the bedside. If there is no IV, midazolam goes intramuscularly and diazepam goes rectally.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Five Minutes Means Call
Start timing the moment it begins. Most seizures stop on their own inside 1 to 2 minutes. Past 5 minutes it is status epilepticus and it needs emergency medication, so do not wait to see whether it settles. Five minutes at the bedside feels far longer than it is, which is why you use a clock rather than a judgement.
Bottom Line: Five minutes, or repeated seizures with no recovery between. Airway first, then a benzodiazepine, then a loading dose. It gets harder to stop the longer you leave it.
07

What you do first

Priority questions give you four defensible actions and ask which comes first. All four are things a nurse would do. That is the trick. You are tested on ordering, not correctness.

The ordering rules
  • Safety before assessment. Move the furniture before you take a pulse.
  • Airway before drugs. Side-lying and suction come before anything in a syringe.
  • Start the clock immediately. It is the only thing that tells you when to escalate.
  • Stay with them. Leaving to find help is rarely the right first action.
  • Do nothing that fights the seizure. Restraining and prising open the mouth are both harm.

During the seizure

  1. 1
    Protect them from what is around them. Move sharp objects. Ease them to the floor if they are standing. Pad under the head. Guide gently rather than restraining.
  2. 2
    Airway. Turn them onto their side when it is safe to do so, so secretions drain rather than pool. Suction as needed. Oxygen. Nothing goes in the mouth.
  3. 3
    Time it. Note the start. Past 5 minutes, activate the rapid response for status epilepticus. Record everything you saw while you can still remember it.
  4. 4
    Stay, and call. Never leave them alone. Get help to you rather than going to find it. Have IV access and emergency drugs ready.
Three things you never do
  • Nothing in the mouth. They cannot swallow their tongue. Objects break teeth, tear tissue, and get aspirated
  • No restraint. Holding a contracting limb causes fractures and dislocations, and does not shorten anything
  • Nothing by mouth. No oral medication during an active seizure. The aspiration risk is far too high
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Nothing in the Mouth
This is the most persistent myth in first aid, and families repeat it constantly. Swallowing your own tongue is not anatomically possible. What is possible is broken teeth, a torn mouth, and aspirating whatever you put in there. Correct it every time you hear it.

Afterwards

  • Position on the side, to keep the airway clear and let secretions drain.
  • Assess the neurology: level of consciousness, orientation, pupils, movement.
  • Look for injuries: bitten tongue, head injury, dislocated shoulder, skin damage.
  • Quiet the room. Dim the lights and cut the stimulation.
  • Reorient gently. They may be confused, drowsy or combative, and combativeness here is postictal rather than deliberate. Do not restrain.
  • Document: total duration, the movements, how long the postictal phase lasted, vital signs, any injury.
  • Check the glucose, because hypoglycemia both causes seizures and follows a long one.

Seizure precautions

PrecautionWhy
Padded side railsStops limbs and head striking the frame
Bed in the lowest positionShortens the fall
Suction at the bedsideClear the airway without leaving the room
Oxygen and a bag-valve-maskVentilate if they stop
IV access maintainedEmergency drugs can go in immediately
Oral airway at the bedsideFor use afterwards, not during
Call light within reachSo they can tell you about an aura before it spreads
Bottom Line: Protect, position on the side, time it, stay. Never restrain, never put anything in the mouth, never give anything orally during a seizure.
08

The drugs and their levels

The goal is no seizures with tolerable side effects, and most patients stay on treatment for years. What you need per drug is the level, the toxicity sign, and the one thing that stops it being given.

DrugSeizure typeTherapeutic levelWhat you watch for
Phenytoin Focal, tonic-clonic 10 to 20 mcg/mL Gum overgrowth, arrhythmias on fast IV push, many interactions. Check a free level if the albumin is low
Valproic acid Most types 50 to 100 mcg/mL Liver toxicity, so monitor LFTs. Pancreatitis. Teratogenic. Weight gain, low platelets
Carbamazepine Focal, tonic-clonic 4 to 12 mcg/mL Aplastic anemia and agranulocytosis, so monitor the CBC. Hyponatremia. Rash, with Stevens-Johnson risk
Levetiracetam Focal, generalized Not routinely monitored Behaviour change: irritability and aggression. Few interactions. Renally cleared, no liver toxicity
Ethosuximide Absence only 40 to 100 mcg/mL First line for absence. GI upset, blood dyscrasias. It does not touch tonic-clonic seizures
Lamotrigine Focal, generalized 3 to 14 mcg/mL Rash. Stop the drug immediately if one appears, because of Stevens-Johnson. Titrate up slowly
Lorazepam Status epilepticus Not applicable First line in status. Respiratory depression, so monitor closely. Lasts hours, not days
Phenytoin goes in slowly

No faster than 50 mg a minute in an adult. A rapid infusion causes fatal arrhythmias and hypotension.

  • Mix in normal saline only, because it precipitates in dextrose
  • Use a filter
  • Monitor the cardiac rhythm throughout

Fosphenytoin is a prodrug that can be given faster and more safely, which is why it appears in the status epilepticus protocol rather than phenytoin itself.

Phenytoin and the gums

Up to half of patients on long-term phenytoin get gingival hyperplasia, an overgrowth of gum tissue.

Teach meticulous oral hygiene, regular dental review, a soft brush and daily flossing. Also watch for hirsutism, acne and coarsened facial features.

Nystagmus is the toxicity sign. Along with ataxia and slurred speech, it is what a level above 20 looks like at the bedside.

Rules that apply to all of them

  • Never stop an antiepileptic abruptly. Sudden withdrawal is a leading cause of status epilepticus. Always taper.
  • Levels are trough levels, drawn before the next dose. Phenytoin, valproic acid and carbamazepine are the ones routinely monitored.
  • Toxicity looks the same across the class: nystagmus, ataxia, drowsiness, slurred speech, confusion.
  • Interactions are the rule, not the exception. Many are metabolised through CYP450 and will affect warfarin, oral contraceptives and each other.
  • Pregnancy matters. Valproic acid and phenytoin are teratogenic, so women of childbearing age need folate and a contraception conversation before the prescription, not after.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Never Stop Them Suddenly
Abrupt withdrawal is one of the commonest causes of status epilepticus. If a patient tells you they stopped their phenytoin because they felt fine, that is the finding, not small talk. The drug is what is keeping them seizure-free, and stopping it can produce a life-threatening seizure.
Bottom Line: Phenytoin 10 to 20, valproate 50 to 100, carbamazepine 4 to 12. Nystagmus means toxic. Never stop any of them abruptly.
09

Children are not small adults

Seizures are one of the commonest neurological emergencies in children, and the types, causes and doses all differ from adults.

Febrile seizures

The commonest seizure in childhood, in 2 to 5 percent of children between 6 months and 5 years, during a rapid rise in temperature.

A simple febrile seizure is generalized, lasts under 15 minutes, and does not recur within 24 hours. It needs no long-term antiepileptic and the outlook is excellent.

The teaching matters more than the treatment here. Parents watching their child convulse assume brain damage. In the great majority of cases febrile seizures cause neither damage nor epilepsy, and saying so plainly is part of the care.

FeatureSimple febrileComplex febrile
DurationUnder 15 minutesOver 15 minutes
TypeGeneralized, the whole bodyFocal, or generalized
Recurrence in 24 hoursDoes not recurMay recur
Later epilepsy riskAbout 1 percent, the same as anyoneRaised, about 4 to 6 percent
What happensTreat the fever, reassure the parentsFurther evaluation, usually EEG and MRI

Syndromes worth recognising

  • Infantile spasms, or West syndrome. Starts at 3 to 12 months. Clusters of brief flexion or extension spasms, often on waking. The EEG shows hypsarrhythmia. Treated with ACTH or vigabatrin, and referral is urgent.
  • Lennox-Gastaut syndrome. Several seizure types beginning between 2 and 6 years, usually with intellectual disability. Often drug-resistant.
  • Childhood absence epilepsy. Ages 4 to 12, with many daily staring spells. Most outgrow it by adolescence. First line is ethosuximide.
  • Juvenile myoclonic epilepsy. Starts in adolescence with morning myoclonic jerks, plus absence and tonic-clonic seizures. Usually lifelong treatment. Valproic acid works well, but levetiracetam is used in girls and women of childbearing age.
Drugs in children
  • Everything is dosed by weight. All paediatric antiepileptic doses are per kilogram
  • Rectal diazepam is the home rescue medication for a prolonged seizure
  • Intranasal midazolam is a newer option for rescue outside hospital
  • Phenobarbital is still used in neonates, though rarely in adults
  • Valproic acid carries its highest risk of fatal liver toxicity in children under 2 on more than one drug
What the parents need to hear
  • First aid: stay calm, protect from injury, turn on the side, time it, nothing in the mouth
  • Call for an ambulance if: it passes 5 minutes, it is the first one ever, breathing is difficult, they were injured, or it happened in water
  • Fever: treat it early with acetaminophen or ibuprofen. But be honest that giving antipyretics preventively does not stop febrile seizures happening
  • School: a seizure action plan on file, staff who know it, and rescue medication if prescribed
  • Activity: swimming with supervision, care with heights, a helmet for cycling. Most things continue with sensible precautions
Bottom Line: Simple febrile seizures need no antiepileptic and carry an excellent outlook. Dose everything by weight. Rectal diazepam is the home rescue.
