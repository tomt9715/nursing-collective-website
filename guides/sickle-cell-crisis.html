Sickle Cell Crisis

The pain crisis hides two killers. Tell them apart, order the four things you do first, and know what keeps the next crisis from starting.

By the end, without notes

  • Separate a pain crisis from the two emergencies hiding inside it
  • Say which crisis drops the reticulocyte count and which raises it

  • State the first action the moment a transfusion reaction starts

01

What actually gets you

Sickle cell crisis is not one thing; it is several emergencies wearing the same word. Most of them present with pain, so pain is where students stop looking.

Points get lost here in three predictable ways, so read these first before anything else.

You treated every crisis as pain

The child hurts, so you medicate and settle in. Meanwhile the chest is filling. Acute chest syndrome brings a new infiltrate, chest pain, hypoxia and fever, and it is the leading cause of death in this disease. Pain control alone does not touch it.

You missed the spleen

Splenic sequestration traps blood in the spleen, and the spleen swells fast. Hemoglobin falls, and a young child slides into hypovolemic shock. Look for the rapidly enlarging spleen and the pallor, not only the pain.

You under-treated the pain, or reached for meperidine

These crises need scheduled opioids, not PRN-only dosing. Believe the pain and treat it. And keep meperidine off the list. Its metabolite normeperidine lowers the seizure threshold.

Bottom Line: Stop studying this as one crisis. Study it as recognition: which crisis is this, and what does that change. And remember the real work happens between episodes, not only during them.
02

The four things

For every crisis below, you should say four things with the book closed. The one-sentence definition. The clue you would actually see. The priority nursing action. The first-line treatment and its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet; familiarity is not recall.

Crisis Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line treatment and teaching point
Vaso-occlusive (pain) Sickled cells clog small vessels and starve tissue of oxygen. Severe bone, back or abdominal pain; swollen hands and feet in an infant. Hydrate, oxygen if hypoxic, scheduled opioids, rest, warmth. IV fluids at 1.5 to 2 times maintenance plus opioids. Heat helps; cold never.
Splenic sequestration The spleen traps large volumes of blood and swells. Rapidly enlarging spleen, pallor, low blood pressure in a child under 5. Restore volume now. Emergent IV fluids and PRBC transfusion. Repeat episodes may mean splenectomy.
Aplastic The marrow briefly stops making red cells. Worsening pallor and fatigue; low reticulocytes; recent parvovirus B19. Support the hemoglobin until marrow recovers. PRBC transfusion. Recovery takes about 7 to 10 days. Contact isolation for parvovirus.
Acute chest syndrome Vaso-occlusion in the lungs with a new infiltrate. Chest pain, cough, fever, hypoxia, new infiltrate on chest X-ray. Oxygen and antibiotics early. Oxygen, broad-spectrum antibiotics, cautious fluids, pain control, incentive spirometry. Exchange transfusion if severe.
Copy this down

Write these four rows on one index card, by hand. Pain crisis clogs vessels. Sequestration drains into the spleen. Aplastic stops the marrow. Chest syndrome fills the lungs. That card is most of the exam.

03

What sickling actually is

Sickle cell disease is an inherited hemoglobinopathy. One DNA mutation changes a single amino acid in the beta-globin chain, and that makes hemoglobin S, or HbS.

When HbS gives up its oxygen, it polymerizes into rigid fibers that bend the red cell into a sickle shape. Sickled cells are fragile, sticky, and poor at flowing through small vessels.

How it is inherited

  • Autosomal recessive. The child needs two sickle genes, one from each parent, to have the disease (HbSS).
  • Sickle cell trait (HbAS) is one copy. It is usually silent, but the carrier can pass the gene on. Two trait parents give a 25 percent chance of an affected child each pregnancy.
  • Most common in African, Mediterranean, Middle Eastern, Caribbean, and Indian ancestry populations.
  • Other variants include HbSC (milder) and HbS beta-thalassemia.

The sickling cascade

Step What happens Clinical effect
1. Deoxygenation or stress HbS loses oxygen in the tissues Cells begin to sickle
2. Polymerization HbS strands clump and distort the cell Rigid, crescent-shaped cells
3. Adhesion and vaso-occlusion Sticky sickled cells block small vessels Ischemia, severe pain, organ damage
4. Hemolysis Sickled cells live only about 10 to 20 days, not 120 Chronic hemolytic anemia, jaundice, gallstones
5. Reperfusion and inflammation Returning blood flow drives oxidative stress and cytokines Endothelial damage, repeat cycles, progressive organ failure

Why symptoms start around 6 months

Infants are born with fetal hemoglobin, or HbF, which does not sickle. Over the first 6 months, HbF is replaced by HbS, and symptoms begin. This is also why hydroxyurea works; it re-induces HbF and cuts the crises.

How it is diagnosed

  • Universal newborn screening in all 50 US states catches the disease at birth.
  • Hemoglobin electrophoresis confirms the genotype, whether HbSS, HbSC, HbS beta-thalassemia, or HbAS trait.
  • The sickle solubility test, or Sickledex, only screens. It cannot tell trait from disease; electrophoresis is the confirmatory test.
The first sign is often dactylitis

For many infants the first crisis is dactylitis. That is painful swelling of the hands and feet from vaso-occlusion in the small bones. Picture a child around 6 to 18 months who will not bear weight, has swollen hands or feet, and is irritable. That child needs a CBC and hemoglobin electrophoresis if the newborn screen has not already named the disease.

Bottom Line: Autosomal recessive HbSS. HbS polymerizes under stress, cells sickle, vessels occlude, and red cells hemolyze. Symptoms start near 6 months as HbF fades.
04

What you see at the bedside

The disease has a baseline picture between crises, and the crisis lays new findings on top of it. Know both, so a change stands out.

Baseline, between crises

Chronic findings
  • Chronic anemia, hemoglobin often 6 to 9 g/dL
  • Pallor, fatigue, systolic flow murmur
  • Jaundice or scleral icterus from hemolysis
  • Delayed growth and puberty
  • Pigment gallstones from chronic hemolysis
Long-term damage
  • Functional asplenia by age 5
  • Avascular necrosis, often the femoral head
  • Renal concentrating defect, enuresis, hematuria
  • Retinopathy; annual eye exams
  • Stroke risk from cerebral vaso-occlusion

During a vaso-occlusive crisis

System What you find Why
PainSevere, often bones, back, abdomen, chestIschemia from vaso-occlusion
Vital signsTachycardia, sometimes low-grade feverStress and metabolic response
Infant hands and feetSwollen, painful; dactylitisOcclusion of small bones
RespiratoryCough, chest pain, hypoxia; think ACSPulmonary vaso-occlusion
AbdominalTender spleen or liver; enlarging spleenSplenic or hepatic sequestration
NeuroAltered mental status, focal deficit, seizureStroke from cerebral occlusion
GUPriapism, hematuria, enuresisUrogenital vaso-occlusion

Labs in crisis

  • CBC: hemoglobin below baseline; reticulocytes high as marrow compensates. In an aplastic crisis they are low.
  • Peripheral smear: sickled cells, target cells, Howell-Jolly bodies (an asplenia marker).
  • Chemistries: raised bilirubin and LDH from hemolysis.
  • Type and crossmatch for transfusion; blood cultures for any fever.
  • Chest X-ray for respiratory symptoms to rule out ACS.
Fever in sickle cell is an emergency

Functional asplenia leaves the child open to overwhelming sepsis from encapsulated organisms. That means Streptococcus pneumoniae, H. influenzae, Neisseria meningitidis, and Salmonella. A single temperature at or above 101 degrees F (38.5 degrees C) needs action. Draw blood cultures, get a CBC and chest X-ray, and give IV ceftriaxone within the first hour. Do not send the child home to watch and wait.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Fever equals cultures plus ceftriaxone
One number matters here. At or above 101 degrees F, this is a septic-until-proven-otherwise child. Cultures first, then IV ceftriaxone inside the hour. The spleen that would normally clear these bacteria is gone.
Bottom Line: Baseline is anemia, jaundice, delayed growth, gallstones. Crisis adds pain, fever, ACS signs, stroke, priapism, and dactylitis in infants. Any fever is an emergency.
05

Tell the crises apart

Discrimination axis: same disease, three different failures.

Three crises look alike at a glance, and all bring pallor, fatigue, and a falling hemoglobin. Two numbers and one exam finding separate them.

Vaso-occlusive: vessels clog

Sickled cells jam the microvasculature, so the tissue goes ischemic and it hurts.

  • Severe pain in bones, back, abdomen, chest
  • Reticulocytes high, marrow still working
  • Spleen not the driver
Sequestration: spleen fills

The spleen traps blood and balloons, so volume leaves the circulation fast.

  • Rapidly enlarging spleen, a left upper mass
  • Reticulocytes high; hemoglobin crashes
  • Pallor, tachycardia, shock in a child under 5
Aplastic: marrow stops

The marrow shuts down briefly, classically after parvovirus B19.

  • Worsening pallor and fatigue
  • Reticulocytes low, the giveaway
  • No enlarging spleen
The axis that separates them

Reticulocytes first. High means the marrow is answering, so this is vaso-occlusive or sequestration. Low means the marrow quit, so this is aplastic; look for a recent parvovirus B19. Then check the spleen. A spleen swelling fast in a young child is sequestration until proven otherwise.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Reticulocytes split the crises
A falling hemoglobin does not name the crisis; the reticulocyte count does. High retics mean the marrow is compensating. Low retics point straight at aplastic crisis and parvovirus B19. Add a fast-growing spleen and you have sequestration.

One cousin belongs here too. Hyperhemolytic crisis drops the hemoglobin fast with a high reticulocyte count and jaundice. It rides on infection or hypersplenism, and you manage it with supportive care and transfusion.

Acute chest syndrome, and other complications

Acute chest syndrome is the leading cause of death

Any child with sickle cell disease, admitted for any reason, who develops cough, chest pain, hypoxia, or a new infiltrate is presumed to have acute chest syndrome until proven otherwise. Treat it hard: oxygen, antibiotics, pain control, incentive spirometry, and prepare for possible exchange transfusion. It can worsen within hours. Do not wait to act.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Presume ACS, do not confirm it slowly
New chest pain, hypoxia, or an infiltrate in a sickle cell child is ACS until proven otherwise. It is the number one killer. Oxygen and antibiotics go in early, not after the workup returns.

Stroke. Cerebral vaso-occlusion; a major pediatric complication. Annual transcranial Doppler ages 2 to 16.

Priapism. Sickling in penile vessels; a painful erection over 4 hours is a urologic emergency. Hydrate, control pain, consult urology; the long-term risk is impotence.

Avascular necrosis. Especially the femoral head; chronic hip pain later.

Functional asplenia by age 5, driving the sepsis risk that makes any fever an emergency.

Bottom Line: Retics high plus pain equals vaso-occlusive. Retics high plus a swelling spleen equals sequestration. Retics low equals aplastic. New chest signs equal ACS, the number one killer.
06

What sets it off

Almost anything that stresses the cell can start the sickling. Low oxygen, concentrated blood, acid, or cold will do it. Teaching the family to spot and avoid triggers is half of chronic care.

Trigger Why it sickles What you teach
DehydrationConcentrated blood sickles morePush fluids all day, extra when sick or hot
HypoxiaHbS polymerizes when deoxygenatedPlan oxygen for altitude, treat sleep apnea and illness
InfectionFever raises metabolic demandVaccinate on time, treat any fever early
Cold exposureVasoconstriction promotes sicklingDress in layers, skip cold pools and ice baths
AcidosisShifts oxygen release toward sicklingTreat shock or dehydration promptly
Stress and hard exerciseRaises demand and can dehydrateAllow moderate activity with steady hydration and rest
High altitudeLower oxygen at altitude triggers sicklingPlan oxygen for unpressurized flight and high altitude
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Memorize HHIDS
The five classic triggers spell HHIDS. Hypoxia, Hydration loss, Infection, Decrease in temperature, and Stress. Name the trigger and you have named the fix. Most family teaching is just preventing HHIDS.
Bottom Line: Triggers are hypoxia, dehydration, infection, cold, acidosis, stress, and altitude. Prevent with hydration, warmth, vaccines, and early fever care.
07

What you do first

Acute crisis care rests on four pillars. Rest to cut oxygen demand. Oxygen if the child is hypoxic. Hydration to reverse sickling. Pain control that is aggressive and scheduled. Everything else supports these four.

The ordering rules
  • Airway and breathing beat everything. Hypoxia or new chest signs get oxygen and a workup first.
  • Believe the pain. Scheduled opioids, not PRN-only, and never meperidine.
  • Warm always, cold never. Cold drives vasoconstriction and more sickling.
  • Any fever means cultures and antibiotics, not watchful waiting.

The priority pillars, in order

  1. 1
    Rest. Limit activity during the crisis. Cluster care so the child can rest. Less oxygen demand means less sickling.
  2. 2
    Oxygen if hypoxic. Give it for a saturation below 92 percent or symptoms. Do not run routine oxygen at a normal saturation; it can suppress erythropoiesis. Use continuous pulse oximetry.
  3. 3
    Hydrate hard. IV fluids at 1.5 to 2 times maintenance, usually D5 half NS or D5NS. Dilute the blood, reduce sickling, flush the occluded vessels. Watch for overload, especially with ACS risk.
  4. 4
    Control the pain. Scheduled opioids plus PRN; PCA for older kids. Add acetaminophen and NSAIDs if the kidneys allow. Reassess every 15 to 30 minutes until it settles.
  5. 5
    Keep the child warm. Warm blankets, warm room. Cold causes vasoconstriction and more sickling.
  6. 6
    Monitor and support. Track intake, output, daily weights, and vitals. Urine output above 1 to 2 mL/kg/hr signals adequate hydration. Give blood products and antibiotics as ordered.

Pain, up close

Sickle cell pain is severe, and it has long been under-treated, often through racial bias and opioid stigma. Believe the child, and treat the pain.

Morphine
Opioid, first-line

IV bolus plus continuous or PCA; the standard first choice.

Monitor: respiratory rate, sedation, pruritus, nausea.

Hydromorphone
Opioid, alternative

Used when morphine falls short or causes side effects.

More potent; dose carefully.

Acetaminophen and NSAIDs
Non-opioid adjunct

Scheduled around the clock; they spare opioids.

Caution: NSAIDs with renal involvement or bleeding risk.

Patient-controlled analgesia
Delivery method

Older kids, usually 7 and up, self-dose within safety lockouts.

It gives the child control and improves pain scores.

Avoid meperidine

Meperidine breaks down into normeperidine. That metabolite is neurotoxic and lowers the seizure threshold. It is not recommended for sickle cell pain. Reach for morphine or hydromorphone instead.

Non-drug relief

  • Heat packs over painful areas. Never cold; cold worsens sickling.
  • Distraction with games, movies, or music; guided imagery; child life specialists.
  • Gentle massage, relaxation, a warm bath.
  • Validate the pain. It lowers the anxiety that amplifies it.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Think HOPR
Every crisis runs on the same four pillars. Hydration at 1.5 to 2 times maintenance. Oxygen only if hypoxic. Pain control with scheduled opioids. Rest to cut oxygen demand. When a question asks for priority, one of these is almost always it. Add warmth, and check for infection.
Bottom Line: HOPR. Hydrate at 1.5 to 2 times maintenance, oxygen if hypoxic, scheduled opioids with no meperidine, and rest. Warm, not cold. Watch intake, output, labs, and infection.
08

Blood, and the reaction

These children are transfused often, during crises, for stroke prevention, or as exchange transfusion in severe ACS. Know the indications, the administration, and above all the first move when a reaction starts.

Types of transfusion

Type When used Goal
Simple PRBC Acute anemia, aplastic or sequestration crisis Raise hemoglobin, replace volume
Chronic transfusion program Stroke prevention after a high TCD or a stroke Keep HbS below 30 percent to stop new occlusion
Exchange transfusion Severe ACS, stroke, multi-organ crisis, major pre-op Drop the HbS concentration fast

Safety before you hang it

  1. 1
    Verify with two nurses. Order, patient identity, and product compatibility, checked by two licensed nurses at the bedside.
  2. 2
    Baseline vitals first. Temperature, heart rate, respiratory rate, blood pressure, and saturation before you start.
  3. 3
    Prime with normal saline. Use blood tubing with an in-line filter. Not LR, not D5W; they hemolyze or clump the cells.
  4. 4
    Start slow and stay. Run slowly for the first 15 minutes and remain at the bedside. Most acute reactions show up here.
  5. 5
    Reassess and finish on time. Recheck vitals at 15 minutes, then every 15 to 30. Complete each unit within 4 hours. Document everything.

Signs of a reaction

Fever and chills, the most common sign

Back, flank, or chest pain

Hypotension or tachycardia

Dyspnea, wheeze, stridor

Hives, itching, flushing

Dark urine from acute hemolysis

Anxiety or a sense of doom

New nausea or vomiting

Reaction: stop first

At the first sign, stop the transfusion. Keep the IV line open with normal saline through new tubing. Do not flush the old tubing; it still holds the reacting blood. Then notify the provider and the blood bank. Send the bag, the tubing, and post-reaction blood and urine samples back. Support vitals, treat symptoms, and document. Stop, save the line, call, send.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Stop, save the line, call, send
The first action is always to stop the transfusion. Then keep the line open with saline through new tubing. Medications and notification come after. Flushing the old tubing pushes reacting blood into the child, so never do it.

Kinds of reaction

  • Acute hemolytic (ABO mismatch): fever, chills, back pain, hypotension, dark urine within minutes. Life-threatening.
  • Febrile non-hemolytic: most common; mild fever and chills; treat with acetaminophen. Use leukoreduced products next time.
  • Allergic or anaphylactic: hives, wheeze, hypotension. Diphenhydramine; epinephrine and washed products for anaphylaxis.
  • TRALI: acute dyspnea within 6 hours; bilateral pulmonary edema without overload.
  • TACO: circulatory overload; slow the infusion and diurese.
  • Delayed hemolytic: days later, from alloantibodies; a risk of chronic transfusion.

The long-term costs

  • Iron overload. Chronic transfusion deposits iron in heart, liver, and glands. Monitor ferritin and start chelation, such as deferasirox or deferoxamine, once ferritin runs above 1,000.
  • Alloimmunization. Antibodies to minor antigens; use extended phenotype-matched blood to lower the risk.
  • Infection. Rare with modern screening, but never zero.
Bottom Line: Transfuse for severe anemia, ACS, and stroke. Two-nurse check, and the first 15 minutes matter most. Reaction means stop, saline through new tubing, call, send the bag back. Iron overload means chelation.
09

Preventing the next crisis

This is a lifelong disease, and daily medicine, routine screening, and prompt care keep crises and damage down. The nurse teaches most of it, in clinic and on the unit.

Disease-modifying therapy

Hydroxyurea
HbF inducer, first-line

Daily oral. It raises fetal hemoglobin, which does not sickle. Fewer crises, less ACS, less stroke, longer survival.

Monitor the CBC for myelosuppression; watch for mouth sores. It is teratogenic.

L-glutamine
Oxidative stress reducer

Oral powder; cuts pain crises in some children.

Approved from age 5 and up.

Crizanlizumab
P-selectin inhibitor

Monthly IV infusion; reduces vaso-occlusive crises.

For patients 16 and up with frequent crises.

Voxelotor
Polymerization inhibitor

Oral daily; raises hemoglobin and lowers hemolysis.

A newer option for chronic anemia.

Infection prophylaxis and vaccines

  • Daily penicillin V from 2 months through at least age 5. It cuts pneumococcal sepsis sharply.
  • Pneumococcal vaccines on schedule, both the conjugate and the polysaccharide.
  • Meningococcal vaccine starting at 9 to 12 months, earlier than the general schedule.
  • Hib and annual influenza, plus all routine childhood vaccines.

Stroke screening

  • Transcranial Doppler every year from age 2 to 16. It reads cerebral blood-flow velocity.
  • Velocity above 200 cm/s means high stroke risk. Start a chronic transfusion program and keep HbS below 30 percent.
  • After a stroke, chronic transfusion continues indefinitely.

Routine maintenance

Folic acid daily to support the marrow.

Annual eye exam to screen for retinopathy.

Hematology clinic visits for growth, development, and labs.

Screen mood for depression and anxiety, and track school impact.

The curative options

  • Hematopoietic stem cell transplant is the only established cure. A matched sibling donor gives the best result.
  • It is reserved for severe disease, given the transplant mortality and graft-versus-host risk.
  • Gene therapy is emerging. It edits the child's own stem cells and is approved for severe disease at specialized centers.
The outlook has changed

In the 1970s, half of these children died before age 20. Today, with newborn screening, hydroxyurea, penicillin, vaccines, and TCD screening, most survive into adulthood. Many go to college, work, and raise families. The disease is still serious, but the path is far better. Say that out loud to families who feel overwhelmed at diagnosis.

Bottom Line: Hydroxyurea, folic acid, penicillin through age 5, pneumococcal and meningococcal vaccines, annual TCD from 2 to 16, eye exams. Transplant and gene therapy can cure severe disease.
10

What the family does at home

Families become the lifelong experts on their child, and nurses lay the foundation at every visit. Teach in plain terms, respect the culture, and repeat it in more than one format.

Top home priorities

Hydrate every day. A water bottle always at hand; extra in heat or illness.

Avoid triggers. Cold, altitude, hypoxia, infection, and hard activity without hydration.

Give daily medicine. Hydroxyurea, folic acid, penicillin; do not skip doses.

Have a home pain plan. Mild crises can be managed with fluids, heat, and oral meds; know when to call.

Keep a thermometer. Any fever at or above 101 degrees F means the ED, right away.

Stay on the vaccine schedule. Pneumococcal, meningococcal, Hib, and flu.

When to come to the ED

Fever at or above 101 degrees F. Always. Never wait.

Severe pain that home meds do not touch

Chest pain, cough, trouble breathing. Think ACS

Sudden weakness, slurred speech, facial droop, vision change. Think stroke

A belly enlarging fast. Think sequestration

Extreme fatigue and pallor. Think aplastic crisis

An erection over 4 hours

Dehydration, vomiting, or refusing to drink

School and activity

  • The child should be in school. Build a 504 plan or health plan with the school nurse.
  • Allow water at all times, extra bathroom breaks, and warm clothing.
  • Encourage moderate activity. Avoid high-intensity competitive sport without a hydration plan, and skip cold pools and ice baths.
  • Protect sleep, and do not over-schedule.

Psychosocial and genetics

  • These children face chronic pain, missed school, stigma, and mental-health strain. Screen for depression and anxiety.
  • Support groups help with isolation.
  • Offer genetic counseling to parents and siblings, and later to teens thinking about future parenthood.
  • Address the systemic issues too: pain-management bias, insurance, and access to specialty care.
Believe the pain, and advocate

Children with sickle cell disease have long been under-treated for pain, especially in the ED. Studies tie that to implicit racial bias. Your job is clear. Believe the pain score. Push for timely opioid dosing. Refuse the drug-seeking label, and document the plan. This pain is real, severe, and under-recognized. Your advocacy protects comfort and saves lives.

Bottom Line: Daily hydration and meds, and a fever plan where 101 degrees F equals the ED. Avoid triggers, build a 504 plan, offer genetic counseling. Believe the pain.
