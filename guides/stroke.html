01

What actually gets you

Stroke is two diseases that look identical at the bedside. One vessel is blocked. The other has burst. The drug that saves the first one kills the second.

That single fact organises the whole topic, and it is where the mistakes cluster.

You treated before you knew which one it was

Facial droop, arm weakness and slurred speech look the same either way. You cannot tell from the bedside. Neither can anyone else.

The CT is not a formality. Giving a clot-buster to someone already bleeding into their brain is fatal. It is the most tested error in this guide.

You treated the blood pressure like a number to fix

A stroke patient at 200/110 looks alarming, so the instinct is to treat it. In an ischemic stroke without tPA, that pressure is keeping the salvageable tissue alive.

There are four different targets in this topic, and they depend entirely on which situation you are in.

You did not ask when it started

Everything about eligibility rests on one timestamp, and the patient often cannot give it to you.

The question is not when they noticed. It is when they were last seen completely normal. An unwitnessed stroke on waking has a last known well of bedtime, not of this morning.

You gave them their morning tablets with water

Up to three-quarters of stroke patients have some swallowing impairment, and they cannot always feel it.

Nothing by mouth until the swallow screen is passed. That includes water, and it includes oral medications.

Bottom Line: Study this as three discriminations: clot or bleed, which hemisphere, and which blood pressure target applies right now.
02

The four things

For each entity below, you should be able to say four things with the book closed: the one-sentence definition, the clue you would see in a patient, the priority nursing action, and the first-line drug with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Entity Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line drug
Ischemic stroke A clot blocks a cerebral vessel and the tissue behind it starves. 87 percent of strokes. Often gradual, sometimes a TIA warning first. CT immediately, then work the tPA clock. Alteplase, 0.9 mg/kg, within 4.5 hours of last known well.
Hemorrhagic stroke A vessel ruptures and blood compresses the brain around it. 13 percent. Sudden and severe, thunderclap headache, vomiting, falling consciousness. Control the pressure hard, and watch the intracranial pressure. No tPA, ever. Labetalol or nicardipine to get systolic under 140.
TIA Ischemia that resolves without leaving an infarct behind. Deficits that came and went, usually within an hour. Treat it as a warning, not an all-clear. Urgent workup. Antiplatelet, plus aggressive risk factor management.
Hemorrhagic transformation An ischemic stroke that starts bleeding, usually after tPA. Sudden headache, falling consciousness, new deficit, rising pressure. Stop the infusion now, then get a stat CT. Nothing to give. Stopping the tPA is the intervention.
Copy this down

Four rows on one index card, by hand. Clot gets tPA, bleed never does, and the CT is what tells you which. Last known well starts the clock. That card is most of the exam.

03

Which artery, which deficit

The brain takes 15 to 20 percent of cardiac output. It burns about a fifth of the body's oxygen and stores almost none. So an interruption shows in seconds, not hours.

Which artery closed decides which deficits appear. Learn the map and you can reason backwards, from symptom to vessel.

ArteryWhat it suppliesWhat you see when it closes
Anterior cerebralFrontal lobe, and the inner surface of the hemisphereLeg weaker than arm, personality change, urinary incontinence
Middle cerebralThe outer surface, including motor and sensory cortexArm and face weaker than leg, aphasia if it is the dominant side, neglect if it is not
Posterior cerebralOccipital lobe and visual cortexVisual field loss, visual agnosia, memory problems
BasilarBrainstem and cerebellumComa, locked-in syndrome, cranial nerve deficits
VertebralMedulla and cerebellumVertigo, ataxia, trouble swallowing, double vision
Left hemisphere or right

This pair comes up constantly. The axis is what the patient cannot do, not which side is weak.

Left hemisphere stroke: right-sided weakness, and aphasia. Language lives on the left in almost everyone. So they cannot find words, or cannot understand them. They usually know something is wrong, which is distressing.

Right hemisphere stroke: left-sided weakness, and neglect. They do not recognise the left side of the world as theirs. They may eat half a plate and say they finished.

Aphasia means they know and cannot say. Neglect means they do not know. That changes how you approach them.

The Circle of Willis

A ring of arteries at the base of the brain. It joins the front circulation from the carotids to the back circulation from the vertebrals.

It exists so one blocked vessel can be bypassed. In practice the anatomy varies a lot, and not everyone has a complete circle. That is part of why the same occlusion does very different damage in different patients.

Four ideas about perfusion

  • Cerebral perfusion pressure. Mean arterial pressure minus intracranial pressure. Normal is 60 to 100 mmHg, and under 50 causes ischemia.
  • Autoregulation. The brain normally holds flow steady across a mean pressure of 60 to 160 mmHg. Acute stroke destroys that. It is why systemic pressure suddenly matters so much.
  • The ischemic penumbra. The ring around the dead core that is starving but not yet dead. This is what reperfusion is trying to save.
  • The ischemic cascade. Within minutes ATP runs out, cell membranes fail, calcium floods in, and neurons die.
Time is brain

Roughly 1.9 million neurons die every minute flow is not restored.

That is why the whole system is built around speed. It is why door to needle is under 60 minutes. Delay is not administrative. It is measured in tissue.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Time = Brain
Document the exact time symptoms started, because it decides whether tPA is possible at all. Ask the family the right question: what time did you last see them completely normal. That is the last known well, and if nobody can give it to you, the patient is not eligible.

ModifiableNot modifiable
Hypertension, the leading modifiable risk factorAge. The risk doubles every decade after 55

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Watch for Atrial Fibrillation
Keep every stroke patient on continuous cardiac monitoring. Blood pools in quivering atria, clots form, and the clot travels to the brain. Finding A-fib changes the prevention plan from an antiplatelet to an anticoagulant, which is a much bigger decision than it sounds.
Bottom Line: The artery predicts the deficit. Left hemisphere gives right weakness and aphasia; right gives left weakness and neglect. The penumbra is what you are racing for.
04

Clot or bleed

Discrimination axis: is the vessel blocked, or open and leaking.

This is the discrimination the guide turns on. You cannot make it clinically. Both give sudden focal deficits in a patient who was fine an hour ago.

Ischemic, about 87 percent

Mechanism: a clot or embolus occludes the vessel. The tissue behind it loses oxygen and glucose.

Where the clot comes from:

  • Thrombotic: atherosclerosis and plaque rupture, forming the clot in place
  • Embolic: travelled from elsewhere, usually the atria in A-fib, or a diseased valve
  • Lacunar: small vessel disease from long-standing hypertension or diabetes

How it presents: often gradual, especially thrombotic. May have had TIA warnings. Focal deficits.

Treatment: tPA if inside the window, mechanical thrombectomy, then antiplatelet or anticoagulation.

Hemorrhagic, about 13 percent

Mechanism: the vessel ruptures. Blood accumulates, compresses tissue, and raises intracranial pressure.

Where the bleed comes from:

  • Intracerebral: chronic hypertension, or amyloid angiopathy
  • Subarachnoid: a ruptured aneurysm, or an arteriovenous malformation
  • Other: trauma, anticoagulants, tumours

How it presents: sudden and severe. A thunderclap headache. Vomiting, falling consciousness and seizures are common, and it is generally worse than an ischemic stroke.

Treatment: no tPA. Blood pressure control, intracranial pressure control, and surgery if indicated.

The CT is the axis

A non-contrast CT of the head comes before any decision about thrombolysis, and it must show no hemorrhage.

There is a useful asymmetry in what CT sees. Fresh blood is bright immediately, so a bleed shows at once. An ischemic infarct may show nothing for 6 to 12 hours.

So the CT is not proving a clot. It is ruling out a bleed. In the first hour, that is what it does well.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
CT Before the Clot-Buster
The scan happens before tPA, without exception. It is there to rule out a bleed, not to confirm a clot, which is why an early normal CT is still good enough to proceed. Give a clot-buster to someone already bleeding and you turn a survivable stroke into a fatal one.

Transient ischemic attack

A brief neurological deficit from ischemia, with no infarct left behind. Symptoms usually resolve within an hour, often within minutes.

A TIA is not a near miss

Between 10 and 15 percent of TIA patients have a stroke within 90 days. The risk is highest in the first 48 hours.

So the patient whose symptoms resolved in the waiting room is not the reassuring one. They are the one with a warning nobody has acted on.

Write this down

Blank paper. Two columns, ischemic and hemorrhagic. Percentage, mechanism, onset, treatment. Then one sentence on why the CT can rule out one and not the other in the first hour.

Bottom Line: Ischemic is a clot and gets tPA. Hemorrhagic is a bleed and never does. Only the CT tells you which, and a TIA is a warning with a 48 hour fuse.
05

Spotting it, and starting the clock

FAST exists so anybody can spot a stroke, not just clinicians. It is deliberately crude. It has to work for a bystander.

FAST

F

Face

Ask them to smile. Does one side droop or feel numb.

A

Arms

Ask them to raise both. Does one drift down, or feel weak.

S

Speech

Ask them to repeat a phrase. Is it slurred, or does it come out wrong.

T

Time

Any one of these, call for help. Note when it started.

BE-FAST, and why the two extra letters were added

The original four miss posterior circulation strokes. That is where the balance and vision problems come from. The expanded version catches them.

LetterWhatHow you assess it
BBalanceSudden loss of balance or coordination, or dizziness
EEyesSudden vision change, blurring, or loss in one or both eyes
FFaceAsk them to smile and look for asymmetry
AArmsBoth arms out, palms up, eyes closed, for 10 seconds. Watch for drift
SSpeechAsk them to repeat a set phrase and listen for slurring
TTimeCall for help, and record when it started
The question that decides eligibility

Not when did you notice. What time did you last see them completely normal.

That is the last known well, and it is where the clock starts. Take a patient found confused at 7 am who was fine at 10 pm. Their last known well is 10 pm. They are 9 hours out, even though they were found an hour ago.

This is why wake-up strokes so often miss the window, and why the family is your most important source of information.

NIHSS

A standardised exam that turns the deficit into a number, 0 to 42. Higher is worse. Its real value is serial. Repeated, it tells you whether they are improving or sliding.

ScoreSeverityWhat it means
0No symptomsNormal exam
1 to 4MinorGood outlook. May not need intervention
5 to 15ModerateA tPA candidate. Moderate risk of disability
16 to 20Moderate to severeSignificant disability likely
21 to 42SeverePoor outlook, high mortality
What the scale actually covers

Eleven items. Level of consciousness, horizontal eye movement, visual fields, facial palsy. Motor arm, motor leg, limb ataxia, sensation. Language, dysarthria, and extinction or inattention.

Nurses in stroke centres are certified to give it. A score is only useful if everyone scores the same way.

Bottom Line: BE-FAST catches it, and the extra two letters exist for posterior strokes. Last known well starts the clock, and NIHSS tracks the trajectory.
06

Who gets tPA

Alteplase dissolves the clot. It is the standard of care for eligible ischemic stroke. The criteria are strict, because it dissolves clots you wanted to keep too.

The window

Time from last known wellWhat it means
0 hoursSymptom onset. This is the number everything is measured from
Up to 3 hoursThe standard window, and where the benefit is greatest
3 to 4.5 hoursThe extended window, for selected patients only
Beyond 4.5 hoursContraindicated
Door to needle, under 60 minutes

From the patient arriving to the infusion starting. Stroke centres are measured on it.

Every 15 minutes of delay costs outcome. So the CT, the labs, the weight and the consent happen in parallel, not in sequence.

Give it when all of these are true
  • Ischemic stroke with a measurable neurological deficit
  • Onset, or last known well, under 4.5 hours ago
  • Age 18 or over
  • CT shows no hemorrhage
  • Blood pressure can be brought under 185/110 mmHg
  • The patient or family consents
Withhold it if any of these are true
  • Hemorrhage on the CT
  • Onset over 4.5 hours ago, or the time is unknown
  • Any previous intracranial hemorrhage
  • Major surgery or trauma within 14 days
  • Gastrointestinal or urinary bleeding within 21 days
  • Arterial puncture at a site you cannot compress, within 7 days
Every exclusion is the same question

Read the list again and notice the pattern. Recent surgery. Recent bleeding. A puncture you cannot press on. A previous brain bleed.

They all ask one thing. Is there somewhere in this patient that will bleed once clotting is switched off. Learn the question and you can derive most of the list.

CategoryAbsolute contraindications
CoagulationINR over 1.7, PT over 15 seconds, platelets under 100,000, or heparin within 48 hours with a raised PTT
Blood pressureSystolic over 185 or diastolic over 110 that cannot be brought down
Blood glucoseUnder 50 mg/dL. Correct the hypoglycemia first, because it can mimic a stroke entirely
CT findingsInfarction across more than a third of the hemisphere
HistoryStroke or head trauma within 3 months, or an intracranial tumour
MedicationsCurrent direct thrombin inhibitors or factor Xa inhibitors
The extended window has four extra exclusions

Between 3 and 4.5 hours, also exclude:

  • Age over 80
  • NIHSS over 25, meaning a very severe stroke
  • A history of both stroke and diabetes
  • Taking oral anticoagulants, whatever the INR

Later means less benefit and the same bleeding risk. So the bar rises.

Giving it, and watching

WhatDetail
Dose0.9 mg/kg, to a maximum of 90 mg. Ten percent as a bolus over 1 minute, the rest infused over 60 minutes
Blood pressureUnder 180/105 mmHg. Every 15 minutes during the infusion, every 30 minutes for 6 hours, then hourly
Neuro checksEvery 15 minutes during the infusion, every 30 minutes for 6 hours, then hourly for 24
AnticoagulantsNone for 24 hours. No heparin, no warfarin, no antiplatelets
Invasive proceduresAvoid for 24 hours where possible. No arterial lines, central lines, NG tubes or catheters
Hemorrhagic transformation

The clot dissolves. Blood returns to vessels that starved and are now fragile, and they leak. It happens to about 6 percent of patients given tPA.

What you are watching for:

  • Sudden neurological decline, a falling level of consciousness, or a new deficit
  • Severe headache
  • New hypertension, nausea, vomiting

Stop the infusion first, then get the stat CT. Stopping is within your scope and takes seconds. You do not need permission to stop a drug that is causing harm.

Bottom Line: 4.5 hours from last known well, 0.9 mg/kg to a maximum of 90. Under 185/110 before, under 180/105 after. Every exclusion asks where this patient might bleed.
07

What you do first

Priority questions give you four defensible actions and ask which comes first. All four are things a nurse would do. That is the trick. You are tested on ordering, not correctness.

The ordering rules
  • Airway before anything. A patient with a falling level of consciousness cannot protect it.
  • Time-critical beats important. The CT and the last known well come before the full history.
  • Nothing by mouth until somebody has assessed the swallow. That includes water and tablets.
  • A new neurological finding outranks every number on the monitor.
  • Know which blood pressure target you are working to before you treat the pressure at all.

The first assessment

  1. 1
    Airway and breathing. Patency, saturation held above 94 percent, respiratory effort. Head of bed at 30 degrees unless there is a reason not to. Suction ready if consciousness is dropping.
  2. 2
    Neurological status. NIHSS and Glasgow Coma Scale. Pupils. Document the baseline properly, because everything after this is measured against it.
  3. 3
    Cardiac and vital signs. Continuous monitoring, looking for atrial fibrillation. Blood pressure to the protocol for this patient's situation. Keep the temperature under 38 C, and treat fever properly, because fever worsens the injury.
  4. 4
    Labs. Glucose every 6 hours, targeting 140 to 180 mg/dL. Coagulation studies. A metabolic panel for electrolytes. Troponin, because the heart is often involved.

Blood pressure, four different answers

SituationTargetWhy
Before tPAUnder 185/110 mmHgRequired for eligibility, and it lowers the bleeding risk
During and for 24 hours after tPAUnder 180/105 mmHgTight control while the clotting system is suppressed
Ischemic stroke, no tPAGenerally under 220/120 mmHgPermissive hypertension, to keep the penumbra perfused
Hemorrhagic strokeSystolic under 140 mmHgAggressive control, to stop the hematoma expanding
Why the targets go in opposite directions

In an ischemic stroke the problem is too little flow. Autoregulation is gone. Perfusion of the penumbra now rides on systemic pressure. Drop it and you finish off the tissue you were saving.

In a hemorrhagic stroke the problem is too much flow, in the wrong place. Higher pressure pushes more blood out and expands the hematoma.

Same organ, same presentation, opposite instruction. So the CT comes before the antihypertensive, as well as before the tPA.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Permissive Hypertension
In an ischemic stroke with no tPA, a pressure up to 220/120 is allowed and often wanted. Autoregulation has failed, so that pressure is what pushes blood around the blockage into the tissue you are trying to save. Treat it only for tPA, or if the pressure is damaging something else.

Swallowing

Nothing by mouth until the screen is passed

Between 37 and 78 percent have dysphagia. Aspiration pneumonia is a leading cause of death afterwards.

Until somebody trained has assessed the swallow:

  • Nothing by mouth. No food, no fluids, no oral medications
  • Head of bed at 30 to 45 degrees
  • Suction at the bedside
  • Meticulous oral care, because a colonised mouth is what gets aspirated
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Nothing By Mouth First
Stroke damages the nerves that run swallowing, and the patient often cannot feel that it has happened. A sip of water can go into the lungs instead of the stomach. Wait for the swallow screen, and remember that oral medications count as by mouth.

What else goes wrong

  1. 1
    Rising intracranial pressure. Headache, vomiting, falling consciousness, and Cushing's triad. Keep the head of bed at 30 degrees and the neck straight, because flexion obstructs venous drainage. Report changes as they happen.
  2. 2
    Seizures. In 2 to 10 percent of stroke patients. Seizure precautions for everyone. Prophylactic anticonvulsants are not routine, though they are sometimes used after a hemorrhagic stroke.
  3. 3
    DVT and pulmonary embolism. High risk, because they are not moving. Compression devices from admission. Prophylactic anticoagulation once it is safe, usually 24 to 48 hours after tPA.
  4. 4
    Skin breakdown. Turn every 2 hours, use pressure-relieving surfaces, score them on Braden, keep the skin clean and dry. A patient who cannot feel one side will not shift their own weight.
Positioning and neglect

When they are on their side, the affected side goes up rather than being lain on.

And approach them from the affected side deliberately. A patient with neglect will not turn toward a world they do not know is there. Making them turn is therapy, not an inconvenience.

Bottom Line: Airway, then neuro baseline, then the right pressure target for this patient. Nothing by mouth until the swallow screen, and rehab starts within 24 to 48 hours.

Antiplatelet or anticoagulant

The choice depends on where the clot was made. Not on how bad the stroke was.

A clot formed on an arterial plaque is platelet-driven. An antiplatelet is the answer. A clot formed in a fibrillating atrium is fibrin in stagnant blood. An antiplatelet barely touches it. That patient needs anticoagulation.

This is why finding A-fib on the monitor changes the discharge prescription entirely.

What the patient takes home
  • Antiplatelet or anticoagulant, exactly as prescribed
  • Blood pressure controlled, and they should know their own numbers
  • Cholesterol managed, usually with a statin
  • Diabetes controlled, and diet changed

Plus stopping smoking, moving more, limiting alcohol, and being able to recognise the symptoms if it happens again.

Bottom Line: Alteplase for the clot, labetalol or nicardipine for the pressure, mannitol for the swelling. Afterwards, antiplatelet for artery clots and anticoagulant for heart clots.
