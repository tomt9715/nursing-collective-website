Cerebrovascular Anatomy & Physiology

Understanding the brain's blood supply

The brain requires a constant supply of oxygen and glucose to function. It receives approximately 15-20% of total cardiac output and consumes about 20% of the body's oxygen. Understanding cerebral circulation is essential for recognizing stroke patterns.

Cerebral Blood Supply

Artery Area Supplied Stroke Symptoms if Occluded
Anterior Cerebral (ACA) Frontal lobe, medial surface of brain Leg weakness > arm, personality changes, urinary incontinence
Middle Cerebral (MCA) Lateral surface, motor/sensory cortex Arm/face weakness > leg, aphasia (if dominant), neglect
Posterior Cerebral (PCA) Occipital lobe, visual cortex Visual field deficits, visual agnosia, memory problems
Basilar Artery Brainstem, cerebellum Coma, "locked-in" syndrome, cranial nerve deficits
Vertebral Arteries Medulla, cerebellum Vertigo, ataxia, difficulty swallowing, diplopia
Clinical Pearl: Circle of Willis

The Circle of Willis is a ring of arteries at the base of the brain that provides collateral circulation. It connects the anterior (carotid) and posterior (vertebrobasilar) circulation, potentially providing backup blood flow during vessel occlusion. However, anatomical variations are common, and not everyone has a complete circle.

Key Concepts in Cerebral Perfusion

  • Cerebral Perfusion Pressure (CPP): MAP - ICP. Normal CPP is 60-100 mmHg. CPP < 50 mmHg causes ischemia.
  • Autoregulation: The brain maintains constant blood flow across MAP 60-160 mmHg. Lost in acute stroke.
  • Ischemic Penumbra: The area of potentially salvageable tissue surrounding the infarct core. Target of reperfusion therapy.
  • Ischemic Cascade: Within minutes of occlusion, ATP depletion leads to cell membrane failure, calcium influx, and neuronal death.
Time is Brain!

Every minute without blood flow, 1.9 million neurons die. This is why rapid recognition and treatment are critical. The phrase "Time is Brain" emphasizes that delays in treatment directly translate to brain damage and disability.

Ischemic vs Hemorrhagic Stroke

Understanding the fundamental differences

Strokes are classified into two main categories based on the underlying mechanism. This distinction is critical because treatment differs dramaticallyâ€”giving tPA to a hemorrhagic stroke patient can be fatal.

Ischemic Stroke

~87% of all strokes
Mechanism
  • Blood vessel occlusion by clot or embolus
  • Brain tissue deprived of oxygen/glucose
  • May be thrombotic or embolic in origin
Common Causes
  • Thrombotic: Atherosclerosis, plaque rupture
  • Embolic: Atrial fibrillation (A-fib), valve disease
  • Lacunar: Small vessel disease from HTN/DM
Presentation
  • Often gradual onset (thrombotic)
  • May have TIA warning signs
  • Focal neurological deficits
Treatment
  • tPA (alteplase) if within window
  • Mechanical thrombectomy
  • Antiplatelet/anticoagulation

Hemorrhagic Stroke

~13% of all strokes
Mechanism
  • Blood vessel rupture causing bleeding
  • Blood accumulates, compresses brain tissue
  • Increased ICP, herniation risk
Common Causes
  • Intracerebral: Chronic HTN, amyloid angiopathy
  • Subarachnoid: Aneurysm rupture, AVM
  • Other: Trauma, anticoagulant use, tumors
Presentation
  • Sudden, severe onset ("thunderclap" HA)
  • Often worse than ischemic stroke
  • N/V, altered LOC, seizures common
Treatment
  • NO tPA! (will worsen bleeding)
  • BP management, ICP control
  • Surgery if indicated (clipping, coiling)
Critical: CT Before Treatment!

A non-contrast CT head must be obtained BEFORE administering tPA to rule out hemorrhagic stroke. The CT must show NO hemorrhage before thrombolytics can be given. CT is fast and readily available, making it the first-line imaging for acute stroke.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
CT Scan Before Clot-Busting Medication!
A CT scan (head imaging) MUST happen before giving tPA (alteplase, the clot-dissolving drug). Why? We need to confirm it's a clot blocking blood flow, not a bleed. If the patient is already bleeding in their brain and we give a clot-buster, we'll make the bleeding much worseâ€”potentially fatal. Memory trick: "No bleed on the screen? Proceed. See red? Stop instead."

Transient Ischemic Attack (TIA)

A TIA is a temporary episode of neurological dysfunction caused by focal brain, spinal cord, or retinal ischemia, without acute infarction. Symptoms typically resolve within 1 hour (most within minutes).

NCLEX Alert: TIA is a Warning Sign!

TIA is often called a "warning stroke" or "mini-stroke." 10-15% of TIA patients will have a stroke within 90 days, with the highest risk in the first 48 hours. TIA requires urgent evaluation and risk factor managementâ€”it should never be dismissed!

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Ischemic (87%) = clot â†’ tPA/thrombectomy. Hemorrhagic (13%) = bleed â†’ BP control, possible surgery. TIA = 'mini-stroke' with symptoms resolving in < 24 hours â€” treat as a warning sign of impending stroke.

FAST Assessment Tool

Rapid stroke recognition for everyone

The FAST assessment is a validated, easy-to-remember tool for rapid stroke recognition. It can be used by anyoneâ€”healthcare providers, patients, or bystandersâ€”to quickly identify stroke symptoms and act fast.

The FAST Assessment

F

Face Drooping

Ask the person to smile. Does one side of the face droop or feel numb?

A

Arm Weakness

Ask them to raise both arms. Does one arm drift downward? Is one arm weak or numb?

S

Speech Difficulty

Ask them to repeat a simple phrase. Is speech slurred or strange?

T

Time to Call 911

If ANY of these signs are present, call 911 immediately. Note the time symptoms started.

Additional Stroke Symptoms (BE-FAST)

The expanded BE-FAST adds two additional components to catch more strokes:

Letter Component Assessment
B Balance Sudden loss of balance, coordination, or dizziness
E Eyes Sudden vision changes, blurry vision, or vision loss in one or both eyes
F Face Facial droopâ€”ask to smile and observe for asymmetry
A Arms Arm driftâ€”hold both arms out, palms up, eyes closed for 10 seconds
S Speech Slurred speechâ€”ask to repeat "The sky is blue in Cincinnati"
T Time Time is criticalâ€”call 911 and note when symptoms started

NIH Stroke Scale (NIHSS)

The NIHSS is a standardized neurological examination used to quantify stroke severity. Scores range from 0-42, with higher scores indicating more severe deficits.

NIHSS Score Stroke Severity Clinical Significance
0 No stroke symptoms Normal exam
1-4 Minor stroke Good prognosis, may not need intervention
5-15 Moderate stroke tPA candidate, moderate disability risk
16-20 Moderate-severe stroke Significant disability likely
21-42 Severe stroke Poor prognosis, high mortality risk
Clinical Pearl: NIHSS Components

The NIHSS assesses 11 items: level of consciousness (LOC), horizontal eye movement, visual fields, facial palsy, motor arm, motor leg, limb ataxia, sensory, language, dysarthria, and extinction/inattention. Nurses should be certified to administer the NIHSS in stroke centers.

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: BE FAST saves lives: Balance, Eyes, Face, Arm, Speech, Time. Document exact time of symptom onset (or 'last known well') â€” this determines tPA eligibility.

tPA Inclusion & Exclusion Criteria

Who qualifies for thrombolytic therapy?

Tissue Plasminogen Activator (tPA/alteplase) is a thrombolytic agent that dissolves blood clots. It's the standard of care for eligible acute ischemic stroke patients, but strict criteria must be met due to bleeding risks.

Door-to-Needle Goal: < 60 Minutes

Stroke centers aim for a door-to-needle time of less than 60 minutes. This means from the moment the patient arrives until tPA infusion begins. Every 15-minute delay in treatment reduces the chance of a good outcome.

Inclusion Criteria (Give tPA)

  • Clinical diagnosis of ischemic stroke with measurable neurological deficit
  • Symptom onset < 4.5 hours (or last known well time)
  • Age â‰¥ 18 years old
  • CT scan shows NO hemorrhage
  • Blood pressure controllable to < 185/110 mmHg
  • Patient/family consents to treatment

Exclusion Criteria (Do NOT Give tPA)

  • Evidence of intracranial hemorrhage on CT
  • Symptom onset > 4.5 hours or unknown time
  • Prior intracranial hemorrhage
  • Major surgery or trauma within 14 days
  • GI or urinary bleeding within 21 days
  • Arterial puncture at non-compressible site within 7 days

Additional Contraindications

Category Absolute Contraindications
Coagulation INR > 1.7, PT > 15 sec, platelets < 100,000, heparin within 48h with elevated PTT
Blood Pressure SBP > 185 or DBP > 110 mmHg that cannot be controlled
Blood Glucose < 50 mg/dL (correct hypoglycemia firstâ€”may mimic stroke!)
CT Findings Multilobar infarction (> 1/3 cerebral hemisphere)
History Stroke or head trauma within 3 months, intracranial neoplasm
Medications Current use of direct thrombin inhibitors or factor Xa inhibitors

Extended Window (3-4.5 hours) Additional Exclusions

For tPA Between 3-4.5 Hours, ALSO Exclude:
  • Age > 80 years
  • NIHSS score > 25 (severe stroke)
  • History of both stroke AND diabetes
  • Taking oral anticoagulants regardless of INR

tPA Administration & Monitoring

Parameter Details

No Invasive Procedures No arterial lines, central lines, NG tubes, or Foley for 24 hours (if possible)
Complication Alert: Hemorrhagic Transformation

The most serious complication of tPA is intracranial hemorrhage, occurring in about 6% of patients. Monitor for:

  • Acute neurological decline (â†“ LOC, new deficits)
  • Severe headache
  • New hypertension, nausea/vomiting
  • STOP the infusion immediately and get STAT CT if suspected!

Priority Nursing Interventions

Evidence-based stroke care

Nursing care during the acute stroke phase focuses on rapid assessment, monitoring for complications, preventing secondary injury, and early rehabilitation. The first 24-72 hours are critical.

Immediate Assessment & Stabilization

Airway & Breathing

Assess airway patency, O2 sat (maintain > 94%), and respiratory status. Position HOB 30Â° unless contraindicated. Suction PRN for decreased consciousness.

Neurological Status

Perform NIHSS and GCS assessments. Monitor for deterioration (new deficits, decreased LOC). Check pupils PERRLA. Document baseline thoroughly.

Vital Signs & Cardiac

Continuous cardiac monitoring (A-fib detection). Strict BP monitoring per stroke protocols. Target temperature < 38Â°C. Treat fever aggressively.

Lab Monitoring

Blood glucose q6h (target 140-180 mg/dL). Monitor coagulation studies. BMP for electrolyte imbalances. Troponin for cardiac involvement.

Blood Pressure Management

Scenario BP Target Rationale
Before tPA administration < 185/110 mmHg Required for tPA eligibility; reduces bleeding risk
During/after tPA (24 hrs) < 180/105 mmHg Strict control during thrombolytic therapy
Ischemic stroke, no tPA Generally < 220/120 mmHg Permissive hypertension maintains cerebral perfusion
Hemorrhagic stroke SBP < 140 mmHg Aggressive control limits hematoma expansion
NCLEX Alert: Permissive Hypertension

In ischemic stroke without tPA, we allow higher BP (up to 220/120) because cerebral autoregulation is impaired, and higher BP may maintain perfusion to the penumbra. Don't aggressively lower BP unless giving tPA or it's extremely elevated!

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Permissive Hypertension (Let the BP Stay High!)
Don't panic if your stroke patient's blood pressure is elevated! If they're NOT getting clot-busting medication (tPA), we actually ALLOW the BP to stay highâ€”up to 220/120 mmHg. Why? The brain needs that extra pressure to push blood around the blockage and keep brain tissue alive. Only treat aggressively if they're receiving tPA or if BP is causing other organ damage.

Dysphagia & Aspiration Prevention

NPO Until Swallow Screen!

All stroke patients must be NPO until a swallowing assessment is completed by a trained professional. Dysphagia occurs in 37-78% of stroke patients and significantly increases aspiration pneumonia risk. Until cleared:

  • Keep NPOâ€”no food, fluids, or oral medications
  • Maintain HOB elevated 30-45Â°
  • Have suction equipment at bedside
  • Provide meticulous oral care
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
NPO First (Nothing By Mouth!)
Keep your stroke patient NPO (nothing by mouth) until they pass a swallow test! Stroke often damages the nerves that control swallowingâ€”up to 78% of stroke patients have trouble swallowing (dysphagia). Even a tiny sip of water can go into their lungs instead of their stomach, causing aspiration pneumonia. Wait for the speech therapist (SLP) to clear them before giving any food, water, or oral medications.

Complications to Monitor

Increased ICP

Monitor for headache, vomiting, decreased LOC, Cushing's triad. Keep HOB elevated 30Â°. Avoid neck flexion. Report changes immediately.

Seizures

Occur in 2-10% of stroke patients. Ensure seizure precautions. Prophylactic anticonvulsants not routine but may be used in hemorrhagic stroke.

DVT/PE

High risk due to immobility. SCDs on admission. Prophylactic anticoagulation when appropriate (usually 24-48h post-tPA or once stable).

Skin Breakdown

Implement pressure injury prevention. Turn q2h. Use pressure-relieving surfaces. Assess Braden score. Keep skin dry and clean.

Drug Use Key Nursing Considerations

Patient Education: ABCD of Stroke Prevention
  • Aspirin/Antiplatelet or Anticoagulant therapy as prescribed
  • Blood pressure control (know your numbers!)
  • Cholesterol management with statins
  • Diabetes control and Diet modification

Also emphasize: smoking cessation, exercise, limiting alcohol, and recognizing stroke symptoms!
