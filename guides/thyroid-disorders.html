01

What actually gets you

This topic looks like a memorisation problem and is not. Almost all of it comes off two ideas: TSH runs opposite to the thyroid, and hyper and hypo are mirror images. Get those and the volume collapses.

You read TSH as though it were thyroid hormone

A high TSH means the thyroid is underactive, not overactive. TSH is the pituitary shouting for more hormone, so it goes up when there is not enough.

Get this backwards and you get every lab question backwards with it.

You memorised two symptom lists instead of one

Hypothyroid and hyperthyroid are opposites at every point. Cold and hot. Gaining and losing. Constipated and loose. Slow and fast.

Learn one list properly and the other is free. Learning both separately is twice the work and twice the confusion.

You warmed the myxedema patient up

They are hypothermic, so the instinct is a heating blanket. That instinct can kill them.

Warming the skin dilates peripheral vessels, blood pools in the limbs, and a heart already running on nothing cannot keep the pressure up. Passive rewarming only.

You forgot which post-op complication comes first

Post-thyroidectomy there are four things that can go wrong, and only one of them kills in minutes.

A hematoma in the neck compresses the trachea. Airway comes before voice, before calcium, before anything.

Bottom Line: Two rules do most of the work. TSH reads backwards, and hyper is hypo flipped. Then learn the two emergencies and the surgery.

The four things

For every condition below, you should be able to say four things with the book closed: the one-sentence definition, the clue you would see in a patient, the priority nursing action, and the first-line drug with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

03

Why the lab reads backwards

The thyroid is a butterfly-shaped gland in the front of the neck, wrapped around the trachea just below the larynx. It sets the metabolic rate of almost every organ.

It makes three hormones worth knowing.

  • T4, thyroxine. About 90 percent of what the gland releases. It is a prohormone, converted to the active form in the tissues, and it has the longer half-life. This is what most labs measure.
  • T3, triiodothyronine. The active form, three to five times more potent than T4. Only a tenth of it comes straight from the gland; the rest is converted from T4 elsewhere.
  • Calcitonin. From the parafollicular cells. It lowers blood calcium by damping osteoclasts, and it matters in thyroid cancer and after surgery.

The loop

  1. 1
    The hypothalamus notices the level is low. It releases TRH, thyrotropin-releasing hormone.
  2. 2
    The anterior pituitary answers. TRH makes it release TSH into the blood.
  3. 3
    The thyroid responds. TSH drives production of T3 and T4. Iodine is the raw material, which is why iodine matters so much later on.
  4. 4
    The rising level shuts the loop down. T3 and T4 inhibit both the hypothalamus and the pituitary, so TRH and TSH fall again.
That is the whole reason TSH is inverted

TSH is not a measure of thyroid hormone. It is a measure of how hard the pituitary is having to ask.

An underactive thyroid means the pituitary shouts, so TSH is high. An overactive thyroid means the pituitary goes quiet, so TSH is low.

High TSH means hypothyroid. Low TSH means hyperthyroid. Once you understand why, you cannot get it backwards.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The TSH Inverse Rule
TSH measures how loudly the pituitary is asking, not how much hormone there is. A weak thyroid makes it shout, so TSH climbs. A strong one makes it go quiet, so TSH falls. That is also why TSH is the best screening test; it moves first.

The same rule, applied to dosing

  • TSH still high on levothyroxine. The pituitary is still asking, so the dose is too low. Increase it.
  • TSH gone too low on levothyroxine. You have made them mildly hyperthyroid. Decrease it.
  • The goal. Titrate to a normal TSH, 0.4 to 4.0 mIU/L, and recheck 6 to 8 weeks after each change.
Bottom Line: T4 is the prohormone, T3 is the active one. TSH is the pituitary asking, so it runs opposite to thyroid function, and that governs both diagnosis and dosing.

The row that breaks the rule, and why

Look at the last row. Low TSH with low T4 does not fit the inverse rule, and that is exactly the point.

The rule assumes the pituitary is working. If the pituitary is the broken part, it cannot shout even when the level is low. Low and low together means the problem is above the thyroid, not in it.

This is also why TSH is the first test but not the only one. Free T4 confirms and quantifies whatever the TSH suggested.

05

Hypothyroidism

Too little thyroid hormone, so every process slows. It is the commonest thyroid disorder and affects women five to eight times more often than men.

Think of the body in hibernation. Heart rate, gut, mood, reflexes and heat production all drop together.

Causes

  • Hashimoto's thyroiditis. The leading cause in the United States, and autoimmune.
  • Thyroidectomy or radioactive iodine, so the treatment caused it.
  • Iodine deficiency, which is the leading cause worldwide.
  • Medications: lithium, amiodarone, interferon.
  • Pituitary failure, which is the rare secondary form.

Hashimoto's thyroiditis

An autoimmune condition where anti-thyroid peroxidase and anti-thyroglobulin antibodies gradually destroy thyroid tissue.

  • The most common autoimmune disease, and the commonest cause of hypothyroidism in the US
  • Gradual. It can take months or years before symptoms appear
  • Often starts with a painless, diffuse goiter
  • As destruction continues the gland shrinks and output falls
  • Strongly genetic, and often keeps company with other autoimmune conditions such as Type 1 diabetes, celiac disease and rheumatoid arthritis
  • Diagnosed by a raised TSH with positive anti-TPO antibodies

Levothyroxine

The rules that get tested

What it is
Synthetic T4, converted to T3 in the tissues
When
30 to 60 minutes before breakfast, empty stomach
Monitoring
TSH every 6 to 8 weeks until stable
How long
For life. Never stopped abruptly
Why the timing rules exist

Food cuts absorption by up to 40 percent, which is why it goes in before breakfast on an empty stomach.

Calcium, iron and antacids bind it directly in the gut, so they are separated by at least 4 hours. This is not fussiness; a patient taking their levothyroxine with their calcium supplement is effectively under-dosed.

It also increases the effect of warfarin, by speeding up the breakdown of clotting factors. Starting or changing levothyroxine in a warfarin patient means watching the INR and expecting the warfarin dose to come down.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Levothyroxine Teaching
Empty stomach, 30 to 60 minutes before breakfast. Four hours away from calcium, iron and antacids. Watch the INR if they take warfarin. Never stopped. And teach them what too much feels like: racing heart, anxiety, weight loss, tremor.
Bottom Line: Everything slow. Hashimoto's is the commonest cause in the US. Levothyroxine on an empty stomach, away from calcium and iron, with an eye on warfarin, for life.
06

Myxedema coma

The severe end of hypothyroidism, and a genuine emergency. The name says coma but the patient may only be confused; altered mental status is always there. Mortality is 30 to 60 percent even treated.

What sets it off

  • Stopping levothyroxine abruptly
  • Infection, which is the commonest trigger
  • Cold exposure
  • Surgery, trauma, or other physiological stress
  • Sedatives, opioids or anesthesia, which depress the nervous system further
  • Stroke, heart failure, or a gastrointestinal bleed
Recognising it
  • Hypothermia, often under 95 F, and low enough that a standard thermometer may not register it
  • Falling consciousness, from lethargy through stupor to coma
  • Respiratory depression, with hypoventilation and CO2 retention
  • Severe bradycardia and hypotension
  • Hyponatremia, dilutional, because free water is not excreted properly
  • Hypoglycemia
  • Non-pitting edema of the face, hands and limbs

If the thermometer reads low in a patient who seems flat and slow, take the temperature again with something that goes lower.

Treatment

  1. 1
    Airway and ventilation. They may need intubation and mechanical ventilation for the respiratory depression. Follow the ABGs.
  2. 2
    IV levothyroxine, loading dose. It has to be IV, because the gut is not absorbing. Some protocols add IV liothyronine for a faster effect.
  3. 3
    IV hydrocortisone, before or with the thyroid hormone. Severe hypothyroidism often sits alongside adrenal insufficiency, and giving thyroid hormone alone can tip them into adrenal crisis.
  4. 4
    Passive rewarming only. Warm blankets and a warm room. No heating blankets, no warmed IV fluids. Aim for no more than 1 F an hour.
  5. 5
    Support everything else. Fluids cautiously, because of the hyponatremia. Dextrose for the low glucose. Treat the trigger, usually an infection. Continuous cardiac monitoring.
Why passive rewarming, and only passive

Skin warmed from outside dilates its blood vessels. In a healthy person that is fine; the heart raises output to compensate.

This heart cannot. It is bradycardic and running on almost no thyroid hormone. So blood pools in the newly dilated limbs, the pressure drops, and you have caused shock while trying to fix hypothermia.

Warm blankets and time. The hormone will restore the temperature from the inside.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Passive Rewarming Only
External heat dilates the peripheral vessels, and this heart has no reserve to compensate. Blood pools, the pressure falls, and you have caused the shock. Warm blankets and a warm room, no more than 1 F an hour. Steroids go in before or with the thyroid hormone.
Bottom Line: Cold, slow, and barely conscious. IV levothyroxine with IV hydrocortisone, and passive rewarming only. Find the trigger, which is usually infection.
07

Hyperthyroidism

Discrimination axis: slow and cold, or fast and hot.

Too much thyroid hormone, so everything accelerates. This is the mirror of section 05, point for point.

Hypothyroid: low and slow
  • Bradycardia
  • Weight gain
  • Cold intolerance
  • Constipation
  • Fatigue, lethargy, depression
  • Dry skin, brittle hair and nails
  • Heavy periods
  • Slowed reflexes
  • TSH high, T3 and T4 low
Hyperthyroid: high and fast
  • Tachycardia, palpitations, atrial fibrillation
  • Weight loss despite eating more
  • Heat intolerance, sweating
  • Diarrhea
  • Anxiety, irritability, insomnia, tremor
  • Warm moist smooth skin
  • Light or absent periods
  • Brisk reflexes
  • TSH low, T3 and T4 high
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Learn One List, Get the Other Free
Every row above is an exact opposite. Cold or hot, gaining or losing, constipated or loose, slow pulse or fast, flat or anxious. Learn the hypothyroid column properly and flip it. On the exam you are looking for the cluster, not the individual symptom.

Graves' disease

The commonest cause of hyperthyroidism, at 60 to 80 percent of cases. It is autoimmune, and the antibodies are the interesting part.

Thyroid-stimulating immunoglobulins imitate TSH and lock onto the same receptor. So the gland is being told to produce constantly, by something the negative feedback loop cannot switch off.

The findings unique to Graves'
  • Exophthalmos. Bulging eyes, from inflammation and swelling behind them. This does not happen in other forms of hyperthyroidism, and it may not resolve once the thyroid is treated
  • Diffuse goiter. Smooth and symmetric, rather than lumpy
  • Pretibial myxedema. Thick waxy non-pitting swelling over the shins, and despite the name it is a hyperthyroid finding
  • Thyroid bruit. A whooshing sound over the gland, from the increased blood flow
What the Graves' patient needs to hear
  • Eye care. Sunglasses for the light sensitivity, artificial tears, head of bed raised at night to reduce the swelling, and tape the lids shut at night if they do not close
  • A calm cool environment, because they cannot tolerate heat
  • A high-calorie diet, to offset the weight they are burning off
  • No caffeine or stimulants, which make the tachycardia and anxiety worse
  • Frequent rest, for the fatigue and muscle weakness

Beta-blockers, and what they do not do

Propranolol is given early, and it works fast. It settles the tachycardia, the tremor, the anxiety and the palpitations.

But it does not lower thyroid hormone at all. It only blocks the effects. It is a bridge while the antithyroid drug takes hold, never the treatment on its own.

Bottom Line: Everything fast. Graves' is the commonest cause, and the exophthalmos is unique to it. Beta-blockers control symptoms only.
08

Thyroid storm

The severe end of hyperthyroidism, and the mirror of myxedema coma. Mortality is 20 to 30 percent even with aggressive treatment.

What sets it off

  • Infection, the commonest trigger
  • Surgery, especially thyroid surgery on a patient who was not made euthyroid first
  • Stopping antithyroid medication abruptly
  • DKA or another metabolic stress
  • Trauma, burns, or stroke
  • Iodinated contrast dye, or a large iodine load
  • Labour and delivery
Recognising it
  • Fever over 104 F. This is the hallmark
  • Severe tachycardia, over 130 to 140, and often into atrial fibrillation
  • Delirium, agitation, psychosis, which can progress to seizures and coma
  • High-output heart failure, from the sustained rate
  • Severe nausea, vomiting and diarrhea
  • Extreme sweating and dehydration
  • Systolic hypertension with a wide pulse pressure

The two emergencies, side by side

Discrimination axis: the temperature.

Myxedema coma: the cold one
  • Hypothermia, under 95 F
  • Bradycardia and hypotension
  • Lethargy, then stupor, then coma
  • Respiratory depression
  • Hyponatremia and hypoglycemia

Treatment: IV levothyroxine with hydrocortisone, and passive rewarming.

Mortality: 30 to 60 percent.

Thyroid storm: the hot one
  • Fever over 104 F
  • Severe tachycardia and hypertension
  • Delirium, then seizures, then coma
  • High-output heart failure
  • Severe dehydration

Treatment: PTU, iodine, beta-blockers, steroids, cooling.

Mortality: 20 to 30 percent.

Treatment: four blocks

  1. 1
    Block new synthesis. PTU or methimazole. PTU is often preferred here, because it also blocks the conversion of T4 to T3 out in the tissues. A loading dose, then frequent maintenance doses.
  2. 2
    Block release, an hour later. Iodine as Lugol's solution or SSKI, to stop the gland releasing the hormone it has already made.
  3. 3
    Block the adrenergic effects. IV propranolol, to bring down the rate, the pressure and the tremor. It also blocks some peripheral conversion. Watch for heart failure.
  4. 4
    Block peripheral conversion. IV hydrocortisone or dexamethasone, which also covers the relative adrenal insufficiency the hypermetabolic state creates.
  5. 5
    Cool them. Cooling blankets, ice packs, acetaminophen. Not aspirin.
  6. 6
    Support everything else. IV fluids with dextrose for the dehydration and glucose use. Oxygen. Continuous cardiac monitoring in intensive care. Treat the trigger.
The hour between step 1 and step 2

Iodine is the raw material for thyroid hormone. Give it to a gland that is still synthesising and you have just handed it more substrate.

PTU or methimazole shuts synthesis down first. An hour later the iodine can do its other job, which is blocking release of what is already stored. Order matters, and the reason is the whole point of the question.

No aspirin for this fever

Aspirin displaces thyroid hormone from the proteins that carry it in the blood. Displaced hormone is free hormone, and free hormone is the active kind.

So aspirin for the fever raises the level of the thing causing the storm. Acetaminophen only.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Four Blocks, in Order
Block synthesis with PTU. Block release with iodine, an hour later and never first. Block the adrenergic effects with a beta-blocker. Block peripheral conversion with steroids. Then cool them with acetaminophen, never aspirin.
Bottom Line: Fever over 104 F, rate over 130, delirium. Four blocks in order, iodine an hour after the antithyroid drug, and no aspirin.
09

The drugs

Discrimination axis: methimazole or PTU.

Methimazole, the default
  • Once daily, so adherence is better
  • Fewer side effects overall
  • Lower risk of liver injury
  • Preferred for everyone except first-trimester pregnancy
  • Blocks hormone synthesis
  • Does not block T4 to T3 conversion
  • Teratogenic in the first trimester
PTU, for two situations
  • Three times daily, so adherence is worse
  • More side effects
  • Black box warning for liver failure
  • Preferred in the first trimester of pregnancy
  • Blocks hormone synthesis
  • Also blocks T4 to T3 conversion
  • Preferred in thyroid storm, for that second mechanism
The axis that separates them

Methimazole is better in almost every way, so it is the default. PTU exists for exactly two situations, and both come from a single extra property each.

First trimester, because methimazole causes specific birth defects and PTU does not. After that, switch back, because PTU's liver risk outweighs it.

Thyroid storm, because PTU also blocks peripheral conversion, and in a storm you want both mechanisms.

First trimester is PTU. Second and third are methimazole. Both cross the placenta, so use the lowest dose that works, and both are acceptable while breastfeeding.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
PTU in the First Trimester Only
Methimazole is linked to aplasia cutis and choanal atresia in the first trimester, so PTU covers that window. After it, switch to methimazole, because PTU carries a higher risk of liver failure. First trimester PTU, then methimazole.
Agranulocytosis

Both drugs can wipe out the white cell count. It is rare and it is life-threatening.

Teach the patient to report a sore throat, a fever or mouth ulcers immediately. Those are infections happening because there are no neutrophils left, and they arrive before anyone thinks to check a count.

Get a baseline CBC with differential before starting, and repeat it if symptoms appear. Below 1000, stop the drug.

Iodine, as a drug

  • What it does: temporarily blocks the release of stored thyroid hormone. Used before thyroidectomy and in thyroid storm.
  • How it goes in: diluted in water or juice, because the taste is strongly metallic. Through a straw, which keeps it off the teeth and the tongue. With food, to settle the stomach.
  • Side effects: a brassy metallic taste, gastrointestinal upset, hypersensitivity.
  • The timing rule: in thyroid storm it goes an hour after the antithyroid drug, never before.

Radioactive iodine

The thyroid concentrates iodine, so radioactive iodine collects there and destroys the gland while leaving everything else alone. It is a definitive treatment.

Goiter and nodules

A goiter is any enlarged thyroid, whatever the cause. It can come with an underactive gland, an overactive one, or a perfectly normal one, which is why the size tells you nothing about the function.

TypeCauseThyroid functionTreatment
EndemicIodine deficiency, the commonest cause worldwideUsually hypothyroidIodine, and levothyroxine
Graves'TSI antibodies driving the glandHyperthyroidAntithyroid drugs, RAI, or surgery
Hashimoto'sAutoimmune inflammationHypothyroid eventuallyLevothyroxine, which may shrink it
MultinodularSeveral nodules togetherVariableDepends on function. Surgery if it is pressing on something
What a big gland presses on

The thyroid sits wrapped around the trachea, with the esophagus behind it and the recurrent laryngeal nerve running alongside. So the compression findings are predictable from the anatomy.

  • Visible or palpable swelling at the front of the neck
  • Trouble swallowing, if it presses on the esophagus
  • Breathlessness or stridor, if it presses on the trachea
  • Hoarseness, if it presses on the recurrent laryngeal nerve
  • A sense of fullness or tightness in the neck

Nodules

Nodules are extremely common. Ultrasound finds them in up to half the population, and 90 to 95 percent are benign. All of them still get evaluated.

  • TSH first. If it is low the nodule may be a hot one, producing hormone on its own, and a scan follows. Hot nodules are almost always benign.
  • Ultrasound. Size, whether it is solid or cystic, calcifications, margins. It also guides the needle.
  • Fine needle aspiration. The gold standard for deciding whether it is cancer. Done for nodules over 1 cm or with suspicious features on ultrasound.
The features that raise concern
  • Radiation to the head or neck, especially in childhood
  • Family history of thyroid cancer or MEN syndrome
  • A nodule that is growing quickly
  • Fixed, hard, and not tender
  • Enlarged lymph nodes in the neck
  • Male sex. Nodules are commoner in women, but more of the ones in men turn out to be malignant
  • Under 20 or over 60
Worth telling the patient

Papillary carcinoma is 80 percent of thyroid cancer and has a cure rate over 95 percent. Treatment is usually total thyroidectomy, then RAI ablation, then lifelong levothyroxine.

A patient waiting on a biopsy result has usually already decided this is the worst news possible. It is one of the most treatable cancers there is, and saying so is accurate rather than kind.

Bottom Line: Goiter size says nothing about function. Most nodules are benign, fine needle aspiration decides, and papillary carcinoma has an excellent outlook.
11

After the thyroid comes out

Discrimination axis: which complication kills fastest.

Four things go wrong after a thyroidectomy, and they are all anatomy. Learn where things sit around the gland and the list writes itself.

Before surgery

  • Make them euthyroid first. Antithyroid drugs and beta-blockers until the levels normalise. Operating on a hyperthyroid patient risks thyroid storm on the table.
  • Lugol's solution for 10 to 14 days beforehand, to reduce the blood supply to the gland and cut surgical bleeding.
  • Record a baseline voice, so you have something to compare to.
  • Teach them to support the neck when they move.
  • Make sure a tracheostomy tray and calcium gluconate are available.

Positioning afterwards

  • Semi-Fowler's, head up 30 to 45 degrees, to reduce swelling and let things drain.
  • Support the head and neck with pillows. No flexion, extension or rotation.
  • Teach them to put their hands behind their head when sitting up, so the neck muscles do not take the strain.

The four complications, in priority order

  1. 1
    Hemorrhage, and the airway behind it. A hematoma in a closed neck compresses the trachea within minutes. Look for swelling at the incision, a feeling of tightness, difficulty breathing or stridor. Check behind the neck, because blood pools posteriorly and the front dressing can look clean. If the airway is going: call the surgeon now, open the sutures at the bedside if it is that urgent, and prepare for intubation.
  2. 2
    Laryngeal nerve damage. The recurrent laryngeal nerve runs right alongside the gland. Damage gives hoarseness, a weak breathy voice, trouble swallowing and aspiration risk. Assess the voice every 2 to 4 hours by asking them to say eee. Some hoarseness from the tube is expected; hoarseness past 2 weeks suggests real injury. Bilateral damage is rare and obstructs the airway completely.
  3. 3
    Hypocalcemia, from the parathyroids. The four parathyroid glands sit on the back of the thyroid and can be bruised or removed with it. Calcium falls, usually 24 to 72 hours later, and the patient goes into tetany. Treatment is IV calcium gluconate, which is why it lives at the bedside.
  4. 4
    Thyroid storm. If they were not made euthyroid first, or if handling the gland dumped stored hormone into the circulation. This is the complication that preoperative preparation exists to prevent.
The two hypocalcemia signs
  • Chvostek's sign. Tap the facial nerve in front of the ear, and the facial muscles on that side twitch
  • Trousseau's sign. Inflate a blood pressure cuff above systolic for 3 minutes, and the hand goes into carpal spasm
  • Tingling around the mouth and in the fingers and toes
  • Muscle cramps, spasms, and eventually seizures

Both signs can appear before the patient reports anything, which is why they are checked rather than waited for.

Four things at the bedside, on every one of these patients
  • Tracheostomy tray, for emergency airway access if a hematoma obstructs
  • Suction, for secretions and blood
  • Oxygen
  • IV calcium gluconate, for tetany
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Airway, Bleeding, Calcium, Communication
Airway is first, always; look for stridor, dyspnea and neck swelling. Bleeding second, and check behind the neck as well as the dressing. Calcium third, with Chvostek's and Trousseau's. Communication fourth, assessing the voice every 2 to 4 hours. Any of the first three is an emergency.

Routine care

  • Vital signs frequently, every 15 to 30 minutes at first
  • Check the incision and dressing, and check behind the neck
  • Voice quality every 2 to 4 hours
  • Calcium levels, plus Chvostek's and Trousseau's, every shift
  • Head elevated and neck supported
  • Manage the drain if there is one, usually a Jackson-Pratt
  • Gentle neck range of motion once the surgeon clears it
  • After a total thyroidectomy, lifelong levothyroxine
Bottom Line: Airway first, because a neck hematoma obstructs in minutes. Then bleeding behind the neck, then calcium at 24 to 72 hours, then the voice.

Iodine and food

Iodine is the raw material for thyroid hormone. Too little and the gland cannot make enough; too much can also disrupt it. That is why it appears in both the diet advice and the drug list.

Where iodine comes from

  • Iodised salt, the main source in the United States
  • Seafood, including fish, shrimp and seaweed
  • Dairy: milk, yoghurt, cheese
  • Eggs
  • Bread and grains, where iodate conditioners are used

Goitrogens

Goitrogens interfere with hormone production and can contribute to a goiter. Cooking largely deactivates them, which changes the advice considerably.

Do not tell them to stop eating broccoli

Cruciferous vegetables are among the most nutritious things a patient eats, and the goitrogenic effect only matters in large raw quantities in someone already iodine deficient or hypothyroid.

Steaming or boiling knocks most of it out. The advice is moderation, cook where possible, and get enough iodine. Blanket avoidance costs the patient more than it gains.

By condition

  • Hypothyroidism: adequate iodine. Limit large raw goitrogen loads. High fibre for the constipation. Watch the weight, since the metabolism has slowed.
  • Hyperthyroidism: high calorie and high protein, to offset what they are burning. Avoid excess iodine. No caffeine or stimulants.
  • Before RAI: a low-iodine diet for 1 to 2 weeks, which makes the thyroid hungrier for iodine and so increases uptake of the radioactive dose.
Bottom Line: Iodine builds the hormone. Goitrogens interfere, but cooking mostly fixes that, so counsel moderation rather than avoidance. Low iodine before RAI.
13

Children are not small adults

Congenital hypothyroidism

One of the most preventable causes of intellectual disability there is, which is why the screening is mandatory.

  • Newborn screening is required in all 50 states. TSH by heel stick at 24 to 48 hours of life
  • Untreated it causes cretinism: intellectual disability, short stature, delayed development
  • Treatment starts within 2 weeks of birth. Levothyroxine as a suspension, or crushed into breast milk or formula
  • The usual cause is thyroid dysgenesis, an absent or underdeveloped gland
  • Early signs: prolonged jaundice, large fontanelles, a large tongue, poor feeding, constipation, low temperature, sleeping too much
  • Lifelong replacement

The window matters more here than almost anywhere else in nursing. Damage done before treatment starts does not reverse.

Older children and adolescents
  • Hashimoto's is the commonest acquired thyroid disorder in children, often appearing in adolescence
  • It can cause growth failure and delayed puberty, so watch the growth chart
  • Graves' occurs in children too. Methimazole is first line; RAI and surgery are held back for refractory cases
  • Levothyroxine dosing is weight-based and proportionally higher than in adults, because children run faster metabolisms
  • Adherence is a real problem in adolescence. Involve them in managing it themselves
  • A drop in school performance may be the first sign anybody notices
  • Thyroid nodules in children are far more likely to be malignant than in adults, 20 to 25 percent against 5 to 10. Biopsy promptly
Bottom Line: Newborn TSH screening is mandatory, and treatment starts within 2 weeks. In older children, hypothyroidism shows as growth failure. Nodules in children get biopsied quickly.
