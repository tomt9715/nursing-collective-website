Respiratory Anatomy Review

Understanding normal respiratory structure helps explain why Mycobacterium tuberculosis has a predilection for the lungs and how the disease spreads through the respiratory system.

Key Respiratory Structures Affected by TB

  • Alveoli: Primary site of TB infection - bacilli are inhaled and deposit in alveolar spaces where they are engulfed by macrophages
  • Bronchi & Bronchioles: Airways can become involved as disease progresses, leading to productive cough
  • Hilar Lymph Nodes: Drain the lungs and become enlarged during primary TB infection (Ghon complex)
  • Pleura: Can become involved leading to pleural effusion and pleurisy
Anatomy Connection

TB typically affects the upper lobes of the lungs, especially the apical and posterior segments. Why? The upper lobes have:

  • Higher oxygen tension (TB bacilli are obligate aerobes)
  • Decreased lymphatic drainage
  • Less blood flow (less immune cell delivery)

Normal Host Defense Mechanisms

Mucociliary Escalator Upper airways TB droplet nuclei are so small (1-5 Î¼m) they bypass and reach alveoli
Alveolar Macrophages Alveoli TB survives and replicates INSIDE macrophages
Cell-Mediated Immunity Systemic TB forms granulomas that wall off infection but don't kill all bacilli

TB Pathophysiology

Tuberculosis is caused by Mycobacterium tuberculosis, a slow-growing, acid-fast bacillus. Understanding the pathophysiology helps explain the clinical course, diagnostic approach, and treatment duration.

Transmission

Critical: Airborne Transmission

TB spreads through AIRBORNE DROPLET NUCLEI - tiny particles (1-5 micrometers) that remain suspended in air for hours. A single cough can produce 3,000 droplet nuclei!

  • Generated when infectious person coughs, sneezes, speaks, or sings
  • Can travel on air currents throughout a room or building
  • Inhaled directly into the alveoli

Disease Progression

From Exposure to Disease

1
Inhalation & Initial Infection

TB bacilli are inhaled and reach the alveoli. Alveolar macrophages engulf the bacteria, but TB has a waxy cell wall that prevents destruction.

2
Replication Inside Macrophages

TB multiplies INSIDE the macrophages for 2-12 weeks. Bacteria spread through lymphatics to hilar lymph nodes and potentially to bloodstream.

3
Immune Response & Granuloma Formation

T-lymphocytes activate macrophages. Granulomas form around infected cells - these are walled-off clusters of immune cells containing TB bacilli. The center becomes caseous (cheese-like) necrosis.

4
Latency or Active Disease

In 90% of people, immune response contains infection = LTBI (latent TB). In 10%, primary infection progresses to active disease. Later reactivation can occur if immunity weakens.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Understanding Granulomas
Granulomas are the hallmark of TB pathology! Think of them as tiny "prisons" the immune system builds around TB bacteria. The granuloma has layers: a center of dead, cheese-like tissue (caseous necrosis) surrounded by macrophages, giant cells, and T-lymphocytes. This containment is good - it's why most people with TB exposure never get sick. But here's the catch: TB can remain dormant inside granulomas for decades, then reactivate if the immune system weakens. This is why we treat latent TB - to eliminate these sleeping bacteria before they can escape.

NCLEX Alert: HIV + TB

HIV-positive patients are 20-30x more likely to develop active TB! TB is a leading cause of death among people with HIV. Always test HIV patients for TB and vice versa. TB can present atypically in HIV - lower lung involvement and extrapulmonary sites more common.

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Primary infection â†’ macrophage response â†’ granuloma formation. If immunity weakens, granulomas break down (reactivation TB), releasing bacteria. Cavitation on chest X-ray = active, highly infectious disease.

Latent vs Active TB

One of the most important distinctions in TB management is differentiating between latent TB infection (LTBI) and active TB disease. This determines treatment approach, isolation requirements, and public health reporting.

Latent TB Infection (LTBI)

Key Characteristics

  • TB bacteria present but dormant
  • NO symptoms
  • NOT contagious
  • Positive TB skin test or IGRA
  • Normal chest X-ray
  • Negative sputum cultures

Treatment

  • Usually single drug therapy
  • 3-9 months depending on regimen
  • Prevents progression to active TB
Active TB Disease

Key Characteristics

  • TB bacteria actively multiplying
  • Symptomatic (cough, fever, night sweats)
  • CONTAGIOUS (pulmonary)
  • Positive TB skin test or IGRA
  • Abnormal chest X-ray
  • Positive sputum smear/culture

Treatment

  • Multi-drug therapy (RIPE)
  • 6-9 months minimum
  • Requires isolation until non-infectious
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Latent vs Active - The Key Distinction
Remember this simple rule: Latent TB is NEVER contagious, Active TB IS contagious! A patient with latent TB has the bacteria "sleeping" inside granulomas - they can't spread it to others, have no symptoms, and have normal chest X-rays. They only need treatment to prevent future reactivation. But a patient with active pulmonary TB is coughing up TB bacteria with every breath - they need airborne isolation immediately until proven non-infectious. For NCLEX, if you see a patient with positive TB test + symptoms + abnormal CXR = active TB = isolation first!

Primary vs Reactivation TB

Primary TB

Characteristics

  • First infection with TB
  • Usually within 1-2 years of exposure
  • Middle or lower lung zones often affected
  • Hilar lymphadenopathy common
  • More common in children, immunocompromised
  • Ghon complex on imaging
Reactivation TB

Characteristics

  • Dormant TB becomes active again
  • Can occur years to decades after primary
  • Upper lobe involvement typical
  • Cavitary disease more common
  • Triggered by immune suppression
  • More infectious (higher bacterial load)
Memory Aid: TB Types

"Primary = Pediatric & Lower, Reactivation = Return & Upper"

  • Primary TB - More common in children, affects lower/middle lungs
  • Reactivation TB - TB "returns" to activity, affects upper lobes
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Latent TB = positive skin test, negative sputum, normal CXR, NOT infectious. Active TB = positive sputum, abnormal CXR, IS infectious. Only active TB requires airborne precautions and full RIPE therapy.

Clinical Manifestations

Active TB typically presents with a constellation of respiratory and systemic symptoms that develop gradually over weeks to months. Recognizing these symptoms early is crucial for prompt isolation and treatment.

Extrapulmonary TB

TB can spread beyond the lungs to virtually any organ. Extrapulmonary TB is more common in immunocompromised patients and children.

Site Name Key Features
Lymph Nodes Scrofula (TB lymphadenitis) Painless, swollen cervical lymph nodes; may drain
Pleura TB pleurisy Pleural effusion, pleuritic chest pain
Bones/Joints Pott's disease (spinal) Back pain, kyphosis, paraplegia risk
Meninges TB meningitis Headache, altered mental status, cranial nerve palsies
Kidneys Genitourinary TB Sterile pyuria, frequency, hematuria
Disseminated Miliary TB Millet seed-like lesions throughout body; life-threatening
NCLEX Priority

TB meningitis and miliary TB are medical emergencies! Miliary TB (disseminated) presents with diffuse tiny nodules on chest X-ray resembling millet seeds. It occurs when TB spreads through the bloodstream. Mortality is high without prompt treatment. Signs include: high fever, hepatosplenomegaly, multiple organ dysfunction.

ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: The hallmark symptoms are persistent cough > 3 weeks + night sweats + unexplained weight loss + low-grade afternoon fever. Hemoptysis suggests cavitary disease and high infectivity.

Diagnostic Testing

TB diagnosis involves screening tests to detect exposure/infection, followed by confirmatory tests to identify active disease. Understanding these tests is crucial for nursing assessment and patient education.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
TST Reading - Induration, Not Redness!
One of the most common mistakes: measuring redness instead of induration! When reading a TB skin test, you must measure the raised, hardened area (induration), NOT the surrounding redness (erythema). Here's how: Palpate the injection site with your fingers to feel the raised area. Use a ruler to measure the widest diameter of the induration transversely (across the forearm). The criteria for positive depends on risk: â‰¥5mm for HIV+, close contacts, immunosuppressed; â‰¥10mm for healthcare workers, high-prevalence populations; â‰¥15mm for those with no risk factors. Remember: the test must be read within 48-72 hours or results are invalid!

Airborne Isolation Precautions

Patients with suspected or confirmed pulmonary TB require immediate airborne isolation to prevent transmission. This is one of the most critical nursing interventions in TB care.

Airborne Isolation Requirements

Airborne Infection Isolation Room (AIIR) - Negative pressure room with at least 6-12 air exchanges per hour. Air exhausted outside or through HEPA filtration. Door must remain closed.
N95 Respirator - ALL persons entering room must wear fit-tested N95 or higher respirator. Standard surgical masks are NOT adequate for TB. Respirator must seal to face.
Patient Mask - Patient wears surgical mask when leaving room for essential transport. Cover coughs and sneezes. Minimize time outside negative pressure room.
Signage & Restricted Access - Post airborne precautions sign on door. Limit visitors. Educate all who enter about requirements.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
N95 - Not Just Any Mask!
A surgical mask is NOT equivalent to an N95 for TB protection! Here's why: TB droplet nuclei are tiny (1-5 micrometers) and can be inhaled around the edges of a loose-fitting surgical mask. An N95 respirator filters at least 95% of airborne particles AND creates a seal against your face. Key points: You must be fit-tested for your N95 to ensure proper seal. Perform a seal check each time you don (inhale - mask should collapse; exhale - check for leaks around edges). Facial hair can break the seal. If you can smell something through your N95, it's not fitting properly!

When to Discontinue Isolation

Criteria Requirement
Treatment Duration Minimum 2 weeks of effective anti-TB therapy
Clinical Improvement Fever resolved, cough improving
Sputum Smears 3 consecutive negative AFB smears collected 8-24 hours apart
Drug Susceptibility No evidence of drug resistance
MDR-TB Alert

Multi-drug resistant TB (MDR-TB) is resistant to at least isoniazid AND rifampin - the two most powerful first-line drugs. These patients remain infectious longer and require extended isolation until multiple negative smears confirm non-infectiousness. Suspect MDR-TB in:

  • Patients from high MDR-TB prevalence areas
  • Previous TB treatment (especially incomplete)
  • Contact with known MDR-TB case
  • Failure to respond to standard therapy
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: Airborne precautions: negative-pressure room, N95 for staff, surgical mask on patient during transport. Continue until 3 consecutive negative sputum AFB smears collected 8â€“24 hours apart.

RIPE Therapy

Active TB requires multi-drug therapy to prevent resistance and ensure cure. The standard first-line regimen is known as RIPE therapy and lasts a minimum of 6 months.

Memory Aid: RIPE

Rifampin + Isoniazid + Pyrazinamide + Ethambutol

"The fruit must be RIPE to treat TB!"

First-Line TB Medications

Drug Mechanism Major Side Effects Nursing Considerations
Rifampin (R) Inhibits RNA synthesis Hepatotoxicity, orange body fluids, drug interactions Turns urine/tears/sweat orange; many drug interactions (OCP, warfarin); monitor LFTs
Isoniazid (H) Inhibits cell wall synthesis Hepatotoxicity, peripheral neuropathy Give pyridoxine (B6) to prevent neuropathy; monitor LFTs; avoid alcohol
Pyrazinamide (Z) Unknown; active in acidic environment Hepatotoxicity, hyperuricemia, arthralgias Used first 2 months only; monitor uric acid and LFTs
Ethambutol (E) Inhibits cell wall synthesis Optic neuritis (vision changes) Baseline and monthly vision testing; report blurred vision or color blindness immediately
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
RIPE Side Effects - What to Monitor
Three drugs (RIP) are hepatotoxic - monitor liver function closely! Here's what to watch for with each drug: Rifampin - Orange body fluids are NORMAL (warn patients!), but it causes major drug interactions - check all medications. Isoniazid - Peripheral neuropathy (tingling hands/feet); give vitamin B6 (pyridoxine) to prevent this. Pyrazinamide - Causes elevated uric acid and joint pain. Ethambutol - The "EYE" drug - causes optic neuritis. Ask about vision changes at every visit. Get baseline eye exam. If patient reports blurred vision, difficulty distinguishing red from green, or visual field changes - stop ethambutol and notify provider STAT!

Standard TB Treatment Phases

Initial Phase (2 months)
All 4 drugs: RIPE

6 months minimum

Directly Observed Therapy (DOT)

DOT - Gold Standard for Adherence

Directly Observed Therapy (DOT) means a healthcare worker watches the patient swallow every dose of medication. This is the recommended approach because:

  • TB treatment is long (6+ months) and adherence drops over time
  • Incomplete treatment leads to drug resistance
  • Protects public health by ensuring treatment completion
  • Allows early detection of side effects and non-adherence
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Treatment Adherence is CRITICAL
The #1 reason for treatment failure and drug resistance is non-adherence! Patients often feel better after 2-3 weeks of treatment and want to stop - but the TB bacteria are NOT gone yet. If treatment is stopped early or taken inconsistently, the remaining bacteria can become drug-resistant, creating a strain that's much harder to treat. This is why DOT (Directly Observed Therapy) is recommended. For NCLEX, if a patient says "I feel better, can I stop taking these pills?" - the answer is always NO! Teach patients they must complete the FULL course even when symptoms resolve.
ðŸŽ¯ Bottom Line: RIPE for 2 months (intensive phase), then Rifampin + INH for 4 more months (continuation phase). DOT ensures adherence. Never use single-drug therapy â€” it breeds resistance.

Nursing Care & Patient Education

Nursing care for TB patients extends beyond medication administration to include infection control, symptom management, patient education, and coordination with public health services.

Priority Nursing Interventions

Key Nursing Actions

1
Implement Airborne Precautions

Place patient in negative pressure room immediately upon suspicion of TB. Ensure N95 respirator is worn by all staff. Keep door closed. Minimize patient transport.

2
Administer Medications & Monitor for Side Effects

Give anti-TB medications as prescribed. Monitor LFTs regularly. Assess vision if on ethambutol. Provide pyridoxine with isoniazid. Report signs of hepatotoxicity.

3
Collect Sputum Specimens

Obtain 3 early morning specimens for AFB smear and culture. Ensure proper technique and biosafety precautions. Label specimens correctly.

4
Maintain Nutrition

Monitor weight regularly. Provide high-calorie, high-protein diet. Address nausea from medications. Small, frequent meals may be better tolerated.

5
Coordinate with Public Health

Report case to health department as required by law. Assist with contact investigation. Arrange for DOT upon discharge. Ensure follow-up appointments.

Patient & Family Education

Topic Key Teaching Points
Disease Understanding TB is curable with proper treatment; caused by bacteria spread through air; not spread by touching surfaces or sharing utensils
Medication Adherence Take ALL medications exactly as prescribed for ENTIRE duration; stopping early causes drug resistance; report side effects but don't stop without provider guidance
Infection Control at Home Cover mouth/nose when coughing; open windows for ventilation; wear surgical mask around others until non-infectious; sleep alone initially
Expected Side Effects Orange urine/tears from rifampin is NORMAL; report: vision changes, numbness/tingling, nausea/vomiting, dark urine, yellowing skin
Follow-Up Care Attend all appointments; sputum tests needed to confirm treatment working; continue until provider says treatment complete
Contact Notification Close contacts need to be tested; health department will assist; this protects loved ones from developing TB
NCLEX Priority: Contact Investigation

TB is a reportable disease! When a case is diagnosed, public health conducts a contact investigation to identify and test people who were exposed. Close contacts (household members, coworkers in enclosed spaces, etc.) are tested and offered treatment for latent TB if positive. This breaks the chain of transmission and is a critical public health intervention.

Signs of Hepatotoxicity - STOP Medications & Notify Provider!
  • Nausea, vomiting, loss of appetite persisting more than a few days
  • Dark urine (tea or cola-colored)
  • Jaundice (yellowing of skin or eyes)
  • Right upper quadrant pain
  • Unexplained fatigue or weakness
  • LFTs elevated >3-5x normal with symptoms
