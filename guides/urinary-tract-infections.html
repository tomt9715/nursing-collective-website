UTI & Pyelonephritis

Three findings separate a bladder infection from a kidney infection, and that separation decides oral or intravenous, home or admitted. Plus the one hospital infection you cause yourself.

1

What actually gets you

A UTI is one infection climbing a ladder. Urethra, bladder, kidneys, bloodstream. Where it has reached decides everything you do.

Points get lost here in three predictable ways.

You waited for burning

An older adult with a UTI often has no dysuria and no fever. What you get instead is new confusion, a fall, or sudden incontinence.

If you are waiting for the textbook symptom in an 84-year-old, you will miss it. New confusion in an older adult means a urinalysis.

You treated the ladder as one disease

Cystitis goes home on five days of tablets. Pyelonephritis gets admitted for two weeks, often intravenously.

Same organism, same infection, entirely different plan. Three findings decide it, and they are all in the question.

You left the catheter in

UTIs are the most common hospital-acquired infection, and most of them come from a catheter that nobody questioned.

Every day it stays in, the risk climbs. The single most effective prevention is taking it out, which is a decision, not a procedure.

Bottom Line: Work out how far up the ladder the infection is, then treat to that level. And ask every shift whether the catheter still needs to be there.

The four things

For every condition below, you should be able to say four things with the book closed: the one-sentence definition, the clue you would actually see in a patient, the priority nursing action, and the treatment with its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Condition Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action Treatment
Lower UTI, cystitis Bacteria colonise the bladder wall. Burning, frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain. No fever. Hydrate and get the culture before antibiotics start. Oral antibiotics for 5 to 7 days. Finish the course.
Pyelonephritis The infection has reached the renal pelvis and tubules. Fever with chills, flank pain, CVA tenderness, nausea. Vital signs every shift, watching for sepsis. Antibiotics for about 2 weeks, often intravenous, usually admitted.

Write these four rows on one index card, by hand. Burning is the bladder. Fever and flank is the kidney. A catheter is a cause. Falling pressure is sepsis. That card is most of the exam.

3

Assessment and the workup

What the kidneys do, what to ask, and which test answers which question.

The kidneys do five jobs. When they stop, all five fail at once, and a UTI that climbs threatens every one of them.

  • Make urine and excrete waste. Urea, creatinine, drugs.
  • Regulate electrolytes and acid-base. Sodium, potassium, bicarbonate.
  • Control fluid and blood pressure, through the renin-angiotensin system.
  • Drive red cell production, by releasing erythropoietin.
  • Activate vitamin D, which keeps calcium balanced.

So a failing kidney means rising BUN and creatinine, electrolytes drifting, blood pressure falling, and the hemoglobin dropping with it.

The older kidney

GFR falls by roughly 1 mL/min every year from around age 35 to 40. That is a slow decline nobody notices until something stresses it.

Older adults are therefore more vulnerable to acute and chronic kidney injury. They also lose the thirst response, so hypernatremia and fluid deficit are common.

Ask about fluid intake directly. Many older patients restrict fluids deliberately, to avoid a trip to the toilet they find difficult.

What to ask

  • Onset. When did it start, and was it sudden or gradual.
  • Pain. Character, location, duration, what triggers it, what relieves it, and how it relates to voiding.
  • Voiding. Frequency, urgency, burning, nocturia, hesitancy, incontinence.
  • The urine itself. Colour, odour, blood, volume.
  • Risk factors. Multiple vaginal deliveries, diabetes, neurological disease, recent catheterisation.
Four words that must be exact
  • Dysuria. Painful or difficult urination.
  • Hematuria. Red cells in the urine, visible or microscopic.
  • Oliguria. Reduced output, under 0.5 mL/kg/hr.
  • Anuria. Under 50 mL in 24 hours. That is a kidney emergency.

Questions use these words precisely, so you have to as well.

The examination

Kidneys are not normally palpable. If you can feel one, that is a finding and possibly an emergency.

Percuss the costovertebral angle. Tenderness there points at the kidney rather than the bladder, and that single manoeuvre separates the two diagnoses.

The tests

TestNormal or purposeWhat it tells you
Urinalysis A screen White cells, red cells, nitrites, leukocyte esterase, protein, glucose. Fast and cheap.
Urine culture Confirmation Identifies the organism and its sensitivities. Collect it before antibiotics start.
Specific gravity 1.005 to 1.030 How concentrated the urine is. Blood, protein and casts all distort it.
Osmolality 300 to 900 The most accurate measure of the kidney's ability to concentrate and dilute.
BUN 10 to 20 mg/dL Rises with kidney stress. Also with dehydration, high protein intake and GI bleeding.
Creatinine 0.6 to 1.2 mg/dL More specific than BUN, because it tracks GFR directly.
Urinalysis or culture

The urinalysis is quick and tells you whether there is an infection. The culture is slow and tells you which drug will treat it.

You need both, and the order matters. The culture must be collected before the first antibiotic dose, or the result will be useless.

Clean-catch midstream is first line. If the patient cannot collect one reliably, straight catheterise for a sterile sample. Urinalysis and culture go in separate containers.

Bottom Line: Percuss the CVA and you have separated bladder from kidney. Urinalysis screens, culture directs, and the culture goes first.
4

How it climbs

Bladder first, kidneys next, bloodstream last. Treating early stops the climb.

Most UTIs are ascending infections. Gut bacteria, usually E. coli, reach the urethra and travel upward. E. coli causes more than 80 percent of uncomplicated UTIs.

  1. 1
    Perineal skin. Gut bacteria colonise the area around the urethra.
  2. 2
    Urethra. They ascend. The distance is much shorter in women.
  3. 3
    Bladder. Cystitis. Dysuria, frequency, urgency. Still local.
  4. 4
    Ureters and kidneys. Pyelonephritis. Fever, flank pain, CVA tenderness. Now systemic.
  5. 5
    Bloodstream. Urosepsis, then septic shock. This is the emergency.
Why women get more of them

The female urethra is about 3 to 4 cm. The male urethra is 15 to 20 cm. It also sits close to the anus.

Less distance to climb, more chance of contamination. Pregnancy, menopause and sexual activity each add to it.

What tips the balance

  • Female sex, pregnancy, menopause.
  • Diabetes. Glucose in the urine feeds bacteria, and neuropathy stops the bladder emptying.
  • Neurological disease. MS, Parkinson's, diabetic neuropathy, stroke, spinal cord injury. A neurogenic bladder means stasis.
  • Incomplete emptying. BPH, prolapse, retention.
  • Obstruction. Stones, tumour, stricture.
  • Instrumentation. An indwelling catheter, cystoscopy, any recent urological procedure.
  • Immunosuppression. Chemotherapy, steroids, HIV.
  • Irritation or abrasion of the urethral mucosa.

Notice how many of those are one mechanism. Urine that sits still gets infected, so anything causing stasis is a risk factor.

Bottom Line: The infection climbs, and each rung has its own picture and its own treatment. Anything that leaves urine sitting still is a risk factor.
5

The bladder

Cystitis. Burning, frequency, urgency, and five to seven days of oral antibiotics.

Bacteria reach the bladder through the urethra, attach to the wall and colonise it.

Normally three defences stop this. Voiding flushes bacteria out. Urine is acidic. The mucosa makes antibacterial peptides. Anything that impairs those, meaning stasis, obstruction, instrumentation or immunosuppression, lets infection take hold.

What you see

  • Burning on urination, which is dysuria.
  • Frequency, meaning voiding more often than every 3 hours.
  • Urgency.
  • Nocturia.
  • Suprapubic or pelvic pain.
  • Incontinence.
  • Hematuria and back pain may be there too.
  • Urine may be cloudy or smell foul.

What you do not see is fever, flank pain or systemic illness. Their absence is as diagnostic as anything present.

What the labs show

  • Urinalysis. Positive leukocyte esterase, positive nitrites, white cells, bacteria, sometimes red cells.
  • Culture. More than 10 to the fifth colony-forming units per mL of a single organism, usually E. coli.

Treatment

  • Oral antibiotics for 5 to 7 days in uncomplicated cases. Nitrofurantoin, TMP-SMX, fosfomycin or a cephalosporin.
  • Hydration. 8 to 10 glasses of water a day unless something contraindicates it.
  • Phenazopyridine for symptom relief. It is a urinary analgesic, not an antibiotic, it turns urine orange or red, and it is short-term only.
  • Finish the course, even once the symptoms have gone.

You know it worked when the pain, urgency, frequency and nocturia are gone, the urine is clear, output is above 0.5 mL/kg/hr, and there are no systemic signs.

The older adult who never mentions burning

Older adults often do not report dysuria and often do not run a fever. The first sign, sometimes the only sign, is behavioural.

New confusion. A fall. Sudden incontinence. Lethargy. Loss of appetite.

Any acute change in mental status in an older adult is a reason to send a urinalysis. This is one of the most reliable rules in the topic.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
The One-Line Differentiator
Burning and frequency with no fever is the bladder. Add fever, flank pain and CVA tenderness and it is the kidney. That one line answers more questions in this topic than anything else you will learn.
Bottom Line: Local symptoms only, no fever, no flank pain. Culture first, then 5 to 7 days of oral antibiotics finished in full. In an older adult, expect confusion instead of burning.
6

The kidney

Pyelonephritis. Systemic, painful, and treated far more aggressively.

A bacterial infection of the renal pelvis, tubules and interstitial tissue of one or both kidneys.

It usually ascends from an untreated lower UTI. It can also seed through the blood. Vesicoureteral reflux, obstruction from stones or BPH, and pregnancy all raise the risk sharply.

What you see

  • Chills and fever.
  • Flank or low back pain.
  • CVA tenderness on percussion.
  • Nausea and vomiting.
  • Headache and malaise.
  • Dysuria may still be present, because the bladder is usually involved too.
  • Leukocytosis, meaning a raised white count.
  • Bacteriuria and pyuria on urinalysis. White cell casts point specifically at the kidney.
Why white cell casts matter

Casts are formed inside the renal tubules. They take the shape of the tubule and then wash out into the urine.

So a white cell cast could only have come from the kidney. White cells alone tell you there is infection somewhere; white cell casts tell you where.

Treatment

  • Antibiotics for about 2 weeks. Renal tissue is much harder to clear than bladder mucosa.
  • Usually intravenous, and usually admitted, for fluids, labs and sepsis surveillance.
  • Hydration, oral or intravenous depending on what the patient tolerates and how the kidneys are working.
  • A follow-up culture 2 weeks after finishing, to confirm the infection has actually gone.
  • Find the underlying cause. A stone, an obstruction, reflux. Treating the infection without the cause just delays the next one.

You know it worked when the patient is afebrile, the CVA tenderness has gone, the follow-up culture is negative, and BUN and creatinine are stable.

This is the one that turns septic

Check vital signs every shift and mean it. Hypotension, tachycardia, tachypnea, altered mental status, falling urine output, or a lactate over 2 all point at urosepsis.

Early fluids and intravenous antibiotics per the sepsis bundle. Pyelonephritis is the commonest source, and it moves faster than people expect.

Bottom Line: Fever, flank pain, CVA tenderness. Two weeks of antibiotics, usually intravenous and usually inpatient. White cell casts prove the kidney is involved.
7

Lower or upper

Discrimination axis: is the infection systemic, or still local to the bladder.

This is the pair the topic is built on. Three findings decide it, and each one is easy to check.

Lower UTI, the bladder
  • Where: bladder and urethra
  • Fever: usually none
  • Pain: suprapubic or pelvic
  • CVA tenderness: absent
  • Hallmarks: dysuria, frequency, urgency, nocturia
  • Systemic illness: minimal
  • Antibiotics: oral, 5 to 7 days
  • Where they are treated: at home
  • Urinalysis: white cells, bacteria, nitrites
Upper UTI, the kidney
  • Where: renal pelvis, tubules, interstitium
  • Fever: high, with chills
  • Pain: flank or low back
  • CVA tenderness: the hallmark
  • Hallmarks: fever, nausea and vomiting, malaise, leukocytosis
  • Systemic illness: prominent
  • Antibiotics: often intravenous, about 2 weeks total
  • Where they are treated: usually admitted
  • Urinalysis: all of the above plus white cell casts
The axis that separates them

Systemic or local. A bladder infection irritates a bladder. A kidney infection makes the whole patient ill.

Three findings carry it: fever, flank pain, CVA tenderness. All three present means upper. All three absent means lower.

Everything downstream follows from that. Oral or intravenous, days or weeks, home or admitted.

8

The one you cause

UTIs are the most common hospital-acquired infection, and most of them come from a catheter.

An indwelling catheter does three harmful things at once. It bypasses the urethral defences. It grows a biofilm that bacteria attach to. And it stops the bladder emptying completely.

Every day the catheter stays in, the risk climbs. That is why the single most effective prevention is not a technique. It is removal.

Preventing it, in priority order
  • Do not place one if you can avoid it. Bladder scan, intermittent catheterisation, or a condom catheter instead.
  • Take it out as soon as possible. Review the need every single day.
  • Aseptic insertion. Sterile gloves, sterile field, perineal cleansing.
  • Keep the system closed. Do not disconnect it without reason.
  • Bag below bladder level, always. Above it, urine runs back.
  • No dependent loops in the tubing.
  • Daily peri-care with soap and water. No special antiseptics.
  • Secure the catheter so it does not drag on the urethra.
  • Sample from the port, never from the bag.

When to suspect one

  • New or worsening fever, chills or flank pain.
  • New confusion or a change in behaviour.
  • Hematuria, or urine that has turned cloudy or foul.
  • Leukocytosis, hypotension or tachycardia, which means you assess for urosepsis.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Ask Why It Is Still In
Most catheters that cause infection were placed for a reason that expired days ago. Convenience is not an indication. If nobody can say why it is still there, that is the answer.
After a urological procedure

Cystoscopy, urodynamics and any instrumentation raise the risk. Some burning, blood-tinged urine and frequency for 24 hours afterwards is expected, and you can reassure the patient.

What is not expected is persistent fever, flank pain, worsening pain, or retention. Those get escalated.

Bottom Line: The catheter is the cause. Prevention is avoidance first, early removal second, and technique third. Bag below the bladder, system closed, sample from the port.
9

Urosepsis

The escalation. Any UTI with systemic signs is urosepsis until proven otherwise.

When a UTI spills bacteria or toxins into the blood, the patient moves from infection to sepsis to septic shock. Pyelonephritis and CAUTI are the commonest sources.

The reason this section exists is that the transition is quiet. The patient does not announce it. The vital signs do.

What you checkThe thresholdWhat it means
TemperatureOver 38 or under 36 degrees CHyperthermia or hypothermia. Both count.
Heart rateOver 90 bpmTachycardia
Respiratory rateOver 20 per minuteTachypnea, and often the earliest sign
Systolic pressureUnder 90 mmHgOr a drop of 40 mmHg or more from baseline
Urine outputUnder 0.5 mL/kg/hrOver 2 hours or more
Mental statusAny changeNew confusion, lethargy, behaviour that is not theirs

A low temperature counts as much as a high one. So does a respiratory rate nobody wrote down.

The first hour
  • Two sets of blood cultures and a urine culture, before antibiotics, as long as that does not delay them.
  • Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics within 1 hour. Narrow them once the cultures report.
  • Fluid resuscitation. Crystalloid at 30 mL/kg if hypotensive or the lactate is 4 or above.
  • Lactate. Draw it, then repeat in 2 to 4 hours.
  • Vasopressors, norepinephrine first, if the MAP stays under 65 despite fluids.
  • Source control. Remove the catheter if it caused this. Drain an obstruction if there is one.

Cultures before antibiotics, but never at the cost of the hour.

Bottom Line: Any UTI patient whose vital signs turn systemic is septic until proven otherwise. Cultures, antibiotics within the hour, fluids, and take out whatever caused it.
10

What you teach before discharge

Water and voiding

  • 8 to 10 glasses of water a day, unless heart or kidney disease says otherwise.
  • Void every 2 to 3 hours during the day. Do not hold it.
  • Empty the bladder completely each time.
  • Void after sex, which flushes out bacteria introduced during it.

Hygiene

  • Wipe front to back, after urinating and after defecating.
  • Shower rather than bathe. If bathing, no bubble bath and no scented products.
  • Cotton underwear. Avoid tight synthetic fabrics.
  • Change out of wet swimsuits and workout clothes promptly.

The antibiotics

  • Finish the whole course, even after the symptoms have gone.
  • Take them with water. Report rash, difficulty breathing, or severe diarrhea, which may be C. diff.
  • Phenazopyridine turns urine orange or red. That is expected, and it is short-term only.

What to avoid

  • Bubble baths, douches, powders and scented feminine products. All irritate the urethra.
  • Holding urine for long stretches.
  • Catheters that are not necessary.
When they should call
  • Fever over 38 degrees C, or chills
  • Flank or back pain, nausea or vomiting
  • Blood in the urine that is new or getting worse
  • Symptoms not improving after 48 to 72 hours of antibiotics
  • New confusion, especially in an older adult. Tell the family to report this one.
  • Reduced urine output, or being unable to urinate at all
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Why Finishing the Course Matters
Stopping when the burning stops leaves the hardier bacteria behind. Those are the ones that come back, and next time they will not respond to the same drug. That is how a simple UTI becomes a recurrent resistant one.
Bottom Line: Water, regular voiding, front to back, void after sex, finish the antibiotics. And tell the family that new confusion is a reason to call.
