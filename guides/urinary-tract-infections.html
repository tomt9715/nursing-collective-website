UTI & Pyelonephritis

Lower UTI, upper UTI (pyelonephritis), and catheter-associated infections — the most common site of hospital-acquired infection. Know the ladder: bladder first, kidneys next.

8 Sections 18 Quiz Questions ~18 Min Read

Learning Objectives

Differentiate lower UTI (bladder) from upper UTI (pyelonephritis) by symptoms, severity, and treatment course.
Identify risk factors that tip a normal urinary tract toward infection and how to modify them.
Recognize costovertebral angle (CVA) tenderness and why it signals pyelonephritis, not cystitis.
Interpret urinalysis and urine culture results (bacteriuria, pyuria, WBC casts) and escalate urosepsis.
Prevent catheter-associated UTIs (CAUTIs) — the most common hospital-acquired infection.
Teach patients hydration, voiding habits, and antibiotic adherence to prevent recurrence.

High-Yield Summary

Ten facts that decide the UTI questions on your exam.

  • Normal urinary tract is sterile above the urethra. UTI = pathogenic microorganisms colonizing the tract. E. coli causes 80%+ of uncomplicated UTIs.
  • Lower UTI (cystitis) classic 5: burning/dysuria, frequency (>q3h), urgency, nocturia, suprapubic pain. Hematuria may be present. Treat: 5–7 days oral antibiotics.
  • Upper UTI (pyelonephritis) classic 5: fever & chills, flank / low back pain, CVA tenderness, N/V, malaise. Treat: 2 weeks antibiotics, often IV, usually hospitalized.
  • Can't tell them apart? Fever + flank pain + CVA tenderness = upper tract. Without those = lower tract. This is the exam distinction.
  • UTIs are the #1 hospital-acquired infection. Most are catheter-associated (CAUTI). Key prevention: aseptic insertion, earliest possible removal, closed drainage, bag below bladder.
  • Risk factors: female, diabetes, pregnancy, neurogenic bladder, immunosuppression, incomplete bladder emptying, catheter/instrumentation, obstruction (stones, BPH).
  • Older adults: often atypical presentation — confusion, falls, incontinence instead of burning/fever. Any new confusion in an elder, think UTI.
  • Urosepsis: any UTI with systemic s/s (hypotension, tachycardia, altered mental status, low UOP). Can progress to septic shock — evaluate every patient with SIRS findings for urosepsis.
  • Urinalysis vs urine culture: UA detects WBCs, RBCs, nitrites, leukocyte esterase (quick screen). Culture confirms organism + sensitivities (guides antibiotic choice). Collect before starting antibiotics.
  • Patient teaching: complete the full antibiotic course, 8–10 glasses water/day, void every 2–3 hours, wipe front to back, void after intercourse, avoid irritants (bubble baths, douches).

Kidney & Urinary Assessment

Functions, history clues, and the diagnostic workup you'll see on every UTI case.

What the kidneys do (why it matters)

The kidneys are multitaskers. When they're not working, BUN and creatinine rise, fluid and electrolytes go sideways, BP and RBC counts drop. A UTI that climbs to the kidneys threatens all of this.

  • Urine formation & waste excretion — urea, creatinine, drugs
  • Electrolyte & acid-base regulation — Na, K, bicarbonate
  • Fluid & BP control — via the renin-angiotensin system
  • RBC production — kidneys release erythropoietin
  • Vitamin D activation — to support calcium balance
Gerontologic considerations

GFR declines about 1 mL/min per year starting age 35–40. Older adults are more prone to acute & chronic kidney injury, hypernatremia and fluid volume deficit (diminished thirst response), incomplete bladder emptying, and incontinence. Mobility and cognition affect whether they can even get to the toilet. Ask about fluid intake — many limit fluids on purpose to avoid voiding.

History questions that matter

  • Chief concern & onset — when did symptoms start? acute or gradual?
  • Pain — character, duration, location, what triggers it, what relieves it, relationship to voiding
  • Voiding — frequency, urgency, burning, nocturia, hesitancy, incontinence
  • Urine — color, odor, blood (hematuria), volume
  • Red-flag definitions:
    • Dysuria — painful or difficult urination
    • Hematuria — RBCs in urine (visible or microscopic)
    • Anuria — <50 mL urine in 24 h (kidney emergency)
    • Oliguria — decreased output (<0.5 mL/kg/hr)
  • Risk factors — multiparous vaginal delivery, diabetes, neurologic disorders (MS, Parkinson's, diabetic neuropathy — all cause incomplete emptying and stasis), recent catheterization

Physical exam & diagnostics

Kidneys are not normally palpable. A palpable enlargement could be an emergency. Obese patients make palpation difficult. Check the costovertebral angle (CVA) — tenderness there points to pyelonephritis (section 3).

Core diagnostic tests

Urinalysis (UA)
Screen

WBCs, RBCs, nitrites, leukocyte esterase, protein, glucose. Fast, cheap.

Urine Culture
Confirms

Organism ID + sensitivities. Collect BEFORE antibiotics. UA and culture go in separate containers.

Specific Gravity
1.005–1.030

Concentration. Altered by blood, protein, casts.

Osmolality
300–900

Most accurate measure of kidney's ability to dilute/concentrate.

BUN
10–20 mg/dL

Elevated = kidney stress. Also rises with dehydration, high-protein intake, GI bleed.

Creatinine
0.6–1.2 mg/dL

Elevated = kidney injury. More specific than BUN — reflects GFR directly.

Clean catch vs straight cath

Clean-catch midstream is first-line. If the patient can't reliably collect (older, immobile, altered) you'll straight cath to get a sterile sample. Put the UA in one container and the culture in another.

How Infection Ascends

Bladder first, kidneys next, bloodstream last. Treating early stops the climb.

Most UTIs are ascending infections: bacteria (usually gut flora, most commonly E. coli) enter the urethra, climb into the bladder, and in some cases continue up the ureters to the kidneys. Untreated pyelonephritis can enter the bloodstream and cause urosepsis.

1. Perineal flora

Gut bacteria colonize the periurethral skin

2. Urethra

Bacteria ascend — shorter in females

3. Bladder (cystitis)

Lower UTI: dysuria, frequency, urgency

4. Ureters → Kidneys

Pyelonephritis: fever, flank pain, CVA tenderness

5. Bloodstream

Urosepsis → septic shock. Emergency.

Why females get more UTIs

The female urethra is short (~3–4 cm vs 15–20 cm in males) and sits close to the anal opening. Less distance for bacteria to climb, more opportunity for contamination. Pregnancy, menopause (loss of estrogen-driven vaginal flora), and sexual activity all add risk.

Risk factors that tip the balance

  • Female sex, pregnancy, menopause
  • Diabetes — high glucose in urine feeds bacteria + neuropathy = incomplete emptying
  • Neurologic disorders (MS, Parkinson's, diabetic neuropathy, stroke, SCI) — neurogenic bladder = stasis
  • Incomplete bladder emptying (BPH, prolapse, retention)
  • Obstruction — kidney stones, tumor, stricture
  • Instrumentation — indwelling catheter, cystoscopy, recent urologic procedure
  • Immunosuppression — chemo, steroids, HIV
  • Gout — mentioned in textbook risk lists
  • Inflammation/abrasion of urethral mucosa
3

Lower UTI (Cystitis)

Bladder infection. Classically dysuria + frequency + urgency. 5–7 days of oral antibiotics.

Lower Urinary Tract Infection

Bladder

Pathophysiology

Bacteria gain access to the bladder through the urethra, then attach to the bladder wall and colonize. Normal defenses include the flushing action of voiding, urine acidity, and mucosal antibacterial peptides — anything that impairs these (stasis, obstruction, instrumentation, immunosuppression) enables infection.

Classic clinical manifestations

  • Burning on urination (dysuria)
  • Urinary frequency — voiding more than every 3 hours
  • Urgency
  • Nocturia
  • Suprapubic or pelvic pain
  • Incontinence
  • Hematuria and back pain may be present
  • Urine may be cloudy or foul-smelling

Diagnostic findings

  • Urinalysis: positive leukocyte esterase, positive nitrites, WBCs, bacteria, possible RBCs
  • Urine culture: >105 CFU/mL of a single organism (most often E. coli)
  • No systemic signs in uncomplicated cases (no fever, no flank pain)

Medical management

  • Oral antibiotics × 5–7 days (uncomplicated) — nitrofurantoin, TMP-SMX, fosfomycin, cephalosporins
  • Hydration — 8–10 glasses of water/day unless contraindicated
  • Phenazopyridine (Pyridium) — urinary analgesic; turns urine orange/red; short-term symptom relief only
  • Complete the full antibiotic course even when symptoms resolve early

Expected outcomes

  • Reports absence of pain, urgency, frequency, nocturia
  • Produces urine that is clear and odorless
  • Adequate urine output >0.5 mL/kg/hr
  • Normal VS; no s/s urosepsis
  • Drinks 8–10 glasses of fluid daily, voids every 2–3 hours
Atypical presentation in older adults

Older adults may not report classic dysuria or fever. The first (or only) sign may be new-onset confusion, falls, lethargy, new urinary incontinence, or reduced appetite. Any acute mental status change in an older adult warrants a UA — UTI is high on the differential.

Florence Tip

The one-line differentiator

Burning + frequency + no fever = lower UTI. Add fever, flank pain, and CVA tenderness = upper UTI (pyelonephritis). Memorize that. It's the NCLEX-style distinction that shows up repeatedly.

4

Pyelonephritis (Upper UTI)

Bacterial infection of the kidney. Systemic, painful, and treated much more aggressively than cystitis.

Pyelonephritis

Kidney

Pathophysiology

A bacterial infection of the renal pelvis, tubules, and interstitial tissue of one or both kidneys. Usually ascends from an untreated lower UTI, but can also seed hematogenously. Vesicoureteral reflux, obstruction (stones, BPH), and pregnancy are major risk boosters.

Classic clinical manifestations

  • Chills and fever
  • Flank pain or low back pain
  • Costovertebral angle (CVA) tenderness on percussion
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Headache, malaise
  • Painful urination (may still have lower UTI features)
  • Leukocytosis — elevated WBC
  • Bacteriuria and pyuria on UA; WBC casts suggest upper-tract origin

Medical management

  • Antibiotics × ~2 weeks — renal parenchymal disease is harder to eradicate than bladder mucosal infection
  • Usually IV antibiotics for 2–3 weeks with hospitalization for close monitoring (fluids, labs, sepsis surveillance)
  • Hydration — oral or IV, depending on intake tolerance and kidney function
  • Follow-up urine culture 2 weeks after completing antibiotics to confirm clearance
  • Identify & treat any underlying cause (stone, obstruction, reflux)

Expected outcomes

  • Afebrile, CVA tenderness resolved
  • Negative follow-up culture
  • Adequate urine output, stable BUN/creatinine
  • No progression to urosepsis
Pyelonephritis can turn septic fast

Pay attention to vital signs every shift. Hypotension, tachycardia, tachypnea, altered mental status, low urine output, lactate >2 = evaluate for urosepsis. Early fluids + IV antibiotics per sepsis bundle. See section 7.

Lower vs Upper UTI — Side by Side

If you can't tell them apart cold, you're going to miss the exam question asking you to.

Lower UTI (Cystitis)

Bladder
  • Location: bladder (and urethra)
  • Fever: usually none
  • Pain: suprapubic / pelvic
  • Flank pain / CVA tenderness: absent
  • Hallmarks: dysuria, frequency, urgency, nocturia
  • Systemic symptoms: minimal
  • Antibiotics: oral × 5–7 days
  • Setting: outpatient
  • Urinalysis: WBCs, bacteria, nitrites

Upper UTI (Pyelonephritis)

Kidney
  • Location: renal pelvis, tubules, interstitium
  • Fever: high, with chills
  • Pain: flank / low back
  • Flank pain / CVA tenderness: hallmark
  • Hallmarks: fever, N/V, malaise, leukocytosis
  • Systemic symptoms: prominent
  • Antibiotics: IV × ~2–3 weeks, total ~2 weeks
  • Setting: usually inpatient
  • Urinalysis: above + WBC casts

Catheter-Associated UTI (CAUTI)

UTIs are the most common hospital-acquired infection — and most of them are catheter-related.

Catheter-Associated UTI

Nosocomial

Why catheters cause UTIs

An indwelling catheter bypasses the urethral defense, creates a biofilm where bacteria attach, and keeps the bladder from fully emptying. Every day a catheter is in, the risk of infection climbs. That's why the single most effective CAUTI prevention is early removal.

Prevention priorities

  • Avoid catheters when possible — use bladder scans, intermittent cath, or condom cath instead
  • Remove the catheter as early as possible — daily necessity review
  • Aseptic insertion technique — sterile gloves, sterile field, perineal cleansing
  • Closed drainage system — do not disconnect unnecessarily
  • Drainage bag below bladder level at all times — prevents retrograde flow
  • No dependent loops in the tubing
  • Peri-care daily with soap and water — no special antiseptics needed
  • Secure the catheter to prevent urethral trauma
  • Obtain cultures from the sampling port, not the bag

When to suspect CAUTI

  • New or worsening fever, chills, flank pain
  • New confusion or behavioral change
  • Hematuria, cloudy or foul-smelling urine
  • Leukocytosis, hypotension, tachycardia (evaluate for urosepsis)
Post-urologic procedure awareness

Patients who had cystoscopy, urodynamics, or any instrumentation are at high CAUTI risk. Some burning, blood-tinged urine, and frequency for 24 hours is expected. Persistent fever, flank pain, worsening pain, or retention needs escalation.

Urosepsis — The Escalation

Any UTI patient with systemic SIRS findings gets evaluated for urosepsis. Hours matter.

When a UTI spills bacteria or toxins into the bloodstream, the patient can progress from infection to sepsis to septic shock. Pyelonephritis and CAUTI are the most common sources. Any UTI with systemic signs is presumed urosepsis until proven otherwise.

Red flags (call the provider)

Temperature
>38°C or <36°C

Hyperthermia or hypothermia

Heart rate
>90 bpm

Tachycardia

Respiratory rate
>20 / min

Tachypnea

Systolic BP
<90 mmHg

Or ≥40 mmHg drop from baseline

Urine output
<0.5 mL/kg/hr

Over ≥2 hours

Mental status
Altered

New confusion, lethargy, unusual behavior

Sepsis bundle priorities
  • Blood cultures x2 + urine culture BEFORE antibiotics (if it doesn't delay them)
  • Broad-spectrum IV antibiotics within 1 hour — narrow once culture results return
  • IV fluid resuscitation — crystalloid 30 mL/kg if hypotensive or lactate ≥4
  • Lactate — draw, repeat in 2–4 h
  • Vasopressors (norepinephrine first-line) if MAP <65 despite fluids
  • Source control — remove the catheter if CAUTI, drain obstruction if present

Prevention & Patient Education

Everything you'll teach a post-UTI patient before discharge.

Hydration & voiding habits

  • Drink 8–10 glasses of water per day (unless contraindicated by heart or kidney disease)
  • Void every 2–3 hours during the day — don't hold it
  • Empty the bladder completely each time
  • Void after sexual intercourse — flushes out bacteria introduced during sex

Hygiene

  • Wipe front to back after urinating and defecating
  • Shower rather than bath; if bathing, avoid bubble baths and scented bath products
  • Wear cotton underwear; avoid tight-fitting synthetic fabrics
  • Change out of wet swimsuits / workout clothes promptly

Medication adherence

  • Complete the full antibiotic course even if symptoms resolve — stopping early breeds resistance and recurrence
  • Take antibiotics with water; report rash, difficulty breathing, severe diarrhea (possible C. diff)
  • Phenazopyridine (Pyridium) turns urine orange/red — this is expected; short-term use only

Things to avoid

  • Bubble baths, douches, powders, scented feminine products (irritate urethra)
  • Holding urine for long periods
  • Unnecessary catheterization

When to call the provider

Warning signs after starting treatment
  • Fever >38°C (101°F), chills
  • Flank or back pain, N/V
  • Blood in urine that's new or worsening
  • Symptoms not improving after 48–72 hours on antibiotics
  • New confusion (especially in older adults) — family should report
  • Reduced urine output, inability to urinate
Florence Tip

Why "finish your antibiotics" matters

Stopping antibiotics when symptoms resolve is how recurrent and resistant UTIs happen. The surviving bacteria are the hardier ones, and next round they won't respond to the same antibiotic. Teach this every time — it's a high-yield NCLEX education point.
