UTI, VUR & Enuresis

Three conditions that form one chain: the fever you cannot afford to call a virus, the reflux it can be hiding, and the bedwetting you never punish.

Reading Time
15-20 minutes
Difficulty Level
Intermediate

The Nursing Collective

By the end, without notes

  • Call a UTI in a febrile infant who has no other source, and defend the workup
  • Pick the right urine specimen for the age, and say why a bag cannot diagnose
  • Trace a febrile UTI to reflux and scarring, and name the test that grades it
  • Separate primary from secondary enuresis, and say which one you work up
  • Name the first-line treatment for bedwetting, and the DDAVP fluid rule
01

What actually gets you

These three conditions travel together. A missed UTI can hide reflux. Reflux scars kidneys. Bedwetting can be the flag that something else is wrong.

Points get lost here in three predictable ways. Read them before anything else.

You waited for the adult UTI story

An infant cannot tell you it burns. So the signs are nonspecific. Fever without a source, poor feeding, irritability, foul urine. If you wait for dysuria and frequency, you miss it. You also cannot diagnose it from a bag specimen; a bag is contaminated, and culture needs a clean catch or a catheter.

You treated the UTI and stopped there

A febrile UTI can be the first sign of reflux. Reflux sends urine back up toward the kidney. That is how a bladder infection becomes pyelonephritis, and how the kidney scars. Reflux is graded, and the study that grades it is a VCUG.

You treated bedwetting like a behavior problem

It is not a choice, so punishment does not fix it. Enuresis is common, and most cases resolve on their own with time. The first-line treatment is a bedwetting alarm, not a consequence. Shaming the child only delays the day it stops.

Bottom Line: Stop studying three separate topics. Study one chain, fever to UTI to reflux to scar, and one rule for the bed, never punish it.
02

The four things

For each condition, say four things with the book closed. The one-sentence definition. The clue you would see in a patient. The priority action. The first-line treatment and its teaching point.

If you cannot say all four out loud, you do not know it yet. Familiarity is not recall.

Condition Definition in one sentence Clue in the scenario Priority action First-line treatment
UTI Bacteria, usually E. coli, ascend the urethra and infect the bladder or kidney. Fever with no source in an infant; dysuria and frequency in an older child. Get a proper urine specimen for culture before antibiotics. Age-appropriate antibiotics. Finish the full course.
VUR The valve at the bladder leaks, so urine flows back up toward the kidney. Recurrent or febrile UTIs in a young child. Get imaging; grade the reflux with a VCUG. Low grade, watch and treat UTIs. High grade, surgery.
Enuresis Involuntary voiding past the age when dryness is expected, about age 5. A school-age child who wets the bed at night. Rule out a secondary cause, then reassure. Bedwetting alarm first-line. DDAVP short-term.

Write these three rows on one index card, by hand. UTI is the infection. VUR is the plumbing behind a repeat infection. Enuresis is the timing that has not caught up. That card is most of the exam.

03

Pediatric UTI

Discrimination axis: age changes the whole presentation.

One infection, three different faces. The younger the child, the less it looks like a UTI.

Age group Typical presentation
Infants under 2 months Fever, poor feeding, vomiting, irritability, jaundice, sepsis-like illness, failure to thrive. Not typical UTI symptoms.
Infants 2 to 24 months Fever is often the only sign. Add vomiting, poor feeding, foul-smelling urine, irritability, failure to thrive.
Toddlers and preschoolers Fever, abdominal pain, vomiting, malodorous urine, new wetting after being dry, dysuria if verbal.
School-age and adolescents Adult-like dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain, hematuria. Pyelonephritis adds fever and flank pain.

Lower or upper

Where the infection sits changes how serious it is.

Cystitis, lower UTI
  • Bladder infection
  • Dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain
  • Usually no fever
Pyelonephritis, upper UTI
  • Kidney infection, much more serious
  • Fever at or above 38.5 C, flank pain, CVA tenderness, systemic illness
  • Carries the risk of renal scarring

The bugs and who gets them

  • E. coli causes about 80 percent, straight from bowel flora.
  • Klebsiella, Proteus show up with structural problems or recent antibiotics.
  • Enterococcus, Staph saprophyticus appear in adolescent girls.
  • Pseudomonas is hospitalized, immunocompromised, or catheterized.
  • First year of life, boys, especially uncircumcised, are at higher risk.
  • After age 1, girls predominate, because the shorter urethra is an easier climb.

Risk factors worth flagging

  • Vesicoureteral reflux, anatomic abnormalities, neurogenic bladder
  • Constipation, a big risk factor that gets overlooked
  • Holding urine and rushed, incomplete voids
  • Bubble baths and harsh soaps, poor hygiene, wiping the wrong way
  • Sexual activity in adolescents
Fever without a source

A febrile infant under 24 months with no obvious source needs a urine culture. No cold, no ear infection, no viral rash, so check the urine. UTI is the most common serious bacterial infection at this age. A missed one becomes pyelonephritis with permanent scarring.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
In an infant, the UTI symptom is fever
Adults say it burns. Infants cannot. For a baby, the UTI symptom is often just the fever, sometimes with vomiting or poor feeding. That is why any febrile infant under 2 years without a clear source gets urine tested. Catching it early is how you prevent the scar.
Bottom Line: Infant UTI is fever, often alone. Older children give you the adult picture. E. coli is the bug, and every unexplained infant fever earns a urine test.
04

Vesicoureteral reflux

Discrimination axis: the grade decides watch or surgery.

Urine should flow one way. Kidney to ureter to bladder, then out. The junction at the bladder works as a one-way valve.

In reflux, that valve leaks. Urine flows backward, up the ureter toward the kidney. Any bacteria in the bladder ride along.

Why it matters

  • It lets a simple bladder infection become pyelonephritis.
  • Repeated kidney infection causes renal scarring, called reflux nephropathy.
  • Long term, that means hypertension, proteinuria, and chronic kidney disease.
  • Reflux is the leading structural cause of pediatric CKD progression.

Two types, and who gets it

  • Primary VUR is congenital, from an abnormal valve at the junction. It is the common form, and it runs in families.
  • Secondary VUR is acquired, from outlet obstruction, neurogenic bladder, or posterior urethral valves.
  • About 1 in 100 children have it; up to 40 percent of children with febrile UTIs do.
  • It is often silent until a UTI reveals it. Prenatal hydronephrosis can be the first clue.

Grades I to V

The grade tells you how far the urine backs up, and how dilated things get.

Grade What the VCUG shows
IReflux into the ureter only, no dilation.
IIReflux up to the renal pelvis, still no dilation.
IIIMild dilation of ureter and pelvis, calyces stay sharp.
IVModerate dilation, with blunting of the calyces.
VSevere dilation, a tortuous ureter, papillary impressions lost.
The grade sets the plan

Grades I to III often resolve on their own as the child grows. You watch, treat UTIs promptly, and sometimes give prophylactic antibiotics, which is debated. Grades IV to V rarely resolve, so they usually need surgery, either ureteral reimplantation or endoscopic injection of Deflux.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Reflux is the reason for the workup
A recurrent or febrile UTI in a young child is not just an infection to treat. It is a question to answer: is urine going backward? That is why the guideline sends these children to imaging. Find the reflux before the scar forms, because the scar is permanent even after you fix the valve.
Bottom Line: Reflux is backward urine flow that sets up pyelonephritis and scarring. You diagnose and grade it with a VCUG. Low grade, watch. High grade, surgery.
05

Enuresis

Discrimination axis: primary or secondary.

Wetting past age 5 has a name. It is common, benign, and frustrating for families. About 15 percent of 5-year-olds and 10 percent of 7-year-olds still wet at night, and most stop by age 10.

The question is always the same. Was this child ever reliably dry?

The types

  • Primary: the child has never been consistently dry.
  • Secondary: dry for at least 6 months, then wetting again.
  • Nocturnal: only at night, the most common form.
  • Diurnal: daytime wetting, more concerning, always evaluate for a cause.

Why primary nocturnal enuresis happens

  • Maturational delay, the most common cause. Waking to a full bladder lags behind peers, then catches up.
  • Strong family history. One affected parent, about 40 percent risk; both, about 75 percent.
  • Deep sleep and difficulty arousing.
  • A smaller functional bladder capacity.
  • Less nighttime ADH, so urine is not concentrated overnight.

Causes of secondary enuresis, always work these up

  • UTI
  • Constipation, where a full rectum presses on the bladder
  • Type 1 diabetes, with polyuria from osmotic diuresis
  • Diabetes insipidus, or a structural or neurologic disorder
  • Emotional stress, family conflict, abuse, a new sibling, divorce
Never shame or punish

Bedwetting is not under the child's voluntary control. Shaming and punishment raise anxiety, lower self-esteem, and delay resolution. Coach parents to normalize it, praise dry nights, and stay neutral about wet ones. Your teaching here often does more than any drug.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Secondary enuresis is a red flag
A child who was dry for months, then suddenly wets again, earns a workup. Common causes are a UTI, new type 1 diabetes, constipation, and emotional trauma. Test the urine for glucose and infection, ask about bowel habits, and screen for stressors. This is rarely nothing.
Bottom Line: Wetting after age 5 counts. Primary, never dry, is usually maturational. Secondary, dry then wet, gets worked up. Never shame.
06

Diagnosis and workup

Discrimination axis: the specimen has to match the age.

How you collect the urine decides whether the result means anything. Get this wrong and you treat a contaminant.

Method When to use Notes
Bag specimen Screening only, in a non-toilet-trained infant High contamination. Never use it for culture. A positive bag confirms with a cath or aspiration.
Catheterization, in and out Definitive culture in a non-toilet-trained infant Sterile sample. Over 50,000 CFU is diagnostic.
Suprapubic aspiration Gold standard in infants Needle draws straight from the bladder. Any growth is diagnostic.
Clean catch midstream Toilet-trained children Clean the perineum, start the void, catch the middle. Over 100,000 CFU is diagnostic.

Reading the urine

  • Dipstick: leukocyte esterase positive, nitrites positive, blood. Nitrites are highly specific for gram-negative UTI, especially E. coli.
  • Microscopy: over 5 WBC per high-power field is pyuria, plus bacteria and red cells.
  • Culture: the gold standard. Growth thresholds depend on the collection method.
  • CBC and CRP: leukocytosis and a high CRP point toward pyelonephritis.
  • Blood culture: for an infant with suspected urosepsis.

Imaging after a UTI

  • Renal ultrasound comes first, to rule out obstruction or an anatomic problem.
  • VCUG, the voiding cystourethrogram, is the gold standard for reflux. Contrast goes into the bladder by catheter, and fluoroscopy during voiding shows whether urine refluxes and to what grade.
  • DMSA scan detects renal scarring and cortical damage from pyelonephritis.

When to image

  • A first febrile UTI in a child under 2 years, with renal ultrasound and sometimes VCUG.
  • Recurrent UTIs at any age.
  • An atypical course, such as a poor antibiotic response or a non-E. coli organism.
  • Any suspicion of an anatomic anomaly.

Working up enuresis

  • History: nocturnal versus diurnal, primary versus secondary, family history, bowel habits, fluid intake, stressors.
  • Exam: abdomen for a fecal mass, genitalia, spine for a sacral dimple or tuft, lower-limb neuro.
  • Urinalysis: rule out UTI, check glucose, check concentration.
  • Voiding diary: wet and dry nights and daytime voids over 1 to 2 weeks.
FlorenceBot Pro Tip
A bag is a screen, never a culture
A bag sits against contaminated skin, so its culture is meaningless. Use it to screen a baby who cannot void on command. If the screen is positive, confirm with a catheter or a suprapubic aspiration before you commit to antibiotics. In a toilet-trained child, a clean catch does the job.
Bottom Line: Cath or aspiration for an infant culture, clean catch for older kids, bag for screening only. VCUG grades the reflux. For enuresis, history plus a urinalysis.
07

Treatment

Three problems, three plans. Antibiotics for the infection, grade-based management for the reflux, and a ladder for the bedwetting.

UTI antibiotics

Setting First-line Notes
Outpatient cystitis Cephalexin, amoxicillin-clavulanate, TMP-SMX, or nitrofurantoin Local resistance guides the choice. Nitrofurantoin is not for infants under 1 month or for pyelonephritis. Treat 7 to 10 days in young children, 3 to 5 days for a simple adolescent case.
Pyelonephritis or sepsis Ceftriaxone, or ampicillin plus gentamicin Infants under 2 months are almost always admitted for IV treatment and a sepsis workup. Step down to oral once improving and culture data are back.
VUR prophylaxis Low-dose TMP-SMX or nitrofurantoin, nightly Used in selected reflux patients to prevent recurrence. It is debated, weighed against resistance. Continue until reflux resolves or is repaired.

VUR management

  • Grades I to III: watch and wait, treat UTIs promptly, consider prophylaxis. Repeat the VCUG in 1 to 2 years to reassess.
  • Grades IV to V: surgical correction, either ureteral reimplantation or endoscopic Deflux injection.
  • Post-op: a urinary catheter for a few days, watch for bleeding and infection, manage pain, keep hydrated.
  • Long term: monitor blood pressure, growth, urinalysis, and periodic renal ultrasound. Existing scarring is permanent.

Enuresis, in order

  1. 1
    Rule out secondary causes. Urinalysis, bowel habits, glucose, stressors. Treat what you find.
  2. 2
    Reassure and educate. Most primary nocturnal enuresis resolves. Frame it as timing, not failure.
  3. 3
    Behavioral measures. Limit fluids 2 hours before bed, void before bed, treat constipation, reward charts, gentle night waking.
  4. 4
    Bedwetting alarm. A moisture sensor wakes the child at the start of voiding. First-line behavioral therapy, with the best long-term results. It takes 2 to 3 months, so commitment matters.
  5. 5
    Desmopressin, DDAVP. A synthetic ADH analog that cuts overnight urine. Good short-term, for sleepovers and camp. Limit fluids for 1 hour before and 8 hours after the dose.
  6. 6
    Imipramine. An older option, rarely first-line now, given cardiac side effects and overdose risk. Reserved for refractory cases, with referral to urology or nephrology.
DDAVP fluid restriction is a safety issue

Desmopressin concentrates urine by holding water. Drink too much during its window and water intoxication follows. The danger is hyponatremia and seizures. Teach parents: no fluids 1 hour before the dose, none for 8 hours after. Use it for short-term situations, not nightly without specialist guidance.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
DDAVP plus free fluids equals seizures
The whole point of DDAVP is to hold water back overnight. So the risk is not the drug, it is the water on top of it. Unrestricted fluids give you hyponatremia and seizures. The teaching is one line: nothing to drink 1 hour before, nothing for 8 hours after.
Bottom Line: UTI gets age-appropriate antibiotics. Low-grade reflux is watched, high-grade is surgery. Enuresis is rule out, then alarm first-line, DDAVP short-term.
08

Nursing and family teaching

Priority questions hand you four reasonable actions and ask which comes first. Order matters more than completeness.

  1. 1
    Workup before reassurance. A febrile infant with no source gets a urine culture, not a wait-and-see.
  2. 2
    Right specimen before treatment. No bag culture. Cath or clean catch, matched to the age, before antibiotics.
  3. 3
    Treat the infection, then ask why. A recurrent or febrile UTI earns imaging for reflux.
  4. 4
    Rule out a cause before you manage bedwetting. Secondary enuresis is worked up, not coached.
  5. 5
    Teach and support before you medicate. For primary enuresis, education and an alarm come before a drug.

UTI prevention teaching

Wipe front to back for girls, taught at every age.

Void regularly, no holding, empty the bladder every 2 to 3 hours.

Treat constipation, since a full rectum presses on the bladder.

Encourage daytime fluids to keep urine flushing through.

Avoid bubble baths and harsh soaps; use loose cotton underwear.

Adolescent girls void after intercourse; cranberry evidence is mixed.

VUR and VCUG care

  • Explain the VCUG at the child's level. The catheter goes in, contrast fills the bladder, the X-ray watches during voiding. Child life services help with the fear.
  • Address embarrassment. Older children feel exposed, so protect privacy and dignity.
  • After the VCUG, push fluids. Burning on the first void or two is normal; real pain or blood is not.
  • After surgery, do catheter care, watch for infection and bleeding, manage pain, follow activity limits.

Enuresis support

  • Start with reassurance. Most children outgrow it. Frame it as body timing, not a moral failure.
  • Involve the child, with a voiding diary, a reward chart, and their own supplies for sleepovers.
  • Use protective bedding, a waterproof mattress cover, to lower the daily stress.
  • Normalize it. About 1 in 10 seven-year-olds wets the bed; the child is not alone.
  • Coach parents to praise dry nights and stay calm about wet ones.

When to report or return

  • UTI: fever past 48 hours on antibiotics, worsening abdominal or flank pain, vomiting, new lethargy.
  • VUR post-op: fever, worse pain, bloody drainage, or inability to void after the catheter comes out.
  • Enuresis: secondary onset, daytime wetting, painful voiding, increased thirst and urination, weight loss.
You are the confidence-builder

Children with recurrent UTIs, reflux procedures, or bedwetting often feel different or bad about it. Small reassurances carry weight. This is common. You are doing everything right. It will not last forever. You usually spend more time with the family than the provider, and that time is teaching time.

Bottom Line: Teach hygiene, fluids, and constipation control. Prepare the child for the VCUG. Normalize the bedwetting, coach the parents, build the child's confidence.
