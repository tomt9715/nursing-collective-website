UTI, VUR & Enuresis

Three of the most common pediatric GU presentations — from the infant with unexplained fever to the bedwetting school-ager. Recognizing, diagnosing, and treating each without missing the connection between them.

7 Sections 14 Quiz Questions ~11 Min Read

Learning Objectives

Recognize age-specific presentations of pediatric UTI and know why infant UTI often looks like "just a fever."
Select the correct urine collection method for age; understand why bag specimens are inadequate for culture.
Understand VUR grading (I–V) and the link between reflux, pyelonephritis, and renal scarring.
Distinguish primary from secondary enuresis and identify when workup is needed.
Apply evidence-based behavioral and pharmacologic management for nocturnal enuresis.
Educate families about prevention, medication adherence, and the emotional weight of bedwetting.

High-Yield Summary

Eight facts that cover almost every pediatric GU question.

  • Pediatric UTI pathogens: E. coli accounts for ~80%. Others: Klebsiella, Proteus, Enterococcus, Staph saprophyticus.
  • Age shifts sex risk: first year of life — boys (esp. uncircumcised) are at higher risk. After age 1, girls are far more common because of shorter urethra.
  • Infant UTI often presents as fever alone — any febrile infant without a clear source needs a UA and culture. Missed UTIs in infants can become pyelonephritis with scarring.
  • Culture collection matters: bag specimens are fine for screening but NEVER for culture (contaminated). For culture: clean catch (toilet-trained), catheterization, or suprapubic aspiration.
  • VUR = retrograde urine flow from bladder back up ureters. Allows bacteria to reach kidneys → pyelonephritis → renal scarring / reflux nephropathy → HTN, CKD.
  • VCUG (voiding cystourethrogram) is the gold standard for diagnosing and grading VUR. Grades I–V; grades I–III often resolve spontaneously, IV–V often need surgery.
  • Enuresis = involuntary voiding after age 5. Primary = never dry. Secondary = dry for ≥6 months, then re-started. Most cases are primary nocturnal (bedwetting) due to maturational delay.
  • Bedwetting alarms are the most effective long-term therapy for primary nocturnal enuresis. Desmopressin (DDAVP) works short-term (sleepovers, camp). Never shame the child — it delays resolution.

Pediatric Urinary Tract Infection

From fever-only infants to dysuric teens

Pediatric UTI

Infection

Epidemiology

  • By age 6: ~7% of girls, ~2% of boys will have had a UTI.
  • First year of life: boys (especially uncircumcised) are at higher risk.
  • After age 1: girls predominate — shorter urethra = easier bacterial ascent.
  • Adolescent girls: onset of sexual activity increases risk.

Common Pathogens

  • E. coli ~80% of pediatric UTIs (from bowel flora)
  • Klebsiella, Proteus — common in structural abnormalities or recent antibiotics
  • Enterococcus, Staph saprophyticus — adolescent females
  • Pseudomonas — hospitalized, immunocompromised, or catheterized

Risk Factors

  • Vesicoureteral reflux (VUR), anatomic abnormalities, neurogenic bladder
  • Constipation — huge risk factor, often overlooked
  • Poor voiding habits (holding, rushing)
  • Bubble baths, harsh soaps (irritation)
  • Poor hygiene, wiping incorrectly
  • Uncircumcised males (first year)
  • Sexual activity (adolescents)

Classification

  • Cystitis (lower UTI): bladder infection — dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain. Usually no fever.
  • Pyelonephritis (upper UTI): kidney infection — fever ≥38.5°C, flank pain, CVA tenderness, systemic illness. Much more serious; risk of scarring.

Signs & Symptoms by Age

Age Group Typical Presentation
Infants (<2 months) Fever, poor feeding, vomiting, irritability, jaundice, sepsis-like illness, FTT. NOT typical UTI symptoms.
Infants (2–24 months) Fever often the only sign; vomiting, poor feeding, foul-smelling urine, irritability, FTT.
Preschoolers / Toddlers Fever, abdominal pain, vomiting, malodorous urine, new-onset incontinence after being dry, dysuria if verbal.
School-age & Adolescents Classic adult-like symptoms: dysuria, frequency, urgency, suprapubic pain, hematuria, sometimes enuresis. Pyelonephritis adds fever and flank pain.
Fever-Without-a-Source in Infants

Any febrile infant under 24 months without an obvious source (no URI, no ear infection, no viral rash) needs a urine culture. UTI is the most common serious bacterial infection in young infants, and missed UTIs lead to pyelonephritis with permanent renal scarring. Don't assume it's "just a virus" in a febrile baby.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Fever Is the UTI Symptom in Infants
Adults complain of "burning when I pee." Infants can't. For a baby, the UTI symptom is often just — fever. Sometimes paired with vomiting, irritability, or poor feeding. That's why the AAP recommends urine testing in any febrile infant under 2 years without a clear explanation. Catching UTI early prevents pyelonephritis and the renal scarring that follows.
🎯 Bottom Line: Infant UTI = fever only. Older kids = adult symptoms. E. coli is the bug. Think UTI in every unexplained febrile infant.

Vesicoureteral Reflux (VUR)

Why recurrent UTI in kids always gets a workup

Vesicoureteral Reflux

Structural

Pathophysiology

Urine normally flows one way: kidney → ureter → bladder. The vesicoureteral junction acts as a one-way valve. When the valve is incompetent, urine can flow backwards from the bladder up into the ureters and even the kidney pelvis — carrying any bacteria from the bladder with it.

Why It Matters

  • Allows a simple bladder infection to become pyelonephritis
  • Chronic pyelonephritis causes renal scarring (reflux nephropathy)
  • Long-term: hypertension, proteinuria, chronic kidney disease
  • VUR is the #1 structural cause of pediatric CKD progression

Two Types

  • Primary VUR — congenital, due to abnormal development of the vesicoureteral junction. Most common form. Often runs in families.
  • Secondary VUR — acquired from bladder outlet obstruction, neurogenic bladder, or posterior urethral valves.

Who Gets It

  • ~1% of all children
  • Up to 40% of children with febrile UTIs have underlying VUR
  • More common in girls; bilateral reflux is not uncommon
  • May be detected on prenatal ultrasound as hydronephrosis

Signs & Symptoms

  • Often asymptomatic until a UTI develops
  • Recurrent or febrile UTIs — the main red flag
  • FTT, poor feeding in infants
  • Hypertension or proteinuria in older children with scarring
  • Prenatal hydronephrosis may be the first clue

VUR Grading (I–V)

Ureter Only

Reflux into the ureter, no dilation.

Pelvis, No Dilation

Reflux to the renal pelvis without dilation.

Mild Dilation

Mild ureter and pelvis dilation, calyces sharp.

Moderate

Moderate dilation; blunting of calyces.

Severe

Severe dilation + tortuous ureter, papillary impressions lost.

Grade = Treatment Approach

Grades I–III typically resolve spontaneously over months to years as the child grows — managed with close monitoring and prompt UTI treatment, sometimes prophylactic antibiotics (controversial).
Grades IV–V rarely resolve on their own — usually require surgical correction: ureteral reimplantation or endoscopic injection (Deflux).

🎯 Bottom Line: VUR = backwards urine flow that sets up pyelonephritis and scarring. Diagnose with VCUG. Low grade = watch. High grade = surgery.

Enuresis (Bedwetting & Daytime Wetting)

Primary vs. secondary — and why you never shame the child

Enuresis

Involuntary Voiding

Definition

Involuntary voiding of urine beyond the age when continence is expected — conventionally after age 5. Common, benign, and frustrating. About 15% of 5-year-olds and 10% of 7-year-olds have nocturnal enuresis; most resolve by age 10.

Types

  • Primary enuresis: the child has never been consistently dry.
  • Secondary enuresis: was dry for ≥6 months, then started wetting again — often triggered by stress, UTI, diabetes, constipation, or abuse.
  • Nocturnal enuresis: only at night (most common form).
  • Diurnal enuresis: daytime wetting; more concerning — always evaluate for underlying cause.

Causes of Primary Nocturnal Enuresis

  • Maturational delay — the most common cause. The ability to wake to a full bladder lags behind peers; eventually catches up.
  • Strong family history (if one parent had enuresis, ~40% risk in child; both parents ~75%).
  • Deep sleep / difficulty arousing from sleep.
  • Smaller functional bladder capacity.
  • Decreased nighttime ADH production (inadequate urine concentration overnight).

Causes of Secondary Enuresis (always work up)

  • UTI
  • Constipation (full rectum compresses bladder)
  • Type 1 diabetes (polyuria from osmotic diuresis)
  • Diabetes insipidus
  • Structural or neurologic disorder
  • Emotional stress, family conflict, abuse, new sibling, divorce
Never Shame or Punish

Bedwetting is not under the child's voluntary control. Shaming, punishment, and blame worsen anxiety, lower self-esteem, and delay resolution. Parents should be coached to normalize the situation, praise dry nights, and avoid making the child feel bad about wet ones. The nurse's teaching here is often more valuable than any medication.

FlorenceBot Pro Tip
Secondary Enuresis Is a Red Flag
A child who had been dry for months or years and suddenly starts wetting again deserves a workup. Common causes: UTI, new-onset type 1 diabetes, constipation, emotional trauma. Test urine (dipstick, glucose, culture), ask about bowel habits, and screen for stressors. This is rarely "nothing."
🎯 Bottom Line: Enuresis after age 5 = pathologic. Primary (never dry) = maturational. Secondary (was dry, now wet) = work it up. Never shame.

Diagnosis & Workup

How to collect urine safely, and when to image

Urine Collection by Age

Method When to Use Notes
Bag specimen Screening only in non-toilet-trained infants High contamination rate; NEVER use for culture alone. Positive bag UA → confirm with cath/SPA.
Catheterization (in/out) Definitive culture in non-toilet-trained infants Sterile sample; positive culture >50,000 CFU is diagnostic.
Suprapubic aspiration Gold standard in infants Direct needle aspiration of bladder; any growth = diagnostic.
Clean catch midstream Toilet-trained children Clean perineum; start void, collect midstream. >100,000 CFU diagnostic.

Urinalysis & Culture

  • UA dipstick: leukocyte esterase (+), nitrites (+), hematuria. Nitrites are highly specific for gram-negative UTI (especially E. coli).
  • UA microscopy: >5 WBC/hpf (pyuria), bacteria, RBCs.
  • Urine culture: gold standard. Growth thresholds depend on collection method.
  • CBC, CRP: leukocytosis and elevated CRP suggest pyelonephritis.
  • Blood culture: in infants with suspected urosepsis.

Imaging After UTI

  • Renal ultrasound — initial imaging to rule out obstruction or anatomic abnormalities.
  • VCUG — voiding cystourethrogram. Gold standard for diagnosing VUR. Contrast instilled via catheter into bladder; fluoroscopy during voiding reveals presence and grade of reflux.
  • DMSA scan — detects renal scarring from pyelonephritis; sometimes used to identify cortical damage.

When to Image

  • First febrile UTI in a child <2 years (renal US, sometimes VCUG)
  • Recurrent UTIs at any age
  • Atypical UTI course (poor response to appropriate antibiotic, non-E. coli organism, abnormal exam)
  • Any suspicion of anatomic anomaly

Enuresis Workup

  • History: pattern (nocturnal vs diurnal), primary vs secondary, family history, bowel habits, fluid intake, stressors.
  • Physical exam: abdomen for fecal mass, external genitalia, spine (sacral dimple/tuft), neuro for lower extremities.
  • Urinalysis — rule out UTI, glucose (diabetes), concentration.
  • Voiding diary — record wet/dry nights and daytime voids for 1–2 weeks.
  • Further testing (renal US, urodynamics, labs) for atypical or refractory cases.
🎯 Bottom Line: Cath or SPA for infant cultures; clean catch for older kids. Bag is screen-only. VCUG for VUR. UA + history for enuresis.

Treatment

Antibiotics, reflux management, and enuresis strategies

UTI Antibiotics

Oral First-Line
Outpatient Cystitis

Cephalexin, amoxicillin-clavulanate, TMP-SMX, or nitrofurantoin (not in <1 month or pyelonephritis).

Local resistance patterns guide choice.

Typical duration: 7–10 days for young children; 3–5 days for adolescents with simple cystitis.

IV First-Line
Pyelonephritis / Sepsis

Ceftriaxone or ampicillin + gentamicin.

Infants <2 months: almost always admitted for IV treatment and rule-out sepsis workup.

Transition to oral once clinically improving and culture data available.

Prophylactic Antibiotics
VUR Recurrence Prevention

Low-dose TMP-SMX or nitrofurantoin nightly to prevent recurrent UTIs in selected VUR patients.

Use is controversial — benefits weighed against resistance and side effects.

Typically continued until reflux resolves or surgical repair.

VUR Management

  • Grades I–III: watch and wait; treat UTIs promptly; consider prophylactic antibiotics. Follow-up VCUG in 1–2 years to reassess.
  • Grades IV–V: surgical correction:
    • Ureteral reimplantation (open or laparoscopic) — highly effective
    • Endoscopic injection (Deflux) — minimally invasive bulking agent at the ureteral orifice
  • Post-op care: urinary catheter for days, monitor for bleeding/infection, pain management, hydration.
  • Long-term monitoring: BP, growth, urinalysis, periodic renal US; scarring is permanent even after reflux is fixed.

Enuresis Management

  1. Rule out secondary causes — UA, review bowel habits, glucose, stressors. Treat underlying cause if found.
  2. Reassurance & education — most primary nocturnal enuresis resolves on its own; frame as a developmental issue, not a failure.
  3. Behavioral measures: limit fluids 2 hours before bed, void before bed, treat constipation, reward charts for dry nights, gentle night awakening to void.
  4. Bedwetting (enuresis) alarm — moisture sensor wakes the child at the start of voiding. First-line behavioral therapy with the best long-term success rates. Takes 2–3 months to work; commitment is essential.
  5. Desmopressin (DDAVP) — synthetic ADH analog. Reduces overnight urine production. Effective short-term (sleepovers, camp). Limit fluids for 1 hour before and 8 hours after the dose to avoid hyponatremia and seizures.
  6. Imipramine — older option; rarely first-line now due to cardiac side effects and overdose risk. Reserved for refractory cases.
  7. Referral — urology or pediatric nephrology for refractory or complex cases (daytime wetting, urgency, infrequent voiding).
DDAVP Fluid Restriction Is a Safety Issue

Desmopressin works by concentrating urine — but if the child drinks too much fluid during that window, water intoxication occurs. Hyponatremia and seizures are the danger. Teach parents: no fluids 1 hour before the dose, no fluids 8 hours after. Use only for short-term situations (sleepover, camping), not nightly without specialist guidance.

🎯 Bottom Line: UTI = age-appropriate antibiotics. VUR low-grade = watch, high-grade = surgery. Enuresis = rule out causes, then alarms first-line, DDAVP short-term.

Nursing Care & Family Education

Prevention, support, and confidence-building

UTI Prevention Teaching

Wipe front to back for girls — teach at every age.

Void regularly — no "holding it"; empty the bladder every 2–3 hours.

Treat constipation — a full rectum presses on the bladder and raises UTI risk.

Encourage adequate fluids during the day.

Avoid bubble baths and harsh soaps; loose-fitting cotton underwear.

Adolescent girls: void after intercourse; consider cranberry products with varying evidence.

During and After a UTI

  • Administer antibiotics on schedule; finish the full course even if symptoms improve.
  • Encourage fluids to dilute urine and flush the bladder.
  • Monitor for fever, improvement of symptoms, and side effects of antibiotics.
  • Report persistent fever, worsening symptoms, or reappearance of symptoms after treatment completion.

VUR & VCUG Care

  • Explain the VCUG in age-appropriate terms: catheter goes into the bladder, contrast goes in, X-ray watches while the child voids. Child life services can help with anxiety.
  • Address embarrassment — older kids may feel exposed. Maintain privacy and dignity.
  • Post-VCUG: fluids to flush, watch for burning (common for first void), dysuria for 1–2 voids is normal; significant pain or blood is not.
  • If surgery: post-op catheter care, monitor for infection/bleeding, pain management, activity restrictions.

Enuresis Support

  • Start with reassurance. Most children outgrow bedwetting. Frame it as a body timing issue, not a moral failure.
  • Involve the child in management — voiding diary, reward chart, carrying supplies to friends' houses.
  • Use protective bedding (waterproof mattress cover). Practical supports reduce stress.
  • Normalize with the child: 1 in 10 seven-year-olds wets the bed — they're not alone.
  • Coach parents to praise dry nights generously and avoid shaming wet ones.
  • Provide information about support groups and online resources (AAP family-friendly resources).

When to Report / Return to Clinic

  • UTI: fever persists >48 hours on antibiotics, worsening abdominal/flank pain, vomiting, new-onset lethargy.
  • VUR post-op: fever, worsening pain, bloody drainage, inability to void after catheter removal.
  • Enuresis: secondary onset, daytime wetting, pain with voiding, increased thirst/urination, weight loss.
The Nurse Is the Confidence-Builder

Children with recurrent UTIs, reflux procedures, or bedwetting often feel "different" or "bad." Small reassurances — "this is really common," "you're doing everything right," "this won't last forever" — have outsized effects on compliance and self-esteem. You often spend more time with the child and family than the provider; that time is teaching time.

🎯 Bottom Line: Teach hygiene, fluid, and constipation prevention. Support VCUG experiences. Normalize bedwetting and coach parents. Build confidence.
