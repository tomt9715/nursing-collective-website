What to Put in Your Clinical Bag

The complete checklist: essentials you need to buy, what school provides, and the extras that make life easier.

The Short Version

  • Must-haves: Stethoscope, penlight, black pens, watch with second hand, badge, pocket reference.
  • School provides (usually): BP cuffs, practice supplies, PPE, clinical paperwork.
  • Nice-to-haves: Extra scrub top, compression socks, snacks, lunch, portable charger.
  • Pack your bag the night before and keep it ready to grab.
  • Less is more—hospitals may have limited storage space, especially for jackets and bulky items.
1

The Essentials (You Need to Buy)

These are items you'll use constantly and should own yourself. Most of these will serve you through nursing school and into your career.

Must-Have Items
  • Stethoscope A Littmann Classic III is the go-to, but any quality stethoscope works. Don't go too cheap—you need to actually hear things.
  • Penlight For pupil checks and looking in mouths/wounds. A cheap one from Amazon works fine.
  • Black pens (several) Pens disappear. Bring at least 3. Black ink only for documentation.
  • Watch with second hand For counting respirations and pulses. Can be analog or digital, just needs seconds visible. Nurses' fob watches are great if you can't wear wristwatches.
  • ID badge and badge holder Usually provided by school, but you may need a retractable badge reel.
  • Pocket reference or app Drug guide, lab values, assessment reference. Can be a pocket book or an app on your phone (check if phones are allowed at your site). Epocrates is a popular free app for drug lookups.
  • Small notepad For jotting patient info, vitals, reminders. Index cards work too.
  • Permanent marker (Sharpie) For labeling IV tubing, dating dressings, etc.
Pro Tip

Write your name on everything, especially your stethoscope. Use a stethoscope ID tag or permanent marker on the tubing. Stethoscopes walk away from clinical sites constantly.

2

What Your School Usually Provides

Most nursing programs provide supplies for skills lab and practice. These typically stay at school or in lab—you don't need to buy your own.

School-Provided (Usually)
  • Blood pressure cuffs Manual BP cuffs for practice. Some schools issue personal ones; check with your program.
  • Scrub top with school logo Required uniform top. You'll likely need to buy pants separately.
  • Practice medications (fake meds) For medication administration practice in lab.
  • IV bags, tubing, and supplies For IV skills practice. Provided in lab.
  • Needles and syringes For injection practice. Never take these from lab.
  • Silicone practice pads For injection and IV practice at home or in open lab.
  • Foley catheter kits For catheterization practice.
  • PPE (gloves, masks, gowns) Provided at clinical sites. You shouldn't need to buy your own.
  • Clinical paperwork and forms Assessment sheets, care plan templates, clinical logs.
Check Your Program

Every program is different. Some require you to buy your own BP cuff and stethoscope kit. Others provide everything. Check your syllabus or ask your clinical instructor before buying duplicates of what's provided.

3

Nice-to-Have Extras

These aren't required, but they make clinical life easier. Add them as you figure out what you actually use.

Don't Bring These

You don't need to bring bandage scissors, hemostats, or other medical supplies—hospitals always have these available. Same with bandages, tape, and other wound care supplies. The facility will provide what you need for patient care.

Nice-to-Have Items
  • Extra scrub top In case something gets on your uniform. Keep one in your car.
  • Compression socks Your feet and legs will thank you. Seriously consider these.
  • Lunch If you're not planning to go out. Pack something easy to eat quickly during your break.
  • Snacks Granola bars, crackers, anything you can eat quickly. You will get hungry.
  • Water bottle Stay hydrated. Get one you can clip to your bag.
  • Portable phone charger Long clinical days drain batteries. Have backup power.
  • Hair ties / bobby pins Hair must be up and secured. Bring extras.
  • Breath mints / gum You'll be close to patients. Fresh breath matters.
  • Deodorant Clinical days are long and physical. Keep some in your bag.
  • Small first aid items Band-aids, ibuprofen, antacids for yourself. Just in case.
From Someone Who's Been There

I didn't bring snacks my first clinical and nearly passed out by 11am. Now I always have a granola bar in my pocket. Also, compression socks changed my life—my feet hurt so much less at the end of the day.

4

What NOT to Bring

Some things seem helpful but aren't worth the hassle or are actively problematic:

  • Expensive jewelry: Leave it at home. You shouldn't wear it anyway, and clinical bags get set down in random places.
  • Your entire drug guide: The full textbook is too heavy. Bring a pocket version or use an app.
  • Too many "just in case" items: You're not preparing for the apocalypse. If you haven't used something in 3 clinical days, take it out.
  • Anything with strong scents: No perfume, cologne, or heavily scented lotions. Patients and staff can be sensitive.
  • Valuables: Your bag may sit in a break room or locker. Don't bring anything you'd be devastated to lose.
The Pocket Test

Most of what you actually need should fit in your scrub pockets: stethoscope around your neck, penlight, pens, notepad, phone. Your bag is for backup supplies and personal items—not everything you might ever need.

5

Choosing the Right Bag

Your clinical bag doesn't need to be fancy, but it should be practical:

  • Size: Big enough for your supplies, small enough to fit in a locker. A medium backpack or large tote works well.
  • Washable: Clinical settings are not clean. You want something you can wipe down or throw in the wash.
  • Pockets/organization: Multiple compartments help you find things quickly instead of digging.
  • Comfortable straps: If it's a backpack, padded straps matter on long days.
  • Neutral color: Black, navy, or gray. Easier to keep looking clean than white or light colors.

You don't need a special "nursing bag." A regular backpack works fine. Some students use small rolling bags if they have a lot to carry, but most facilities don't have much space for large bags.

Storage Space Reality

Many hospitals have very limited space for student belongings. Lockers might be small or shared. If you live somewhere cold, think carefully about your jacket—there may not be a good place to store a bulky winter coat. Pack accordingly and leave what you can in your car.

6

Keeping Your Bag Ready

The key to a stress-free clinical morning is having your bag ready to grab. Here's how to maintain it:

  • Restock after every clinical: Check your bag that evening. Replace anything you used or ran out of.
  • Clean it regularly: Wipe down the outside weekly. Wash it monthly or whenever it looks dirty.
  • Check expiration dates: If you keep medications (for yourself) or snacks, rotate them out.
  • Keep it in the same place: By the door, in your car, wherever—but always the same spot so you grab it automatically.
  • Do a "dry run" before your first clinical: Pack everything, wear your full uniform, make sure nothing is missing.
Night-Before Routine

Every night before clinical, I do a quick check: stethoscope, badge, pens, phone charger, snacks, water. Takes 30 seconds. Beats the panic of realizing you forgot something at 5am.

Quick Reference Checklist

Print this out or screenshot it for a quick pre-clinical check:

Pre-Clinical Bag Check
  • Stethoscope
  • Penlight
  • Black pens (3+)
  • Watch with second hand
  • ID badge
  • Pocket reference/phone
  • Notepad or index cards
  • Sharpie
  • Snacks
  • Water bottle
  • Clinical paperwork
  • Phone charger

Everything else is bonus. If you have these items, you're ready for clinical.
