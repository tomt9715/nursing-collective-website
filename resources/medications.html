How to Study Medications

Focus on drug classes, not individual drugs. Once you understand how beta-blockers work, you can apply that knowledge to any "-olol" drug. Look for patterns: suffix clues (like -pril, -olol, -statin) often tell you the drug class.

Cardiac Medications

Blood pressure control, heart rate management, and cardiac protection

Metoprolol (Lopressor)
Beta-blocker (-olol)
HTN, angina, post-MI, heart failure, arrhythmias
  • Hold if HR <60 or SBP <100
  • Monitor for bradycardia, fatigue, dizziness
  • Don't stop abruptly - taper off
  • Masks hypoglycemia symptoms in diabetics
Lisinopril (Zestril)
ACE Inhibitor (-pril)
HTN, heart failure, diabetic nephropathy
  • Watch for angioedema (swelling of lips/tongue)
  • Dry cough is common side effect
  • Monitor potassium (can cause hyperkalemia)
  • Contraindicated in pregnancy
Losartan (Cozaar)
ARB (-sartan)
HTN, heart failure (when ACE-I not tolerated)
  • Similar to ACE-I but NO dry cough
  • Still monitor potassium
  • Contraindicated in pregnancy
  • Good alternative if ACE-I cough occurs
Amlodipine (Norvasc)
Calcium Channel Blocker (-dipine)
HTN, angina
  • Peripheral edema (ankle swelling) common
  • Can cause headache, flushing
  • Avoid grapefruit juice
  • Works well with other BP meds
Digoxin (Lanoxin)
Cardiac Glycoside
Heart failure, atrial fibrillation (rate control)
  • Hold if HR <60 - ALWAYS check apical pulse
  • Narrow therapeutic range (0.5-2.0 ng/mL)
  • Toxicity signs: N/V, visual changes (yellow-green halos)
  • Low K+ increases toxicity risk

Diuretics

Fluid management for HTN, heart failure, and edema

Furosemide (Lasix)
Loop Diuretic
Edema, heart failure, HTN, acute pulmonary edema
  • Causes potassium loss - monitor K+
  • Monitor I&O, daily weights
  • Can cause ototoxicity (especially IV push too fast)
  • Give in AM to avoid nighttime urination
Hydrochlorothiazide (HCTZ)
Thiazide Diuretic
HTN (often first-line), mild edema
  • Also causes potassium loss
  • Increases sun sensitivity
  • Can increase blood glucose
  • Take in morning
Spironolactone (Aldactone)
Potassium-Sparing Diuretic
Heart failure, HTN, ascites, hyperaldosteronism
  • RETAINS potassium - watch for hyperkalemia
  • Don't give with K+ supplements or ACE-I without monitoring
  • Can cause gynecomastia in men
  • Often paired with loop diuretics to balance K+

Pain Medications

Analgesics from mild OTC options to controlled substances

Acetaminophen (Tylenol)
Non-opioid Analgesic/Antipyretic
Mild-moderate pain, fever
  • Max 3-4g/day in healthy adults (less with liver disease)
  • Hidden in many combo products - check for duplicates
  • No anti-inflammatory effect
  • Safe for most patients including pregnancy
Ibuprofen (Advil, Motrin)
NSAID
Pain, inflammation, fever
  • Take with food - GI bleeding risk
  • Avoid in renal impairment, heart failure
  • Can increase BP and counteract antihypertensives
  • Avoid in 3rd trimester pregnancy
Morphine
Opioid Analgesic (Schedule II)
Moderate-severe pain, MI chest pain, dyspnea in palliative care
  • Respiratory depression - monitor RR (hold if <12)
  • Keep Narcan (naloxone) available
  • Causes constipation - order stool softener
  • Causes sedation, hypotension, N/V
Hydrocodone/APAP (Norco)
Opioid Combo (Schedule II)
Moderate pain
  • Contains acetaminophen - watch total daily dose
  • Same opioid precautions as morphine
  • Common post-op and discharge pain med
  • High abuse potential
Tramadol (Ultram)
Opioid-like Analgesic (Schedule IV)
Moderate pain
  • Lower abuse potential but still controlled
  • Seizure risk, especially with SSRIs
  • Serotonin syndrome risk with antidepressants
  • Can cause N/V, dizziness, constipation

GI Medications

Acid reduction, nausea control, and bowel management

Omeprazole (Prilosec)
Proton Pump Inhibitor (-prazole)
GERD, peptic ulcer, H. pylori (with antibiotics)
  • Take 30-60 min before meals
  • Long-term use: fracture risk, B12/Mg deficiency
  • Can interfere with Plavix absorption
  • Most effective acid suppressant
Famotidine (Pepcid)
H2 Blocker
GERD, heartburn, stress ulcer prevention
  • Less potent than PPIs but fewer long-term concerns
  • Can cause headache, dizziness
  • Adjust dose in renal impairment
  • Often used PRN for breakthrough symptoms
Ondansetron (Zofran)
Antiemetic (5-HT3 Antagonist)
Nausea/vomiting (post-op, chemo, general)
  • Can cause headache, constipation
  • QT prolongation risk at high doses
  • ODT (dissolving tablet) good for active vomiting
  • Very commonly used - safe and effective
Docusate (Colace)
Stool Softener
Constipation prevention (especially with opioids)
  • Takes 1-3 days to work
  • Prevention, not treatment of acute constipation
  • Encourage fluids
  • Almost always ordered with opioids

Antibiotics

Common anti-infectives you'll see frequently

Amoxicillin
Penicillin-type
Ear infections, strep throat, UTI, respiratory infections
  • Check penicillin allergy - can be life-threatening
  • Can cause diarrhea, rash
  • Take full course even if feeling better
  • Safe in pregnancy
Azithromycin (Z-pack)
Macrolide
Respiratory infections, STIs, skin infections
  • QT prolongation risk
  • GI upset common
  • Often used if penicillin allergy
  • Convenient 5-day course
Ciprofloxacin (Cipro)
Fluoroquinolone (-floxacin)
UTI, respiratory, GI infections
  • Tendon rupture risk - stop if tendon pain
  • Avoid in children, pregnancy
  • Don't take with dairy or antacids
  • Can cause CNS effects (dizziness, confusion)
Ceftriaxone (Rocephin)
Cephalosporin (3rd gen)
Serious infections: pneumonia, meningitis, gonorrhea
  • Cross-reactivity with penicillin allergy (~1%)
  • Given IV or IM
  • Can cause diarrhea
  • Broad spectrum - common in hospital
Vancomycin
Glycopeptide
MRSA, serious gram-positive infections, C. diff (PO)
  • Red Man Syndrome if infused too fast - slow the rate
  • Monitor trough levels and renal function
  • Ototoxic and nephrotoxic
  • IV for systemic, PO for C. diff only

Respiratory Medications

Bronchodilators and corticosteroids for airway management

Albuterol (ProAir, Ventolin)
Short-Acting Beta-2 Agonist (SABA)
Acute bronchospasm, asthma rescue, COPD exacerbation
  • Fast-acting (minutes) - RESCUE inhaler
  • Causes tachycardia, tremors, nervousness
  • Rinse mouth after use
  • If using >2x/week, asthma not controlled
Fluticasone (Flovent)
Inhaled Corticosteroid (ICS)
Asthma maintenance, COPD
  • NOT for acute attacks - maintenance only
  • Rinse mouth to prevent thrush
  • Takes days-weeks for full effect
  • Use spacer for better delivery
Prednisone
Oral Corticosteroid
Asthma/COPD exacerbations, inflammation, allergic reactions
  • Don't stop abruptly if >7-10 days - taper
  • Raises blood glucose
  • Can cause insomnia, mood changes, increased appetite
  • Long-term: osteoporosis, adrenal suppression

Anticoagulants

Blood thinners for clot prevention and treatment

Heparin
Anticoagulant (IV/SubQ)
DVT/PE treatment and prevention, ACS, dialysis
  • HIGH ALERT medication - double check dose
  • Monitor PTT (goal usually 1.5-2.5x normal)
  • Antidote: Protamine sulfate
  • Watch for HIT (heparin-induced thrombocytopenia)
Enoxaparin (Lovenox)
Low Molecular Weight Heparin
DVT prophylaxis, DVT/PE treatment
  • SubQ injection - don't rub site after
  • No routine monitoring needed (unlike heparin)
  • Adjust dose in renal impairment
  • Common post-op DVT prophylaxis
Warfarin (Coumadin)
Vitamin K Antagonist
A-fib stroke prevention, mechanical valves, DVT/PE
  • Monitor INR (goal usually 2-3)
  • Many drug and food interactions (vitamin K foods)
  • Antidote: Vitamin K
  • Takes 3-5 days to reach effect
Aspirin (ASA)
Antiplatelet
MI/stroke prevention, acute MI
  • Low dose (81mg) for prevention
  • GI bleeding risk - take with food
  • Hold before surgery as directed
  • Chew during acute MI for faster absorption

Diabetes Medications

Insulin and oral agents for blood glucose control

Regular Insulin (Humulin R)
Short-Acting Insulin
Mealtime coverage, sliding scale, IV for DKA
  • HIGH ALERT - always double check dose
  • Onset 30-60 min, peak 2-4 hrs
  • Give 30 min before meals
  • Only insulin that can be given IV
Insulin Lispro (Humalog)
Rapid-Acting Insulin
Mealtime coverage
  • HIGH ALERT medication
  • Onset 15 min, peak 1-2 hrs
  • Give within 15 min of meal start
  • Can give right before or after eating
Insulin Glargine (Lantus)
Long-Acting Insulin
Basal coverage (background insulin)
  • HIGH ALERT - never give IV
  • Do NOT mix with other insulins
  • No peak - lasts ~24 hours
  • Give at same time daily
Metformin (Glucophage)
Biguanide
Type 2 diabetes (often first-line)
  • Hold before contrast dye procedures
  • Lactic acidosis risk (rare but serious)
  • GI upset common - take with food
  • Doesn't cause hypoglycemia when used alone
