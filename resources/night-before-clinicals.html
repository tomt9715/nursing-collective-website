How to Prepare the Night Before Clinicals

Whether you have patient access or you're going in blind, here's how to set yourself up for a good clinical day.

The Short Version

  • Many students don't get patient access the night before—only the professor sees charts.
  • If you're going in blind, you can still prepare. Focus on general readiness.
  • If you DO have access, research efficiently—don't try to know everything.
  • Pack your bag, lay out your clothes, and get to bed early. Seriously.
  • Morning-of routines matter. Give yourself buffer time.
1

The Reality: Many Students Go In Blind

Here's something that surprises a lot of students: at many programs, you don't get to see your patient's chart the night before. Only your professor has access to hospital records. You show up in the morning, get your assignment, and figure it out from there.

If that sounds stressful, it is—at first. But it's also how real nursing works much of the time. Nurses get patients they've never seen before. They adapt. You will too.

The Reality Check

Don't assume everyone else has more information than you. In many clinical groups, everyone is equally in the dark until morning. If classmates seem more prepared, they might just be better at faking confidence (a useful nursing skill, actually).

This guide covers both scenarios: what to do when you have patient access, and what to do when you don't.

2

Your First Day: What to Expect

If this is your very first clinical day ever, take a breath. You most likely won't be thrown straight into patient care.

First days are typically about orientation:

  • Tours and layout: You'll learn where things are—supply rooms, medication rooms, break rooms, bathrooms, emergency equipment.
  • Introductions: Meeting the staff, understanding the unit culture, learning who to go to for what.
  • Paperwork and logistics: Badge access, locker assignments, documentation systems.
  • Observation and shadowing: Following nurses or your instructor around, watching how things work.

Later clinical days will gradually involve more patient interaction—but even then, you typically won't be "assigned" a patient the way a nurse is. Instead, you'll follow along with a nurse or your instructor, or get rotated to different areas for varied experiences.

How Clinical Structure Usually Works

Students don't usually get independent patient assignments. You might be paired with a nurse and help with their patients, work with your instructor as a group, or rotate between different areas of the unit. The structure varies by program and clinical site, so ask your instructor what to expect.

3

If You DON'T Have Patient Access

Going in blind doesn't mean you can't prepare. Here's what you can do:

General Knowledge Prep
  • Review common conditions for your unit. Med-surg? Brush up on heart failure, COPD, diabetes. Peds? Review developmental stages and common childhood illnesses.
  • Review your skills. What have you covered in lab recently? Mentally walk through those procedures.
  • Review medications. Know the "big" drug classes—cardiac meds, blood thinners, insulin, pain meds, antibiotics.
  • Review normal vital signs for the population you're seeing (adults vs. peds vs. geriatric).
Practical Prep
  • Pack your clinical bag with everything you might need.
  • Lay out your uniform. Check for stains, wrinkles. Know where your ID badge is.
  • Review any paperwork your instructor requires (assessment forms, care plan templates).
  • Know your morning timeline. What time do you need to leave? Build in buffer time.
  • Get to bed early. You need to be alert, not caffeinated and foggy.
Pro Tip

Make a "cheat sheet" of things you always want to remember—normal lab values, vital sign ranges, common med side effects. Keep it in your pocket for quick reference. This is studying that helps you no matter who your patient is.

4

If You DO Have Patient Access

Lucky you—but don't fall into the trap of trying to know everything. You have limited time. Be strategic.

What to Look Up First
  • Admitting diagnosis: Why are they here? What's the main problem?
  • Relevant history: Other conditions that affect care (diabetes, heart disease, kidney issues).
  • Current medications: Focus on the scheduled ones. Know what they're for and major side effects.
  • Allergies: Critical. Know these cold.
  • Recent vitals and trends: What's their baseline? Anything concerning?
  • Today's plan: Any procedures, tests, or discharges planned?

Don't try to memorize the entire chart. You'll overwhelm yourself. Focus on what helps you give safe care and anticipate needs.

From Someone Who's Been There

I used to spend hours reading every note in the chart. Then I'd show up exhausted and forget half of it. Now I focus on: diagnosis, meds, allergies, and what to watch for. That's what actually matters for one clinical day.

Medication Research Shortcut

  • For each scheduled med, know: What is it for? Major side effects? Nursing considerations?
  • Don't memorize PRN meds unless they're being used frequently.
  • Flag anything you'll need to look up before giving (like insulin dosing or controlled substances).
5

The Night-Before Checklist (Everyone)

Regardless of patient access, do these things the night before:

  1. Pack your clinical bag. Check that you have everything—stethoscope, penlight, pens, pocket reference, snacks, water bottle. See our clinical bag guide for the full list.
  2. Prepare your uniform. Lay it out. Make sure it's clean, pressed, and complete (including socks, appropriate shoes, and ID badge).
  3. Set multiple alarms. One is not enough. Set two on your phone plus a backup if you have one. Place one across the room so you have to get up.
  4. Plan your morning meal. Have something ready to grab. You need fuel but you don't want to spend precious morning time cooking.
  5. Know your route. If it's your first time at the site, know exactly where to go, where to park, and how long it takes.
  6. Complete any paperwork. Fill out what you can on any clinical paperwork so you're not scrambling.
  7. Go to bed early. Aim for 7-8 hours. You're not going to perform well running on 4 hours of sleep and anxiety.
Don't Do This

Pulling an all-nighter to "prepare more" backfires. Sleep deprivation impairs judgment, slows your thinking, and makes you more anxious. A well-rested student who knows less will outperform an exhausted student who read everything.

6

The Morning Of

Your morning routine sets the tone for the whole day. Here's how to start strong:

  • Wake up with buffer time. You should have at least 30 minutes more than you "need." Things go wrong. Alarms don't go off. Traffic happens.
  • Eat something. Even if you're nervous and not hungry. Your brain needs glucose. A banana and peanut butter is better than nothing.
  • Do a quick review. If you prepped the night before, skim your notes while eating. Don't try to learn new things—just reinforce what you already know.
  • Double-check your bag. Quick visual scan: stethoscope, badge, pens, phone, paperwork.
  • Arrive early. 15-20 minutes early is ideal. It shows professionalism and gives you time to settle in, use the bathroom, and calm your nerves.
Nervous? Try This

In the car or before you walk in, take 5 slow deep breaths. Inhale for 4 counts, hold for 4, exhale for 6. This activates your parasympathetic nervous system and genuinely reduces anxiety. It's not just "woo-woo"—it's physiology.

7

Once You Get Your Assignment

You've arrived, you're in your scrubs, and now you have a patient. Here's your rapid morning prep:

  1. Get the essentials first: Room number, name, diagnosis, allergies, code status. Write these down immediately.
  2. Check the MAR: What meds are due during your shift? Any that need to be given soon?
  3. Review recent notes: What happened overnight? Any changes? New orders?
  4. Check vitals trends: What's their baseline? Anything out of range?
  5. Ask the nurse: "Anything I should know about this patient?" They'll tell you the stuff not in the chart.
  6. Make a plan: What's your priority? What do you need to do first?

Then go introduce yourself to your patient. From there, you're in clinical mode—one task at a time, asking for help when needed.

8

When Plans Change (And They Will)

Sometimes your patient gets discharged before you arrive. Sometimes they're in a procedure all morning. Sometimes you get reassigned to someone completely different. This is normal.

How to handle it:

  • Don't panic. This happens to everyone. It's not a reflection of you.
  • Ask your instructor: "What would you like me to do?" They'll reassign you or find you another learning opportunity.
  • Stay flexible. The ability to adapt is a core nursing skill. Today is practice.
  • Use downtime wisely. If you're waiting for a new assignment, observe, review your notes, or ask if you can help with another student's patient.
Remember This

The students who do best in clinicals aren't the ones who never face problems—they're the ones who handle problems calmly. Flexibility and adaptability are more valuable than perfect preparation.
